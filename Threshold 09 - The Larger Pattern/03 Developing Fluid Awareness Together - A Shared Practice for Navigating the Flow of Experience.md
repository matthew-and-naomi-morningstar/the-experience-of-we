# Developing Fluid Awareness Together: *A Shared Practice for Navigating the Flow of Experience*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Awareness flows like water.

It moves along paths of least resistance, shaped by habit, emotion, and the terrain of past experience. When something triggers us — a word, a memory, a sensation — our awareness rushes along familiar channels, often before we realize where it's going. We find ourselves caught in reactions, stuck in old patterns, swept along by currents we didn't choose.

This happens within each of us. And it happens between us.

When we're together, our awareness flows not only within ourselves but through our connection. Your state affects mine. My reaction triggers yours. We can spiral together into reactivity, or we can learn to navigate the flow together — supporting each other in recognizing what's happening, pausing before automatic response, and choosing where our awareness goes next.

This practice builds on the awareness sequence: First we develop presence (being here — available and landed in the same moment). Then interoception (sensing what's happening inside our bodies). Then awareness (sensing what's happening between us — the relational field). Then mindfulness (observing without becoming — something is happening, but it isn't "me"). Then meta-awareness (observing our own observing).

Fluid awareness is the culmination: trajectory control — "I can guide where my awareness goes next." It removes the constraint of compulsion. We're no longer helplessly swept along by the current. We can consciously participate in awareness's flow, redirecting it toward what serves us and our relationship.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to navigate the flow of awareness with skill and intention.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or taking turns
- **Practicing the exercises** when we're both regulated enough to learn
- **Returning to this guide** when we want to work with awareness together
- **Building shared vocabulary** for talking about the flow of experience

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what fluid awareness feels like when it's happening, the quality of conscious navigation versus being swept along.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: the awareness spiral, how patterns form, and how redirection becomes possible.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate fluid awareness: the concrete steps for slowing the spiral and navigating it together.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Fluid awareness is an experience

*Fluid awareness is what it feels like when we can consciously navigate where our attention goes.*

### When awareness is fluid

There's a distinctive quality when we can navigate awareness consciously:

- **Spaciousness.** There's room between stimulus and response. We're not compressed into automatic reaction.
- **Choice.** We sense options. The automatic path isn't the only path.
- **Groundedness.** Even when awareness moves, we're not lost in it. There's a growing stability in how we relate to what arises.
- **Flow.** Awareness moves, but we're moving with it rather than being dragged by it.
- **Collaboration.** When practicing together, we sense each other's awareness and can support its movement.

### When awareness is rigid or compulsive

In contrast, when we're caught:

- **Compression.** No space between trigger and reaction. It just happens.
- **Compulsion.** We're pulled along channels we didn't choose and can't seem to exit.
- **Identification.** We're fused with what we're experiencing — we don't just feel anger, we become it.
- **Tunnel vision.** Awareness narrows to what confirms the reactive pattern.
- **Isolation.** Even together, we're caught in separate reactive streams.

### The water metaphor

Awareness is like water:
- It **flows** naturally along paths of least resistance
- It's **shaped** by the terrain it moves through (our conditioning, habits, beliefs)
- It can be **redirected** with skill, without forcing
- Over time, it **erodes old channels** and **carves new ones**
- It responds to **temperature and pressure** (our emotional states)

Every time we consciously redirect awareness, we carve new channels. Over time, these new channels become the paths of least resistance. This is how patterns change — not through force but through repeated, gentle redirection.

## Fluid awareness is a process

*Awareness operates in a continuous spiral — noticing, sense-making, and responding — and fluid awareness is the capacity to consciously participate in that spiral.*

### The three phases

Awareness operates in a continuous spiral:

**Noticing → Sense-making → Responding → Noticing...**

**Noticing:** Something enters awareness — a sensation, thought, feeling, or event. Attention lands somewhere.

**Sense-making:** We perceive and interpret what we've noticed — making meaning, recognizing patterns, forming understanding. This happens almost instantly; we perceive *as* something.

**Responding:** Something arises — a feeling, thought, impulse, or action. Usually automatic. But within responding, there's a **choice point**: a sliver of space where something other than the automatic could happen.

Then we notice again — but shaped by what just happened. Our response changed something, and we perceive a shifted landscape. This is what makes it a spiral: we return to noticing, but at a new place.

### The spiral's direction

The spiral can move in three directions:

**Integration:** We're perceiving more, understanding more flexibly, responding more creatively. We feel more open, alive, connected. The spiral moves toward greater coherence.

**Fragmentation:** We're perceiving less, interpreting more rigidly, responding more reactively. We feel closed, depleted, disconnected. The spiral moves toward dissolution.

**Reinforcement:** We're perceiving what we always perceive, interpreting the same way, responding on autopilot. We feel stuck in the familiar. The spiral maintains its existing pattern.

Direction builds momentum. Each cycle makes the next cycle more likely to continue that direction. This is why catching fragmentation early matters — and why nurturing integration matters.

### Why practice together

Practicing fluid awareness together offers unique advantages:

**Mutual witnessing.** We help each other notice what's happening — catching patterns we'd miss alone.

**Shared regulation.** When one of us is caught, the other's presence can help redirect.

**Relational patterns.** Much of our reactive awareness is triggered relationally. Practicing together addresses patterns at their source.

**Two perspectives.** Together we see more of the spiral than either could alone.

### The eight steps of fluid awareness

The spiral runs constantly and fast. These eight steps slow it down and create space for conscious participation.

**Steps 1-2** deepen **Noticing**
**Steps 3-5** deepen **Sense-making**
**Steps 6-8** deepen **Responding**

#### Deepening Noticing

**Step 1: Recognize**
Noticing what's arising — a sensation, thought, feeling, or event entering awareness. The first act of conscious participation: catching what's present.

**Step 2: Acknowledge**
Putting experience into language — giving it a name that makes it communicable and workable. "I'm feeling anxious." "There's tension in my chest." Acknowledgment brings what we've noticed into sharper focus.

#### Deepening Sense-making

**Step 3: Observe Without Judgment**
Creating space where we witness what we've noticed without evaluating it as good or bad. This interrupts the automatic rush from noticing to reactive interpretation.

**Step 4: Allow**
Letting experience be present without resistance — creating space for clearer sense-making by stopping the push against what is. Allowing is active presence, not passivity.

**Step 5: Understand**
Making sense with depth and curiosity — forming a hypothesis about why awareness is flowing this direction. What need, pattern, or history is activated? This is conscious sense-making rather than automatic interpretation.

#### Deepening Responding

**Step 6: Redirect**
Finding the choice point within responding — the moment where we can consciously choose an alternative direction. This is where the spiral's direction can change.

**Step 7: Engage**
Implementing the redirection through embodied action — not just thinking about the new direction but actually moving into it. Taking breaths, changing posture, speaking differently.

**Step 8: Reflect and Integrate**
Encoding the learning so the next cycle starts from a better place. "What did we learn? What will we do differently next time?"

#### Using the steps flexibly

The steps don't have to be used rigidly:

- Sometimes we only need a few steps — even just recognizing and acknowledging can shift the spiral
- We can enter at any step — if we already understand the pattern, we might go straight to redirect
- The steps deepen with practice — what takes effort becomes fluent over time

## Fluid awareness is a practice

*These are our best practices for developing fluid awareness together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.*

### We practice the fluid check-in

A regular practice for sharing what's flowing and moving through the steps together.

- **We create space** — 10-20 minutes, settled and present.
- **One of us shares what's flowing** in awareness — a current experience, a recurring pattern, something arising.
- **The other witnesses** with non-judgmental presence.
- **Together, we move through the steps:**
  - *Noticing*: What are you recognizing? How would you name it?
  - *Sense-making*: Can we observe without judgment? Can you allow it? What's underneath?
  - *Responding*: Where might you redirect? What's one way to engage? What's the learning?
- **We switch roles.**
- **We close together**, appreciating the practice.

*When to use: Daily or weekly as regular practice. When one of us is caught in a pattern. When we want to build our fluid awareness capacity.*

---

### We catch reactive flow together

Interrupting reactive patterns in real-time.

- **Either of us notices reactivity** arising and names it: "I notice we're getting reactive" or "I'm caught in something."
- **We pause.** We take a breath.
- **We recognize together:** Each briefly names what we're experiencing.
- **We allow together:** We let reactive states be present without acting on them.
- **We get curious:** "What got triggered?"
- **We choose together:** "What do we actually want here?"
- **We engage the redirect:** We take action — breathing, changing position, speaking from a different place.
- **We return** to what we were doing, or take a break if needed.

*When to use: In moments of conflict or reactivity. When we feel ourselves getting caught in familiar patterns. When the spiral is heading toward fragmentation.*

---

### We map patterns together

Understanding recurring patterns in our individual and shared awareness.

- **We choose a pattern to explore** — something that recurs, a reactive loop, a way we get stuck.
- **We map it through the spiral:**
  - What are we typically noticing when this pattern activates?
  - What sense do we make of it? How do we interpret?
  - How do we respond? What's the automatic reaction?
  - What do we notice next? How does the response change what we perceive?
- **We understand together:** What need or wound or history is this pattern protecting or expressing?
- **We identify choice points:** Where in the spiral could we intervene?
- **We plan:** What will we do next time? How can we support each other?
- **We appreciate the pattern:** Even unhelpful patterns were once adaptive.

*When to use: In calm moments, as reflective practice. For patterns that keep recurring. When we want to understand rather than just react.*

---

### We navigate in real-time

Working with difficult moments as they arise using the steps.

- **A difficult experience arises** — external event, conflict between us, strong emotions.
- **Either of us calls for navigation:** "Let's navigate this together."
- **We move through the steps together**, in real-time:
  - *Noticing*: "What's happening? What are we each experiencing?"
  - *Sense-making*: "Can we be with this? What's it about?"
  - *Responding*: "What do we want to choose? What action serves that?"
- **We might not complete all steps** — even the first few can change the flow.
- **We appreciate the practice.**

*When to use: When something challenging arises and we want to work with it consciously. When we want to practice rather than just react.*

---

### We track the spiral's direction

Sensing whether our shared awareness is moving toward integration, fragmentation, or reinforcement.

- **We check in** during or after an interaction: Which direction is the spiral moving?
- **We look for signs:**
  - *Integration*: More open, perceiving more, responding more creatively
  - *Fragmentation*: More closed, perceiving less, responding more reactively
  - *Reinforcement*: Stuck on autopilot, perceiving and responding the same old way
- **We name what we find:** "I think we're fragmenting." "This feels integrative."
- **We respond to the direction:**
  - If integrating: appreciate it, stay with it
  - If fragmenting: slow down, pause, intervene
  - If reinforcing: bring fresh awareness to disrupt autopilot

*When to use: Periodically during interactions. As a regular check-in practice. When we want to be conscious of where we're heading.*

---

### We build fluid awareness culture

Making conscious navigation ordinary rather than exceptional.

- **We value the practice** — we treat awareness work as important.
- **We name patterns with kindness** — "There's that pattern again" without judgment.
- **We support redirection** — when one of us chooses a new direction, the other supports it.
- **We appreciate growth** — we notice when patterns shift, when redirects become easier.
- **We keep practicing** — this develops as long as we practice.

*When to use: As ongoing orientation. When we want fluid awareness to become how we navigate together, not just a technique we occasionally use.*

## Working with challenges

**When one of us is more activated than the other.** Sometimes one of us is caught while the other is more regulated.

**What we've found helps:**

- **The more regulated person offers grounded presence without taking over.** Staying calm helps the other settle.
- **Don't force the steps.** Sometimes just being present is enough.
- **Ask "What do you need?" rather than assuming.** The caught person knows best what would help.

---

**When both of us are caught.** Sometimes we're both reactive, both flowing in unhelpful directions.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Name it.** "We're both caught right now."
- **Take space if needed.** Brief separation can help.
- **Use external anchors:** breath, grounding, the physical environment.
- **Return to basics.** Even just noticing together is a start.
- **Be patient.** It takes longer when both are activated.

---

**When we navigate differently.** We may have different styles, speeds, or needs in the practice.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Respect differences.** We don't have to do it identically.
- **Communicate about what helps.** "I need more time at the allowing step."
- **Adapt the practice to serve both of us.**
- **Learn from each other's approaches.** Different styles can complement each other.

---

**When the pattern is between us.** Sometimes what needs navigating is our relational dynamic itself — how we trigger each other.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Recognize we're both part of the pattern.**
- **Take ownership of our own contribution.**
- **Get curious without blaming.** "How do we each participate in this?"
- **Look for relational redirects** — shifts in how we're relating, not just individual shifts.
- **Practice when not activated** to build capacity for activated moments.

## The larger view

We are not isolated. Our relationship exists within larger systems — families, communities, the world. The awareness we develop together connects to larger patterns.

When we practice fluid awareness:
- We become a node of conscious navigation in a world often swept by reactive flow
- We model what intentional awareness practice looks like
- Our capacity ripples outward to those we touch
- We participate in the ongoing evolution of how humans relate to awareness

When we're caught in reactive flow:
- We contribute to larger patterns of reactivity
- Our unconscious patterns radiate outward

Every time we consciously navigate awareness together, we carve new channels that become easier to find. We demonstrate that choice is possible even in reactivity. We build capacity that serves more than just ourselves.
