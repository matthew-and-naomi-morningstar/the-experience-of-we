# Grieving Together: *A Shared Practice for Holding Loss*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Loss is part of life. And grief is how we metabolize it.

People die. Relationships end. Dreams don't happen. Chapters close. The self we once were gives way to someone new. These losses — large and small, sudden and gradual — accumulate across a life. They need somewhere to go. They need to be felt, honored, and slowly released. This is the work of grief.

Grief is often lonely. We withdraw. We feel that our sorrow is too much for others. We smile and say we're fine while the weight presses on our chest. The culture offers us little — a few days of bereavement leave, the expectation that we'll be over it soon, the discomfort of people who don't know what to say.

But grief was never meant to be carried alone. Humans have always grieved in community — wailing together, sitting in vigil, sharing stories of the dead, holding each other through the unimaginable. When we grieve together, something shifts. The loss doesn't become smaller, but we become larger. We discover we can hold what we could not hold alone.

This is the practice: being present with each other's grief, and grieving our shared losses together. Not fixing, not rushing, not explaining — just being with.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to hold and move through grief.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing even with small losses**, to build the muscle before grief arrives
- **Returning regularly**, because grief doesn't follow a schedule and we may need this guide at unexpected times

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what grief feels like, what it's like to grieve together, and what shifts when loss is shared.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how grief works, what it needs, and how companionship serves it.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the concrete ways we support each other through mourning.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Grief is an experience

*Grief is the felt landscape of loss — vast, shifting, and demanding.*

### The felt sense of grief

Grief has many faces, but some common qualities:

- **Weight.** A heaviness that settles over everything.
- **Waves.** Grief comes and goes. One moment bearable, the next overwhelming.
- **Hollowness.** An emptiness where something used to be.
- **Disorientation.** The world has changed and we haven't caught up.
- **Yearning.** A longing for what's gone — the ache of absence.
- **Fatigue.** Grief is exhausting. The body carries what the mind can't.

Grief is not a single feeling but a landscape — vast, shifting, demanding.

### Grieving alone

When we grieve in isolation:

- **Heaviness increases.** What we carry alone weighs more.
- **Silence builds.** We don't share, and the grief goes underground.
- **We doubt ourselves.** "Am I grieving wrong? Too much? Too long?"
- **The world feels distant.** Others continue normally while we're in the underworld.
- **Movement slows.** Without witness, grief can get stuck.

Solitary grief has its place — we need time alone with our sorrow. But only solitary grief is incomplete.

### Grieving together

When grief is shared:

- **We're not alone.** Someone is here in the darkness with us.
- **The weight distributes.** We're not carrying it all ourselves.
- **Expression becomes possible.** Tears that wouldn't come alone can come.
- **Stories can be told.** We speak the loss, and speaking is part of metabolizing.
- **Movement happens.** Grief that is witnessed can complete itself.

Something about being accompanied allows grief to do what it needs to do.

### The different griefs

We grieve many things:

**Deaths.** The most obvious loss — someone who was here is gone.

**Endings.** Relationships, jobs, chapters — things that end before death.

**Transitions.** The self we were before, the life we had, the future we imagined.

**Disappointments.** Dreams that didn't materialize, hopes that weren't met.

**Collective losses.** What's happening in the world, to communities, to the planet.

**Old griefs.** Losses that were never fully mourned and still live in us.

All of these deserve attention. All of these can be held together.

**A reflection for us:** What losses have we held together? How did shared grieving change the grief — and change us?

## Grief is a process

*Grief isn't a problem to be solved; it's a passage to be moved through, and it moves in its own time.*

### What grief is

Grief is the natural process by which we metabolize loss. It is not a problem to be solved but a passage to be moved through.

Grief includes:
- **Acknowledging.** Recognizing that something has been lost.
- **Feeling.** Letting the emotions of loss move through us.
- **Expressing.** Giving the grief somewhere to go — tears, words, ritual.
- **Remembering.** Honoring what was, keeping the lost thing in heart.
- **Integrating.** Gradually adjusting to a world that now lacks what's gone.
- **Continuing.** Learning to live forward while carrying the loss.

### Grief is not linear

Grief doesn't follow a neat progression. It moves in spirals and waves:

- We think we're through, and it returns.
- Triggers bring it back unexpectedly.
- Some days are okay; others are not.
- Years later, something opens the wound again.

This is not failure. This is grief's nature. It has its own timeline, and the timeline is not straight.

### What grief needs

Grief needs certain conditions to move:

**Acknowledgment.** The loss must be recognized as real and significant.

**Permission.** We must be allowed to grieve — by ourselves and by others.

**Expression.** Grief needs somewhere to go: tears, words, sound, movement.

**Witness.** Being seen in our grief helps it move.

**Time.** Grief cannot be rushed. It takes as long as it takes.

**Meaning.** Eventually, we need to make some sense of the loss — even if the only sense is "this happened."

### What blocks grief

Grief can get stuck when:

**It's not acknowledged.** The loss is minimized or denied.

**It's not permitted.** We're told to move on, be strong, get over it.

**It's not expressed.** We hold it in, and it stays in.

**It's not witnessed.** We grieve alone without anyone to share it.

**It's rushed.** We try to hurry through to the other side.

**It's complicated.** Guilt, ambivalence, trauma make the grief harder to move.

### How companionship serves grief

When someone grieves with us, they:

- **Witness.** They see our pain, which makes it real.
- **Validate.** Their presence says: this matters, your grief is legitimate.
- **Hold.** They carry some of the weight so we don't carry it all.
- **Permit.** Their acceptance of our grief gives us permission.
- **Accompany.** They walk with us through the passage.

They don't fix the grief. They don't take it away. They simply ensure we're not alone in it.

**A reflection for us:** How do we each grieve? Do our grieving styles complement or conflict? What would help us hold grief together more fully?

## Grief is a practice

These are our best practices for holding and moving through grief together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We make space for grief

Creating room for mourning to happen.

- **When loss comes, we don't rush past it.** We pause.
- **We ask:** "What has been lost? What needs to be grieved?"
- **We clear time** — not for fixing, but for feeling.
- **We let responsibilities slide when they can,** to make room for grief.
- **We recognize that grief is work,** and it takes energy.
- **We protect the grieving person** — and ourselves — from premature demands to "be okay."

*When to use: When loss arrives. When we sense there's grief that needs attention. When mourning has been delayed or denied.*

---

### We witness grief without fixing

Holding space for loss without trying to change it.

- **When our companion is grieving, we sit with them.**
- **We don't offer solutions,** silver linings, or explanations.
- **We don't try to make them feel better.** We let them feel what they feel.
- **We say:** "I'm here." "I'm with you." "Take all the time you need."
- **We hold silence if they need silence.** We receive words if they need to speak.
- **We stay** — even when their pain makes us uncomfortable.

*When to use: When our companion is grieving. When we're tempted to fix. When presence is more needed than words.*

---

### We share our grief

Letting the other in to our mourning.

- **When we're grieving, we share it** rather than hiding it.
- **We say:** "I'm sad about this. I need to grieve."
- **We let ourselves be seen in the rawness,** even when it feels vulnerable.
- **We don't perform being okay** when we're not.
- **We ask for what we need:** "Can you just sit with me?" "Will you listen while I talk about them?"
- **We receive their presence** as the gift it is.

*When to use: When we're grieving. When we're tempted to hide our loss. When we need companionship in sorrow.*

---

### We grieve shared losses together

Mourning what we've both lost.

- **When we share a loss** — a death, an ending, a disappointment — we grieve it together.
- **We create space to both be in grief,** not just one caretaking the other.
- **We tell stories.** We remember what was. We honor what's gone.
- **We let ourselves cry together.**
- **We hold each other** — literally or figuratively — in the shared loss.
- **We recognize that shared loss can bring us closer** if we let it.

*When to use: When we've experienced a loss together. When we both need to grieve, not just one of us. When shared mourning would help us process.*

---

### We honor through ritual

Creating practices that serve grieving.

- **We light candles, visit places, mark anniversaries.**
- **We create rituals that acknowledge loss:** moments of silence, shared remembering.
- **We tell stories of the dead or the gone.** Remembering is honoring.
- **We create small shrines** or keep objects that connect us to what's lost.
- **We make grief visible and tangible** through action.
- **We recognize that ritual gives grief a form** it can move through.

*When to use: For significant losses. For anniversaries. When grief needs a container.*

---

### We respect grief's timeline

Not rushing the passage.

- **We don't put time limits on grief** — our own or each other's.
- **We accept that grief takes as long as it takes.**
- **We don't suggest that someone should be "over it by now."**
- **We stay available over time** — grief may need companionship months or years later.
- **We check in:** "How are you doing with [the loss]?" even after time has passed.
- **We trust the process,** even when it's slow.

*When to use: Always. When we're impatient with grief. When grief continues longer than expected.*

---

### We grieve the old and unfinished

Attending to losses that were never fully mourned.

- **We recognize that old griefs can live in us,** unfinished.
- **We give permission:** "If there's old grief, it's welcome here."
- **We create space to revisit losses** that weren't fully processed.
- **We don't judge "old" grief** as less valid than fresh grief.
- **We understand that grief doesn't expire** — it waits until we're ready.
- **We help each other complete** what was left incomplete.

*When to use: When old losses surface. When we sense unfinished grief. When something from the past is asking for attention.*

## Working with challenges

**When we grieve differently.** One of us cries; the other goes quiet. One talks; the other withdraws.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Different styles aren't wrong;** they're different. We learn each other's ways.
- **We ask:** "How do you grieve? What do you need?"
- **We don't interpret their style as wrong.** We create space for both ways.
- **One person's expression doesn't invalidate another's containment.**

---

**When we don't know what to say.** Words feel inadequate in the face of loss.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Presence matters more than words.** "I'm here" is enough.
- **Honesty is sufficient:** "I don't know what to say, but I'm with you."
- **We avoid cliches that minimize** — "They're in a better place" rarely helps.
- **We let silence be okay.** Sometimes just sitting with someone says more than words could.

---

**When their grief triggers ours.** Witnessing their loss opens our own unfinished grief.

**What we've found helps:**

- **This is common and not a failure.** We take care of ourselves too.
- **We can name it:** "Your grief is touching mine."
- **We can grieve together,** but we don't make their grief about our grief.
- **We seek our own support if needed.** Our triggered grief is also asking for attention.

---

**When grief goes on and on.** It's been a long time and they're still grieving.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Grief takes its own time.** We don't pathologize prolonged grief.
- **We stay present.** Some losses are simply large, and large grief takes longer.
- **If grief seems completely stuck** — no movement at all for a long time — professional support might help; not to hurry grief, but to unblock it.

---

**When we're too depleted to companion.** We want to support but we're exhausted.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We can't companion from empty.** We take care of ourselves.
- **It's honest to say:** "I want to be here for you and I'm depleted. Let me rest, and I'll return."
- **We help them find other companions too.** A network of support prevents any one person from being overwhelmed.

## The larger view

Grief is not a detour from life. It is part of life. A full life includes loss, and a full relationship must have room for mourning.

When we can grieve together, we're saying: nothing is excluded. Not just the joys, not just the growth, but also the sorrows. We can hold the whole of life together — the arrivals and the departures, the beginnings and the endings.

There is intimacy in shared grief. To see someone in their sorrow, and to be seen in ours — this creates a bond that happy times alone cannot forge. We discover that we can be with each other in the darkness. This knowledge changes everything.

Grief is also part of how we honor what matters. We grieve because we loved. The depth of grief reflects the depth of connection. To grieve fully is to acknowledge that something mattered, that someone was precious, that we are capable of profound attachment.

## Our commitment to grieving together

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to hold loss together. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will make space for grief. We will witness without fixing. We will share our grief. We will mourn shared losses together. We will honor through ritual. We will respect grief's timeline. We will attend to old and unfinished griefs.

We will not always do this well. We will sometimes be uncomfortable with grief. We will sometimes try to rush or fix. We will sometimes withdraw when we should stay.

But we now have a shared practice for the dark passages. We know that grief shared is grief that can move. We know that companionship makes mourning possible. We know that loss is part of life and must have its place in our relationship.

May we learn to hold loss together. May we witness each other's grief with patience and love. May we honor what has been lost by letting it matter.

Grief is the price of love. Let us grieve together.
