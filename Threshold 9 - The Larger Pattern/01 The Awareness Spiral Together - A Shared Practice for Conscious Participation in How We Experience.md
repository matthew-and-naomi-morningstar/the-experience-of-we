# The Awareness Spiral Together: *A Shared Practice for Conscious Participation in How We Experience*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Experience is not a single moment. It's a continuous unfolding — a spiral that moves through us constantly, shaping what we notice, how we make sense of it, and how we respond. This spiral runs whether we're aware of it or not. But when we become conscious of it, something changes. We gain the possibility of participating in how we experience, rather than simply being carried along by automatic patterns.

This practice is about learning to see the spiral, to recognize its direction, and to consciously participate in its movement — together.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to understand and consciously participate in the spiral of experience.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go**, especially when we're both regulated and curious
- **Returning regularly**, because awareness is not something we master once but something we keep deepening

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it feels like to move through the spiral, to notice its direction, to participate consciously versus being swept along.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: the three phases of the spiral, its possible directions, and how conscious participation becomes possible.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate awareness of the spiral: the concrete ways we observe, track, and consciously participate in it together.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## The awareness spiral is an experience

*The awareness spiral is what it feels like to witness the continuous unfolding of experience — the movement from noticing to sense-making to responding that shapes everything between us.*

### The three phases as felt experience

**Noticing.** Something enters awareness. A sensation, a thought, a feeling, something the other person said or did. Attention lands somewhere. This has a quality of arrival — something that wasn't here is now here.

**Sense-making.** Almost instantly, we perceive what we've noticed *as* something. We recognize it, categorize it, interpret it. "That's frustration." "They're pulling away." "This is dangerous." Sense-making has a quality of meaning-assignment — raw experience becomes intelligible.

**Responding.** Something arises in reaction — a feeling, an impulse, a thought, an action. We respond to what we've made sense of. Responding has a quality of movement — something goes out from us, changes something.

Then we notice again. But we notice a changed landscape — our response has altered things, and we perceive from a new place. This is the spiral: we return to noticing, but never to the same spot.

### When we're conscious of the spiral

There's a distinctive quality when we can see the spiral happening:

- **Witnessing.** We experience the spiral while also watching it unfold. There's a part of us that observes the process, not just lives it.
- **Spaciousness.** Because we can see what's happening, there's more room. The phases don't collapse into each other.
- **Choice points.** We sense places where we could intervene — different sense-making, different response.
- **Participation.** We feel like active participants in our experience rather than passive recipients.

### When we're unconscious of the spiral

In contrast, when the spiral runs without awareness:

- **Automaticity.** Things just happen. We notice, make sense, respond — but it's one seamless flow we're not tracking.
- **Compression.** The phases collapse. Noticing-sense-making-responding happens so fast it feels like a single event.
- **No choice points.** There's nowhere to intervene because we don't see the process happening.
- **Being lived.** Experience happens to us. We're passengers, not participants.

**A reflection for us:** When have we noticed ourselves spiraling upward in awareness — each noticing leading to deeper noticing, each insight opening new understanding?

## The awareness spiral is a process

*The spiral doesn't just happen once — it runs continuously, builds momentum in a direction, and interacts between us in ways that amplify or dampen our shared experience.*

### The spiral's three phases

**Noticing** is where attention lands. Something enters awareness — a sensation in the body, a thought, a feeling, something in the environment, something the other person did or said. We can't control what initially catches attention, but we can learn to notice what we're noticing.

**Sense-making** is how we perceive and interpret what we've noticed. This happens almost instantly — we don't just notice raw sensation, we perceive *as*. "That sensation is anxiety." "That tone means they're upset with me." "This situation is dangerous." Sense-making draws on our history, our patterns, our current state. Two people notice the same thing and make different sense of it.

**Responding** is what arises next — emotion, thought, impulse, action. We respond to what we've made sense of, not to raw reality. If we interpreted the tone as hostile, we respond to hostility. If we interpreted it as tiredness, we respond to tiredness. Responses can be internal (a feeling arises) or external (we do or say something).

### The spiral continues

Each response changes things. It changes our internal state, and it may change the external situation. When we respond to what we interpreted as hostility with defensiveness, the other person now has our defensiveness to notice and make sense of.

This is why it's a spiral, not a cycle. We return to noticing, but from a different place. The landscape has shifted. What we notice next is shaped by what we just did.

### The spiral's direction

The spiral can move in three directions:

**Integration.** We notice more. Our sense-making is more accurate, more flexible, more nuanced. Our responses are more creative, more aligned with what we actually want. We feel more open, more alive, more connected. The spiral is moving toward greater coherence — both within ourselves and between us.

**Fragmentation.** We notice less — our awareness narrows to what confirms our reactive interpretation. Sense-making becomes rigid, defensive, threat-focused. Responses become reactive, automatic, protective. We feel more closed, more depleted, more disconnected. The spiral is moving toward dissolution.

**Reinforcement.** We notice what we always notice. We make sense the way we always make sense. We respond how we always respond. The spiral maintains its existing pattern — neither integrating nor fragmenting, but deepening the familiar grooves.

### Direction builds momentum

Whichever direction the spiral moves, it tends to continue that direction. Integration makes more integration likely. Fragmentation accelerates fragmentation. Reinforcement deepens reinforcement.

This is why catching the direction early matters. A spiral heading toward fragmentation is easier to redirect in the first few turns than after it's built momentum. And a spiral moving toward integration is worth nurturing — it will continue to build.

### The spiral between us

When we're together, we don't just have individual spirals — we have interacting spirals. What I notice, make sense of, and respond to becomes something you notice, make sense of, and respond to. Our spirals influence each other.

This can create:

- **Upward spirals.** Your integration supports my integration, which supports yours further. We amplify each other's movement toward coherence.
- **Downward spirals.** Your fragmentation triggers my fragmentation, which triggers yours further. We accelerate each other's dissolution.
- **Stabilizing spirals.** We help each other maintain patterns, for better or worse.

Understanding the spiral between us is the key to consciously shaping our shared experience.

**A reflection for us:** Where in the awareness spiral do we tend to get stuck? Do we sense well but fail to name? Name well but fail to integrate?

## The awareness spiral is a practice

These are our best practices for cultivating awareness of the spiral together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We observe the spiral together

Learning to see the spiral as it happens.

- **We create space** — a regular time to practice spiral observation together.
- **One person shares a recent experience** — something they noticed, how they made sense of it, how they responded.
- **Together, we slow it down:** *Noticing* — what exactly did you notice? What caught your attention? *Sense-making* — how did you interpret that? What meaning did you assign? *Responding* — what arose in response? What did you do or feel?
- **We look for the continuation:** What did you notice next? How did your response change what you perceived?
- **We switch roles.**
- **We close together,** appreciating the practice.

*When to use: As regular practice to develop spiral awareness. When we want to understand a specific experience more deeply. When we want to learn to see the process that usually runs unconsciously.*

---

### We track the spiral's direction together

Sensing whether we're moving toward integration, fragmentation, or reinforcement.

- **We check in during or after an interaction:** Which direction is the spiral moving?
- **We look for signs:** *Integration* — noticing more, making sense more flexibly, responding more creatively, feeling more open. *Fragmentation* — noticing less, making sense more rigidly, responding more reactively, feeling more closed. *Reinforcement* — noticing the same things, making sense the same way, responding on autopilot.
- **We name what we find:** "I think we're fragmenting." "This feels integrative." "We're in a familiar groove."
- **We respond to the direction:** If integrating — appreciate it, nurture it, stay with it. If fragmenting — slow down, pause, intervene before momentum builds. If reinforcing — bring fresh awareness to disrupt autopilot, try something different.

*When to use: Periodically during interactions. As a regular check-in practice. When we want to be conscious of where our shared experience is heading.*

---

### We intervene at each phase

Learning to participate consciously at each point in the spiral.

- **Intervening at noticing:** We ask — "What are we paying attention to? What else might we notice?" We deliberately expand what we're noticing, including things we've been filtering out. We notice what we're noticing together, comparing where attention is landing.
- **Intervening at sense-making:** We ask — "How are we interpreting this? What other interpretations are possible?" We offer alternative sense-making, not to be right but to expand options. We check our interpretations against each other's experience: "Is that what's happening for you?"
- **Intervening at responding:** We ask — "What response is arising? Is that the response we want?" We pause before automatic response to see what else might be possible. We choose a response consciously rather than letting the automatic one happen.

*When to use: When we want to participate more consciously in the spiral. When we notice we're in automatic mode. When the current direction isn't serving us.*

---

### We support each other's spiral

Using our interacting spirals to help each other.

- **When one of us is fragmenting and the other is more integrated:** The more integrated person stays grounded, offering stability. We don't try to fix the other's spiral, but our grounded presence can help regulate it. We ask: "What do you need?" rather than assuming.
- **When both of us are fragmenting:** We name it: "We're both caught in something." We take space if needed — brief separation can interrupt mutual escalation. We return to basics: breath, grounding, the present moment.
- **When we're both integrating:** We appreciate it: "This feels good — let's stay with it." We notice what's supporting the integration. We let the upward spiral continue without trying to control it.

*When to use: When one or both of us is caught in a spiral we want to shift. When we want to consciously support each other's experience.*

---

### We map recurring spirals

Understanding patterns that run repeatedly in our relationship.

- **We choose a recurring pattern to explore** — a spiral that keeps happening between us.
- **We trace it through the phases:** What does one person typically notice that starts this spiral? What sense do we each make of it? What are our typical responses? How does that lead to the next round of noticing?
- **We identify the direction:** Is this spiral usually integrating, fragmenting, or reinforcing?
- **We find potential intervention points:** Where in this spiral could we do something different?
- **We plan together:** Next time this starts, what will we try?
- **We appreciate the pattern:** Patterns formed for reasons. Understanding helps more than judging.

*When to use: For spirals that keep recurring. When we want to understand our relational patterns more deeply. When we want to consciously change a pattern rather than just react to it.*

---

### We practice slowing the spiral

The faster the spiral runs, the less conscious participation is possible. Slowing it creates space.

- **When we notice a spiral accelerating,** either of us can call for slowing: "Let's slow this down."
- **We pause. We breathe.**
- **We take one phase at a time:** "What am I noticing right now?" "What sense am I making of that?" "What response is arising?"
- **We share what we find,** one at a time.
- **We choose consciously:** Given what we're each experiencing, what do we want to do?
- **We take one step, then check in again.**

*When to use: When spirals are accelerating beyond our ability to participate consciously. When we need to interrupt automatic escalation. When we want to practice conscious participation in real-time.*

## Working with challenges

**When one person sees the spiral and the other doesn't.** Sometimes one of us is aware of the process while the other is fully caught in it.

**What we've found helps:**

- **The aware person doesn't lecture or explain the spiral** — that usually increases reactivity.
- **Offer grounded presence.** Ask simple questions: "What are you experiencing right now?"
- **Model slowing down.** Trust that your regulated presence will help them regulate.
- **Explain the spiral later,** when both are calm.

---

**When talking about the spiral becomes another spiral.** Meta-conversation can itself become reactive.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Notice if the conversation about the spiral is fragmenting.**
- **Name it:** "I think we're getting caught in the conversation about what's happening."
- **Take a break.** Sometimes we need to just be with each other rather than analyze our experience.

---

**When the spiral is moving fast and we can't slow it.** Sometimes momentum has built beyond our ability to intervene.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Don't force it.** Take physical separation if needed.
- **Let the spiral complete its run** rather than fighting it.
- **Come back when energy has settled.**
- **Process together afterward:** "What happened there? What can we learn?"

---

**When we have different interpretations of the spiral.** We may see the same interaction very differently.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Both versions are data.** We're not trying to establish the "true" version but to understand what each experienced.
- **Say:** "I interpreted it this way. You interpreted it that way. Both are real."
- **Look for what each interpretation reveals** about our sensitivities and needs.

---

**When spirals keep repeating despite awareness.** We see the pattern but keep falling into it anyway.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Seeing is the first step, not the solution.** Patterns have momentum.
- **Keep practicing** — each time we catch the spiral a little earlier, we build the capacity to catch it earlier still.
- **Celebrate small shifts.** Patterns that formed over years won't change in weeks.
- **Patience is part of the practice.**

## The larger view

The awareness spiral isn't just a personal or relational phenomenon. It's how consciousness itself operates — the continuous process by which we participate in reality.

When we learn to see the spiral and participate in it consciously, we're developing a fundamental capacity: the ability to shape our own experience rather than simply being shaped by it. This doesn't mean controlling everything — much of what we notice, how we make sense, and how we respond emerges from depths we can't access directly. But within the spiral, there are choice points. There are places where something other than the automatic could happen.

Finding those choice points, learning to use them, and supporting each other in using them — this is the practice.

## Our commitment to the awareness spiral

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to become more aware of how experience unfolds — in ourselves and between us. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We won't always see the spiral. We will still get caught in automatic patterns. We will still fragment. We will still reinforce grooves we'd rather not deepen.

But now we have a map. We know the phases: noticing, sense-making, responding. We know the directions: integration, fragmentation, reinforcement. We know that direction builds momentum. We know we can intervene — at each phase, early in the spiral, together.

May we learn to see the spiral as it moves through us. May we learn to participate consciously in how we experience. May we support each other's movement toward integration. May our interacting spirals amplify what we most want to become.

The spiral is always moving.

Let us learn to move with it together.
