# Navigating Catalysts Together: *A Shared Practice for Recognizing and Engaging Crossroads*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Some moments are ordinary. We move through them without much consequence.

Other moments are charged. They carry transformative potential. Something is at stake. The relationship could shift — for better or worse — depending on what happens next.

These are catalyst moments. A catalyst moment is a threshold — a crossroads where our relationship stands at a choice point. Enough energy has accumulated that change becomes possible. The moment demands something of us. How we respond will shape what follows.

Catalyst moments come in many forms: a conflict that reaches a critical point, a vulnerability unexpectedly shared, a truth that suddenly becomes clear, a hurt that surfaces and asks to be addressed, a decision that must be made together, a pattern that finally becomes visible. What makes them catalysts isn't their content but their charge — the sense that this moment matters, that what happens here will ripple forward.

This guide is about learning to recognize these crossroads when they arrive, and then navigate them in ways that serve our connection rather than damage it.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop shared skill in recognizing and working with moments of transformative potential.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing the awareness exercises** when not in a charged moment
- **Returning regularly**, because catalyst moments keep arriving and our capacity to meet them keeps growing

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what a catalyst moment actually feels like — the heightened energy, the sense of stakes, the feeling of standing at a threshold.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: where catalysts come from, how they move, and the directionality they can take toward connection or disconnection.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to engage catalysts: recognizing them, staying present to them, and working with them in ways that tend toward transformation rather than damage.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Catalysts are an experience

*A catalyst moment is what it feels like when the relationship stands at a crossroads.*

Catalyst moments have characteristic signatures. Learning to recognize them helps us engage consciously rather than react automatically.

When a catalyst moment is present:

- There's more charge in the field than usual; something has intensified
- It feels like what happens here matters more than in ordinary moments
- The moment may feel stretched, weighted with significance
- Focus narrows to what's happening right now
- The nervous system registers that something important is occurring — through tension, heightened alertness, or strong emotion
- There's a feeling of standing at a doorway; something could shift
- Multiple outcomes feel possible; the future feels unwritten

## Catalysts are a process

*Catalyst moments don't appear from nowhere. They arise from specific conditions, carry directional potential, and move the relationship toward either greater coherence or greater fragmentation.*

### The nature of catalysts

In chemistry, a catalyst is something that enables a reaction to occur — it facilitates transformation without being consumed by it. Relational catalysts work similarly.

**Catalysts enable transformation.** They create conditions where change becomes possible. They concentrate enough energy that the system can shift.

**Catalysts are neutral.** They aren't inherently positive or negative. A catalyst is potential — it could lead toward greater connection or toward greater disconnection, depending on how it's engaged.

**Catalysts generate energy.** When a catalyst moment arises, there is charge in the field — heightened awareness, emotional activation, increased stakes.

**Catalysts focus attention.** Something that might have stayed in the background becomes foreground. What was implicit becomes explicit. The invisible becomes visible.

**Catalysts present choice.** In a catalyst moment, multiple paths are possible. The moment is a fork, and we must choose a direction — even if that choice is made unconsciously.

### Types of catalyst moments

**The conflict catalyst.** When conflict reaches a certain intensity, it becomes a catalyst. Something must shift — the conflict can't simply continue without consequence. Emotions run high, stakes feel elevated, we're at an impasse. The opportunity: deeper understanding, resolution of long-standing issues, new patterns — if engaged skillfully.

**The vulnerability catalyst.** When one of us shares something vulnerable — a fear, a need, a truth, a wound — it creates a charged moment. How this vulnerability is received will matter. Something tender has been offered that could be hurt. The opportunity: deepened trust and intimacy — if the vulnerability is received with care.

**The truth catalyst.** When a truth becomes visible — something that was hidden, denied, or unconscious — the moment becomes charged. A pattern has become undeniably visible, an elephant has been acknowledged, someone has said what wasn't being said. The opportunity: clarity and freedom — if the truth is held without collapse or attack.

**The decision catalyst.** When a decision must be made that will affect the relationship, that moment of decision becomes a catalyst. A fork is reached where different paths are possible, commitment or change is being asked for. The opportunity: alignment with shared values and direction — if the decision is made together with care.

**The rupture catalyst.** When a rupture occurs — a betrayal, a harm, a significant breach — it creates a charged moment that will shape the relationship's future. Trust has been damaged, something has been broken, the relationship can't simply continue as before. The opportunity: deeper understanding and stronger repair — if met with honesty and care.

**The joy catalyst.** Not all catalysts are difficult. Moments of unexpected joy, success, or deepening love are also catalysts. Something wonderful has happened, joy is present and could be shared or missed. The opportunity: strengthened bond and shared memory — if fully entered together.

### Where catalyst moments come from

**Accumulated tension.** Small dissonances build until a moment concentrates them enough to demand attention.

**Unexpected revelation.** Something is shared or discovered that changes the field instantly.

**External events.** Life events — illness, loss, change, success — create catalyst moments in the relationship.

**Internal shifts.** One of us has a realization or change that affects how we can be together.

**Vulnerability risked.** Someone shares something that raises the stakes of the moment.

**Conflict escalation.** A disagreement reaches a point of intensified charge.

**Repeated patterns.** A recurring dynamic surfaces in a way that makes it unavoidable.

### The directionality of catalysts

Every catalyst moment has directional potential. The energy can move toward:

**Syntropy (greater coherence):**
- Deeper understanding
- Increased trust
- Strengthened connection
- Growth and expansion
- Resolution and integration

**Entropy (greater fragmentation):**
- Misunderstanding
- Eroded trust
- Damaged connection
- Contraction and disconnection
- Unresolved rupture

**The same catalyst can lead either direction.** A conflict can become a breakthrough or a wound. A vulnerability shared can deepen intimacy or create distance. A truth revealed can heal or harm.

What determines the direction isn't the catalyst itself, but how we engage with it.

**A reflection for us:** Is there a catalyst moment we're in right now — something asking us to choose, to change, or to recommit? How are we engaging with it?

## Catalysts are a practice

These are our best practices for recognizing and engaging catalyst moments together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We recognize when we're in a catalyst moment

The first practice is learning to identify when a catalyst is present. Recognizing a catalyst moment allows us to engage it consciously rather than reacting automatically.

- **We tune into the charge.** Is there more energy in this moment than usual? Does something feel heightened?
- **We notice the stakes.** Does it feel like what happens here matters? Are there consequences to how we respond?
- **We sense the crossroads.** Does the moment feel like a fork? Could this go different directions depending on what we do?
- **We name what we sense.** "This feels like an important moment." "I think we're at a crossroads here."
- **We confirm together.** "Does this feel like a catalyst to you too?"

*When to use: Whenever we sense heightened charge, elevated stakes, or the feeling of being at a threshold. When we want to move from automatic reaction to conscious engagement.*

---

### We acknowledge the moment explicitly

When a catalyst is recognized, acknowledging it explicitly helps us engage it well. Explicit acknowledgment creates a container for the catalyst — it moves us from unconscious reaction to conscious engagement.

- **We pause.** Before rushing forward, we take a breath.
- **We name the moment.** "This is a significant moment." "What happens right now matters."
- **We honor the charge.** We don't minimize or dismiss the intensity. We let the moment have its weight.
- **We acknowledge the stakes.** "This could bring us closer or push us apart."
- **We commit to engaging well.** "I want to handle this in a way that serves us."

*When to use: Once we've recognized a catalyst moment. When we want to create intentionality before plunging forward. When we need to slow down enough to choose how to engage.*

---

### We track the direction energy is moving

In a catalyst moment, we can sense which direction things are heading — toward connection or toward disconnection. Direction can be changed while the catalyst is still active; once the moment passes, the direction is set.

- **We track the trajectory.** As we engage, we notice: Are we moving toward each other or away? Is this opening or closing?
- **We notice body signals.** Does my body feel like we're moving toward safety or toward threat? Am I relaxing or tensing?
- **We notice the field.** Is the space between us warming or cooling? Opening or contracting?
- **We share what we notice.** "I notice this seems to be bringing us closer." "I feel like we're starting to pull apart here."
- **We adjust if needed.** If we're headed toward disconnection, we pause and redirect.

*When to use: During the catalyst moment itself. When we want real-time feedback on where this is heading. When we sense we might be going off course.*

---

### We stay present to the charge

The temptation in charged moments is to escape — through withdrawal, deflection, escalation, or premature resolution. Catalysts transform through contact; avoiding the charge prevents the transformation that's possible.

- **We resist the urge to flee.** We notice the pull to escape the intensity and choose to stay.
- **We resist the urge to rush.** We don't jump to fixing or resolving before we've been with the moment.
- **We stay with the charge.** We allow the energy to be present without trying to discharge it prematurely.
- **We breathe together.** Shared breath helps us stay regulated while remaining present.
- **We trust the moment.** We believe that being fully present will serve better than avoiding.

*When to use: When we notice ourselves wanting to flee, deflect, or rush to resolution. When the intensity is uncomfortable but the moment needs our presence. When we're tempted to discharge energy rather than work with it.*

---

### We engage with care

How we engage a catalyst moment shapes where it leads. Skillful engagement directs the catalyst toward transformation rather than damage.

- **We slow down.** In catalyst moments, slowing creates space for wisdom rather than reactivity.
- **We speak from experience, not accusation.** "I feel..." rather than "You always..."
- **We stay curious.** Even in charged moments, we try to understand rather than just react.
- **We take breaks if needed.** If we're too dysregulated to engage well, we pause — but with commitment to return.
- **We remember we're on the same side.** The catalyst is something we face together, not something that makes us adversaries.
- **We express care.** Even in difficult catalyst moments, we communicate that we care about each other and the relationship.

*When to use: Throughout the catalyst moment. When we want to ensure our engagement serves connection. When we're at risk of letting reactivity take over.*

---

### We learn from catalyst moments after they pass

After a catalyst moment has resolved, we can extract its teaching. Each catalyst moment teaches us about our relationship and about engaging charged moments skillfully.

- **We return to the moment together** when we're both regulated.
- **We reflect on what we noticed.** What signaled this was a catalyst? What type was it? Which direction did the energy move? What influenced that direction?
- **We identify what worked.** What did we do that served the moment?
- **We identify what we'd do differently.** If the moment didn't go well, what might we try next time?
- **We appreciate the growth.** Even difficult catalysts are opportunities. We acknowledge what we learned.

*When to use: After a catalyst has passed and we're resourced enough to reflect. When we want to build capacity for future catalysts. When a difficult moment needs to be integrated.*

---

### We build capacity together over time

Each catalyst we navigate builds our capacity for the next one.

- **We develop shared language** for recognizing catalysts. We use the vocabulary from this guide.
- **We build trust** that we can handle intensity together. Each successful navigation becomes evidence.
- **We become less afraid of charged moments.** What once overwhelmed us becomes workable.
- **We learn our patterns.** We notice where we tend to escalate, avoid, or miss catalysts.
- **We celebrate growth.** We acknowledge when we've handled a catalyst better than we would have before.

*When to use: As an ongoing orientation. When we want to mark progress in our capacity. When we're building the relational resilience that comes from navigating difficulty together.*

## Working with challenges

**When we miss the catalyst.** Sometimes we don't recognize a catalyst moment while it's happening. We react automatically, and only later realize what the moment was.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Build the habit of checking** for catalyst qualities in heightened moments
- **Develop sensitivity** through regular practice with the recognition exercises
- **Learn from missed moments** so we recognize similar ones next time
- **Be gentle with ourselves;** recognition is a skill that develops over time

---

**When we escalate rather than engage.** Sometimes in catalyst moments, we intensify the conflict, make the rupture worse, or damage what could have been repaired.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Slow down** when we notice heightened charge
- **Take breaks** when we're too activated to engage well
- **Remember our shared commitment** to the relationship
- **Trust that pause doesn't mean abandon;** we can return when we're more regulated

---

**When we avoid the catalyst.** Sometimes we flee — changing the subject, withdrawing, minimizing, or pretending nothing significant is happening.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Recognize avoidance** as it's happening
- **Build tolerance for intensity** gradually
- **Trust that engaging will ultimately serve** better than avoiding
- **Support each other** to stay present

---

**When we read the moment differently.** Sometimes one of us sees a catalyst where the other sees something ordinary — or we read the type or significance differently.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Trust each other's perceptions** rather than dismissing them
- **Investigate the difference** with curiosity
- **Treat one person's sense of heightened stakes as valid** even if the other doesn't fully share it
- **Use difference as information** about our different experiences

---

**When a catalyst goes badly.** Sometimes despite our intentions, a catalyst moment leads toward disconnection rather than connection.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Recognize that not every catalyst will be navigated well;** this is part of being human
- **Return to repair** when we're able
- **Learn from what happened** without shame
- **Trust that the next catalyst is another opportunity** to practice
- **Remember that building capacity** is a long-term process

## The larger view

Catalyst moments are the crossroads of our relationship. They're the places where what we've been building is tested, where what's been hidden becomes visible, where the relationship has the chance to deepen or the risk of fragmenting.

We can't prevent catalyst moments from arriving. Life will keep bringing them: conflicts that intensify, vulnerabilities that surface, truths that demand acknowledgment, decisions that must be faced. The question isn't whether these moments will come but whether we'll meet them together with skill and care.

What we practice here isn't about making catalyst moments comfortable. They aren't meant to be comfortable. They're meant to be transformative. Our work is to develop the capacity to stay present to the charge, to track the direction energy is moving, and to engage in ways that serve connection rather than damage it.

Over time, something shifts. Catalyst moments that once overwhelmed us become workable. The crossroads that once felt like threats begin to feel like opportunities. Not because the moments get easier, but because we get stronger — together.
