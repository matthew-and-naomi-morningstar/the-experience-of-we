# Working with Dissonance Together: *A Shared Practice for Engaging and Metabolizing Friction*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Something feels off between us. We may not be able to name it precisely, but we feel it — a friction, a tension, a subtle wrongness in the space we share. Perhaps it shows as irritation, unease, or the sense that we're not quite meeting. Perhaps it emerges as conflict, disagreement, or the collision of needs we both hold. Perhaps it's just a persistent discomfort that won't resolve.

This is dissonance: the felt sense of being out of sync.

Our usual responses are to make it go away. We suppress it, pretending everything is fine while tension lives on underneath. We explode it, converting friction into conflict that discharges but doesn't heal. We avoid it, stepping back from each other or from the topics that trigger it. We explain it away, rationalizing until we no longer have to feel it.

But dissonance doesn't want to go away. It wants to be heard. Because dissonance is not a problem; it is information. It is the field between us telling us something that needs our attention.

This guide offers a different path: learning to metabolize dissonance — working with the friction rather than against it, extracting what it has to teach us, and letting what we learn change something real between us. It builds on *Recognizing Dissonance Together*, which develops the skill of noticing when something is off. Here, we focus on what comes next: engaging with what we've noticed.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to work with relational friction.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing when dissonance emerges** between us — these practices work in the moment
- **Returning regularly**, because working with friction is not something we master once but something we keep practicing

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what dissonance actually feels like in our bodies and in the space between us.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how dissonance arises, what creates it, and how it can be metabolized rather than suppressed or exploded.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: receiving dissonance, inquiring into it, extracting its teaching, and integrating what we learn.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Dissonance is an experience

*Dissonance is the felt sense of being out of sync — the friction we feel in our bodies, in our interaction, and in the space between us.*

Dissonance has characteristic signatures — tension in the body, friction in the field between us, awkwardness in our interactions. *Recognizing Dissonance Together* explores these signatures in detail: how dissonance shows up across different dimensions (emotional, cognitive, energetic, temporal, value, physical) and across different intensities (subtle to intense). Familiarity with those patterns is the foundation for the work described here.

This guide picks up where recognition leaves off. Once we've noticed dissonance, what does it feel like to actively engage with it?

### The experience of engaging

When we choose to work with dissonance rather than avoid it:

- **Courage.** We're choosing to face what would be easier to ignore.
- **Vulnerability.** We're opening ourselves to whatever the friction reveals.
- **Discomfort.** Staying with friction is genuinely uncomfortable — the body wants to flee.
- **Connection.** Paradoxically, choosing to engage with dissonance together can feel deeply connecting.
- **Movement.** When we truly stay with friction, something begins to shift — the stuck energy starts to flow.

### The experience of metabolizing

When dissonance is being processed — genuinely worked through rather than suppressed or exploded:

- **Relief.** Something that was stuck begins to move.
- **Clarity.** What was confusing or vague becomes understandable.
- **Tenderness.** Working through friction often opens something tender between us.
- **Learning.** We're discovering something true about ourselves, each other, or our relationship.
- **Integration.** What we've learned begins to settle into how we are with each other.

**A reflection for us:** When have we worked through dissonance together and come out the other side with something valuable? What made that possible?

## Dissonance is a process

*Every relationship moves between resonance and dissonance. This isn't failure; it's the natural rhythm of two distinct people in connection.*

The question is not whether dissonance will appear, but whether we'll work with it skillfully. *Recognizing Dissonance Together* explores the common sources of dissonance — unmet needs, accumulated small misses, different states colliding, triggered patterns, unexpressed truth, external stress, and change. Here, we focus on what to do once dissonance has been recognized.

### The metabolic process

Like a body metabolizing food — breaking it down, extracting nutrients, eliminating waste — we can metabolize relational friction:

**Recognizing.** We sense dissonance, name it, map where it lives, trace it to source. This is the first half of the work; we can't metabolize what we haven't recognized.

**Receiving.** We let ourselves actually feel the dissonance together rather than defending against it. We stay with it before rushing to fix.

**Inquiring.** We explore what the dissonance is made of — what needs, values, or histories are activated. We get curious together.

**Extracting.** We find the information — what is the dissonance telling us about ourselves, each other, our relationship?

**Integrating.** We let what we learned change something — our understanding, our behavior, our way of being together.

**Releasing.** We let go of what remains after the information is extracted. The dissonance has served its purpose.

### What blocks metabolism

**Suppression.** Pushing dissonance down, pretending it isn't there. The friction remains undigested, accumulating as distance or resentment.

**Explosion.** Converting dissonance immediately into reactive conflict. The energy is discharged but the information is lost.

**Avoidance.** Withdrawing from each other or from topics that trigger friction. The dissonance is never faced, never metabolized.

**Premature resolution.** Rushing to make dissonance go away before extracting its information. "Let's just move on" before the learning is complete.

**A reflection for us:** What's our typical pattern when dissonance arises? Do we engage, avoid, escalate, or freeze? What would we like our pattern to be?

## Working with dissonance is a practice

*These are our best practices for recognizing and metabolizing relational friction. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.*

Working with dissonance has two movements: first we recognize it, then we metabolize it. The recognition practices — naming dissonance, doing check-ins, mapping dimensions, tracing to source, tracking patterns, and creating safety for honesty — are developed in *Recognizing Dissonance Together*. Here, we focus on the second movement: what we do once dissonance has been recognized.

### We receive dissonance together

Before we can metabolize dissonance, we must receive it — let ourselves actually feel it together rather than defending against it.

- **We pause.** We stop other activities. We give this our attention.
- **We feel it in our bodies.** Each of us notices where the dissonance lives. We might share: "I feel it in my chest." "Mine is in my stomach."
- **We stay with it.** Rather than rushing to fix or explain, we simply stay with the felt sense of friction together.
- **We resist immediate solutions.** The urge will arise to solve, fix, or explain. We let that urge be present without acting on it yet.
- **We breathe together.** A few conscious breaths help us stay present with discomfort.

What we might say: "Let's just feel this for a moment before we try to figure it out." "I notice I want to fix this immediately. Can we stay with it a bit longer?"

*When to use: After naming dissonance, before trying to solve it. When we're tempted to rush past the uncomfortable part. When we need to actually be with what's present before processing it.*

---

### We inquire together

Once we've received the dissonance, we explore it — what is it made of? What might it be communicating?

- **We get curious together.** We adopt shared curiosity: "What is this about?" Not blame, not defense — curiosity.
- **We each share what we notice.** Taking turns: "For me, it seems connected to..." "What I notice on my side is..."
- **We listen to understand, not to rebut.** When one speaks, the other listens — genuinely trying to understand.
- **We look for what's underneath.** Surface dissonance often points to something deeper. We ask: "What might be underneath this?"
- **We consider multiple possibilities.** We don't lock in on one explanation. We hold multiple possibilities.

Questions we might explore: "What do each of us need that isn't being met?" "What values might be in tension?" "Is this about right now, or is something older getting activated?" "What hasn't been said between us?"

*When to use: After receiving the dissonance. When we need to understand what we're working with. When curiosity is more useful than conclusions.*

---

### We extract the information

The purpose of metabolizing is to learn what the dissonance came to teach us.

- **We name what we're learning.** "What this dissonance seems to be telling us is..."
- **We distinguish what belongs to whom.** Some information is about one of us, some about the other, some about our relationship, some about external factors. We sort it together.
- **We check with each other.** "Does this ring true for you?" We test the information against both of our experiences.
- **We let it land.** When we find information that resonates, we let it actually land. We don't rush past it.
- **We appreciate the dissonance.** We recognize that the friction brought us something valuable.

What we might discover: "We have different needs around this that we haven't acknowledged." "There's something I've been afraid to say." "This reminds both of us of old patterns." "External stress has been leaking into our connection."

*When to use: After inquiring together. When we're ready to name what we've learned. When we want to honor the teaching the dissonance brought.*

---

### We integrate and release

Metabolism isn't complete until what we learned changes something.

- **We name what needs to change.** Based on what we learned, what needs to be different?
- **We make agreements if needed.** If the dissonance points to needed changes, we make specific agreements.
- **We adjust our understanding.** We let what we learned update how we see each other and our relationship.
- **We acknowledge the work.** We recognize that we did something meaningful together. "Thank you for doing this with me."
- **We release what remains.** After extracting and integrating, we let go of residual tension. The dissonance has served its purpose.

What integration might look like: specific agreements ("Let's check in about this weekly"), updated understanding ("I understand now why this matters so much to you"), changed behavior ("I'll be more mindful of this"), deeper knowledge ("We now know something about ourselves we didn't before").

*When to use: After extracting the information. When we're ready to let the learning become real. When we need to complete the metabolic cycle rather than leaving it unfinished.*

## Working with challenges

For challenges related to recognizing dissonance — when one person sees it and the other doesn't, when we don't want to admit it, when we can't find the source — see *Recognizing Dissonance Together*.

**When dissonance feels overwhelming.** Sometimes dissonance is so intense we struggle to work with it clearly.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Acknowledge the intensity** without having to fully explore it yet.
- **Take care of our nervous systems first** — regulation before metabolizing.
- **Return to the work when we're more resourced.**
- **Seek outside support if needed.**

---

**When we get stuck in metabolizing.** Sometimes we try to process dissonance and get stuck.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Name the stuckness:** "We seem stuck."
- **Check for missing conditions** — is there enough safety? Is one of us too dysregulated?
- **Take a break if needed** and return later.
- **Go slower** — stuckness often means we're moving too fast.
- **Accept that some dissonance may need outside support.**

---

**When dissonance reveals something difficult.** Sometimes the information is hard to receive — a fundamental difference, a deep hurt, a pattern that will be hard to change.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Stay with each other.** Don't abandon each other in the difficulty.
- **Hold what we learned with care** — difficult information is still valuable.
- **Take time** — big learnings take time to integrate.
- **Remember our care for each other.**
- **Accept that some information takes multiple passes** to fully metabolize.

---

**When not all dissonance resolves.** Some dissonance doesn't fully resolve — and that's not always a problem.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Recognize different types of ongoing dissonance:** growth dissonance (the friction of being stretched together), creative dissonance (tension that generates new possibilities), values dissonance (genuine differences we hold with respect).
- **Part of maturity is learning to live with dissonance that serves our relationship** rather than needing to resolve everything.

## The larger view

Working with dissonance is not a one-time fix. It's a shared practice we carry forward.

We don't let dissonance accumulate. We process as we go rather than letting friction pile up. We check in regularly, asking each other: "Is there any friction between us that needs attention?" We metabolize completely — we don't leave processing half-done. We extract the information and integrate the learning. And we make time for this, because metabolism can't happen if we're always rushing. We protect time for this work.

The friction between us will come. It always does, in any living relationship. What matters is whether we meet it with curiosity, receive what it has to teach us, extract its wisdom together, and let what we learn make us wiser, closer, more deeply connected.

Every relationship exists in a constant dance between resonance and dissonance. The goal isn't to eliminate dissonance; it's to develop the shared capacity to work with it skillfully. When we can do this — when friction becomes information rather than threat — something fundamental shifts. We stop fearing the moments when things feel off. We start trusting that the friction itself is guiding us toward something truer.

## Our commitment to working with dissonance

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to work with the friction between us rather than against it. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will receive dissonance together rather than defending against it. We will inquire with genuine curiosity. We will extract its teaching and let that teaching change us. We will integrate what we learn and release what remains.

We won't always do this perfectly. We will sometimes suppress, explode, or avoid. We will sometimes get stuck. We will sometimes need breaks, help, or multiple attempts.

But we now have a shared practice. We know that dissonance is information, not failure. We know how to recognize it and how to metabolize it. We know that the friction between us is not our enemy; it is our teacher.

May we sense early when something is off. May we name it with courage and receive it with openness. May we extract its wisdom together. May we let what we learn make us closer rather than more distant.

The dissonance will come. Let us learn from it together.
