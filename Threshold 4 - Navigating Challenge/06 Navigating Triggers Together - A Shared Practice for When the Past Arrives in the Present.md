# Navigating Triggers Together: *A Shared Practice for When the Past Arrives in the Present*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Something happens. A word, a tone of voice, a gesture, a silence that lasts a beat too long. And suddenly one of us is somewhere else — not metaphorically, but neurologically. The eyes change. The body tightens or goes still. The response that comes is too fast, too big, too sharp for what just happened. Something in the present has activated something from the past, and the past has arrived uninvited.

This is what it means to be triggered: the nervous system recognizes a pattern that once meant danger and responds as if the danger is happening now. It doesn't matter that the present moment is safe. The body doesn't know the difference. It's reacting to a threat it remembers, not the situation in front of it.

Everyone carries triggers. They aren't a sign of weakness or pathology; they're the nervous system's memory of what once overwhelmed it. We all have places where we're wired to react before we can think, where the body makes a protective decision faster than conscious awareness can intervene. The question is not whether triggers will surface between us. They will. The question is whether we can learn to navigate them together: recognizing what's happening, staying connected through it, and tending to what gets stirred up once the activation passes.

This guide is about building that capacity. Not eliminating triggers, which may not be possible or even desirable, but developing a shared practice for meeting them with honesty, care, and skill.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to navigate moments when the past arrives in the present.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing when we're both regulated**, so that the skills are available when activation comes
- **Returning regularly**, because our understanding of each other's triggers deepens over time, and our capacity to navigate them grows with practice

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it actually feels like to be triggered, and what it feels like to witness our partner being triggered.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how triggers form, how they activate, and how they move through the relational field between us.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: recognizing triggers, signaling them, responding to each other during activation, and processing what got stirred up afterward.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Being triggered is an experience

*Being triggered is what it feels like when the nervous system responds to something in the present as if it were something from the past.*

Before we understand triggers intellectually, we know them in the body — as a jolt, a flood, a sudden narrowing of the world. They arrive as a sudden shift in state: one moment we're here, present and available; the next moment, something has changed. The shift is involuntary. It happens faster than thought.

### What being triggered feels like from the inside

When we're triggered:

- The body reacts before the mind catches up. Heart pounding, muscles tightening, breath catching, stomach clenching — the alarm has sounded before we even know what set it off.
- The present moment narrows or disappears. We lose access to the here and now — what's in front of us gets replaced or overshadowed by what's behind us.
- Emotions arrive with an intensity that doesn't match the situation. The flash of rage, the flood of shame, the wave of terror, the collapse into helplessness. The feeling is real, but its size belongs to something older.
- We react in ways that surprise us. Words come out that we didn't plan. We withdraw, attack, freeze, or scramble to appease. The reaction feels automatic, as if someone else is driving.
- We may not know we're triggered in the moment. Awareness often comes after the fact; in the grip of activation, it can feel completely justified. It's only later that the disproportionality becomes visible.

### What it feels like to witness a triggered partner

When our partner is triggered:

- Confusion. The response doesn't match what happened. We said something ordinary and got back something enormous.
- Walking on eggshells. We sense the activation and tighten around it — monitoring our words, our tone, our every move.
- Taking it personally. The intensity of their reaction feels directed at us, even when it isn't about us.
- Helplessness. We want to fix it and can't — the thing that's hurting them isn't something we can reach. It's in the past.
- Our own activation. Their trigger response triggers something in us. Now two nervous systems are escalating.

### The gap between the trigger and the present

There's a telling signature that distinguishes a trigger response from a present-tense reaction: the intensity doesn't fit. A forgetting becomes an abandonment. A disagreement becomes a betrayal. A moment of distance becomes proof of rejection. When the body's response exceeds what the situation warrants, something older is being activated. The present has become a doorway, and the past has walked through it.

This doesn't mean the present-tense experience is irrelevant. It doesn't mean we're wrong to feel what we feel. It means there are two things happening at once: the present situation and the historical pattern it resembles. Both are real. Both need attention. But they need different kinds of attention.

**A reflection for us:** Can we each name a moment when one of us was triggered and we could sense it? What did it feel like in the room? What happened between us?

## Being triggered is a process

*Triggers don't appear randomly. They form through specific experiences, activate through recognizable patterns, and move through the relational field in ways we can learn to understand.*

### How triggers form

The nervous system is a learning system. When an experience is overwhelming enough that we can't process it in the moment, the system stores the pattern as a threat signature: the sounds, the sensations, the emotional tone, the relational dynamics that accompanied the original event. This isn't a conscious choice. The body decides what constitutes danger based on what has actually been dangerous.

Triggers can form from a single overwhelming event — a moment of terror, a betrayal — or from repeated exposure over time. A child who was screamed at once in a terrifying moment may develop a trigger around raised voices. A child who was consistently dismissed over years may develop a trigger around being unheard. Trauma is not the only source; any experience that overwhelmed the system's capacity to process can leave a trigger behind. Being humiliated. Being abandoned. Being controlled. Being invisible. The nervous system catalogues all of it.

What makes triggers so potent is their speed. The amygdala processes threat information faster than the prefrontal cortex can evaluate it. The body is already reacting before the mind has had a chance to assess whether the threat is real. This is adaptive in genuine danger. It becomes disruptive when the "danger" is a tone of voice that resembles a parent's, a facial expression that recalls a past betrayal, or a silence that echoes an old abandonment.

### The trigger cycle

Trigger responses tend to follow a recognizable sequence:

**Activation.** Something in the present resembles something from the past. The nervous system fires. The body shifts into a protective state: fight, flight, freeze, or appease. This happens in seconds or less.

**Reaction.** The protective state produces behavior: a sharp reply, a withdrawal, a flood of tears, a blank stare, a desperate attempt to please. This is the visible part of the trigger, the behavior the other person sees and responds to.

**Aftermath.** The activation begins to subside. We start to realize that the reaction was disproportionate. Shame, confusion, or exhaustion may follow. "Why did I do that?" "What just happened?"

**Repair.** If we have the skills and the willingness, we return to the moment. We name what happened. We tend to any harm the reaction caused. We explore what got activated and why.

The cycle isn't always this neat. Sometimes activation and reaction blur together. Sometimes the aftermath lasts hours or days. Sometimes repair doesn't happen, and the unprocessed trigger gets added to the relational burden between us. But the basic shape holds, and recognizing it gives us something to work with.

### How triggers move through the relational field

Triggers don't stay contained in one person. They interact with the relational field and with the other person's nervous system.

**Trigger cascades.** One person's trigger response triggers the other — she shuts down, which triggers his abandonment wound; his desperate pursuit triggers her overwhelm. Two nervous systems are now escalating, each one feeding the other's activation. This is one of the most common and destructive patterns in intimate relationships.

**Atmospheric activation.** Even when a trigger response isn't directed at us, we feel it. The room changes. The air gets thick. We register the other person's activation in our own body and begin to tighten around it.

**Misattribution.** The triggered person experiences the intensity as being caused by the present situation. They may genuinely believe their partner did something terrible, because the emotional truth of the trigger is overwhelming. From the inside, it doesn't feel like the past; it feels like *right now*.

### Separation in the present moment

In the framework we're developing together, triggers are a form of Separation activated in real time. The original event was a moment when the system became overwhelmed and couldn't maintain its coherence; it had to split something off to survive. The trigger is that split replaying — the system fragmenting again along the same fault line, the past's Separation reactivating in the present's field.

This means that working with triggers is, at its core, a practice of reunion. We're helping the nervous system learn that the old threat is not the present reality. We're staying connected through the activation rather than letting Separation win again. We're creating new experiences that gradually update the system's threat map.

### The difference between the trigger and the response

One distinction matters deeply: the trigger itself is involuntary. We don't choose to be activated. The nervous system fires before consent is possible. But what we do with the activation, once we recognize it, is where practice lives. The trigger is not our fault. What we do after we notice it is our responsibility.

This distinction protects both people. The triggered person isn't blamed for their activation. The witnessing partner isn't obligated to accept harmful behavior just because a trigger is involved. We can hold both truths: this activation is real and not chosen, and the behavior it produces still has impact that matters.

**A reflection for us:** What patterns have we noticed when one of us gets triggered? Do our triggers interlock? Can we describe the cycle without blame?

## Navigating triggers is a practice

These are our best practices for navigating triggers together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice recognizing when we're triggered

The first skill is learning to tell the difference between a present-tense reaction and a trigger response. This isn't always clear in the moment, but there are signals we can learn to read.

- **We notice the intensity.** If the emotional charge is disproportionate to what happened, a trigger is likely involved. A forgotten errand doesn't warrant devastation; a mild disagreement doesn't warrant rage. When the feeling is bigger than the moment, something older is present.
- **We check the body.** Trigger activation has a physical signature: racing heart, shallow breath, tunnel vision, muscle tension, numbness, nausea. We learn to recognize our own particular patterns. "When I feel this in my body, I'm probably triggered."
- **We notice the speed.** Triggers fire fast. If the reaction arrived before we had time to think, that speed itself is a clue.
- **We watch for familiar territory.** Triggers tend to run the same routes. If we find ourselves in a place we've been many times before, if the fight feels eerily similar to fights we've already had, if the emotion has a quality of *here we are again*, we're likely in trigger territory.
- **We allow for uncertainty.** Sometimes we genuinely aren't sure whether we're triggered or legitimately responding to the present. That's okay. We don't need a diagnosis to proceed with care. "I'm not sure if this is a trigger or not, but it's big" is an honest and useful thing to say.

Learning to recognize triggers in real time takes patience. We won't always catch them. Sometimes we'll only see it in hindsight. That's still valuable; every recognition builds the capacity for earlier recognition next time.

*When to use: Whenever a reaction feels bigger than the moment. When we notice ourselves in a familiar emotional landscape. As an ongoing self-awareness practice.*

---

### We signal triggers to each other

Naming a trigger as it's happening is one of the most vulnerable and useful things we can do. It changes the entire dynamic.

- **We use clear, simple language.** "I think I'm being triggered right now." "Something just got activated in me." "This is hitting something old." We don't need to explain or justify; we just name what's happening.
- **We signal early when we can.** The sooner we can name the trigger, the better. Early signaling gives both of us more room to respond skillfully. But we don't pressure ourselves; sometimes we can't signal until the activation has already run its course.
- **We agree on our language in advance.** We decide together how we'll signal triggers: a phrase, a word, a gesture. Having this pre-arranged means we don't have to find language in the middle of activation — when language is hardest to access.
- **We receive the signal with care.** When our partner signals a trigger, we don't take it as a conversation-ender, a manipulation, or a free pass. We take it as information: something important is happening in their nervous system right now.
- **We let the signal change the interaction.** Once a trigger has been named, we're no longer having the conversation we thought we were having. We shift our focus from the content to the person. The topic can wait; the nervous system can't.

Signaling a trigger is a relational act, not a personal one. It says: *I trust you enough to tell you that I'm not fully here right now. I trust that you'll meet me with care rather than using this against me.* It takes courage, and it changes everything.

*When to use: When we recognize we're triggered, as early as we can name it. When we notice ourselves reacting disproportionately. When we want to give our partner context for what's happening.*

---

### We respond to a triggered partner

How we respond when our partner is triggered can either help them move through the activation or deepen it.

- **We don't take it personally.** This is the hardest and most important step — and the most generous. The intensity isn't about us, even when it's directed at us. The reaction belongs to something older. We remind ourselves: this is their nervous system, not their assessment of me.
- **We don't try to fix it.** The impulse to make it better, to talk them out of it, to explain why they shouldn't feel this way, is natural. It also doesn't work. A triggered nervous system can't be reasoned with. Logic won't reach what's happening at the level of the body.
- **We stay steady.** The most useful thing we can offer is a regulated nervous system. Not frozen, not reactive in return, just present. Our calm is something they can borrow. Our steadiness says: *the danger your body is perceiving is not here right now.*
- **We communicate safety through the body.** Tone of voice matters more than words. A slow, warm, even voice. An open posture. Unhurried movement. Eye contact if it helps, space if that's what they need. The body reads safety through these channels.
- **We ask what they need.** "What would help right now?" "Do you want me closer or do you need space?" "Would it help to talk or to be quiet?" We offer rather than assume.
- **We hold our own experience for now.** If their trigger response hurt us, we hold that. Not forever, but for now. Our feelings matter and deserve processing, but the middle of someone's activation is not the moment for it. We tend to our hurt later, together.

This is not about becoming a therapist or a rescuer. It's about being a steady relational presence while someone's nervous system moves through an old storm.

*When to use: When our partner signals a trigger or when we can see one happening. When their reaction seems larger than the present moment. When we want to be helpful rather than inadvertently making it worse.*

---

### We create space during activation

When a trigger is live, the priority is creating enough space for the nervous system to begin settling.

- **We pause.** We stop the conversation, the argument, the interaction that activated the trigger. We don't need resolution right now; we need settling.
- **We orient to the present.** Triggers pull us into the past; orienting to the present is the counterbalance. "Look around the room. What do you see?" "Feel your feet on the floor." "Notice my hand on yours." Simple sensory information that anchors us here.
- **We breathe.** Slow, deliberate breaths signal safety to the nervous system. Breathing together can be especially regulating; it recruits co-regulation.
- **We move if it helps.** Sometimes the body needs to discharge activation through movement: walking, shaking, standing up, changing rooms. We follow what the body asks for.
- **We use touch if welcome.** A hand on the back, an arm around the shoulder, holding hands. Touch can be profoundly regulating during activation, but only when it's wanted. We always check.
- **We give it time.** Trigger activation doesn't resolve instantly. The neurochemistry needs time to process. We resist the urge to rush back to normal before the body is ready.

The goal isn't to eliminate the activation. It's to create conditions where the nervous system can begin its own settling process, with the support of the relational field around it.

*When to use: During active trigger responses. When one or both of us is activated and needs grounding. Before attempting to process or discuss what happened.*

---

### We process triggers together after the activation settles

Once the nervous system has calmed and both of us are regulated, we can explore what happened. This after-the-fact processing is where the deepest learning occurs.

- **We wait until we're both regulated.** Not immediately after the activation — not while either person is still raw. We wait until we genuinely have capacity, even if that's hours or a day later.
- **We approach with curiosity, not blame.** "What happened there?" "What got activated?" Not "Why did you react like that?" or "You always do this." Curiosity opens; accusation closes.
- **We let the triggered person lead.** They share what they experienced, what they think got activated, what the trigger might be connected to. The witnessing partner listens. This is their story to tell.
- **We name the present-tense component.** Sometimes the trigger was activated by something real in the present that also needs addressing. We acknowledge both layers: "The trigger is about something old, and the thing you said also landed hard."
- **We trace the thread when it's visible.** If the triggered person can connect the reaction to its source, that's valuable. "I think that happened because it reminded me of..." This isn't required and shouldn't be forced. Sometimes the source isn't clear, and that's okay.
- **We tend to any harm.** If the trigger response caused hurt to the other person, we acknowledge it. Being triggered explains a reaction; it doesn't erase its impact. "I understand why you reacted that way, and it still hurt when you said that" is a sentence that can hold both truths.

Processing triggers together is an act of intimacy. We're saying: *I'll show you where I'm most reactive, most defended, most shaped by what came before. And I trust you to receive that with care.*

*When to use: After a trigger event, when both people have settled. When we want to understand what happened rather than just move past it. When we're ready to tend to both the trigger and any relational impact.*

---

### We build a shared map of each other's triggers

Over time, we develop knowledge of each other's trigger landscape. This shared map is one of the most practical and intimate things we can build together.

- **We share what we know about ourselves.** "Raised voices are hard for me." "When I feel dismissed, something old fires." "Sudden changes in plan can send me into a spiral." We name our triggers as we discover them, without shame.
- **We pay attention to patterns.** We notice which situations, topics, tones, or dynamics consistently produce activation. We start to see the map, even before it's spoken.
- **We hold the map with care.** Knowing someone's triggers is a form of power — and we commit never to use this knowledge as a weapon. Never provoking a trigger intentionally, never throwing it in their face during a fight, never dismissing their experience because we "know what it's really about."
- **We use the map for navigation, not avoidance.** Knowing each other's triggers doesn't mean we tiptoe around them forever. It means we can approach sensitive territory with awareness; we can signal when we're heading into difficult terrain; we can prepare ourselves and each other.
- **We update the map.** Triggers shift over time. Some soften as they're processed. New ones may emerge. The map is a living document, not a fixed diagnosis.

The shared trigger map is a form of relational knowledge that only deepens with time. It says: *I've been paying attention to what hurts you. I carry that knowledge gently.*

*When to use: As an ongoing practice of relational learning. When we discover new triggers in ourselves or each other. When we want to navigate known sensitive territory with care.*

---

### We distinguish triggers from intuition

Not every strong reaction is a trigger. Sometimes the body's alarm system is reading something real in the present.

- **We take strong reactions seriously before categorizing them.** The instinct to dismiss a reaction as "just a trigger" can be its own form of silencing. We investigate before we label.
- **We check the signature.** Triggers tend to have certain qualities: the reaction is faster than thought, the intensity is disproportionate, there's a quality of familiarity or regression. Intuition has a different quality — it often arrives as a quiet knowing, a settling rather than an activation, a clarity rather than a flood.
- **We listen for the body's different voices.** The triggered body is loud, urgent, flooded. The intuitive body is firm, clear, grounded. We learn to tell the difference, knowing they can sometimes overlap.
- **We consider both possibilities.** "This could be a trigger, and it could also be that something real is happening here." We don't have to choose one explanation. We investigate both.
- **We respect the "both" space.** Sometimes a reaction is triggered *and* contains accurate information about the present. The pattern from the past is activating, and the thing in the present that resembles it is also genuinely problematic. These can coexist.

This distinction matters because reducing every strong reaction to "that's just your trigger" strips the person of their perceptive capacity. Some alarm bells ring because there is an actual fire.

*When to use: When we're tempted to dismiss a reaction as "just a trigger." When a strong response seems to contain real information. When we're unsure whether we're perceiving or projecting.*

---

### We recognize when triggers need professional support

Some trigger responses are beyond what relational practice can address. Knowing where that line falls is part of caring for each other well.

- **We watch for signs that the activation is too much.** Dissociation: losing contact with the body, the room, or the sense of self. Flashbacks: re-experiencing past events as if they're happening now. Panic: activation so intense that it overwhelms all coping. Prolonged dysregulation: activation that doesn't settle over hours or days. These are signals that professional support may be needed.
- **We name this without shame.** "I think this might need more than just us" is not an admission of failure. It's a recognition that some wounds require specialized care, just as some physical injuries require a doctor rather than a first-aid kit.
- **We hold the line between relational practice and clinical work.** We can navigate triggers together; we cannot treat trauma. We can be a steady presence; we cannot be a therapist. We can support each other; we cannot replace professional help when it's needed.
- **We seek support as a relational act.** Getting professional help for triggers isn't abandoning the shared practice; it's an extension of it. "I'm getting help with this so that it doesn't keep running us" is one of the most caring things we can say.
- **We continue our relational practice alongside any professional work.** Therapy and relational practice are complementary, not competing. What we learn in professional settings can deepen our shared work, and what we learn together can inform professional work.

The willingness to seek help when it's needed is a sign of relational maturity, not weakness. We care for each other by knowing our limits.

*When to use: When triggers consistently overwhelm our capacity to navigate them. When activation involves dissociation, flashbacks, or prolonged dysregulation. When we sense that the material beneath the trigger needs professional support to process safely.*

## Working with challenges

**When one person's triggers trigger the other.** This is perhaps the most common challenge in navigating triggers together — my shutdown triggers your pursuit; your pursuit triggers my overwhelm. Two activated nervous systems, each escalating the other.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Name the cascade.** "We're triggering each other right now." Naming it breaks the automatic cycle, even slightly.
- **Stop engaging with the content.** The topic that started this is no longer the point. The point is that both nervous systems need settling.
- **Take space if needed.** When both people are activated, neither can serve as the calm presence the other needs. Separating briefly, with care, is sometimes the only option.
- **Process separately first, then together.** Each person asks: "What's mine here?" Then we bring our separate understanding back to the shared space.
- **Map the interlocking pattern together.** Over time, we can identify our specific trigger cascades. "When I do X, it activates Y in you, and then your Y activates Z in me." Understanding the sequence gives us intervention points.

---

**When triggers feel shameful.** Many of us carry shame about being triggered at all. "I should be over this." "I'm too sensitive." "What's wrong with me?" The shame can be more painful than the trigger itself.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Normalize triggers.** Everyone has them. They're a function of having a nervous system that learns from experience. There is nothing wrong with us for being triggered.
- **Separate the trigger from identity.** Having a trigger is not the same as being broken. It means something happened to us that our system hasn't finished processing. That's human, not defective.
- **Receive each other's triggers without judgment.** When our partner shares a trigger, we don't flinch. We don't minimize. We say: "Thank you for telling me."
- **Notice the shame without feeding it.** "I notice I feel ashamed about this" is more useful than spiraling into the shame. We can observe it without being consumed by it.

---

**When we accidentally trigger each other repeatedly.** Sometimes we keep stepping on the same mine. The same topic, the same tone, the same dynamic keeps producing activation despite our best intentions.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Get specific together.** "What exactly is the thing that activates it? Is it the words, the tone, the timing, the context?" The more precisely we can identify the trigger point, the more skillfully we can navigate around it.
- **Develop alternatives together.** "When you need to bring up X, it would help if you approached it like this." We collaborate on finding ways to address necessary topics without triggering the activation.
- **Accept imperfection.** We will sometimes trigger each other despite our best efforts. This isn't evidence that we don't care. It's evidence that triggers are deeply wired and navigating them is genuinely difficult.
- **Repair quickly.** When we accidentally trigger each other, we acknowledge it promptly rather than letting the hurt accumulate. "I'm sorry. I hit that spot again."

---

**When a trigger response causes harm.** Being triggered explains a reaction. It doesn't excuse the harm that reaction may cause. Sometimes what we say or do while activated genuinely hurts the other person.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Acknowledge the harm separately from the trigger.** "I was triggered, and what I said still hurt you. Both of those are true."
- **Take responsibility for impact.** Even when the reaction was involuntary, its impact on the other person is real and deserves acknowledgment.
- **Repair the relational damage.** The practices in *Repairing Together* apply here. The trigger provides context; it doesn't replace the need for repair.
- **Work on reducing harm over time.** As we become more aware of our triggers, we can begin to interrupt the reactive behavior even when the activation is present. This is a skill that builds gradually.
- **Forgive without dismissing.** The witnessing partner practices holding both realities: forgiveness for the triggered reaction, and honest acknowledgment that it hurt.

## The larger view

Triggers are not character flaws. They are the nervous system's memory of what once threatened us — stored in the body, firing without our consent. They are evidence of a system that learned to protect itself. That the protection sometimes misfires in the present doesn't make it wrong; it makes it outdated.

Working with triggers together is one of the most intimate things we can do. It means trusting another person with the places where we are most reactive, most defended, most shaped by what came before. It means letting someone see the moments when we lose our composure, our clarity, our capacity to be fully present. It means believing that they will still be there when the activation passes.

And from the other side: it means choosing not to take it personally. It means staying steady when someone's nervous system is telling them we're the threat. It means learning the difference between what's about us and what's about something that happened long before we arrived. It means holding space for a kind of pain we didn't cause and can't fix — simply because we love the person carrying it.

Every trigger navigated together is a small act of reunion. The past arrives, and instead of the old outcome, something new happens — someone stays. Someone sees us. The alarm fires, and this time, it's met with presence instead of the thing the nervous system expected. Over time, these new experiences don't erase the old ones. But they lay down new pathways. The body begins to learn that this relationship, this moment, is not that old threat. The trigger doesn't vanish, but its grip loosens. The past still visits, but it stops running the show.

This is what it means to heal in relationship: not the absence of triggers, but the presence of someone who will navigate them with us.

## Our commitment to navigating triggers

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice meeting each other's triggers with honesty and care. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will get triggered. We will sometimes trigger each other. We will sometimes react before we can name what's happening. We will sometimes cause hurt in the grip of activation, and we will sometimes take personally what isn't about us.

But we now have a shared practice. We know what triggers are and how they work. We know how to signal them, how to respond, how to create space during activation, and how to process them together afterward. We know that navigating triggers is not about eliminating them; it's about meeting them with the awareness and care they deserve.

May we recognize when the past arrives. May we signal it with courage. May we hold each other steady through the activation. May we trust that what we navigate together makes us more resilient, more connected, and more free.

The past will visit. Let us meet it together.
