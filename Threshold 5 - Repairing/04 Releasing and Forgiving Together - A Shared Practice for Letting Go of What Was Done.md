# Releasing and Forgiving Together: *A Shared Practice for Letting Go of What Was Done*

---

Hello, dear companions.

There's a particular weight that comes from carrying something against someone we love.

It isn't the weight of the injury itself — that has its own pain, its own healing. It's the weight that remains *after* the repair: the guardedness that stays up, the flinch that hasn't relaxed, the way the past slips into the present like a filter over everything that happens between us. We've talked about it. We've taken responsibility. We've done the repair work. And still, something hasn't let go.

This is the territory of forgiveness — not the repair itself, but what comes after repair. The metabolic completion that allows the injury to become part of our history rather than the lens through which we see each other.

Forgiveness is one of the most misunderstood relational acts. It's been weaponized as an obligation ("You should forgive"), cheapened into a performance ("I forgive you" before anything has actually moved), and confused with bypass ("I'm over it" when the body clearly isn't). Real forgiveness is none of these. It's a bodily event — something that happens in the nervous system when the conditions are right, when the grief has been felt, when the accountability has been genuine, when the organism is ready to release what it's been holding.

It can't be forced. It has its own timeline. And it's one of the most courageous things two people can do together.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to release what has been done between them and move forward without the weight of the past.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing with honesty**, especially about what we're actually carrying and whether we're genuinely ready to release it
- **Returning regularly**, because forgiveness often completes in cycles rather than in a single moment

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what unforgiveness feels like in the body, and what genuine releasing feels like when it happens.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: what forgiveness actually is, what it requires, and what blocks it.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate forgiveness: the specific ways we create conditions for genuine releasing.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Forgiveness is an experience

*Forgiveness is what it feels like when something the body has been holding against another person finally lets go.*

### What unforgiveness feels like

Most of us know this feeling — even when we don't name it:

- **A wall that stays up.** We've repaired, we've talked, we've moved on. But somewhere in our body, something remains braced. A guardedness that won't fully relax.
- **The past leaking into the present.** A current disagreement activates the old injury. A small misstep gets the full weight of the unresolved thing. We're not responding to what's happening now; we're responding to what happened then.
- **A heaviness between us.** Something dense in the relational field — not active conflict, but a residue. A cloudiness where there used to be clarity.
- **Keeping score.** A mental ledger we don't want to keep but can't seem to close. A quiet accounting of what was done, still running in the background.
- **Withdrawal of the full self.** We're present but not entirely. We participate but hold something back. Part of us has retreated to a place the other person can't reach — and we're not sure how to come back.

Unforgiveness isn't a choice we're making. It's a state the body is in. The nervous system determined that something was done that warrants continued vigilance, and it hasn't yet received the signal that it's safe to stand down. Understanding this is essential: we can't simply decide to forgive any more than we can decide to fall asleep. We can create the conditions, but the release itself is something the body does.

### What genuine releasing feels like

When forgiveness actually happens:

- **Something unlocks.** A physical release — in the chest, the jaw, the shoulders, the belly. Tension we'd been carrying so long it felt normal suddenly isn't there.
- **The past becomes the past.** The injury doesn't disappear from memory, but it stops being a live presence. It becomes something that happened, not something that's happening.
- **Breath deepens.** The body literally breathes more freely. The constriction lifts.
- **The other person becomes visible again.** We see them as they are now, not through the filter of what they did. Their full personhood returns to our perception.
- **The field clears.** The heaviness between us lifts. There's more space, more air, more possibility.
- **We feel lighter — and sometimes grief follows.** The release of what we were carrying often brings tears. Not sadness about the injury, but grief for the time we spent holding on. This grief is part of the completion.

### What premature forgiveness feels like

It's important to recognize the difference between genuine release and its performance:

- **The words come but the body doesn't follow.** We say "I forgive you" but nothing shifts physically. The wall stays up.
- **We perform resolution.** We go through the motions because we're tired of the conflict, or because we've been told we should forgive, or because we want to be the kind of person who forgives.
- **The injury returns.** We thought we'd let go, but the same resentment resurfaces — in the next argument, in a quiet moment, in a dream. It wasn't released. It was suppressed.

Premature forgiveness isn't forgiveness — it's bypass wearing forgiveness's clothing. It delays genuine release by pretending it's already happened.

**A reflection for us:** Is there something between us that we've repaired but not fully released? Something the mind has moved past but the body still carries?

## Forgiveness is a process

*Forgiveness has its own logic, its own prerequisites, and its own timeline. Understanding how it works helps us stop trying to force it and start creating the conditions for it.*

### What forgiveness actually is

Forgiveness is the metabolic completion of the repair cycle.

When someone injures us in relationship, a process begins: recognition of the injury, accountability for what happened, repair of what was damaged. These steps are necessary — without them, forgiveness would be bypass. But they're not sufficient. After accountability and repair, something in the body may still be holding on. The injury has been addressed, but it hasn't been *released*.

Forgiveness is that release. It's the moment when the nervous system, having verified that accountability was genuine and repair was real, finally lets go of the vigilance it's been maintaining. It's the organism saying: *It's safe to stand down now. I don't need to keep watch over this anymore.*

This is why forgiveness feels physical — because it is. It's a reorganization of the nervous system's threat assessment. The injury is reclassified from active danger to completed event.

### What forgiveness is not

Clarity about what we're not talking about:

**Forgiveness is not condoning.** Releasing an injury doesn't mean it was acceptable. We can forgive what was done while being clear that it should never have happened and must not happen again.

**Forgiveness is not forgetting.** The memory of what happened remains. What changes is its charge — its ability to activate us, to filter the present, to keep us braced. We remember without being held captive.

**Forgiveness is not reconciliation.** In some contexts — particularly where abuse or ongoing harm is involved — forgiveness may happen without restoring the relationship. Releasing what we carry doesn't obligate us to return to vulnerability with the person who injured us. Within the context of this guide, we're primarily working with forgiveness between people who are choosing to continue together. But even here, forgiveness and trust operate on different timelines.

**Forgiveness is not an obligation.** No one is owed forgiveness. It can't be demanded, earned through sufficient apology, or extracted through pressure. It arrives when the conditions are met and the organism is ready — not before.

**Forgiveness is not a single event.** Deep injuries often require multiple rounds of releasing. We may forgive and then find the resentment returning — not because the forgiveness wasn't real, but because the injury had layers that revealed themselves over time. Each round of releasing is genuine; none of them need be the last.

### The prerequisites

Genuine forgiveness requires specific conditions. Without them, any releasing we attempt will be premature:

**Accountability must be genuine.** The person who caused the injury must have truly owned their impact — not explained, not justified, not minimized. If accountability is incomplete, the nervous system has good reason to stay vigilant. It knows the danger hasn't been fully acknowledged.

**Repair must be real.** The damage must have been tended to — not perfectly, but genuinely. If repair has been performative or partial, the body knows. It won't release vigilance over an injury that hasn't been adequately addressed.

**The pattern must have shifted.** If the same behavior is continuing, forgiveness isn't possible — because the injury isn't over. We can only release what has actually stopped. Forgiving an ongoing pattern isn't forgiveness; it's accommodation.

**Grief must have space.** Before we can release, we must mourn. The injury cost us something — trust, safety, time, innocence, a version of the relationship we believed in. That cost needs to be felt, not skipped over. Forgiveness that bypasses grief is forgiveness that won't hold.

### How forgiveness relates to grief

This connection is essential and often overlooked:

Every injury involves loss. Something was damaged or taken — trust, safety, a belief about the other person, a version of the relationship. Before we can release the injury, we must mourn what it cost. Otherwise, the grief gets trapped inside the unforgiveness, and we hold on to the resentment partly because we haven't finished grieving what was lost.

The sequence matters: injury → accountability → repair → grief → release. When grief is skipped, the cycle can't complete. Many people who "can't forgive" are actually stuck in unmourned grief. When the grief is finally felt, the forgiveness often follows naturally.

### What blocks forgiveness

Several common patterns prevent forgiveness from completing:

**Incomplete accountability.** If we sense the other person doesn't fully understand what they did, the body won't release. There's a precision to this — the nervous system knows the difference between genuine owning and a performance designed to make the problem go away.

**Ongoing danger.** If the pattern hasn't changed, if the behavior continues in subtler forms, the body stays vigilant for good reason. We can't release what isn't over.

**Unmourned grief.** The loss hasn't been felt. We've moved from injury to repair without pausing to grieve what was lost. The grief keeps the wound open.

**Identity attachment.** Sometimes we hold on to unforgiveness because the injury has become part of our identity — "I am the one who was wronged." Releasing the injury feels like losing a piece of ourselves. This isn't vanity; it's a real structural challenge.

**Fear of vulnerability.** Forgiveness means lowering the wall. If we lower it, we become vulnerable to being hurt again. The unforgiveness serves a protective function that the body may not be ready to relinquish.

**A reflection for us:** When we think about something between us that hasn't been fully released — what do we think is keeping the body from letting go? Is it incomplete accountability, unmourned grief, ongoing fear, or something else?

## Forgiveness is a practice

These are our best practices for cultivating the conditions for genuine releasing. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice recognizing what we're carrying

Before we can release something, we need to know it's there. This practice brings unforgiven material into awareness.

- **We check for residue.** We ask ourselves honestly: is there something between us that we've addressed but not fully released? Something that still activates us, still colors how we see the other person?
- **We notice the body's signals.** Tightness when certain topics arise. A guardedness that doesn't match the current moment. The impulse to bring up old injuries during new conflicts. These are signs that something remains held.
- **We name what we find.** "I think I'm still carrying something from when..." We bring it into the open — not as accusation, but as honest information about what's present.
- **We don't judge ourselves for carrying it.** Having unfinished business is normal. The body releases on its own timeline, not ours. Naming what we carry is the beginning of the process, not evidence of failure.
- **We ask our partner to receive without defending.** When we name what we're carrying, the other person's job is to listen — not to re-explain, not to apologize again, not to feel attacked. Just to hear what's still present.

*When to use: Periodically, as a relational hygiene practice. When we notice old injuries surfacing in current interactions. When something between us feels unfinished.*

---

### We practice verifying the ground

Forgiveness requires that accountability and repair are genuinely complete. This practice checks whether the ground is solid enough for releasing to happen.

- **We revisit the accountability.** Does the person who caused the injury fully understand what they did and how it impacted us? Not a performance of understanding — genuine comprehension. We check: "Do you understand what that cost me?"
- **We assess the repair.** Has the damage been tended to? Not perfectly — but genuinely. Does the repair match the injury? We check: "Does what's been done to repair this feel sufficient to you?"
- **We look at the pattern.** Has the behavior actually changed? Not just in the immediate aftermath, but sustainably? If the pattern is still active, we name it honestly: "This is still happening, and I can't release something that isn't over."
- **We acknowledge gaps if they exist.** If accountability or repair is incomplete, we don't try to force forgiveness. We go back and tend to what's missing. This isn't regression — it's foundation work.
- **We confirm readiness.** If accountability is genuine, repair is real, and the pattern has shifted, we acknowledge it: "The ground feels solid to me." This isn't a transaction — it's a shared assessment that the conditions for releasing are present.

*When to use: Before attempting to release a significant injury. When we're unsure whether our inability to forgive reflects an internal block or an incomplete repair process.*

---

### We practice completing the grief

Mourning what the injury cost — so that grief doesn't keep the wound open.

- **We name what was lost.** The injury took something: trust, safety, innocence, a belief about the other person, time, a version of the relationship. We name it specifically. "I lost my certainty that you would always be honest with me." "I lost the feeling of being completely safe."
- **We let ourselves feel the loss.** Not just acknowledge it — feel it. In the body. This may bring tears, anger, or a deep sadness. We stay with it, using the emotional processing skills we've built.
- **The other person witnesses the grief.** The person who caused the injury witnesses the cost — not to be punished, but because their witness is part of the completion. They see what it cost. They hold that.
- **We don't rush through grief to get to forgiveness.** Grief has its own timeline. If we try to shortcut it — to get to the "good part" where we've forgiven and moved on — we skip the step that makes real releasing possible.
- **We notice when the grief begins to settle.** There's a point where the mourning feels sufficient — not that the loss disappears, but that it's been honored. Something in the body softens. This settling is often the doorway to forgiveness.

*When to use: After accountability and repair are established. When forgiveness seems blocked and we suspect unmourned grief. When we need to honor what was lost before we can release it.*

---

### We practice releasing in the body

Forgiveness is a somatic event. This practice works with the body directly, creating conditions for the nervous system to let go.

- **We settle into presence together.** We use our co-regulatory practices to create a settled, safe space between us.
- **We locate the holding.** Where in the body is the unforgiveness living? Chest, jaw, belly, shoulders, throat? We bring awareness to that place.
- **We breathe into the holding.** Slow, deep breath directed toward where the tension lives. Not forcing release — inviting it. Creating space for the body to do what it's ready to do.
- **We soften if softening comes.** If the body begins to release — through tears, trembling, a deep exhale, a loosening of muscles — we let it happen. We don't control the release; we allow it.
- **We stay connected to each other.** Touch if welcome, eye contact, shared breathing. The relational channel reinforces the body's sense that it's safe to let go.
- **We accept whatever happens.** Sometimes the body releases. Sometimes it doesn't — not yet. Both are valid. If nothing moves, we honor where the body is and return another time.

The body doesn't release on command. This practice is an invitation, not a technique. Some sessions will produce dramatic release; others will be quiet, incremental. Both count. What matters is the repeated, gentle invitation to let go.

*When to use: When the prerequisites are met and we're ready to invite the body to release. As a regular practice when working with long-held injuries. When we want to support what the body is ready to do.*

---

### We practice offering forgiveness

The relational act of saying, with the full weight of our being: I release this.

- **We wait until the body is ready.** Not when we think we should forgive — when we actually feel the releasing happening. The words follow the body, not the other way around.
- **We speak simply.** "I forgive you for this." "I'm letting this go." "I release what I've been carrying." The words don't need to be elaborate. They need to be true.
- **We let the other person receive it.** Forgiveness is a gift. We offer it and let it land. We don't explain it, justify it, or dilute it with caveats.
- **We stay present with what follows.** Offering forgiveness can bring relief, tears, tenderness, or unexpected emotion. We stay with whatever comes.
- **We don't add conditions.** "I forgive you, but..." is not forgiveness. If there are conditions that need to be met, those belong in the accountability conversation, not in the releasing.

Offering forgiveness is a vulnerable act. We're saying: I am choosing to lower this wall. I am choosing to be undefended with you again in this place where I was injured. This deserves to be received with the gravity it carries.

*When to use: When the body is genuinely ready to release. Not before.*

---

### We practice receiving forgiveness

Being forgiven is its own vulnerable experience — and many of us are worse at receiving forgiveness than at giving it.

- **We let it land.** When our partner offers forgiveness, we resist the urge to deflect ("You don't have to forgive me"), minimize ("It wasn't that big a deal"), or rush past ("Great, so we're good?"). We let the offering arrive.
- **We feel what it brings.** Receiving forgiveness can surface guilt, relief, gratitude, grief, or shame. We let ourselves feel whatever comes rather than performing the appropriate response.
- **We accept the gift.** "Thank you. I receive this." Simple, direct, honest. We don't argue with their decision to forgive.
- **We don't use forgiveness as absolution.** Being forgiven doesn't erase what happened. It releases the ongoing hold the injury has on the relationship. We remain responsible for what we did — forgiveness doesn't undo accountability.
- **We honor the weight of what we've been given.** Someone chose to lower their wall with us after we gave them reason to build it. That's an act of tremendous courage. We honor it by continuing to be the person who deserves that trust.

*When to use: When forgiveness is offered. When we notice ourselves deflecting or rushing past being forgiven.*

---

### We practice self-forgiveness together

Sometimes the hardest person to forgive isn't the other — it's ourselves. This practice brings self-forgiveness into the relational space.

- **We name what we're carrying against ourselves.** "I haven't forgiven myself for what I did." "I still punish myself for how I handled that." We bring the self-directed unforgiveness into the open.
- **We let our partner witness.** Self-forgiveness in isolation often fails because shame keeps the material locked. Having someone witness our self-directed pain — without judgment — changes the conditions.
- **We apply the same process.** The prerequisites are the same: accountability (have we genuinely owned what we did?), repair (have we tended to the damage?), grief (have we mourned what our actions cost — ourselves and others?). Only then can releasing follow.
- **Our partner offers what we can't give ourselves.** Sometimes we need to hear: "You've taken responsibility. You've done the repair. It's okay to let yourself off the hook now." External permission can unlock what internal permission can't.
- **We treat ourselves with the same compassion we'd offer each other.** If our partner had done what we did and had taken full accountability, would we forgive them? Then we can begin to extend that same compassion inward.

Self-forgiveness isn't self-indulgence. It's the completion of a process that leaves us better able to show up for the relationship — less burdened, less defensive, less driven by the need to prove we're not the person who did that thing.

*When to use: When we're carrying guilt or shame about something we've done — in the relationship or outside it. When self-punishment is interfering with our ability to be present.*

---

### We practice renewing forgiveness

Deep injuries often need to be released more than once. This practice normalizes the return and makes space for re-releasing.

- **We recognize the return without alarm.** "That old feeling is back." The resentment, the guardedness, the filter over how we see each other. It's returned — and that's not a sign that our forgiveness was fake.
- **We understand why it happens.** Deep injuries have layers. Each round of releasing addresses the layer that was accessible at the time. Deeper layers reveal themselves later — often triggered by new events that echo the original injury.
- **We don't panic or pretend.** We don't suppress the returning resentment to protect the narrative that we've forgiven. And we don't despair that it came back. We simply notice: this needs another round.
- **We re-engage the process.** We check the ground (is there new accountability or repair needed, or is this the same injury requiring deeper releasing?). We feel the grief if more grief is present. We invite the body to release again.
- **We stay patient with the timeline.** Some injuries take years to fully release. Each genuine round of releasing makes the return less intense and less frequent. We trust the trajectory even when the moment feels discouraging.

*When to use: When we thought we'd forgiven but the old feeling returns. When new situations activate old injuries. When we need to release the same thing at a deeper level.*

---

### We practice living into forgiveness

What comes after releasing — the ongoing orientation that follows genuine forgiveness.

- **We let the other person be new.** Once we've released, we practice seeing them as they are now — not through the lens of what they did. This is an active discipline, especially at first.
- **We notice the old filter and set it down.** When we catch ourselves interpreting current behavior through the old injury, we name it: "That's the old filter. That's not what's happening now." We set it down and look again.
- **We let trust rebuild at its own pace.** Forgiveness and trust are on different timelines. We may have genuinely released the injury while still rebuilding trust. Both are real. We don't force trust to match the pace of forgiveness.
- **We protect what was released.** We don't bring the forgiven injury back as ammunition in future conflicts. What was released is released. Using it later as a weapon betrays the forgiveness.
- **We celebrate what's possible now.** With the weight gone, something new becomes available — more openness, more ease, more room. We notice what's different and let ourselves enjoy it.

*When to use: After genuine forgiveness. As an ongoing practice of living in the released space rather than returning to the old patterns. When we want to protect and honor the forgiveness that's been given.*

## Working with challenges

**When forgiveness is demanded or pressured.** "You should forgive me." "It's been long enough." "If you loved me, you'd let this go." Pressure to forgive is one of the most common ways forgiveness gets distorted.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We name the pressure.** "Asking me to forgive before I'm ready makes it harder, not easier."
- **We distinguish the request from the demand.** Wanting forgiveness is human. Demanding it is coercive. We can honor the desire while refusing the pressure.
- **The one who caused the injury focuses on accountability and patience.** Their job is to make the ground solid and then wait — not to manage the timeline.
- **We trust the body's pace.** If the body isn't ready, no amount of pressure will produce genuine release. Only patience and continued ground-building will.

---

**When the injury keeps happening in subtler forms.** The overt behavior has stopped, but variations continue — smaller versions, new expressions of the same underlying pattern.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We name what we see.** "The big version stopped, but I'm still seeing the pattern in other ways."
- **We return to accountability.** The pattern hasn't fully changed, which means the conditions for forgiveness aren't met yet. This isn't a failure of forgiveness — it's accurate information.
- **We address the root, not just the branches.** If the pattern keeps expressing in new forms, the underlying driver needs attention. This may require deeper work — possibly with professional support.
- **We're honest about where we are.** "I can't release this while it's still happening." This honesty protects both people from the false peace of premature forgiveness.

---

**When we've said the words but the body hasn't followed.** We've offered forgiveness — sincerely, we thought — but the guardedness remains. The wall is still up.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We don't criticize ourselves.** The intention was genuine even if the release isn't complete. Verbal forgiveness and somatic release operate on different timelines.
- **We check for unfinished grief.** Often this gap indicates that we haven't fully mourned what was lost. We return to the grief work.
- **We try somatic practices.** If the words have been said, the body may need direct attention — breathing, releasing exercises, co-regulatory settling — to catch up.
- **We give it time.** Sometimes the body needs to test the new reality — to verify, through experience, that the danger has genuinely passed — before it fully releases. Lived experience of safety is more convincing to the nervous system than words.

---

**When self-forgiveness is harder than forgiving the other.** For many of us, releasing what we hold against ourselves is far more difficult than releasing what we hold against our partner.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We bring the self-directed unforgiveness into the relational space.** Shame thrives in isolation. Speaking it to someone who loves us changes its conditions.
- **We examine what standard we're holding ourselves to.** Often it's an impossible one — a standard of perfection that no human could meet.
- **We let our partner's compassion reach us.** If they've forgiven us and we haven't forgiven ourselves, their compassion is data. We let it challenge our self-judgment.
- **We recognize when self-punishment has become identity.** "I'm the one who did that terrible thing" can become a fixed position. We ask: is our refusal to forgive ourselves actually serving anyone, or has it become a way of maintaining a particular self-image?

## The larger view

Forgiveness is not a virtue we perform. It's a metabolic act — the completion of a cycle that, left unfinished, keeps the past alive in the present and locks relational energy in maintaining vigilance over old injuries.

Every unfinished forgiveness cycle consumes resources. The body spends energy maintaining the wall, scanning for danger, filtering current experience through past injury. This is metabolically expensive. When forgiveness completes — genuinely, in the body — that energy is freed. It becomes available for connection, for creativity, for being fully present with each other. The relationship regains access to vitality that was locked in the past.

This doesn't make forgiveness easy or quick. Some injuries are deep, and their releasing takes years. Some may never fully complete — and honoring that is healthier than performing a resolution that hasn't happened. The practice is not about achieving forgiveness but about creating the conditions where releasing can occur when the organism is ready.

In a culture that either demands instant forgiveness ("just let it go") or glorifies grudges ("never forget"), the actual practice of working with releasing is rare. It requires patience from both people — the one who was injured and the one who caused the injury. It requires genuine accountability, honest grief, and a willingness to let the body lead. It's slow, unglamorous work. And it's some of the most important work a relationship can do.

What we release between us doesn't stay between us. People who have learned to forgive genuinely carry less armor into every interaction. They are more flexible, more generous, more capable of repair in all their relationships. The capacity for releasing is one of the most consequential relational capacities there is — not because it erases the past, but because it frees the future.

---

## Our commitment to releasing and forgiving

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice working with forgiveness honestly — neither forcing it nor avoiding it. Not perfectly, but genuinely.

We won't always get this right. We'll sometimes demand forgiveness before it's ready. We'll sometimes hold on longer than we need to. We'll sometimes perform releasing before it's real. We'll sometimes struggle to forgive ourselves long after the other person has let go. We are human, and this is some of the most delicate relational work there is.

But we now have a shared practice. We know what forgiveness actually is — a bodily release, not a cognitive decision. We know what it requires — genuine accountability, real repair, completed grief. We know it has its own timeline, and that timeline deserves our respect. And we know that when releasing genuinely happens, something opens between us that was closed — and what becomes possible in that open space is worth everything it took to get there.

May we carry less against each other with each passing year. May we grieve what was lost and release what can be released. May we give each other the time this work requires. May we discover that forgiveness, when it's real, doesn't diminish us — it frees us.

What we can release, we don't have to carry. Let us learn to let go together.
