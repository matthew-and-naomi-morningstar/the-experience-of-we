# Sharing Perspectives Together: *A Shared Practice for Holding Multiple Views*

---

Hello, dear companions.

We do not see the same world.

This sounds dramatic, but it is true in the most ordinary way. Standing in the same room, living through the same moment, we see different things. Not because one of us is right and the other wrong, but because each of us looks from somewhere — shaped by our history, our body, our needs, our position in life. There is no view from nowhere. Every view is from somewhere.

This creates one of the central puzzles of relationship: we are sharing a life, but we are not sharing a perception. What seems obvious to you is invisible to me. What I cannot stop noticing, you have never seen. We reach different conclusions about the same conversation and genuinely cannot understand how the other arrived where they did.

The usual responses don't work well. We try to convince each other — and create conflict. We abandon our view to adopt the other's — and lose ourselves. We pretend to agree while privately maintaining our position — and create something false between us.

This guide is about a different possibility. Learning to share what we see without imposing it. Learning to receive what the other sees without losing our own ground. Learning to hold both views at once — and discovering what becomes visible only when perspectives meet.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to navigate different perspectives with skill, honesty, and care.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go** — try the perspective-sharing exercises with real topics between you
- **Returning regularly**, because our perspectives keep evolving and so does our capacity to share them

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it actually feels like when perspectives are shared well between us, and what it feels like when they aren't.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: the capacities perspective-sharing requires and the movement from individual views toward mutual understanding.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate this capacity: the specific exercises that help us share, receive, and hold multiple views.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Sharing perspectives is an experience

*Sharing perspectives is what it feels like when we can see through each other's eyes — without losing our own.*

Good perspective-sharing feels like expansion. Our world gets larger. We understand something we didn't understand before — not because we were told, but because we were allowed to see from somewhere we've never stood.

We can often feel the difference in the body. When we're gripping a perspective tightly — identified with it, needing to be right — there's contraction: a narrowing, an urgency, a bracing. When space opens between perspectives — when we can hold ours and receive another's — there's a softening, a widening, something closer to breathing. Both signals are worth tracking. They tell us which mode we're in before our thoughts do.

When perspectives are shared well between us:

- We feel seen — our view is witnessed, not just heard
- We feel curious about each other's view rather than threatened by it
- Differences become interesting rather than dangerous
- We understand each other's actions better, because we understand where they're looking from
- We discover things that neither of us could see alone
- There is a richness to our conversation — a sense that we are actually exploring together

When perspective-sharing breaks down:

- We feel unseen — our view dismissed, argued down, or simply not taken in
- Differences escalate into conflict, with each of us defending our position
- We feel pressured to agree, or we pressure the other
- One of us dominates while the other accommodates
- We pretend to share a view that we don't actually share
- Conversation feels like debate or performance rather than genuine exchange

The difference between these two experiences often comes down to a few specific capacities — and those capacities can be developed with practice. It's worth naming that this can feel unnatural at first. Most of us have been trained to defend our position or accommodate the other's, not to explore difference together. The practice asks for something different from either of those defaults, and it takes time to develop.

**A reflection for us:** When has hearing the other person's perspective genuinely changed how we see something? What made that possible?

## Sharing perspectives is a process

*Perspective-sharing involves specific capacities that build on each other — from owning our own view to receiving another's to holding both at once.*

### Understanding what a perspective is

A perspective is not an opinion. It's not a preference. It is something more fundamental — it is **how reality appears from where we stand**.

Our perspective is shaped by:

- **Where we are:** our physical, social, and relational position. What is visible from where we stand.
- **What we've lived:** our history, our experiences, the patterns our past has taught us to recognize.
- **How we're wired:** our nervous system, our sensitivities, our perceptual tendencies. What we notice naturally and what we tend to miss.
- **What we need:** what we're oriented toward, what matters to us. Attention is always selective, and need shapes selection.
- **Who we are:** our identity, our roles, our sense of self. These frames shape what we perceive and how we interpret it.

Every perspective is partial. This is not a flaw; it is a structural feature of perception itself. To see anything is to see it from somewhere. And to see from somewhere is to not see from somewhere else.

This means:
- No single perspective captures the whole
- Every perspective contains something true — what is visible from that position
- Every perspective is incomplete — what is invisible from that position
- Every perspective can also be inaccurate, or shaped by limited or distorted information — partiality is not the same as equal validity
- Two perspectives together reveal more than either one alone

### The four capacities this practice develops

**Owning our view.** Recognizing that what we see is a view from somewhere, not the view from everywhere. This allows us to share without imposing — because when we know our view is a view, we can offer it as such.

**Holding our ground.** Staying connected to what we see even while hearing something different. Not hardening into defensiveness. Not collapsing into accommodation. Staying rooted while remaining open.

**Receiving the other's view.** Not just hearing words but genuinely attempting to see what the other person sees — to stand in their position temporarily and perceive from there. This is more than listening. It is entering.

**Holding multiple views at once.** The most demanding capacity — maintaining awareness of our own perspective and the other's simultaneously, without collapsing them into each other or choosing between them. Tolerating the tension of genuine difference.

### Why this matters for us

If perspectives are partial, then relationship offers something isolation cannot: access to more of reality.

When we can only see from our own position, we are limited to what our position reveals. When we can receive each other's perspective, we gain access to what the other's position reveals. When we can hold both views at once, we approximate something closer to the whole than either of us could reach alone.

This isn't just intellectual; it is relational. When perspectives can't be shared between us, we feel unseen. We can't understand each other's actions. Conflict escalates because we can't grasp what the other sees. Intimacy stays shallow because we can't truly meet. Sharing perspectives is how partial beings come to know more than their partiality allows.

### The foundations of perspective-sharing

Before we move into practices, here are the qualities that make genuine perspective-sharing possible.

**Humility about our own view.** Sharing begins with the recognition that what we see is not the whole picture. Our view is real — it captures something true — and it is also partial. Other positions reveal other truths. Humility is not self-doubt. It is accuracy about the limits of any single vantage point.

**Curiosity about the other's view.** When difference is met with curiosity rather than threat, perspectives can actually be exchanged. Curiosity asks: "What do you see from there? What is visible to you that is invisible to me?" This is a fundamentally generous orientation.

**Safety to differ.** Perspectives can only be shared honestly if it is safe to disagree. If naming a different view carries punishment — ridicule, dismissal, anger — we stop sharing what we actually see and start performing agreement. Real perspective-sharing requires that difference is welcome.

**Regulation.** When our nervous systems are activated, difference registers as threat. We defend our position, attack the other's, or collapse. Perspective-sharing requires enough regulation that we can hold difference without our survival systems taking over.

**A reflection for us:** What makes it hardest for us to hold multiple perspectives at once? When do we most tend to collapse into a single view?

## Sharing perspectives is a practice

These are our best practices for developing our capacity to share, receive, and hold multiple views. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice owning our perspectives

Before we can share our view well, we need to recognize it as a view — not as the truth, but as how things look from where we stand.

- **We notice the language we use.** Do we say "the truth is..." or "what I see is..."? Do we say "obviously..." or "from where I stand..."? Language that claims universal truth forecloses sharing. Language that locates us opens it.

- **We practice locating ourselves.** "From where I sit..." "The way I see it..." "My experience of this is..." "Given my history with this, what stands out to me is..." These phrases signal that we are offering a perspective, not declaring reality.

- **We own our interpretation separately from our observation.** "What I notice is [observation]. The story I tell myself about it is [interpretation]." This distinction helps the other person understand our view without having to adopt it.

- **We name what shapes our view.** "I know my history with this probably makes me more sensitive to..." "I'm looking at this from the position of..." Naming what shapes our perspective helps the other person hold it appropriately.

This practice seems simple, but it changes everything. When we own our view as a view, we can offer it with conviction without imposing it — because we genuinely understand that someone standing elsewhere might see differently, and also clearly.

*When to use: Every time we share a perspective. This is the baseline practice.*

---

### We practice sharing what we see

This is the practice of offering our perspective clearly and fully — without imposing it, but also without diluting it.

- **We ground first.** Sharing from dysregulation tends to produce imposition or collapse. We take a breath. We feel our feet. We settle enough to speak from clarity rather than reactivity.

- **We describe what we see, not just our conclusions.** "What stands out to me is..." "What I notice is..." We share the perception itself, giving the other person access to our *seeing*, not just our summary.

- **We share fully.** We don't truncate our view to avoid disagreement. We say what we actually see, even if it differs from what the other sees.

- **We share without overwhelming.** We pause. We check in. We make room for response. Sharing is not a monologue; it is an offering. We avoid talking at length without pause, without checking, without room for the other.

- **We invite response.** "Does this land for you?" "What do you see?" "How does this look from where you stand?" Sharing is not broadcasting. It is opening a conversation.

- **We own rather than universalize.** We say "what I see is..." rather than "everyone can see that..." or "it's obvious that..." We offer our view rather than demanding agreement or dismissing alternatives.

*When to use: Whenever we want to share our view on something. Especially valuable when we suspect we see things differently.*

---

### We practice receiving what the other sees

Receiving a perspective is not the same as hearing words. It involves a genuine attempt to see what the other person sees — to step into their position and perceive from there.

- **We set aside our view temporarily.** Not abandoning it — putting it aside so we can fully attend to what is being offered. We'll return to it.

- **We listen for the view behind the words.** We ask ourselves: "What must the world look like for them to say this? Where are they seeing from? What history shapes this? What needs orient their attention?" We resist the urge to listen only to build counterarguments.

- **We reflect back what we hear.** "What I'm hearing is..." "It sounds like from your position, you see..." Reflecting confirms that we received it and allows correction if we didn't.

- **We ask deepening questions.** "Can you say more about that?" "What makes that particularly visible to you?" "Help me see what you're seeing." These questions help us enter their perspective more fully.

- **We acknowledge the view.** "I can see how, from where you stand, this would look that way." Acknowledgment is not agreement; it is recognition that their perspective is a real view of something real. We receive fully before comparing or responding.

True reception is generous. It gives the other person's perspective room to be itself — to land fully, to be genuinely understood before any response. We don't translate their words into our frame or perform reception while taking nothing in.

*When to use: Whenever the other person is sharing their perspective. Especially important when their view differs from ours.*

---

### We practice holding both views at once

This is the most demanding practice — maintaining our perspective and the other's simultaneously, without collapsing into agreement or opposition. This capacity develops over time. We don't need to hold multiple views perfectly — even glimpsing the other perspective for a moment while staying connected to our own is already the practice working. What we're building is a muscle, not a switch.

- **We articulate both views internally.** "From my position, I see X. From their position, they see Y. Both are views of something real." Simply naming both perspectives helps us hold them.

- **We notice the pull to resolve.** When holding two views creates tension, we notice the contraction — the desire to choose, to collapse, to decide who's right. We breathe through it rather than obeying it.

- **We stay in the question.** Rather than rushing to resolution, we stay with the question that multiple perspectives pose: "What is going on that shows up this way to me and that way to them?"

- **We look for what each perspective reveals.** Instead of asking "which is right?", we ask "what does each view see that the other misses? What becomes visible only when both are held together?"

- **We tolerate genuine complexity.** Sometimes two views reveal that reality itself is complex — that the situation genuinely looks different depending on where you stand, and neither view is wrong. We let that be true without needing to simplify it.

This practice feels like spaciousness. When we can hold two views at once, something opens up — a wider awareness that contains more than either of us holds alone.

*When to use: When we've both shared our perspectives and they differ. When we want to explore what becomes visible in the space between our views. During disagreements.*

---

### We practice perspective-sharing about us

Some of the most powerful — and most vulnerable — perspective-sharing happens when the topic is each other, or the relationship itself.

- **We share what we see about each other.** "The way I experience you is..." "What I notice about how you are in this area is..." We offer our perception of the other person as a view, not as a verdict.

- **We share what we see about us.** "The way I see our relationship right now is..." "What I notice about how we are together is..." We offer our perception of the relationship itself.

- **We receive with extra care.** Hearing someone else's perspective on *us* is more activating than hearing their perspective on anything else. We give ourselves room to be affected, to feel, to process — without immediately defending. We move slowly here, and only with enough safety between us. Perspectives about each other land deep, and we prioritize care over completeness. Not everything needs to be said today.

- **We remember that perspectives about each other are still perspectives.** Partial. Shaped by position. Containing truth and also incompleteness. Even perspectives about us don't define us; they reveal what is visible from that position.

- **We look for what we can learn.** Sometimes the other person sees something about us that we genuinely can't see from the inside. Their perspective is a mirror — imperfect, but often revealing.

This is where perspective-sharing becomes most intimate. It is also where the foundations matter most — safety, regulation, humility, curiosity.

*When to use: When we want to share observations about each other or about the relationship. When something about the other person or the dynamic needs to be named. With care and consent.*

---

### We practice co-creating shared perspective

Sometimes — with mutual consent — we move from holding two views to building a view we can both inhabit. This is not compromise (each giving up something) but integration (finding what genuinely contains both).

- **We make sure individual perspectives have been fully shared first.** We don't skip this step. Premature synthesis produces false agreement.

- **We check consent.** "Would you be interested in trying to develop a shared view on this?" Co-creating shared perspective can't be coerced. It must be freely chosen.

- **We identify common ground.** What do both perspectives agree on? What is visible from both positions? We start with what is shared.

- **We map the differences.** Where do our views diverge? We name the differences clearly — not as problems but as information.

- **We inquire together.** "What might we both be seeing aspects of?" "What larger picture might include both our views?" "What becomes visible when we hold them together?"

- **We test what emerges.** As a shared perspective begins to form, we test it: "Does this capture what you see?" "Can you inhabit this view?" Both of us should be able to say yes.

- **We hold it lightly.** Shared perspectives are provisional. They can evolve as new information emerges or circumstances change. We don't carve them in stone.

Shared perspective isn't always the goal. Sometimes maintaining distinct views is exactly what's needed. The practice is knowing when integration serves us and when held difference serves us better.

*When to use: When coordinated action requires a common view. When the relationship would benefit from a shared understanding. When both of us genuinely want to understand together, not just understand each other. Never without consent.*

---

### We practice perspective-sharing in conflict

This is where the practice becomes hardest — and most valuable. During conflict, our nervous systems narrow, and the other person's perspective stops being interesting and starts being threatening.

- **We regulate first.** If we are too activated, perspective-sharing is impossible. We settle enough that we can hold someone else's view without our survival systems hijacking the conversation.

- **We share our view without attacking theirs.** "Here's what I see" rather than "You're wrong about this." We state our perspective as our perspective.

- **We receive their view even though it's hard.** The other person's perspective on the conflict will be different from ours. That's the whole point. We listen to understand, not to refute.

- **We resist the pull to win.** Conflict pulls us toward victory — proving our view and defeating theirs. Perspective-sharing during conflict asks us to hold both views, which means giving up the goal of winning.

- **We look for what the conflict reveals.** Often, when we can hold both perspectives on a conflict, we discover that the disagreement is pointing to something neither of us initially saw — a deeper need, an unacknowledged pattern, a genuine complexity.

- **We don't force resolution.** Sometimes perspective-sharing in conflict leads to resolution. Sometimes it leads to understanding without agreement. Both are valuable outcomes.

*When to use: During disagreements. After conflicts, when we want to understand what happened. When we notice we're locked in opposing positions.*

---

### We practice perspective check-ins

Regular, low-stakes perspective-sharing builds the capacity we need for the high-stakes moments.

- **We build it into our rhythm.** Periodically — over dinner, on a walk, at a check-in — we share how we see something. Not because there's a problem, but because we're practicing.

- **We choose a shared topic.** Something we've both experienced — a family event, a decision we're facing, something we read or watched, a change in our lives.

- **We each share our view.** Taking turns, we describe what we see. We use perspective-owning language. We share fully.

- **We receive with genuine curiosity.** We listen. We reflect back. We ask deepening questions. We notice what's different and what's shared.

- **We notice what becomes visible.** After both views are shared, we pause: "What do we see together that neither of us saw alone?"

The more we practice this with low-stakes topics, the more natural it becomes when stakes are high.

*When to use: Regularly, as ongoing practice. Before difficult conversations. As a way of staying connected and curious about each other's inner world.*

---

### We practice recovering when perspective-sharing goes wrong

It will go wrong sometimes. We will impose, collapse, pretend to agree, or get stuck. This practice helps us recognize and recover from the common failure modes.

**When one perspective dominates (imposition):**
- We name it. "I notice we've been mostly exploring one view."
- We rebalance. "I'd like to make sure the other perspective gets equal space."
- We return to basics — sharing without imposing, receiving fully.

**When one of us abandons their view (collapse):**
- We check in. "Did you genuinely shift, or are you accommodating?"
- We invite the perspective back. "I want to make sure I understand what you originally saw."
- We slow down. "Let's take more time before moving toward agreement."

**When we've created false agreement:**
- We test it. "Can each of us describe this shared view in our own words?"
- We surface hidden differences. "Is there anything about this that doesn't quite fit?"
- We're willing to go back. "Maybe we moved too fast. Let's return to our individual views."

**When we're stuck in opposing positions:**
- We reframe. Holding difference might be the appropriate outcome, not a failure.
- We change the question. "What would we need in order to act together, even seeing differently?"
- We bring new information. Sometimes stuckness indicates something is missing from both perspectives.

*When to use: Whenever perspective-sharing has gone off track. When we sense we're performing agreement rather than genuinely sharing.*

## Working with challenges

**When we have very different levels of this capacity.** Some of us are naturally more comfortable holding multiple views. Others have a stronger need for certainty, a harder time setting aside their own frame, or less practice receiving views that differ from their own.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We don't make one style the standard.** The more flexible person doesn't get to be the "better" one.
- **We practice at the edge.** The person who finds this harder practices receiving a little more. The person who finds it easier practices patience.
- **We start with low stakes.** We build the capacity with topics that don't trigger us before applying it to what matters most.
- **We value the attempt, not just the result.** Trying to receive another's perspective, even imperfectly, is the practice.

---

**When power differences shape whose perspective counts.** In many relationships, one person's perspective consistently carries more weight — because of personality, social power, emotional force, or habit. The other person learns to adapt to the dominant view rather than sharing their own.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We name the pattern.** "I notice your perspective tends to take up more space than mine." Or: "I notice I tend to defer to your view."
- **We actively rebalance.** The person whose view dominates practices making more room. The person who defers practices holding their ground.
- **We notice whose perspective shapes decisions.** Not just what we say — what we do. Whose view actually determines how we act?
- **We treat this as structural, not personal.** Power dynamics shape perspective-sharing even between people with good intentions. Without attention, perspective-sharing can reinforce existing power dynamics rather than balance them — the more dominant perspective gets validated as "shared" while the less powerful one gets absorbed or silenced.

---

**When the other person's perspective feels wrong.** Sometimes what the other person sees doesn't just differ from our view; it feels factually wrong, morally problematic, or based on bad information.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We still receive it first.** Even a perspective we believe is wrong contains information about the person holding it. We receive before we respond.
- **We distinguish between "wrong view" and "different view."** Some differences are genuinely about accuracy. Others are about position. We get clear about which we're dealing with.
- **We share our concern without dismissing their perspective entirely.** "I see this very differently, and here's why. But I want to understand why it looks that way to you."
- **We accept that some disagreements are real.** Not all perspective differences can be resolved. Sometimes two people genuinely see differently, and both have reasons.

---

**When we're afraid to share what we actually see.** Sometimes we hold back our real perspective because sharing it feels dangerous — it might upset the other person, create conflict, or reveal a gap between us we'd rather not face.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We name the fear.** "There's something I see that I've been hesitant to share."
- **We create safety for honesty.** "I want to hear what you really see, even if it's hard for me."
- **We share with care, not with caution.** Care means we share thoughtfully. Caution means we don't share at all. Care serves the relationship. Caution protects it in ways that eventually harm it.
- **We remember that unshared perspectives don't disappear.** They go underground — and emerge later as resentment, distance, or misunderstanding.

## The larger view

Sharing perspectives is how we bridge the fundamental gap between separate people.

Without this capacity, we can't truly attune — because attunement requires understanding where the other person is looking from. Without it, we can't resolve conflict — because resolution requires understanding what the other person sees. Without it, we can't co-create anything meaningful — because co-creation requires integrating different views.

When we can share perspectives well, we gain access to more of reality than either of us has alone. Our conversations become richer. Our decisions become wiser. Our understanding of each other deepens.

Perspective-sharing is not something we master once. Our perspectives keep changing — because we keep changing. New experiences, new information, new positions in life give us new views. The practice of sharing them continues for as long as we are in relationship. This isn't exhausting. It is one of the great pleasures of sustained relationship — the ongoing discovery of how the other person sees, the continuous expansion of our own view through contact with theirs.

When we develop the capacity to share perspectives between us, we become more capable of it everywhere — with friends, family, colleagues, strangers, people who see the world very differently than we do. In a world that increasingly sorts people into groups that see the same things and dismisses groups that see differently, the capacity to genuinely receive another's perspective is not just a relational skill. It is a contribution to the collective intelligence that our communities, our societies, and our species desperately need.

What we develop between us ripples outward.

---

## Our commitment to sharing perspectives

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to share what we see with honesty, to receive what the other sees with generosity, and to hold the complexity that arises when our views meet. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will not always do this well. We will impose. We will collapse. We will pretend to agree. We will get stuck. We will sometimes find the other person's view so foreign that we can't enter it, or find our own view so certain that we can't set it aside.

But we now have a shared practice. We know what perspective-sharing involves. We know the capacities it requires. We know how to recover when it goes wrong. And we know what becomes possible when it goes right — a richer understanding, a deeper connection, access to more of reality than either of us can reach alone.

May we share what we see with clarity and humility. May we receive what the other sees with genuine curiosity. May we hold complexity without rushing to resolve it. May we discover together what neither of us can see alone.

We are partial beings, seeing from somewhere, always limited, always incomplete. But together — sharing our views with care — we come closer to the whole.

Let us see together.
