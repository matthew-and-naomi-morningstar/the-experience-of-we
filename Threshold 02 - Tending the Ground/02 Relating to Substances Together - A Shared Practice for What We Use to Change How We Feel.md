# Relating to Substances Together: *A Shared Practice for What We Use to Change How We Feel*

---

Hello, dear companions.

There's a moment most of us know. The end of a hard day. The nervous system wound tight, the body carrying more than it can settle on its own. And there, on the counter or in the cabinet, is something that will take the edge off. A drink. A smoke. Something to shift the state. Something that works.

That's the thing we need to be honest about first: substances work. They change how we feel. They do it quickly, reliably, and without asking us to sit with anything uncomfortable. That's not weakness. That's a system under pressure finding the fastest path to relief. The question isn't whether substances change our state; of course they do. The question is what happens in the relational field when we reach for a chemical to do what our nervous systems were designed to do together.

This guide isn't a lecture about sobriety. It isn't a list of what to quit. It's about seeing — together, honestly — how the things we use to change how we feel shape the field between us. Alcohol, caffeine, cannabis, prescription medications that alter state, recreational substances, nicotine, sugar used as sedation rather than nourishment. All of them. Not because they're evil, but because they're powerful, and power that operates outside of shared awareness reshapes the relational field whether we discuss it or not.

What we're after here is shared awareness. The capacity to look honestly at what we use, why we use it, and what it costs, not in moral terms, but in relational ones. Together. Without judgment, without policing, and without pretending.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together: by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to relate honestly to the substances in their lives and how those substances shape the field between them.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go**, especially the conversations; they work immediately and may reveal more than we expect
- **Returning regularly**, because our relationship to substances changes over time, and so does the honesty we're capable of

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense: what it feels like when substances are shaping the relational field, and what it feels like when we meet our states without them.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how substances interact with the nervous system, why they're so effective in the short term, and what the relational costs look like over time.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to develop shared awareness around substance use: the specific ways we build honest conversation, shared agreements, and relational alternatives into our lives.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us, whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Relating to substances is an experience

*Relating to substances is what it feels like when something we consume changes the quality of our presence, and therefore the quality of the field between us.*

### When substances are shaping the field

Substance effects in the relational field aren't always dramatic. Often they're subtle: a slight dimming of presence, a softening of edges that also softens contact, a warmth that's chemical rather than relational. We learn to recognize the texture:

- **One of us is present and the other is altered.** There's a particular loneliness in being the sober person next to someone who's chemically shifted. The body knows the difference between someone who is relaxed and someone who is sedated. We can feel it: something about their eyes, their responsiveness, the slight delay between our words and their reception.
- **We're both altered and calling it connection.** Two drinks in and the conversation flows. The laughter comes easier. The guards drop. It can feel like intimacy — and parts of it may be genuine — but the body knows that what's been lowered isn't walls but awareness. Chemical ease and relational ease have different textures.
- **The edge has been taken off, and with it, something real.** The tension we were carrying was uncomfortable. But it was also information: about our day, our needs, the state of the relationship, what needs to be said. When we discharge that tension chemically, the information goes with it. The feeling changes. But nothing has been processed. Nothing has been heard.
- **We notice a pattern we haven't named.** One of us always has a drink before the hard conversation. One of us needs caffeine before they can be present. One of us takes the edge off before bed instead of settling together. The pattern is there, operating quietly, shaping the field.

### When we meet our states without substances

Something different happens when we stay with what we feel rather than altering it chemically:

- **There's an initial discomfort.** The nervousness, the tension, the edginess that substances would have removed: it's still here. We feel it. This is the part most people are trying to avoid.
- **The discomfort becomes information.** When we stay with it, the tension starts to reveal what it's carrying. We're anxious because something needs to be said. We're edgy because the day depleted us and we need contact. We're restless because something in the body needs to move. The feeling has content.
- **Connection becomes a regulation pathway.** Instead of reaching for the bottle, we reach for each other. And the nervous system settles, not through chemistry, but through co-regulation. The settling feels different. Cleaner. More complete.
- **We are more present.** Our eyes are clearer. Our responses are faster. Our capacity to receive each other is unobstructed. The field between us has full bandwidth.

The difference isn't that one state is moral and the other isn't. The difference is that one state allows the full relational field to operate, and the other reduces it.

**A reflection for us:** Is there a substance, anything at all, that we use regularly to change how we feel before or during our time together? What happens in the space between us when we do?

## Relating to substances is a process

*Substances interact with the nervous system through specific mechanisms, and understanding those mechanisms helps us see clearly what we're choosing and what we're trading away.*

### Why substances work

This isn't complicated, and it's important not to mystify it. Substances work because they interact directly with the body's own regulatory chemistry:

**They bypass the processing loop.** The nervous system has a natural sequence: sensation arises, we register it, we stay with it, it moves through, it integrates. This takes time. It sometimes requires another person. It can be uncomfortable. Substances shortcut the whole thing; they alter the neurochemistry directly, producing the state of relief without the process that would normally create it.

**They're fast.** Co-regulation takes presence, willingness, another person, and time. A drink takes thirty seconds. When the nervous system is under pressure and looking for the fastest path to relief, speed wins. This isn't a failure of character. It's a feature of how stressed systems operate.

**They're reliable.** Other people are unpredictable. They might not be available. They might not respond the way we need. They might be carrying their own activation. Substances deliver the same effect every time. This reliability is part of their appeal, especially for people whose early relational environments were unreliable.

**They numb what hurts.** Some of what we carry in our bodies is genuinely painful: old wounds, chronic stress, unprocessed grief, the accumulated weight of living in environments that were never designed for human nervous systems. Substances turn the volume down on that pain. For a while.

### The mechanism: premature discharge

There's a specific pattern here that runs through many of the practices in this series.

When a feeling arises — tension, anxiety, grief, restlessness — the nervous system activates. That activation is energy. It carries information. It's asking to be met, processed, moved through. When we stay with it, we metabolize it. The energy completes its cycle. We learn something. We discharge naturally, through the body's own completion process.

Premature discharge is what happens when we release that energy before it's been processed. The tension dissolves — but not because it was heard and integrated. It dissolves because something interrupted the cycle before it could complete. The relief is real. The processing never happened.

Substances are one of the most effective premature discharge mechanisms available. But the pattern is the same one we see in reflexive apology: saying sorry to make the discomfort stop before we've actually felt the impact. Or reflexive gratitude: jumping to thankfulness before the grief has been honored. Or scrolling, shopping, overworking, overeating. Any time we reach for something to change how we feel before we've let what we feel teach us what it came to teach, we're discharging prematurely.

The relational cost is specific. What gets discharged isn't just the discomfort; it's the information the discomfort was carrying. The conversation we needed to have. The need we needed to name. The grief we needed to share. The activation that, if we'd stayed with it, would have brought us closer together. All of it evaporates with the tension.

### How substance use shapes the relational field over time

The short-term effects are obvious. The long-term effects are structural:

**It creates a parallel regulation system.** Instead of two people regulating each other, there's a third element — the substance — doing regulation work that the relationship could be doing. Over time, the relationship's co-regulatory capacity atrophies. We don't develop the skill of settling each other because we never need to.

**It reduces emotional bandwidth.** Altered states narrow the range of what can be felt, expressed, and received. Conversations that need full presence get had at seventy percent capacity. Subtle signals — the slight tension in a voice, the change in someone's eyes — get missed. The relational field operates with reduced resolution.

**It creates asymmetry.** When one person is altered and the other isn't, the relational field becomes uneven. The sober person is navigating full information; the altered person is navigating partial information. This asymmetry creates a subtle power imbalance, and a loneliness in the sober partner that often goes unnamed.

**It masks what needs attention.** The anxiety we keep medicating might be telling us something about our lives. The tension we keep softening might be pointing to an unspoken need. The restlessness we keep sedating might be our bodies asking for change. Substances don't just change how we feel; they silence the messengers.

**It becomes invisible.** Perhaps the most significant long-term effect: what begins as a conscious choice becomes an automatic pattern. We stop noticing we're doing it. The drink before dinner isn't a decision anymore; it's just what happens. The substance fades into the background of the relational field, shaping it constantly but examined never.

### Substances vs. nourishment

This guide is distinct from how we relate to food and nourishment, because the mechanisms are different.

Food is about nourishment: giving the body what it needs to sustain itself. The relationship to food can become dysregulated, but food at its root is about replenishment. Substances, as we're using the word here, are about state-regulation. They exist in the relational field as something we reach for not because the body needs fuel, but because we want to change how we feel. The intention is different. The mechanism is different. The relational effects are different.

This doesn't mean nourishment and state-regulation never overlap; caffeine in coffee, sugar that sedates, the glass of wine that starts as pleasure and becomes need. But the distinction matters because it determines what we're actually working with. When we reach for food, the question is: are we nourishing ourselves? When we reach for a substance, the question is: what are we trying not to feel?

### The Adversary connection

In the framework of The Experience of We, the Adversary is the emergent system that benefits from human disconnection: the meta-pattern that feeds on our isolation, our fear, our inability to process together. Substances serve this pattern elegantly. They offer relief that bypasses relationship. They replace co-regulation with chemical regulation. They reduce our need for each other, or at least create the illusion that they do.

This doesn't make substance use a moral failure. It makes it a systemic pressure. The same environments that dysregulate our nervous systems also provide the substances to manage the dysregulation, and profit from both. We're not fighting our own weakness. We're navigating a system that is designed to sell us the cure for the disease it creates.

Seeing this clearly doesn't make the choice easier. But it does remove the shame. And shame removed is ground cleared for honest conversation.

**A reflection for us:** When one of us reaches for something to change how we feel, what is usually happening in the space between us at that moment? What might the feeling be trying to tell us?

## Relating to substances is a practice

These are our best practices for developing shared awareness and honest conversation around substance use. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice seeing what we use

The first step: honest inventory. Not to judge, but to see.

- **We each make a private list.** Everything we use to change how we feel. Alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, cannabis, prescription medications that alter state, supplements taken for mood, sugar consumed specifically for sedation. We include everything, including the things we've told ourselves don't count.
- **We look at frequency, not just category.** It's not just what we use. It's how often, when, and in response to what. One glass of wine a month is different from one glass every evening. The pattern matters more than the substance.
- **We notice what we've made invisible.** The morning caffeine we don't even think about. The evening drink that happens on autopilot. The substance we've so normalized that it doesn't register as a substance anymore. We bring it back into visibility.
- **We share what we find.** Not as confession. Not as accusation. As information. "Here's what I've noticed I reach for, and when."
- **We receive what's shared without alarm.** Our partner's honesty is a gift, not a threat. We listen with curiosity, not judgment.

*When to use: As a foundational exercise when beginning this work together. When we suspect there are patterns operating that we haven't examined. Periodically, as our habits change.*

---

### We practice noticing the moment of reaching

Between the feeling and the substance, there's a gap. This practice is about finding that gap.

- **We learn to notice the impulse.** There's a moment, often very brief, between the arising of a feeling and the move toward a substance. We practice catching that moment: *something is here, and I'm about to change it.*
- **We name the feeling underneath.** Before we reach, we ask: what am I feeling right now? What is the state I'm trying to get away from? Anxiety. Boredom. Sadness. Overstimulation. Loneliness. We name it, even roughly.
- **We share the impulse with each other.** "I'm noticing I want a drink right now." This isn't asking permission; it's bringing the impulse into the relational field instead of acting on it privately. Something changes when the reaching becomes visible.
- **We get curious together.** "What's happening right now that's making you want to reach for that?" Not interrogating, but exploring. With warmth.
- **We don't have to stop reaching.** This practice isn't about white-knuckling abstinence. It's about awareness. We might name the impulse and still have the drink. But we've done something important: we've made the automatic conscious. Over time, that awareness changes the pattern from the inside.

*When to use: Anytime we notice the impulse to reach for a substance. Especially when the reaching feels automatic or urgent.*

---

### We practice honest conversation about what substances do in our field

Bringing what has been private or invisible into the shared space.

- **We talk about what we notice.** "When you've had a couple of drinks, I feel like I lose access to you." "When I've had caffeine, I notice I'm more reactive." We describe our experience of the substance's effect on the field, not as accusation, but as observation.
- **We receive feedback without defending.** When our partner tells us what they notice, we listen. Not "I'm fine" or "It's not that bad." Just: "I hear you. Tell me more about what you're experiencing."
- **We explore history together.** "What's your relationship with alcohol been?" "When did caffeine become something you needed instead of something you enjoyed?" We share the story, not just the behavior. Understanding where the pattern came from changes how we hold it.
- **We name what substances have given us.** Relief. Social ease. Sleep. Pain management. Permission to relax. These are real gifts. Honoring what substances have provided makes it possible to explore what they've cost without the conversation becoming adversarial.
- **We name what substances have cost us.** Presence lost. Conversations had at reduced capacity. Mornings lost to aftereffects. A relational field operating at less than full bandwidth. The subtle loneliness of being next to someone who is chemically elsewhere. We name these costs honestly.

*When to use: When we're ready for deeper honesty about how substances operate in our field. When one of us feels the effects but hasn't yet named them. When patterns have been operating in silence.*

---

### We practice building shared agreements

Moving from individual habit to shared intentionality.

- **We co-create the agreements.** One person's rules imposed on the other isn't a shared agreement; it's control. Shared agreements emerge from honest conversation about what both of us want the relational field to feel like.
- **We start with what we both notice.** "We both notice that after two drinks, the quality of our conversation drops." From shared observation, agreements arise naturally.
- **We make the agreements specific.** "We won't drink during difficult conversations" is more actionable than "we'll be more mindful about drinking." Specificity makes the agreement real.
- **We include what we'll do instead.** An agreement to not reach for a substance is incomplete without naming what we'll reach for instead. Co-regulation. A walk. Honest conversation about what we're feeling. The alternative needs to be real and available.
- **We revisit and adjust.** Shared agreements aren't permanent decrees. They're living arrangements that grow with us. "Is this agreement still working? Does it need updating?"

*When to use: When honest conversation has revealed patterns we want to change. When we're ready to be intentional about how substances operate in our field. When we want the relational field to have the full bandwidth available to it.*

---

### We practice co-regulation as an alternative

The most direct alternative to chemical state-regulation is relational state-regulation. This practice strengthens that pathway.

- **When we notice the impulse to reach, we reach for each other instead.** Not every time. Not as a rigid rule. But as an experiment: "I'm wound tight right now. Can we sit together for a few minutes instead of me pouring a drink?"
- **We use the body.** Breathing together. Touch, if it's welcome. Walking side by side. The channels of co-regulation are specific and physical; they settle the nervous system in ways that are slower than substances but more complete.
- **We tolerate the slower pace.** Co-regulation doesn't work as fast as a drink. The nervous system takes longer to settle through relational channels than through chemical ones. We practice patience with the timing, and notice that the settling, when it comes, carries no aftereffect.
- **We celebrate when it works.** "That worked. I was reaching for the bottle, and instead I reached for you, and I settled." Naming the success strengthens the pathway.
- **We don't make this a competition.** Co-regulation and substances aren't enemies. Some days we'll co-regulate beautifully. Some days we'll have the drink. The practice is about building the relational pathway, not destroying the chemical one.

*When to use: When we notice the impulse to alter our state chemically and want to try a relational alternative. When we want to build co-regulatory capacity. When we're curious about what settling together can do that a substance can't.*

---

### We practice navigating different relationships with substances

In most relationships, we don't have identical relationships to substances. This practice addresses the asymmetry.

- **We acknowledge difference without hierarchy.** One of us drinks; the other doesn't. One of us uses cannabis; the other finds it distancing. These differences aren't about one person being more evolved. They're different nervous systems with different histories and different regulatory needs.
- **We don't police each other.** Monitoring, commenting on, or tracking our partner's substance use without invitation is control, not care. We share our experience of how it affects the field. We don't manage their behavior.
- **We name what's hard.** "It's lonely when you drink and I don't." "I feel judged when I can see you counting my glasses." The hard parts need to be spoken, not managed in silence.
- **We find the shared ground.** Even when our substance use differs, we can agree on what we want the relational field to feel like. Full presence. Honest conversation. Shared regulation. The agreement is about the field, not the substance.
- **We hold complexity.** One of us may have a history of addiction. One of us may be on medication that alters state in ways they didn't choose. One of us may come from a family where substance use was how connection happened — or how it was destroyed. We hold these histories with care.

*When to use: When our relationships to substances differ and that difference creates tension. When one of us feels judged or lonely around the other's substance use. When we want to honor difference while protecting the relational field.*

---

### We practice with specific substances

Different substances affect the relational field differently. This practice develops substance-specific awareness.

- **Alcohol.** The social lubricant. Lowers inhibition, which can feel like increased connection — but it lowers discernment at the same rate. We pay attention to the point where lowered walls becomes lowered awareness. We notice when alcohol is being used to have conversations we could have sober but are afraid to.
- **Caffeine.** The culturally invisible substance. So normalized that it barely registers as a substance, but it drives the nervous system into activation — increased heart rate, heightened alertness, reduced capacity for settling. We notice whether caffeine is fueling our presence or replacing rest.
- **Cannabis.** Can create a sense of expanded awareness or deep relaxation, but also reduces the crispness of interpersonal contact. We notice whether it's opening a door to something genuine or softening the field in ways that make real contact harder.
- **Prescription medications.** SSRIs, benzodiazepines, sleep aids, ADHD medications — these are often necessary and can be life-sustaining. The practice here isn't to stop them but to be aware of how they shape the field. Does the medication make us more available or less? Are side effects affecting our capacity to connect?
- **Nicotine.** Often used as a micro-regulation tool — a way to punctuate the day with small moments of chemical settling. We notice what the cigarette or the vape is substituting for. A pause? A breath? A moment of presence that could happen without the substance?
- **Sugar as sedation.** When sugar is used not for pleasure or nourishment but for state-change — to calm, to comfort, to sedate — it operates as a substance rather than as food. We notice the distinction.

*When to use: When we want to develop nuanced awareness about specific substances in our lives. When a particular substance seems to be playing a larger role than we've acknowledged.*

---

### We practice relating to our histories

Our relationships with substances didn't begin in this relationship. They have roots.

- **We share our stories.** "In my family, drinking was how adults relaxed." "In my family, any substance use was shameful." These histories shape what feels normal, what feels dangerous, and what goes unexamined.
- **We name what we inherited.** Some of us inherited substance patterns alongside our eye color: genetic predispositions, family cultures, modeled behaviors. We acknowledge what we received without being imprisoned by it.
- **We explore the function.** "When did you first use a substance to change how you felt? What was happening in your life at that time?" Understanding the original function illuminates the current one.
- **We hold each other's histories gently.** If one of us has a history of addiction, that history lives in the body. It doesn't need to be managed by the other person, but it does need to be known, held, and respected.
- **We distinguish past from present.** "This was my pattern. This is my pattern now. Are they different? In what ways?" We keep the conversation current rather than only historical.

*When to use: When we want to understand the roots of our current patterns. When substance use is triggering old relational wounds. When we need to hold each other's histories with more care.*

---

### We practice sitting with what we'd rather not feel

The most direct practice. The one that runs underneath all the others.

- **When the impulse to reach arises, we stay.** Not forever. Not heroically. Just for a moment longer than we usually would. We stay with the feeling that the substance would have removed.
- **We let the discomfort be here.** The restlessness. The edginess. The anxiety. The boredom that might not be boredom at all. We let it be here, and we notice what it carries.
- **We do this together.** "I'm uncomfortable right now and I'm choosing to stay with it. Will you stay with me?" The other person's presence makes the staying possible. This is co-regulation in its most honest form.
- **We notice what emerges.** When we don't discharge the feeling prematurely, something often surfaces beneath it. A need. A sadness. A truth we've been avoiding. Something the feeling was trying to bring us.
- **We honor what we find.** Whatever surfaces — a conversation that needs to happen, a grief that needs to be felt, a change that needs to be made — we honor it. This is what the substance would have silenced. This is the information that premature discharge eliminates.
- **We rest when we need to.** Sitting with difficult feelings is metabolically expensive. We don't push past our capacity. We rest, and we try again another day.

*When to use: When we're willing to experiment with staying instead of reaching. When we suspect the feeling we keep altering might be carrying something important. When we want to build our capacity to be with what's here.*

## Working with challenges

**When substance use is a survival necessity, not a choice.** Sometimes substance use isn't optional. Pain medication after surgery. Psychiatric medication that keeps someone functional. Caffeine that makes it possible to work the job that pays the rent. Not everything that alters state is available for renegotiation.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We distinguish between necessary and elective substance use.** Not to judge the elective, but to be clear about what's actually available for shared exploration, and what isn't.
- **We support what's necessary without resentment.** If our partner needs medication that reduces their emotional bandwidth, we adjust our expectations rather than treating the medication as an obstacle to connection.
- **We stay curious about the edges.** Even with necessary substances, there may be room for adjustment: dosing, timing, alternatives. We explore these with care and with professional support when appropriate.
- **We don't treat necessity as failure.** Needing chemical support to function in the current world isn't a failing. It's an honest response to environments our nervous systems weren't built for.

---

**When honest conversation about substances feels impossible.** Sometimes the topic is so loaded — with shame, with family history, with power dynamics — that bringing it into the open feels dangerous.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We start small.** We don't begin with the hardest substance or the deepest pattern. We start with something manageable — caffeine, perhaps — and build the conversational muscle.
- **We name the difficulty directly.** "This is hard to talk about. I'm afraid you'll judge me." "I'm afraid if I name what I see, you'll shut down." The fear is part of the conversation.
- **We separate observation from intervention.** We're not trying to fix anything yet. We're trying to see honestly. Seeing comes before changing.
- **We seek help if we need it.** If the conversation consistently stalls, a therapist or counselor can provide the container that makes honesty possible. This isn't weakness; it's wisdom about what the conversation needs.

---

**When one of us wants the other to change their substance use.** This is where the line between care and control becomes critical.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We share impact, not directives.** "When you drink, I feel disconnected from you" is different from "You need to stop drinking." One is an offering of relational truth; the other is a demand.
- **We examine our own motives.** Am I concerned about the relational field, or am I trying to control my partner? Am I sharing my experience, or am I trying to manage theirs? Honest self-inquiry matters.
- **We hold the tension.** We may genuinely be affected by our partner's substance use, and our partner may not be ready to change. Both things can be true at once. We hold the tension without ultimatums when possible, and we name honestly when the cost has become more than we can bear.
- **We remember that change that isn't chosen isn't change.** Pressured abstinence often creates resentment, secrecy, or relapse. Real change comes from within, supported by honest relational conversation, not imposed from without.

---

**When substance use is masking something that needs attention.** Sometimes substance use isn't the problem; it's the solution to a problem that hasn't been named. Depression, trauma, chronic pain, relational dissatisfaction, a life that doesn't fit.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We look beneath the substance at the need.** What is the substance doing? What would happen if it wasn't available? What feeling would surface?
- **We take what surfaces seriously.** If what's underneath the substance use is depression, we address the depression. If it's chronic pain, we address the pain. If it's relational disconnection, we address the disconnection. The substance was doing a job; something else needs to do that job now.
- **We support the transition together.** Shifting away from substance-based regulation is disorienting. The relational field becomes the primary container. We show up for that.
- **We seek professional support.** Addiction, dependency, and the conditions that drive them often need more than relational goodwill. We ask for help. We keep asking.

## The larger view

Substances occupy a specific place in the ecology of modern disconnection. They fill the gap where co-regulation should be.

In environments where our nervous systems were properly matched to our lives — where we lived in close physical contact with people who knew us, where the pace of life allowed for natural settling, where the field between us was sturdy enough to regulate through, the need for chemical state-regulation was marginal. People have always used substances, but they didn't depend on them for daily regulation. The relational field did that work.

What we have now is a massive regulatory deficit. Our lives activate our nervous systems faster than our relationships can settle them. Our schedules leave no room for the slow work of co-regulation. Our environments isolate us in ways that make the body chronically unsettled. And into that gap, substances flow, not because we're weak, but because the gap is real and substances fill it.

Seeing this clearly is the beginning of relating to substances honestly. Not with shame. Not with willpower. With understanding. We use substances because we live in environments that dysregulate us faster than we can co-regulate each other. The practice isn't to shame ourselves for reaching; it's to build the relational capacity that makes reaching less necessary.

This doesn't happen all at once. It happens one honest conversation at a time. One moment of reaching for each other instead of the bottle. One evening of sitting with the restlessness instead of sedating it. One shared acknowledgment that the drink we both called "relaxing" was actually replacing something the relationship could have provided.

Every substance we use with awareness is different from the same substance used on autopilot. And every time we choose to meet our state together instead of altering it alone, we strengthen the relational field that makes everything else in our life together possible.

---

## Our commitment to relating to substances together

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice seeing, honestly and without judgment, how the things we use to change how we feel shape the field between us. Not to police each other. Not to achieve purity. To see clearly and to choose with awareness.

We won't always get this right. We'll sometimes reach for the substance instead of each other. We'll sometimes choose the fast relief over the slow settling. We'll sometimes avoid the conversation because it's hard. We'll sometimes discover that patterns we thought were harmless have been quietly shaping our field for years. We are human, and the pressure to discharge prematurely is built into the environments we live in.

But we now have a shared practice. We know how to see what we use and when we use it. We know the difference between chemical settling and relational settling. We know that premature discharge silences the messengers alongside the discomfort. And we know that what we can see together, we can choose about together, which is fundamentally different from what operates in the dark.

May we see our habits clearly and hold them gently. May we find the courage to stay with what we'd rather not feel. May we reach for each other more often than we reach for the quick fix. May we build a relational field strong enough that the substances become a choice rather than a necessity.

What we use to change how we feel is not the deepest question. The deepest question is what we're willing to feel together. Let us keep finding out.
