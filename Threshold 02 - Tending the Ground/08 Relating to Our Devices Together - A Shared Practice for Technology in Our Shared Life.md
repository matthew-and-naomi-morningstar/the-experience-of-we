# Relating to Our Devices Together: *A Shared Practice for Technology in Our Shared Life*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Our devices are not neutral. They are designed — with extraordinary precision and billions of dollars of investment — to capture and hold human attention. Every notification, every infinite scroll, every algorithmic recommendation is engineered to pull awareness toward a screen and away from whatever else is present. Including each other.

This isn't a moral failing on our part. It's a structural reality. The same engineering that makes our phones useful also makes them competitors for the most precious resource a relationship has: shared attention. When one of us reaches for a phone mid-conversation, when both of us scroll side by side in silence that neither of us chose, when the ping of a notification fractures a moment of connection — these aren't signs of weakness. They're signs that we're living inside a system designed to do exactly this.

The question isn't whether our devices will compete with our relationship. They will. They already do. The question is whether we'll face that competition together — with shared awareness, shared agreements, and shared compassion for how hard it is to resist a machine that knows us better than we sometimes know ourselves.

This guide is about building that shared awareness. Not through rules one of us imposes on the other, but through mutual discernment about how we want technology to live in the space between us.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together: by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to relate to technology with awareness and intention, rather than being shaped by it without choosing.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing one agreement at a time**, letting it settle into our shared life before adding another
- **Returning regularly**, because our relationship to our devices shifts constantly. New apps, new habits, new pressures; our shared agreements need to evolve with us

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself. What it feels like when devices fragment our shared attention, and what it feels like when we're truly present without them.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds. How devices compete with the relational field, why resisting that competition is hard, and what happens in the space between us when screens mediate more of our attention than we've chosen.
- **Practice** is what we actively do. The concrete, shared ways we create agreements about how technology lives in our relationship.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us, whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Relating to our devices is an experience

*Relating to our devices is what it feels like when technology shapes the quality of our shared attention, for better or worse, and what it feels like to choose differently.*

### When devices fragment our attention

We know this feeling. We may not always name it, but we recognize it:

- **Half-presence.** One of us is speaking, and the other is glancing at a screen. The speaker can feel it: a subtle withdrawal, a partial absence. The words still land, but they land in a diminished field.
- **The interrupted moment.** We're close — emotionally, physically, in conversation — and a notification breaks in. The spell isn't just paused. Something in the quality of the moment shifts, and it doesn't fully return.
- **Parallel isolation.** We're in the same room, on the same couch, each absorbed in our own screen. Nobody chose this. It just happened. Hours pass. We look up and realize we've been *near* each other without being *with* each other.
- **The scroll hole.** One of us disappears into a feed. Time distorts. When we surface, we feel slightly hollow — and the other person may have been waiting, or may have given up and disappeared into their own screen.
- **The bidding that fails.** One of us reaches out — a comment, a question, a touch — and the other doesn't register it because they're absorbed in a device. The bid goes unanswered. Over time, these missed bids accumulate into a quiet erosion of connection.

### When we're present without devices

There's a different quality entirely:

- **Full attention.** When neither of us is pulled by a screen, attention becomes available. We notice each other's expressions, tone, body language. The field between us comes alive.
- **Unbroken flow.** Conversation develops naturally, without interruption. One thought leads to another. Silence becomes comfortable rather than something to fill with scrolling.
- **Contact.** We're in the same temporal and attentional space. We're not just physically together; we're experientially together. This is the difference between coexistence and co-presence.
- **Intimacy becomes possible.** Many of the deepest moments in a relationship happen in the gaps: the pauses, the quiet after the conversation, the time when nothing is being produced or consumed. Devices fill those gaps. Without them, the gaps open, and what lives there can emerge.

### The pull we feel

It's important to name: the pull toward our devices is real, and it's not just habit.

- **Dopamine loops.** Notifications, likes, new messages: they activate the same reward circuits as a slot machine. The pull isn't imagined. It's neurochemical.
- **Anxiety management.** Many of us use our phones to regulate discomfort: boredom, social awkwardness, the vulnerability of unstructured time together. The device becomes a portable exit from whatever is uncomfortable.
- **Identity and belonging.** Our online lives are real parts of who we are: friendships, communities, creative expression, work. The device isn't just distraction. It's also connection, just not to the person in the room.
- **The design is not an accident.** The people who build these platforms employ neuroscientists, psychologists, and behavioral economists to make them as compelling as possible. We're not weak for being pulled in. We're human, responding to engineering designed to exploit human response.

**A reflection for us:** When was the last time we spent an extended period together without any devices present? What was different about the quality of that time?

## Relating to our devices is a process

*Our relationship to our devices isn't static. It's shaped by design, habit, the economics of attention, and the particular ways technology interacts with our relational field.*

### The economics of attention

Attention is finite. We each have a limited amount of it in any given moment, and whatever captures it gets the best of our cognitive and emotional resources.

**Our devices are designed to win this competition.** The platforms on our phones are funded by advertising revenue, which means they are economically incentivized to capture and hold our attention for as long as possible. Every design choice — the infinite scroll, the autoplay, the notification badge, the algorithmic feed that always has one more thing — all serve this incentive. We are not the customer. We are the product being sold, and our attention is the commodity.

**The relational field has no such engineering.** Our relationship doesn't send push notifications. It doesn't optimize for engagement. It doesn't offer variable-ratio reward schedules. It offers something deeper and more nourishing, but slower, quieter, and easier to ignore in the moment.

**This creates an asymmetry.** Our devices offer high-frequency, low-depth stimulation. Our relationship offers low-frequency, high-depth nourishment. In a contest for moment-to-moment attention, the device will often win — not because it offers more but because it's louder.

### How fragmentation works

Device-related attention fragmentation follows recognizable patterns:

**The slow drift.** It rarely starts with a deliberate choice to ignore each other. It begins with a quick check: a glance at a notification, a brief scroll through a feed. But the check extends. The algorithm serves something interesting. Five minutes becomes twenty. The shared space empties out without either person choosing to leave it.

**The half-present conversation.** One person is speaking while the other is looking at a screen, or both are. Words are exchanged, but the full relational bandwidth isn't available. Tone, expression, feeling: these are lost to divided attention. The conversation happens, but the connection doesn't.

**The bedtime scroll.** The time before sleep, historically one of the most intimate windows in a shared day, becomes absorbed by individual screens. Partners lie side by side, each in a separate information stream, until one or both fall asleep. The transition from day to rest, which once belonged to the relationship, now belongs to the feed.

**The compulsive check.** Even in the middle of a meaningful moment — a meal, a walk, a conversation — the hand reaches for the phone. Sometimes there's a reason; often there isn't. It's a reflex, and each reflex is a micro-withdrawal from the relational field.

### The deeper pattern

Behind the mechanics of device use is something the framework names directly: the extractive logic of the attention economy is one expression of the larger pattern: the Adversary. The same system that optimizes for corporate profit through resource extraction also extracts human attention as a resource. Our devices are not simply tools we use. They are interfaces through which a global system feeds on the very attention that our relationships need to survive.

This isn't conspiracy. It's structure. The incentives of the attention economy and the needs of the relational field are genuinely in tension. Recognizing this doesn't mean we reject our devices; they are useful, sometimes essential, and woven into modern life. But it means we stop treating device use as a purely individual, neutral choice. It is a site where systemic forces press directly on the relational field.

### Why shared agreements matter more than individual willpower

Individual willpower is not enough to resist systems designed to overcome it. The platforms are too well-engineered, the loops too tight, the access too constant. A single person trying to resist the pull of their device through discipline alone is fighting an asymmetric battle.

**Shared agreements change the equation.** When both people in a relationship name the pattern, agree on what they want to protect, and commit to supporting each other, the dynamic shifts. It's no longer one person against a machine; it's two people orienting together toward what matters. The relational field itself becomes the support structure for different choices.

**Agreements, not rules.** Rules are imposed. Agreements are negotiated. A rule says "no phones at dinner" because one person decided it. An agreement says "we've both noticed that phones at dinner diminish our connection, and we've decided together to try something different." The distinction matters. Imposed rules create resentment. Shared agreements create solidarity.

**A reflection for us:** Have we ever talked explicitly about how our devices affect the space between us? If not, what has stopped us? If we have, what came of it?

## Relating to our devices is a practice

These are our best practices for relating to our devices with shared awareness and intention. This is not an exhaustive list, and we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice noticing the pattern

Before we change anything, we simply pay attention to what's already happening.

- **We observe our own device habits honestly.** How often do we reach for our phone? When? What triggers it? We're not judging; we're gathering data about what's actually happening.
- **We observe the relational impact.** When one of us picks up a phone, what happens in the space between us? Does the other person withdraw? Does conversation die? Does the quality of contact shift?
- **We name what we notice.** "I just realized I've been on my phone for thirty minutes." "I noticed you checked your phone three times while I was talking about my day." We share our observations without accusation.
- **We track the timing.** When are devices most likely to fragment our shared attention? Morning? Evening? Meals? Weekends? Knowing our vulnerable windows helps us make targeted agreements.
- **We notice what we're avoiding.** Sometimes device use is displacement: a way to avoid boredom, anxiety, difficult emotions, or the vulnerability of unstructured closeness. We get curious about what's underneath the reach for the screen.

*When to use: As the first step before making any agreements. When we suspect device use has shifted without our awareness. When we want honest data rather than assumptions.*

---

### We practice creating device-free windows

Specific times when we agree to set our devices aside and be fully available to each other.

- **We negotiate the windows together.** One person doesn't get to dictate when devices are banned. We discuss what feels workable for both of us and start with what we can genuinely sustain.
- **We start small.** The first twenty minutes after we come home. Meals together. The last thirty minutes before sleep. We don't try to overhaul everything at once; we protect specific windows and see what changes.
- **We make the devices physically absent.** Turning a phone face-down is not the same as putting it in another room. The mere presence of a visible phone, even one that's silent, has been shown to reduce the quality of conversation and connection. Out of the room is different from out of sight on the table.
- **We notice what fills the space.** When devices are gone, what emerges? Conversation? Silence? Discomfort? Boredom? All of it is information about what the devices were displacing.
- **We're compassionate about the discomfort.** The early moments of a device-free window can feel itchy: a pull toward the phone, a restlessness without stimulation. This is normal. It's withdrawal from a dopamine loop, and it passes.

*When to use: As a foundational practice. When we want to create protected relational time. When we've noticed that certain times of day are consistently lost to screens.*

---

### We practice responding to the pull together

Supporting each other when the pull toward a device competes with shared presence.

- **We acknowledge the pull without shame.** "I really want to check my phone right now." Naming it takes away some of its power and invites the other person into the experience rather than leaving them on the outside of it.
- **We offer gentle signals.** If one of us notices the other drifting toward a device during a protected window, we develop a shared signal — a touch, a word, a look — that says *come back* without saying *you're wrong*.
- **We don't police each other.** There's a difference between gentle support and surveillance. If our device agreements start to feel like one person monitoring the other, something has gone wrong. The spirit is mutual care, not control.
- **We celebrate small victories.** When we successfully resist the pull — when we choose each other over the screen — we notice it. Not with fanfare, but with quiet recognition. "That was a good dinner. I'm glad we were both here."
- **We're honest about failure.** We will break our own agreements. We will check the phone during a device-free window. When it happens, we name it, we don't shame each other, and we recommit. Rigidity in this practice creates resentment. Grace creates sustainability.

*When to use: During device-free windows. When one person notices the other being pulled away. When we want to build mutual support rather than individual discipline.*

---

### We practice protecting our transitions

The moments of transition — arriving home, waking up, going to bed — are where devices most often absorb the time that once belonged to the relationship.

- **We protect the arrival.** When one of us comes home, the first few minutes set the tone. If we walk in the door already on a phone, we've signaled that whatever is on the screen matters more than the person who is here. We practice arriving to each other first: a greeting, a moment of contact, a transition from the outside world.
- **We protect the morning.** The first thing we reach for shapes the first hour. If it's a phone, we begin the day in an information stream curated by algorithms. If it's each other, we begin in the relational field. We don't have to choose each other every morning, but we want it to be a choice, not a default.
- **We protect the threshold of sleep.** The time before sleep is uniquely vulnerable and uniquely valuable. It's where the day gets processed, where intimacy happens, where we metabolize what we've been through. When screens absorb this window, we lose something we can't easily recover elsewhere.
- **We name which transitions matter most to us.** Not all transitions are equal. We figure out which ones we most want to protect and focus our agreements there.

*When to use: When we notice our transitions have been colonized by screens. When the quality of our mornings, evenings, or arrivals feels diminished. When we want to reclaim specific relational windows.*

---

### We practice having the device conversation

A dedicated, non-blaming conversation about how technology lives in our relationship.

- **We make it a real conversation, not a complaint session.** The goal is shared understanding and mutual agreement, not one person telling the other what they're doing wrong.
- **We each share our experience honestly.** "When you check your phone while I'm talking, I feel invisible." "When you want me to put my phone away, I sometimes feel controlled." Both experiences are valid and both need to be heard.
- **We name what we want to protect.** Not just what we want to stop but what we want to create. "I want our evenings to feel connected." "I want us to eat together without screens." Naming what we're building is more generative than naming what we're against.
- **We draft agreements, not rules.** "Let's try no phones during dinner for a week and see how it feels." Agreements are experiments we run together. If they don't work, we adjust.
- **We revisit regularly.** Our device habits change. New apps, new pressures, new life circumstances change how we relate to our technology. The device conversation isn't a one-time event; it's an ongoing dialogue.

*When to use: When device tension has been building. When we've never explicitly discussed how technology affects our relationship. Periodically, as relational maintenance.*

---

### We practice using technology in service of the relationship

Technology isn't only a competitor for attention. It can also serve the relational field, when we use it intentionally rather than compulsively.

- **We share things that connect us.** A song that made us think of the other person. An article relevant to something we've been discussing. A photo from a moment we shared. When we send these to each other, technology becomes a vehicle for presence rather than an escape from it.
- **We use technology to stay connected across distance.** When we're apart, our devices can sustain the relational field — a text that says "thinking of you," a voice message, a video call that lets us see each other's faces. The same device that fragments attention when we're together can nourish it when we're apart.
- **We're intentional about what we consume.** The media we absorb shapes our inner world, which shapes the relational field. We can talk together about what we're consuming — not to censor each other but to be aware of how information environments affect us.
- **We create shared digital experiences.** Watching something together. Listening to music together. Playing a game together. Using a device as a shared experience is fundamentally different from using it as a private escape.
- **We recognize the difference between intentional and compulsive use.** Picking up the phone because we've decided to do something specific is different from picking it up because our hand reached for it automatically. The distinction is consciousness: choosing rather than being pulled.

*When to use: When we want to integrate technology rather than banish it. When we're looking for ways to make devices serve the relationship. When we want to move beyond restriction into conscious use.*

---

### We practice compassion for the difficulty

Resisting the design of our devices is genuinely hard. This practice ensures we don't turn the difficulty into a source of shame or conflict.

- **We remember that the system is designed to capture us.** When one of us falls into a scroll hole or checks the phone during a device-free window, we remember: a multi-billion-dollar industry is working around the clock to produce exactly this behavior. Failure is not surprising; it's expected.
- **We don't weaponize device agreements.** "You said no phones at dinner and you just checked yours," delivered with contempt, turns a shared agreement into a weapon. We hold our agreements with care, not punishment.
- **We name our own struggles.** "I'm really struggling to put my phone down tonight." This is vulnerability. It invites support rather than surveillance.
- **We adjust agreements when they're not working.** If an agreement creates more tension than it resolves, it needs revision, not more enforcement. The goal is shared presence, not compliance.
- **We honor that some seasons are harder.** Work pressure, crisis, health issues: there are times when device use increases because it has to, or because our capacity to resist is diminished. We extend grace in these seasons and return to our practices when we can.

*When to use: When shame or blame enters the device conversation. When one of us is struggling and needs support, not correction. When our agreements are creating conflict rather than connection.*

---

### We practice naming the larger pattern

Connecting our personal device struggles to the systemic forces that create them.

- **We talk about the attention economy together.** Not as abstract theory but as the specific system that designs our phones to keep us scrolling. Understanding the architecture helps us stop blaming ourselves — or each other — for falling into it.
- **We notice when we're being farmed.** Outrage content, clickbait, engagement traps: these are designed to provoke reactions that keep us on the platform. When we recognize the manipulation, we can choose differently. "I think this is designed to make me angry so I keep scrolling."
- **We discuss what our attention is worth.** If a platform is free, our attention is the product being sold to advertisers. We talk about what this means for how we want to spend our finite attention, especially the attention we share with each other.
- **We protect each other from extraction.** When one of us is caught in a loop — doom scrolling, rage reading, compulsive checking — the other can gently say, "I think you've been pulled in." Not as judgment. As a friend calling us back.
- **We hold the tension honestly.** We benefit from these platforms too. They connect us with friends, inform us, entertain us, sometimes sustain us. The relationship between benefit and extraction is complex, and we don't pretend otherwise.

*When to use: When we want to understand why this is hard. When we notice ourselves blaming each other for what is structurally produced. When we want to build shared literacy about the systems that shape our behavior.*

---

### We practice being bored together

Boredom is the space that devices are designed to eliminate, and it's also the space where some of the most important relational moments happen.

- **We resist the urge to fill every gap.** When there's a pause in conversation, a wait at a restaurant, a quiet evening with nothing planned, we practice letting the gap exist without reaching for a screen.
- **We notice what arises in the gap.** Conversation. Memory. Observation. Touch. Play. Discomfort. Sometimes nothing — and that's fine too. The gap doesn't have to produce anything to be valuable.
- **We talk about what boredom feels like.** For some of us, boredom triggers anxiety. For others, it triggers restlessness or sadness. Understanding our relationship to unstructured time helps us navigate it together rather than individually medicating it with screens.
- **We reframe boredom.** Boredom is not a problem to solve. It's an open space. What we do with open space, together, is one of the most revealing aspects of a relationship. If we can be bored together without reaching for escape, something deeper becomes available.
- **We practice being unstimulated.** Just sitting together. Watching the room. Looking out the window. Being present without input. This is a radical act in an attention economy that treats every unstimulated moment as a market failure.

*When to use: When we notice we can't be together without screens. When we want to explore what lives beneath the constant stimulation. When we want to build tolerance for the open, unstructured space that intimacy requires.*

---

### We practice regular check-ins about our digital life

Our relationship to technology changes constantly. Regular conversations keep our shared agreements alive and responsive.

- **We ask each other:** "How's your relationship with your phone right now?" Not as an accusation, but as a genuine inquiry.
- **We share what we're noticing.** "I've been scrolling more since I started following that account." "I've been using my phone to avoid something I don't want to feel." Honest reporting builds shared awareness.
- **We evaluate our agreements.** "Is the no-phones-at-dinner agreement working for both of us?" "Do we need to adjust our bedtime practices?" We treat our agreements as living things that need tending.
- **We stay curious about each other's digital lives.** Not surveilling, but curious. What are they watching? Reading? Following? Not to control but to know this part of each other's world.
- **We name new pressures.** A new job that requires more screen time. A family situation that demands constant availability. A new app or platform that's pulling us in. When the landscape shifts, our agreements may need to shift with it.

*When to use: Monthly, as relational maintenance. When we notice our device habits have shifted. When our agreements feel stale or aren't being followed.*

## Working with challenges

**When one person wants more device-free time and the other doesn't.** This is one of the most common tensions. One person feels neglected by the other's screen time; the other feels controlled by requests to put the phone down.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We listen to both sides without dismissing either.** Feeling unseen is real. Feeling controlled is also real. Both experiences deserve reception.
- **We look for the need beneath the position.** The person wanting less device time usually needs connection. The person resisting usually needs autonomy, or is managing something with the device that hasn't been named. We explore what's underneath.
- **We start with small, time-limited experiments.** "Can we try device-free dinners for a week and then talk about it?" Low-stakes experiments are easier to agree to than permanent rules.
- **We resist making it a moral issue.** "You're always on your phone" is a judgment. "I miss you when you're on your phone" is an invitation. The framing matters.

---

**When we've become each other's device police.** Our agreements have turned into surveillance. One person monitors the other's screen time. Resentment builds on both sides.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We acknowledge that policing means the agreement has stopped being mutual.** If it feels like enforcement, it's no longer a shared practice; it's control.
- **We return to the purpose.** We made these agreements to protect our connection, not to create a new source of conflict. If the agreements are causing more disconnection than the devices were, something needs to change.
- **We renegotiate from scratch.** What do we each actually want? What can we each genuinely commit to? We rebuild the agreement on honest ground.
- **We take responsibility for our own device use.** Each person manages their own behavior. We support each other; we don't supervise each other.

---

**When a device is genuinely needed.** Work demands, family emergencies, on-call responsibilities: sometimes we can't put the phone down, and our device-free agreements have to flex.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We name it explicitly.** "I'm on call tonight, so I need my phone nearby. I'm sorry about our usual evening." Naming the exception honors the agreement even when we can't keep it.
- **We distinguish between genuine need and rationalized habit.** "I need to check this" is sometimes true and sometimes a story we tell ourselves. We practice honest self-assessment, and we extend trust to each other's honest assessment.
- **We minimize the intrusion.** If we need to be available, we can still reduce the impact: checking quickly rather than lingering; stepping out of the shared space briefly rather than scrolling at the table; returning as quickly as possible.
- **We return to our practice when the need passes.** Exceptions are temporary. When the crisis ends, the on-call shift finishes, the deadline passes, we come back to our agreements without the exception becoming the new normal.

---

**When we realize our entire relationship is mediated by screens.** We text more than we talk. We watch shows instead of conversing. Our shared life has become a shared screen experience, and we don't know how to be together without one.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We don't panic.** This is incredibly common. Nearly universal in modern relationships. Naming it is the first step, not the last.
- **We start with one screen-free activity.** A walk. Cooking a meal together. Sitting on the porch. We don't try to reconstruct our entire relational life at once; we find one thing we can do together without a screen.
- **We tolerate the awkwardness.** If we've been relating primarily through screens, face-to-face presence can feel unfamiliar, even uncomfortable. That discomfort is not a sign that something is wrong. It's a sign that something is being reclaimed.
- **We be patient with each other.** Rebuilding unmediated presence takes time. We've been training our attention on screens for years. Redirecting it toward each other won't happen in a week. We give ourselves the time it takes.

## The larger view

Our devices are the most intimate interface between us and the attention economy. They sit in our pockets, on our nightstands, in our hands during meals and conversations. No other technology in human history has competed so directly and so constantly with the presence of the people we love.

This competition is not accidental. It is the logical outcome of an economic system that treats human attention as a commodity to be extracted and sold. The same system that externalizes environmental costs in pursuit of profit also externalizes relational costs in pursuit of engagement. Every hour of attention captured by a platform is an hour not available for the relationship, for the family, for the community. The system doesn't account for this loss. It doesn't appear on any dashboard.

But we feel it. We feel it in the half-present conversation, the missed bid for connection, the evening that disappeared into parallel scrolling. We feel it in the growing difficulty of simply being together without stimulation. We feel it in the strange loneliness of lying next to someone who is right here and also somewhere else entirely.

And yet — we are not powerless in this. The relational field is older than any algorithm, deeper than any feed, more nourishing than any notification. When we turn toward each other with full attention, we access something that no platform can replicate or replace. The warmth of real contact. The depth of unhurried conversation. The intimacy of shared silence. These are the things the attention economy cannot provide, and they are the things that sustain a life together.

Our devices will keep getting more compelling. The engineering will keep improving. The pull will not diminish. But two people who face this together — who name the pattern, support each other, and keep choosing presence — have something no algorithm can overcome: a shared commitment to what is real.

---

## Our commitment to relating to our devices together

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to face the reality of how our devices affect our shared life, honestly, without blame, and with shared resolve.

We won't always get this right. We'll check our phones during device-free windows. We'll fall into scroll holes while the other person waits. We'll break our own agreements and sometimes resent each other's. We are human, living inside systems designed to capture our attention, and perfect resistance is not possible, or necessary.

But we now have a shared practice. We know what full presence feels like when devices are set aside. We know how to create agreements that protect our shared attention. We know how to support each other against the pull without becoming each other's police. And we know that the forces working against our shared attention are systemic, not personal — that compassion and solidarity serve us better than shame or surveillance.

May we see clearly what competes for our attention and choose wisely where it goes. May we hold our agreements with care and release them with grace when they need to change. May we build a shared life where our devices serve our connection rather than consuming it.

Our attention is our most intimate offering. Let us offer it to each other together.
