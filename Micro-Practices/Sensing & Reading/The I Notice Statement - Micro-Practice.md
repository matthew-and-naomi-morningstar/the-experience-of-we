# The "I Notice" Statement: *A Micro-Practice*

---

There's a small space between what we feel and what we tell ourselves about what we feel. The body registers a sensation. The mind wraps a story around it. We mistake the story for the sensation, and we act on the story.

The "I notice" statement is the practice of staying in that small space, just long enough to name what's actually there before the story takes over. "I notice I'm feeling frustration" is different from "I'm angry at you." The first names what I can feel. The second turns that feeling into a story about you.

The shape is simple. You start a sentence with "I notice." You finish it with something you can actually point to: a sensation, a thought passing through, a sound in the room, an emotion arriving. That's the whole practice. The two words at the front do most of the work.

What makes this practice unusual is that it works just as well alone as with someone else. Solo, naming what you notice is a complete mindfulness practice. With another person, it adds layers: shared attention, shared reality, two people slowly becoming available to the same room.

---

## The practice

- **Start the sentence with "I notice."** The stem itself does a surprising amount of the work. It primes you to report rather than interpret.
- **Name only what you can actually feel, see, hear, or think:** a sensation, a thought, a sound, an emotion, a visible behavior. If a story is attached ("...because you don't care"), leave it off, at least at first.
- **Use it to name, not to project.** When a charged feeling rises, say "I notice I feel frustrated" before deciding who it's about. Naming alone often reveals that the nearest person wasn't the whole cause, or wasn't the cause at all.
- **Check for shared reality when you want to.** Add a second half: "...do you notice that too?" Let the other person confirm, adjust, or add what they're noticing.

"I notice" works best when it names your own experience or something concrete enough that another person could recognize it too. "I notice my chest is tight" is different from "I notice you don't care."

You don't have to use it constantly. A few "I notice" statements folded into a conversation can quietly shift its quality. Most of the practice's effects come from accumulation over time, not intensity in any single moment.

---

## What this does

The space between sensation and story is where most relational reactivity lives. The sensation rises. The story names a cause, often the nearest available one (the person across from you). The body acts on the story. By the time you've responded, the story is the only thing in the room.

The "I notice" statement interrupts that sequence by staying with the sensation longer. Not suppressing the story. Just slowing it down enough that you can see it forming. Often what reveals itself in that small extra time is that the story was wrong, or partial, or about something else entirely. The practice teaches the body-mind to report before it concludes.

The deeper gift, especially in close relationship, is what happens when two people practice this together over time. We begin to borrow each other's attention. One person catches body language; the other catches what was said. Between us, we hold more of reality than either could alone.

Shared reality is built one noticing at a time.
