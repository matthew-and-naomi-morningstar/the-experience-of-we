# Sensing Our Bodies Together: *A Micro-Practice*

---

There's a sense most of us were trained out of. Not sight or hearing or touch. The one that tells us, from inside, what's happening in the body right now. The dull tightness in the stomach. The lifting in the chest when something delights us. The early signal of hunger. The early signal of needing to move.

Most of us don't notice these signals until they reach an extreme. We don't notice hunger until we're starving. We don't notice thirst until we're parched. We don't notice anxiety until it's already loud enough to derail the day. Somewhere along the way, the body learned to stop reporting unless the reporting could no longer be ignored.

This is called interoception, the sense that lets us feel what's happening inside the body, and many of us have a quieter version of it than our bodies are capable of. The practice of sensing our bodies together restores this sense between two people, by using a relationship to remind both of us to listen.

Two people asking each other "what are you feeling in your body right now?" turns out to be one of the fastest ways to relearn this sense. The asking creates the pause. The pause creates the noticing. Over time, the noticing becomes more available without being asked.

---

## The practice

- **Ask each other.** "What are you feeling in your body right now?" Not "how are you feeling?" That question often pulls us into story. The body-version asks for sensation: tightness, warmth, fluttering, heaviness, pressure, energy. A natural moment to ask is when you notice your partner's face change (a wince, a smile, a tightening). The expression is the body already speaking.
- **Answer with sensation, not story.** A clenching in the chest. A heat in the face. A heaviness in the legs. Resist the urge to jump to interpretation ("I'm anxious about Thursday"). Start with what the body is actually doing right now.
- **Share proactively when you notice something.** Either of you can offer. "There's a fluttering in my stomach." "My shoulders just dropped." Naming a sensation when it arrives makes it shareable before it gets lost.

You don't have to use this every time. A few exchanges a day is more than enough to start. The skill grows quietly.

---

## What this does

The body is always communicating. Heart rate, muscle tension, breath, gut sensation, temperature, posture. All of it is information, all the time. Most of us have learned to ignore the gentle communications until they become loud ones. By then, we're managing crises that started as quiet signals hours or days earlier.

Sensing the body together rebuilds the habit of listening to the early signals. Two people asking each other regularly creates a feedback loop that an individual practice rarely matches. What one person notices, the other can ask about. What one person misses, the other often catches.

The deeper move is what happens to relational difficulty. So much of what we name as "I'm bored with you" or "I'm anxious about us" begins, on closer inspection, as a body sensation that got attached to a story. Going back to the sensation, *what's actually happening in my chest right now?*, keeps us tethered to what's true before the story gets to take over.

The body remembers how to report. The practice is just remembering to ask. Over time, what two people build together is bigger: a shared capacity to sense what's actually happening, and to know where our experiences align and where they don't.
