# Just Ask Me: *A Micro-Practice*

---

There's a kind of worry we sit with alone. A quiet recurring fear that the other person is mad at us. Or disappointed in us. Or pulling away. Or sees us differently than they used to. Whatever its specific shape, the worry has a particular pattern: it surfaces in our body, we don't know if it's real or imagined, and we try to figure it out by reading the situation rather than asking.

We don't ask because asking feels exposing. Or because we've been in relationships where asking didn't get a real answer. Or because we've been told we're "too much" for needing reassurance. So the worry stays inside us, and we work around it instead of through it.

Just Ask Me is the standing invitation to bring the worry into the open. A simple, prior agreement: whenever this specific fear shows up, one of us can ask, and the other commits to answering honestly, every time.

It works because the agreement closes the loop the worry feeds on. The worry feeds on uncertainty. Honest answering, available reliably, is what uncertainty cannot survive.

---

## The practice

Choose a recurring worry that one of you carries. Something specific and persistent. Then build a standing agreement around it, calmly, before the worry is active.

- **Name the worry out loud.** "I keep getting the feeling you might be mad at me." Make the implicit explicit. This is the threshold.
- **Offer the standing agreement.** "Any time you feel this way, just ask me. I'll answer honestly, every time."
- **Honor the agreement.** When the worry surfaces, the asker asks. The answerer responds with truth: "No, I'm not." Or, "Yes, there's something here, but I'm willing to talk about it with you." Both answers must be honest, and both kept simple in the moment, for the practice to work.
- **Use it as many times as needed.** Asking many times isn't a failure or a burden. The repetition is the practice working.

Once the answer has been given honestly, the asking is done for that moment. The next move isn't to keep cross-examining it. It's to let the body practice receiving what was said.

The honesty commitment is the engine. "I'm not mad" only lands as reassurance if both people trust that real tension would also be named when it's there. The agreement isn't "I will always reassure you." It's "I will not make you guess."

---

## What this does

Recurring relational worry usually lives in the dark. It can't be examined, because nothing checks it against reality. So it keeps generating evidence for itself, and we keep working around it, and the worry calcifies into an assumption about the relationship that may have nothing to do with what's actually true.

Just Ask Me drags the worry into the light, on demand, with consent on both sides. The asker doesn't have to figure it out alone. The answerer doesn't have to guess at what's going on inside the other. The worry has a place to go besides inward.

Over time, the practice rewrites something deeper. An unconscious habit of expecting the worst gets replaced by a habit of finding out the truth. The worry may stop needing to arrive so loudly.
