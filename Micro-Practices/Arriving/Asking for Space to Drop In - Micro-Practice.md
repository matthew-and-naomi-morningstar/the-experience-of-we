# Asking for Space to Drop In: *A Micro-Practice*

---

There's a small performance most of us run when we cross into shared space: that we're more present than we actually are. We say hello, we begin the next thing, all while a significant part of our attention is still finishing the previous one. It works, sort of. The shared space proceeds. But the quality of the first thirty minutes is doing the catching-up work that no one named.

Asking for space to drop in is the explicit alternative. It's the small move of saying out loud: I'm not actually here yet, and I need a few minutes to arrive before I can be.

It sounds simple. In practice, it's harder than it looks, because most of us were trained to perform presence rather than to admit we don't have it yet. The practice asks for honesty at the threshold instead.

When it's in place, the room doesn't have to absorb the cost of unmarked arrival. The partner who's been ready isn't left wondering what's off. The person who needs time gets it. The first thirty minutes stop being recovery work.

---

## The practice

When you cross into shared space and you're not fully here yet, name the gap and ask for time.

- **Notice you haven't actually arrived.** The cue is usually some version of: my body is here, my attention is still elsewhere. The phone call from earlier. The meeting that didn't end well. The drive that wasn't long enough.
- **Name it out loud, with a specific ask.** "I need ten minutes to land before I'm fully present with you." Or: "Give me twenty minutes to put the day down before we talk." Specific time helps; vague asks don't.
- **Take the time you asked for.** Not multitasking. Not scrolling. Actually arriving. A walk, a shower, sitting quietly, transitioning your body and attention out of the previous context.
- **Return when you said you would.** The time is the agreement. Honoring it is what makes the Buffer usable next time.

The ask isn't a rejection of the person waiting. It's a way of making sure they get your real presence instead of your leftover attention.

If you ask for ten minutes and discover you need twenty, that's fine. Update the ask out loud rather than just disappearing.

---

## What this does

A relationship that doesn't have this practice ends up with two patterns, both costly. Either we pretend to be present when we're not, and the partner reads the half-presence as distance, distraction, or rejection. Or we go silent and unavailable without explanation, and the partner reads the withdrawal as something they did.

The practice interrupts both patterns at the threshold. The naming says: I'm not here yet, and I want to be. The ask says: I need this much time. The honest gap becomes information instead of confusion. The practice protects the relationship from mistaking transition lag for relational distance.

What this teaches the relationship is that arrival is a process, not a moment. Crossing the door isn't the same as being here. The body needs help completing the transition. Saying so is part of what it means to share space well.

Over time, the practice does something quieter. You start noticing the un-arrived state earlier, before you've stepped into the shared room. The ask can be smaller because it gets used sooner. Eventually the transition itself becomes part of how you move through the day, and the cost of unmarked arrival mostly stops happening.
