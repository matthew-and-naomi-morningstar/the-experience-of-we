# Grounding Shared Presence Through Our Senses: *A Micro-Practice*

---

We can't will ourselves into the present moment. We can decide to be here, sit down, even close our eyes, and still spend the next five minutes mentally finishing an email. The mind doesn't take direction the way the body sometimes can.

The senses give us a simpler doorway. They're already in the room. They've been in the room the whole time. We just haven't been listening to what they're telling us.

This is the practice of using what's already present (sights, sounds, sensations) as the doorway back into shared experience. Not as a meditation. Not as a mindfulness exercise. Just as a quick, structured way of saying, out loud, what the body has been noticing all along.

When two people do it together, something more happens. The naming becomes a small landing strip we build for each other. By the time we've each said three things, there's usually more of us in the room.

---

## The practice

Take turns naming what you sense, right now, in this room.

- **One of you starts.** Name something you see. Just one thing, plainly. ("I see the light on the wall.")
- **Name something you hear.** ("I hear the refrigerator.")
- **Name something you feel in your body.** A sensation, not an emotion. ("I feel my feet on the floor.")
- **The other takes a turn.** Same three: see, hear, feel.
- **Close with each other.** "I'm more here now." "Me too."

You don't have to find anything interesting. The boring observations work just as well. The naming itself does the arriving.

If one of you can only find two of the three, that's fine. The practice isn't a checklist.

---

## What this does

Most of the time our attention is somewhere other than where our bodies are. Replaying, planning, scrolling internally. Present-moment sensation gives attention something real to return to. The more honestly we contact what we can see, hear, and feel right now, the more our awareness has a place to land.

Doing it together adds a second layer. We're not just landing in our own bodies. We're landing in the same room, with each other, with shared evidence that we've both arrived.

Presence becomes easier when it's externally shared. We're not each privately trying to be mindful. We're building a small common world out loud.
