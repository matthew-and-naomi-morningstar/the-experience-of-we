# Communing with the Natural World Together: *A Shared Practice for Remembering We Are Inside the Living World*

---

Hello, dear companions.

There's something we need to say plainly, because it's easy to miss: the natural world is not a place we go. It's the place we already are.

The air we're breathing right now came from trees and algae and ocean surfaces. The water in our cells fell as rain. The bacteria in our gut are an ecosystem as complex as any forest floor. The light hitting our eyes was filtered by atmosphere, shaped by clouds, bent by the curve of the earth. We are not separate from the living world and never have been. What we call "going to nature" is really just becoming conscious of what we've been inside all along.

But most of us don't experience it that way. We experience nature as background — a setting, a resource, a destination for weekends. Something we appreciate, photograph, enjoy, and leave. Our daily environments are climate-controlled, screen-lit, acoustically uniform. The sensory richness our nervous systems evolved inside has been replaced by something flatter, more predictable, more human-scaled. And this loss is so total, so normalized, that we don't even feel it as loss. We just feel vaguely depleted and don't know why.

This guide is about re-establishing a relationship. Not with an abstraction called "nature," but with the actual living world — the air, the soil, the water, the light, the sound, the other species, the weather, the rhythms of the day and the season — that we are always, already, inside of. And it's about doing this together, because when two people share this relationship, the relational field between them expands to include something larger than either of them. The third presence changes everything.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to feel, sense, and relate to the living world they are part of.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing outside when possible**, letting the natural world participate in your reading
- **Returning regularly**, because the forces that sever this relationship are persistent, and re-establishing it is ongoing work

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects, **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice**, we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it feels like to be in genuine sensory contact with the living world, and what its absence costs us and our relationship.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how the perception of nature as background formed, what happens neurobiologically when we re-attune, and why this relationship matters for the relational field between us.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the specific ways we open our senses, include the living world in our relational field, and build a sustained relationship with the earth we are inside of.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Communing with the natural world is an experience

*Communing with the natural world is what it feels like when we stop treating the living environment as backdrop and begin experiencing it as a field we are inside of — a shift from observation to participation that changes everything about how we feel, how we relate, and how we understand what we are.*

### When we are in relationship with the living world

There's a distinctive quality to being genuinely present to the natural world — not as spectators, but as participants:

- **The body settles differently.** Not the settling of relaxation, but the settling of coming home. Something in the nervous system recognizes this environment. The shoulders drop. The breath deepens. The low-level vigilance that most of us carry without noticing begins to dissolve.
- **Attention softens and widens.** Instead of the narrow, task-focused attention that screens and built environments demand, our awareness becomes diffuse, receptive, wide-angled. We notice peripherally. We hear layers of sound. We sense temperature, movement, texture. This is the attention our species spent most of its existence in — and it feels like relief to return to it.
- **The relational field expands.** When we're in genuine sensory contact with the living world together, the space between us isn't just between us anymore. There's a third presence — the environment itself — and it holds us differently than walls and ceilings do. Conversation becomes easier, or silence becomes more comfortable, because the pressure on the dyad to generate all the connection is reduced. The living world participates.
- **We feel held.** Not in the metaphorical sense of emotional holding, but in the literal sense of being inside something larger that is functioning, alive, and not asking anything of us. The earth beneath us. The sky above. The air moving. There's a support that comes from being inside a living system, and when we feel it, something in us releases.
- **Aliveness increases.** There's a vitality that comes from sensory contact with the living world — the feel of wind, the smell of soil after rain, the sound of water. These are not luxuries. They're the inputs our nervous systems were calibrated for, and receiving them after deprivation feels like drinking water when you didn't know you were thirsty.

### When this relationship is absent

The loss is so normalized that naming it can feel strange. But:

- **The sensory world narrows.** Indoor environments offer controlled temperature, uniform lighting, flat surfaces, predictable sounds. The sensory richness that our nervous systems evolved to process — fractal patterns, nonlinear soundscapes, variable light, chemical signals from soil and plants — is absent. We adapt to the narrowing, but the adaptation costs us.
- **Low-level depletion accumulates.** Without regular contact with living environments, a subtle fatigue builds that isn't resolved by rest or entertainment. It's a sensory and relational deficit — the absence of inputs the nervous system expects and the absence of a relationship the organism was built to maintain.
- **The relational field contracts.** When our entire environment is human-made, the relational field between us carries everything. All regulation, all stimulation, all meaning must come from each other or from screens. This is an enormous burden on a dyad, and it shows up as the vague sense that the relationship is carrying more weight than it should.
- **Extraction patterns remain invisible.** When we don't experience ourselves as part of the living world, we can't feel what we're doing to it. The disconnection that enables ecological destruction is the same disconnection that enables relational extraction — and it operates at the perceptual level, not the moral one. We don't mistreat what we feel ourselves to be inside of.
- **We forget what we are.** Without the living world reflecting us back to ourselves — as organisms, as animals, as participants in a web of life that is vastly larger and older than human civilization — we default to an identity that is entirely social, entirely human, entirely abstract. Something important drops out of our self-understanding.

**A reflection for us:** When was the last time we were in genuine sensory contact with the living world together — not passing through it, but inside it, present to it, affected by it? What did we notice? What happens between us when we've been indoors too long?

## Communing with the natural world is a process

*The shift from perceiving nature as background to experiencing it as a living field we are inside requires a perceptual reorientation — from observation to relationship, from object to participant — that is simultaneously neurobiological, relational, and ecological.*

### How nature became background

This is worth understanding, because it shapes what we're working against.

For most of human history, the living world was not a concept. It was reality. Our ancestors didn't "go to nature" because they were never not in it. Their nervous systems, their attention patterns, their relational structures, their entire way of being were shaped by continuous sensory exchange with living environments.

The shift happened gradually, then totally. Agriculture concentrated us. Architecture enclosed us. Industrialization separated work from land. Urbanization severed the daily rhythms of weather, season, and light from the rhythms of human life. And the digital revolution completed the process: we now live primarily in symbolic environments — screens, text, images, feeds — that provide massive cognitive stimulation while offering almost no sensory nourishment.

The result is that most modern humans have a *conceptual* relationship with the natural world — we know it exists, we may value it, we may advocate for it — but not a *sensory* one. We think about nature rather than feeling ourselves inside it. And the body knows the difference.

### What happens when we re-attune

When humans re-enter living environments with genuine sensory attention, measurable changes occur:

**The nervous system shifts toward parasympathetic dominance.** Heart rate slows. Blood pressure drops. Cortisol decreases. This isn't relaxation in the leisure sense — it's the nervous system returning to the regulatory state it evolved to function in. Built environments keep us in a low-level sympathetic activation that we've normalized as baseline. Natural environments restore actual baseline.

**Attention patterns change.** The narrow, directed attention that screens and tasks demand gives way to what researchers call "soft fascination" — a wide, receptive, effortless awareness. This is the attention pattern our species spent hundreds of thousands of years in. It's restful without being dull, alert without being taxing. It restores the prefrontal cortex in ways that sleep and leisure alone don't.

**Sensory processing deepens.** Natural environments provide the fractal visual patterns, layered soundscapes, variable light conditions, and chemical signals (phytoncides from trees, microbial compounds from soil) that our sensory systems were calibrated to process. Receiving these inputs after deprivation is like resuming a conversation the body has been waiting to continue.

**This isn't just calming — it's re-patterning what "normal" feels like.** Regular exposure to living environments doesn't just reduce stress temporarily. It recalibrates the nervous system's sense of baseline. Over time, the body begins to recognize the narrowness of built environments, the flatness of screen-light, the poverty of controlled air — not from a concept, but from felt contrast.

### The third presence in the relational field

Here is where this practice becomes specifically relational, and specifically different from individual nature immersion:

When two people enter the living world together with genuine attention, the natural environment becomes a participant in the relational field. Not a passive backdrop for their conversation or activity, but an active presence that shapes how they relate.

**The pressure on the dyad reduces.** In indoor environments, the two people are the entire field. All stimulation, all regulation, all meaning must be generated between them. In natural environments, the living world carries some of that load. There's more to attend to together. There's more to be held by. The relationship can breathe.

**Shared attention becomes non-self-referential.** When we watch a river together, listen to rain together, notice an animal together — our attention is synchronized, but it's not directed at each other or at ourselves. This creates connection without the weight of processing. It's one of the easiest forms of togetherness available to us.

**Co-regulation happens through the environment.** The calming inputs of the natural world don't just regulate each person individually — they regulate the field. Two nervous systems settling simultaneously in a living environment create a shared regulatory state that is different from, and often easier than, the co-regulation they can achieve through direct interpersonal effort alone.

**The relationship gains context.** Inside the living world, our concerns take on a different proportion. Not diminished, but contextualized. We're still us. Our relationship still matters. But we're also two organisms inside a living system that was here long before us and will continue long after. This is not a depressing thought. It's a grounding one. It puts what's between us inside something larger, and that larger holding changes how we carry it.

### Why this is systemic, not sentimental

The disconnection from the living world is not a lifestyle choice or a failure of appreciation. It's a structural condition of modern life. Most people live in built environments, work in built environments, commute through built environments, and recreate in built environments. The few hours spent "in nature" each month are insufficient to maintain what is fundamentally a full-time relationship.

Naming this matters because it removes the guilt. We're not disconnected from the earth because we don't care about it. We're disconnected because the systems we live inside were designed without reference to the living world — and in many cases, in active opposition to it. The practice isn't about feeling bad about this. It's about feeling what's missing and choosing, together, to restore what we can.

**A reflection for us:** How much of our shared life happens inside built environments? What would it feel like to include the living world more deliberately in our relational field — not as a special occasion, but as an ongoing relationship?

## Communing with the natural world is a practice

These are our best practices for re-establishing and sustaining a shared relationship with the living world. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice sensory contact

Returning to the body's relationship with the living world through direct sensation.

- **We go outside with our senses open.** Not to exercise, not to get somewhere, but to feel. What does the air feel like on skin? What do we hear when we stop and listen — really listen? What does the ground feel like under our feet? We let our senses lead, not our thoughts.
- **We slow down enough to perceive.** Speed prevents contact. We walk slowly. We stand still. We sit on the ground. We give our senses time to register what's actually here — the temperature gradient between sun and shade, the direction of the wind, the layers of sound.
- **We touch.** Tree bark. Grass. Stone. Water. Soil. Our bodies know the world through touch in a way that sight alone can't provide. We put our hands on things. We take our shoes off when we can.
- **We breathe with attention.** The air we're breathing was produced by the living world. We let that register. We notice the quality of outdoor air — its temperature, its movement, its smell — and we let the breath be a point of conscious exchange between our bodies and the atmosphere.
- **We share what we're sensing.** "Feel this." "Smell that." "Listen." We point each other toward what our senses are finding. We make the sensory experience shared rather than parallel.

*When to use: Every time we're outside together. When we want to shift from thinking about the environment to being in sensory contact with it. When we've been indoors too long and the body needs to reconnect.*

---

### We practice including the living world in our relational field

Deliberately expanding the space between us to include the environment as a participant.

- **We acknowledge the third presence.** When we sit outside together, we notice what's here beyond just us. The tree. The wind. The light. The birds. We let these be part of the encounter, not just scenery behind it.
- **We share attention with the environment.** Instead of facing each other and talking, we sometimes sit side by side and face the world together. We watch the same sky, listen to the same water, feel the same breeze. Our connection happens through shared orientation toward the living world.
- **We let the environment hold us.** Instead of generating all our own regulation through conversation and processing, we let the living world carry some of the weight. We rest in the environment's steadiness — the tree that doesn't need us to do anything, the creek that doesn't care about our problems, the ground that holds us without condition.
- **We talk less.** In natural environments, words are often less necessary. The sensory richness fills the space that conversation usually fills. We practice being together in the living world with less narration and more reception.
- **We notice what changes between us.** When we include the natural world in our relational field, something shifts — usually a softening, an easing, a sense of being less alone in a way that isn't about each other. We name this when we feel it.

*When to use: When we want to expand the relational field beyond the dyad. When the relationship feels like it's carrying too much. When we need co-regulation and direct interpersonal effort feels like too much work.*

---

### We practice reciprocal attention

Moving from consuming the natural world to relating to it.

- **We attend to what's actually here.** Not the idea of nature. Not the aesthetic. The actual organisms, weather systems, and processes happening around us right now. What is this tree doing? What are those birds communicating? What is the light doing at this hour?
- **We notice that we're being perceived.** We are not invisible in the living world. Birds respond to our presence. Insects adjust. The ground registers our weight. The air we exhale is taken up by plants. We let ourselves feel observed, included, and participated with — not just observing.
- **We ask what this place needs.** Not in the grand ecological sense (though that matters), but in the immediate, relational sense. Is there trash? Is there something damaged? Is there a way we can tend this place, even slightly? This shifts us from consumers of beauty to participants in care.
- **We return to the same places.** Relationship requires continuity. We develop ongoing relationships with specific places — a particular park, a stretch of river, a tree outside our home — and we return to them across seasons. We notice how they change. We let them know us.
- **We express gratitude.** Not performatively. But when we feel genuinely nourished by a place, we say so — to each other, and if it doesn't feel strange, to the place itself. "Thank you for this." The expression completes a relational circuit that reception alone leaves open.

*When to use: When we want to move beyond appreciation toward relationship. When we want to develop an ongoing connection with a specific place. When we notice that our orientation toward nature is still primarily consumptive.*

---

### We practice sharing meals outside

One of the oldest human practices: eating together in the living world.

- **We take meals outside when we can.** Not every meal. But when the weather and our circumstances allow, we eat together outside — porch, garden, park, any contact with open air and living things.
- **We let the meal be simple.** This doesn't require elaborate picnic preparation. Coffee on the back step. Lunch on a bench. Dinner on the porch. The point is contact with the living world during an activity we already share.
- **We notice how it changes the meal.** Eating outside changes the sensory field entirely — sounds, light, air, temperature. We notice how this affects our conversation, our pace, our sense of each other.
- **We acknowledge the chain.** The food we're eating came from the living world. Soil, water, sun, labor. When we eat outside, this connection is more visible. We let it register — not as guilt, but as belonging.

*When to use: As a simple, regular practice that doesn't require planning or travel. When we want to bring the living world into an everyday relational activity.*

---

### We practice weather as relationship

Relating to the weather as a living condition, not an inconvenience.

- **We go outside in conditions other than pleasant.** Rain. Wind. Cold. Heat. The living world doesn't stop when conditions are uncomfortable, and neither should our relationship with it. We experience weather — with appropriate care for safety — rather than hiding from it.
- **We notice what different weather does to us.** How does rain affect our mood, our conversation, our bodies? What about wind? Cold? Deep heat? We become students of our own responses to atmospheric conditions.
- **We stop complaining about weather.** Or at least, we notice when we do. "Bad weather" is a human judgment, not a fact. The rain doesn't know it's ruining our plans. We practice relating to weather as it is rather than as we wish it were.
- **We follow the light.** We pay attention to the quality of light at different hours and seasons. Dawn light. Midday light. The golden hour. Moonlight. We let light be a teacher and a source of shared attention.
- **We track the seasons together.** What's blooming? What's dying back? What's migrating? We pay attention to the seasonal rhythms our ancestors lived inside and that most of us have lost track of. We make seasonal change a topic of shared conversation.

*When to use: When our relationship with the natural world has become conditional on comfort. When we want to expand our tolerance for the full range of living conditions. When we want to be less governed by the narrowness of climate control.*

---

### We practice tending

Caring for a piece of the living world as a shared relational practice.

- **We grow something together.** A garden. A houseplant. Herbs on the windowsill. Anything that lives and that we tend. The practice of caring for another living thing together creates a shared relationship with life that is concrete and ongoing.
- **We care for a place.** We adopt a stretch of park, a section of trail, a creek bank. We pick up trash. We notice changes. We become stewards of a specific piece of the living world, not in the abstract but in the particular.
- **We feed the birds.** Or any practice of deliberately inviting the living world closer. Bird feeders, pollinator gardens, leaving wild edges in our yard. We create conditions for other species to participate in our shared environment.
- **We compost.** Returning organic matter to the soil is one of the most tangible ways to participate in the living world's cycles. Watching decomposition, smelling soil, handling the transformation of waste into nourishment — this is relationship with process, not just with beauty.
- **We let something be wild.** In our yard, on our balcony, in a pot — we let something grow without managing it. We let the living world show us what it does when we stop controlling it. This is a practice in trust and in humility.

*When to use: When we want an ongoing, daily practice of relationship with the living world. When we want to shift from receiving to reciprocating. When we want to include other species in our shared life.*

---

### We practice being in darkness together

Entering natural darkness without artificial light — one of the most powerful and most eliminated natural experiences.

- **We go outside at night.** Not with flashlights, not under streetlights. We find the darkest place available to us — a backyard with the porch light off, a park after dusk, a rural road, anywhere the sky opens and the ground dims. We let ourselves be in the dark.
- **We let our eyes adjust.** It takes ten to twenty minutes for the eyes to fully adapt. We stay long enough for this to happen. The world that emerges — shapes in starlight, the depth of a sky without light pollution, the way trees become silhouettes — is a world most modern humans never see. We let it arrive.
- **We listen to the night.** Darkness amplifies sound. The nocturnal world has its own soundscape — insects, wind, distant water, the particular quality of silence that only exists after dark. We let our ears become our primary sense and we share the listening.
- **We feel what darkness does to the nervous system.** For some, it brings deep calm — the parasympathetic system engaging in the absence of visual stimulation. For others, it activates vigilance or fear. Both responses are honest. We notice what arises and we hold each other through it.
- **We reclaim the night.** Our ancestors lived half their lives in darkness. The firelit hours, the star-watching hours, the quiet hours before sleep — these were relational and restorative. Artificial light has eliminated them so completely that most of us have never experienced genuine shared darkness. We practice returning to what was lost.

*When to use: When we want to experience a dimension of the natural world that modern life has erased. When we want deep sensory stillness. When we want to share something ancient and rarely accessed.*

---

### We practice being with water together

Entering relationship with water — one of the most universally co-regulatory elements in the natural world.

- **We go to water.** A river. A lake. The ocean. A creek. A fountain. A rain shower. We seek contact with water in its living forms — not from a tap, but from the world. We go where water is doing what water does.
- **We listen to water.** The sound of moving water is one of the most consistently calming inputs the nervous system can receive. A stream, rain on a roof, waves — we let the sound enter us. We don't talk over it. We let it be the primary voice in the field.
- **We touch water.** Hands in a stream. Feet in a lake. Standing in the rain. The sensory experience of water on skin is immediate and undeniable — it pulls us out of abstraction and into the body. We share this. "Feel this."
- **We bathe together.** Not necessarily in the romantic sense — though that can be part of it. Shared bathing is one of the oldest human practices: hot springs, rivers, communal baths. We create our own version. A shared bath. A swim together. Standing under a waterfall or a garden hose on a hot day. Water between us becomes a shared medium.
- **We watch water.** Rain on a window. A river's current. Waves. The way light moves across the surface of a pond. Water is endlessly fascinating to the human visual system because it never repeats. We let ourselves be absorbed by it together — shared attention without effort, connection without conversation.
- **We notice what water does for us.** After time near or in water, we check in: what shifted? Often there's a settling, a softening, a sense of having been cleaned that goes beyond the physical. We name this. We let it be part of our shared understanding of what nourishes us.

*When to use: When we need co-regulation and want the living world to help. When we feel stagnant or stuck. When we want sensory immersion that is immediate and grounding. When we want connection that doesn't require words.*

## Working with challenges

**When one of us doesn't feel connected to nature.** One person feels deeply at home in the living world; the other finds outdoor time boring, uncomfortable, or meaningless.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We start with sensation, not meaning.** Don't ask them to feel connected to nature. Ask them to feel the wind. The difference is enormous. Sensation is accessible even when meaning isn't.
- **We go short and comfortable.** Ten minutes on the porch. A warm drink outside. We don't start with the wilderness expedition. We start with the doorstep.
- **We stay curious about what's underneath.** Sometimes discomfort in nature is about the absence of stimulation, the confrontation with silence, or a lifetime of indoor conditioning. We explore gently, without judgment.
- **We respect different nervous systems.** Some people genuinely find certain natural environments overstimulating — insects, heat, unpredictable sounds. We work with what's tolerable, not what's ideal.

---

**When nature becomes aesthetic consumption.** We're photographing more than we're feeling. We're curating the experience rather than having it.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We leave the phone behind.** Or in the bag. Or at least, we designate phone-free portions of our time outside.
- **We notice the difference.** Between experiencing a sunset and capturing a sunset. Between being in a forest and taking a photo of a forest. The body knows the difference. We let ourselves feel it.
- **We ask: am I receiving or acquiring?** Receiving is open. Acquiring is grasping. The distinction is felt in the body, and naming it helps us return to contact.
- **We don't moralize about it.** Photos are fine. Sharing beauty is fine. The practice is about ensuring that documentation doesn't replace experience, not about banning documentation.

---

**When we use nature to bypass relational work.** Seeking the beauty and calm of the natural world to avoid addressing what's difficult between us.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We check our motivation honestly.** "Are we going for a walk to be in the living world together, or to avoid the conversation we need to have?"
- **We hold both.** Sometimes the walk is exactly the right place to have the difficult conversation. The living world can hold conflict differently than a living room can. The bypass is when we go outside *instead of* addressing what's here.
- **We let nature support the hard work, not replace it.** After a difficult conversation, a walk together can integrate what was processed. The order matters.

---

**When we project meaning onto nature without actually relating to it.** Talking about "oneness" or "connection to the earth" without any sensory grounding — living in the concept rather than the relationship.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We return to the senses.** "What do you actually feel right now? In your body? What are you hearing? What's the temperature of the air on your skin?" Concepts dissolve; sensation remains.
- **We relate to what's specific, not what's general.** Not "nature" — this tree. Not "the earth" — this soil. Not "the ecosystem" — that bird, right there. Specificity is the antidote to abstraction.
- **We practice silence instead of narration.** If we're talking about the experience more than we're having it, we stop talking. We let the living world be the teacher instead of our commentary about it.

## The larger view

We live inside the living world the way a fish lives inside water. It's so total, so constant, so fundamental that we've stopped feeling it. The practice of communing with the natural world is, at root, a practice of remembering — not learning something new, but recovering a relationship that is already present and has been present since before we were born.

This recovery has consequences far beyond the personal. When we experience ourselves as participants in the living world rather than observers of it, something shifts in how we relate to everything. The extraction patterns that drive ecological destruction become viscerally wrong — not because we've adopted a moral position, but because we can feel the relationship being violated. The separation that enables us to treat the earth as a resource becomes perceptually untenable, because we've felt, in our bodies, that we are inside what we were treating as outside.

When we do this together — when two people share the practice of including the living world in their relational field — the relationship itself becomes embedded in something larger than human concerns. The dyad gains a context. The field expands. And the ancient truth that humans are not separate from the earth, that we never were, becomes not a belief to hold but an experience to rest in.

This is quiet work. It doesn't look like much from the outside. Two people sitting on the ground. Two people walking slowly through a park. Two people standing in the rain, saying nothing. But underneath the quietness, something is being re-established — a relationship with the living world that we were born into, that modernity severed, and that can be restored, one sensory moment at a time.

## Our commitment to communing with the natural world together

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to remember that we are inside the living world and to let that remembering shape how we relate — to each other, to the earth, and to what sustains us.

We won't always do this well. We'll sometimes go weeks without genuine contact with the living world. We'll sometimes treat nature as backdrop, as aesthetic, as something to consume rather than relate to. We'll sometimes forget that we are organisms, that we are animals, that we are participants in something vastly older and more complex than our daily concerns.

But we now have a shared practice. We know that the living world is not a place we visit but a field we are inside of. We know that including it in our relational space changes the quality of our connection. We know that sensory contact with living environments is not a luxury but a regulatory necessity. And we know that the forces that sever this relationship are systemic, and that re-establishing it is an act of both intimacy and resistance.

May we go outside together. May we slow down enough to feel what's here. May we let the living world hold us when we are tired of holding each other. May we remember, in our bodies, that we belong to the earth — and that belonging is the ground on which everything else we practice stands.

Let us return to the world together.
