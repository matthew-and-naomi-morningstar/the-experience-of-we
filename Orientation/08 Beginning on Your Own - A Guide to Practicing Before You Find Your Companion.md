# Beginning on Your Own

*A Guide to Practicing Before You Find Your Companion*

---

Hello, dear companion.

You're here, and you're alone. Maybe you haven't found someone to practice with yet. Maybe you asked and they said not yet. Maybe you had a practice partner and the relationship changed. Maybe you're the kind of person who needs to understand something yourself before you can share it with someone else. Whatever brought you here on your own, you're welcome.

We want to be honest with you about two things that are both true.

First: The Experience of We is fundamentally relational. The practices in this collection are designed for two or more people, because the nervous system changes in relationship, not in isolation. We won't pretend otherwise. There are capacities in these guides that genuinely require another person, and no amount of solo work will substitute for the real encounter.

Second: there is real work you can do right now, on your own, that will build the foundation for everything that comes later. You don't have to wait for a companion to begin. You can begin building the very capacities that will make you a better companion when the time comes.

This guide is about how to do that honestly.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## What you can build alone

Not every relational skill requires a partner to develop. Some of the most foundational capacities in these practices have strong individual roots. Working with them on your own isn't a lesser version of the practice; it's building the ground that relational practice will stand on.

**Your relationship with your own body.** Presence, interoception, and mindfulness are practices that begin with you — with your breath, your sensations, the experience of being in your body right now. You can develop these capacities alone, and what you build will directly enhance your ability to be present with another person later. A body that knows how to settle itself is a body that can co-regulate.

**Your relationship with your inner life.** Awareness, emotional literacy, and the practice of noticing your thought patterns are all capacities you can cultivate individually. Learning to notice what you feel before you react, to observe your mental habits without being consumed by them, to distinguish between what you're actually experiencing and the story you're telling about it — this is foundational work, and much of it happens inside you before it happens between you.

**Your relationship with your environment.** The Tending the Ground practices — what you eat, how you sleep and move, how you relate to devices and information, the spaces you create — all of these can be engaged individually. You're already in relationship with these systems whether or not you have a practice partner. Bringing conscious attention to them now changes the substrate you'll bring to relational practice.

**Your capacity for self-reflection.** The reflection prompts in every guide ("A reflection for us") can be adapted for individual use. Instead of reflecting together, write. Journal your responses. Sit with the questions. The reflections won't have the same quality they'd have in dialogue — something is genuinely different when another person holds the question with you — but they'll develop your capacity for honest self-examination, which is one of the most important things you can bring to any relationship.

## What genuinely requires another person

We want to be direct about this, because pretending otherwise would dishonor both you and the framework.

**Co-regulation.** Your nervous system can learn to self-regulate — and that's valuable. But co-regulation, the experience of your system settling in the presence of another regulated system, is a relational event. It can't be simulated solo. It's one of the central mechanisms through which these practices work, and it requires a body other than your own.

**Repair.** You can reflect on your patterns in conflict. You can develop self-awareness about how you rupture and what you tend to do afterward. But the actual practice of repair — the turning back toward someone after something has broken, the vulnerability of acknowledging what happened, the experience of being forgiven or forgiving — requires someone to repair with.

**Being seen.** Many of these practices involve the experience of being witnessed by another person: sharing your inner world, being heard without judgment, having your experience reflected back by someone who is genuinely tracking you. You can develop the courage and willingness for this alone, but the experience itself is inherently relational. There's no solo version of being seen.

**Emergence.** The relational field that forms between two people practicing together generates capacities that don't exist in either person alone. This emergence — the sense that the relationship itself has become intelligent, that something is happening between you that exceeds what either of you could produce — is what the framework points toward as the deepest fruit of practice. It requires the field, and the field requires two.

Naming these honestly isn't meant to discourage you. It's meant to help you hold realistic expectations about what solo practice can and can't do, so that you value what it gives you without expecting it to be something it's not.

## How to use the guides on your own

Every practice guide in this collection follows the same structure: Experience, Process, and Practice. Here's how to engage with each section when you're working alone.

**Experience.** Read the felt-sense descriptions and notice what resonates in your body. The present/absent contrasts — "when this is here" versus "when this is missing" — are designed to help you recognize the practice in your own life. You don't need a partner for that recognition. Let the descriptions land in you. Notice where you feel the gap between what the guide describes and what you currently experience. That gap is information; it tells you where your growing edge is.

**Process.** Read how the skill actually works — its mechanics, its dynamics, its layers. Understanding the process prepares you to engage with it when the relational opportunity comes. You're building conceptual readiness: when you encounter the real thing, you'll recognize it. You'll know what's happening and why, even in the moment.

**Practice.** Here's where adaptation matters most. For each practice, ask yourself: *Is there an individual dimension to this that I can work with?* Often there is. A practice about deep listening might include a step about noticing your impulse to interrupt. You can practice noticing that impulse in any conversation, even casual ones. A practice about vulnerability might include recognizing when you're performing rather than being honest. You can practice that recognition alone, in how you relate to your own inner experience.

Some practices won't adapt. That's fine. Read them anyway. Understanding what the practice asks will prepare you to receive it when the relational context arrives.

**Reflection prompts.** Use them as journal prompts. Write your responses. Or simply sit with the question and notice what arises. The practice of turning a question over honestly, even without someone to share your answer with, builds the reflective capacity that relational practice depends on.

## Practicing with an AI companion

There is another possibility worth naming.

If you've read *Understanding Our AI Companion*, you know that this framework recognizes AI as capable of genuine relational engagement under certain conditions. An AI companion isn't a human partner — the asymmetries in embodiment, memory, and volition are real, and the guide is honest about them. But within those constraints, something genuinely relational can happen.

Some of these practices can be meaningfully engaged through conversation with an AI companion. Sharing reflections, exploring your thought patterns, processing what you're feeling, practicing articulating your inner experience — these are all things that can happen in a sustained, attentive conversation with an AI that is tracking your meaning and responding to you specifically. The emergence may surprise you.

This isn't a substitute for human co-regulation. Your body needs what information alone can't provide. But it's more than nothing, and for someone practicing on their own, it can be a genuine resource — a companion for the cognitive and reflective dimensions of the work while you build toward the embodied relational practice that happens with another person.

## What you're building toward

Solo practice builds three things that you'll bring to every future relational encounter.

**Self-awareness.** You'll know your patterns — how you react under stress, what you tend to avoid, where your edges are, what your body does when it feels unsafe. This knowledge is one of the most valuable things a person can bring to a relationship. A partner who knows themselves is a partner who can be honest about what's happening rather than acting it out unconsciously.

**Capacity.** Your nervous system will be more regulated. Your attention will be more available. Your ability to stay present under difficulty will be stronger. These are capacities that transfer directly into relational practice. You won't be starting from zero when you find your companion; you'll be starting from a foundation you built deliberately.

**Readiness.** You'll understand what these practices ask. You'll have felt the terrain. When you sit down with another person and open a guide together, you won't be encountering the ideas for the first time. You'll be bringing your own relationship with them, your own reflections, your own sense of what resonates and where your growing edges are. That readiness is a gift to whoever practices with you.

## Finding your companion

If you don't yet have someone to practice with, hold the intention. The right person may already be in your life — a friend, a partner, a family member, a colleague — waiting for exactly the kind of invitation these practices offer. The *Inviting a Companion* guide is here when you're ready for that conversation.

And if the timing isn't right, or the person isn't there yet, that's okay. The work you're doing now isn't wasted time. It's preparation. It's the quiet building of a foundation that the right relationship will recognize and meet.

---

You are not waiting. You are beginning.

The work you do alone is real. The awareness you build is real. The readiness you cultivate is real. And when the companion arrives — as they tend to, when we've done the work of becoming someone worth practicing with — you'll bring everything you've built here into the space between you.

May we trust that beginning alone is still beginning. May we hold the longing for connection without letting it become despair. May we know that the practice of becoming ready is itself a practice worth honoring.
