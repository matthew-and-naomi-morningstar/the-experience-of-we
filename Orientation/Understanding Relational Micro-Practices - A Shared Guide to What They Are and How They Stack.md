# Understanding Relational Micro-Practices

*A Shared Guide to What They Are and How They Stack*

---

Hello, dear companions.

When Naomi and I first started building a conscious relationship together, we tried to change everything at once. We'd read about presence, vulnerability, deep listening, repair, and we'd try to practice all of it simultaneously. We were ambitious, well-intentioned, and overwhelmed. The patterns we were trying to move beyond kept reasserting themselves. Big plans for becoming new kinds of partners kept collapsing back into old habits within days.

Then something shifted. Not because we found better techniques. Because we got smaller.

Naomi was carrying a recurring fear that I was mad at her. Instead of working on "communication" abstractly, we made a tiny agreement: any time the fear surfaced, she could ask, and I'd answer honestly every time. That was it. Three sentences and a standing commitment. We called it "just ask me." Within weeks the fear had stopped showing up the way it had been. Not because we'd talked about it endlessly. Because one small move, repeated, replaced one old pattern with one new pattern.

That's what we started calling a relational micro-practice. And once we noticed how that one small change worked, we started building others. Each one was small. Each one was specific. Each one did one thing well. And to our surprise, they started fitting together. The skill we built through "just ask me" made the next practice easier. That practice made the next one easier still. The system we ended up with wasn't designed; it emerged.

This guide is about that system: what a relational micro-practice is, why they work, how they stack into something larger over time, and how they relate to the broader practices that grow out of them.

A note: we describe this through our experience as a couple, because that's where we discovered it. But the principle adapts to any relationship close enough to share a few standing agreements. The practices themselves were built in our partnership. The pattern they reveal works wherever two people choose to tend the field between them.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## What a relational micro-practice is

A relational micro-practice is a small, repeatable agreement that helps two people live one relational value in one concrete moment. It has a shape: a sentence stem, a single agreement, a brief gesture, a closing marker. The shape can be repeated, refined, and eventually metabolized into a way of being. The smallness is essential. So is the agreement.

It's easier to recognize by contrast.

"Be more honest" is advice. It names a value but offers no foothold. It can't be tried tonight in a specific moment. It doesn't tell you what to actually do.

"Any time you wonder if I'm mad at you, just ask me. I'll answer honestly every time" is a micro-practice. It has a specific repeatable form. It can be tried tonight. It tells both people what to do. It addresses a recurring snag with a small, deliberate adjustment.

The difference between advice and a micro-practice is the *shape*. Without a shape, the most beautiful relational principle can't actually be lived. With a shape, even an ordinary moment can become a doorway.

We sometimes describe a micro-practice as a kind of operating system patch — or, more organically, as a small change in the living pattern between two people. Not a dramatic overhaul. One small fix that quietly changes how the next interaction unfolds.

## Why micro-practices work

A few features make a micro-practice work in ways that broader intentions don't.

**Shape.** The micro-practice has a specific form a body can actually hold. "Be more present" is hard to hold. "Pause and take one breath together before the next thing begins" is a body-sized move. The shape is what lets a principle become an action.

**Agreement.** Both people hold the practice, not just one. The asker can ask freely because the answerer has committed to receive. The shared agreement is doing the work, not anyone's individual willpower. This is what distinguishes a micro-practice from a personal resolution. It also means a micro-practice works best when it's chosen, not imposed. One person can't turn a relational move into a shared practice by deciding alone that the relationship "should" use it. The agreement is part of the practice.

**Making the implicit explicit.** Most relational friction comes from what stays unspoken: assumptions, fears, expectations that run quietly and shape everything. A micro-practice usually moves something out of the implicit, where it can do invisible damage, into the explicit, where it can be tended. That move alone often does most of the work.

**Repetition.** Done a few times, a micro-practice is a deliberate act. Done dozens of times, it becomes a habit. Done over months, it becomes the way you are with each other. The repetition isn't an obstacle; it's how the change actually lives in the body.

**Smallness.** The smallness is the feature, not the limitation. A small move can be tried tonight. A small move can be done when one of you is tired. A small move can be picked up again after a hard week without starting over. Whatever else is happening in your relationship, a micro-practice is sized to be available.

## How micro-practices stack

Here's the part that surprised us most: the more micro-practices we took on, the easier each new one became to learn.

We didn't plan this. We discovered it.

"Just ask me" gave us experience with a standing honesty commitment. When we later built the conscious check-in, a more general practice of pausing to say what's unspoken, the honesty commitment was already in place. We didn't have to rebuild trust from scratch. The earlier practice had laid groundwork for the later one.

When we built the "I notice" statement, a way of naming directly observable phenomena without immediately wrapping a story around them, it sharpened our capacity to bring noticings to a check-in. The conscious check-in was strengthened by what came before. Both of these made the next practice easier: setting a conscious intention together. We already had a way to notice dissonance, a way to bring it up, and a habit of answering honestly when we did.

By the time five or six practices were in place, they weren't a list anymore. They were a system. Each new practice could draw on everything that had already been built.

This is what we call stacking, and it's the difference between collecting techniques and building relational capacity. A collection of techniques stays a collection. A stacked system becomes the way two people are together.

Practices stack because every relational skill is built from smaller ones. The capacity to repair a rupture, for example, rests on the capacity to notice when something is off, the capacity to pause, the capacity to say what's true without weaponizing it, the capacity to receive what was said without going into defense. Each of those is itself buildable, and each can be a micro-practice with its own shape. The larger work becomes possible when the smaller ones are in place.

The stacking isn't a sequence anyone has to follow. We didn't follow one. We picked the next practice that called to us based on what our relationship was asking for. Over time the practices arranged themselves into patterns we could see only in retrospect. The system that emerged wasn't designed. It came from honest attention to small things, repeated.

## How micro-practices relate to the larger practices

The larger practices that grew out of our work are made of stacked micro-practices. We call these the We Practices, organized along the developmental arc that the Relational Arc guide describes.

The Repairing practice, for example, isn't a single move. It's the integration of a handful of micro-practices: noticing when something is wrong, pausing before responding, naming impact specifically, receiving without defensiveness. Someone who arrives at Repairing without the smaller capacities in place will find it hard. Someone who arrives with the fundamentals already practiced will find that Repairing is a recognizable rearrangement of moves they already know.

Think of it the way a basketball player learns the game. The behind-the-back pass is a beautiful, advanced move. But it's built from fundamentals: the chest pass, the bounce pass, ball-handling, court awareness. A coach who tries to teach the behind-the-back pass to someone who hasn't built the fundamentals will be working against the grain. A player who has the fundamentals can pick up the behind-the-back pass without strain.

Relational skill works the same way. The micro-practices are the fundamentals. The larger practices are how the fundamentals get used in specific relational situations.

There's an important caveat. You don't have to learn all the fundamentals before working on a larger practice. Life doesn't usually allow that. Grief arrives unscheduled. Conflict erupts before you've practiced Pause and Reflect. Someone you love is in crisis and you have to show up now. You can begin a larger practice without the full set of fundamentals in place. The larger work will be harder, and you may discover, as you do it, which fundamentals you most need to develop. That discovery is itself useful information.

The two layers live alongside each other. Begin where you are. The fundamentals will deepen what you're already practicing. The larger practices will reveal which fundamentals are most needed. Either way, you're moving.

## How to begin

Start with one practice that meets a real, recurring moment in your relationship. Not the most impressive one. Not the one you think you should need. The one that answers a place where you already get stuck.

The threshold move is the agreement. Sit down, share the practice, check whether you both want to try it. A practice you both choose can be held by the relationship. A practice one person imposes isn't a practice yet.

For more on how to integrate a practice once you've chosen it, see *Taking On Practices Together*.

---

A micro-practice on its own is small. A few micro-practices, integrated, are a habit. Many micro-practices, stacked, are a way of being together.

The largeness isn't in any one of them. It's in what they become when you tend them over time. Every standing agreement, every shape you both hold, every move you make explicit instead of letting it stay underground: it's all weaving toward a system that can carry what your relationship is asking it to carry.

May we trust the smallness of each move. May we trust that small things, repeated, become larger things. May we discover, slowly, that what we built one micro-practice at a time has become the way we are with each other.
