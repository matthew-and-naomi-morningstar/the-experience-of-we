# Taking On Practices Together

*A Shared Guide to How We Build Relational Skills*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Before you dive into the practice guides, we want to share something we learned the hard way: how change actually works in a relationship.

When we first started this work, we tried to change everything at once. We'd read about presence, co-regulation, deep listening, vulnerability, and we'd try to practice all of it simultaneously. It was overwhelming. We'd forget what we were working on. We'd get frustrated with each other and with ourselves. We'd fall back into old patterns and feel like we'd failed.

Then something shifted. We stopped trying to transform our entire relational life in one sweep, and we started doing something much simpler: we picked one small practice and we did it together until it became part of how we were with each other. Then we picked another. And another. Over time, our life became a tapestry — not a dramatic overhaul, but a steady weaving of small, deliberate practices into the fabric of our daily relationship. Each one was manageable. Each one was specific. And each one, once integrated, became the foundation for the next.

That's the method underneath everything in We Practices. This guide explains how it works.

A note: we describe this method through our experience as a couple, because that's where we learned it. But the method itself — one small practice at a time, integrated gradually, until it becomes part of how you are together — works in any relational configuration. Friends practicing presence during their weekly dinners. A small group building shared listening skills. A family learning to check in differently. Even someone practicing alone, building capacities one at a time. The shape adapts; the principle holds.

## Why small practices work

The nervous system changes gradually. This isn't a limitation; it's how lasting change actually happens.

When we try to become fundamentally different relational partners overnight, the nervous system resists. When this happens, it's not malfunctioning; it's protective. Wholesale change triggers overwhelm, and overwhelm triggers the very patterns we're trying to move beyond: shutdown, defensiveness, regression to what's familiar. The ambition to transform everything at once often produces the opposite of transformation.

But a single, specific practice is small enough to integrate without triggering that resistance. Pausing before you respond. Checking in with your body during a difficult conversation. Asking "what are you feeling right now?" instead of assuming. These are small acts. They don't feel dramatic. They don't require you to be a different person. They just ask you to do one thing, consistently, until it becomes natural.

The consistency matters more than the intensity. A practice done imperfectly every day rewires the nervous system more effectively than a practice done perfectly once. Repetition is how the body learns. And the body is where relational skill lives — not in what you know, but in what you do without having to think about it.

## How to choose a practice

Start where something calls to you.

Read through a guide together and notice what resonates. Not what you think you *should* work on, but what draws your attention. Sometimes the practice you need most is the one that produces a slight discomfort when you read it — a recognition that this is exactly the thing you've been avoiding. Sometimes it's the one that produces relief: *yes, this is what we need.*

The Relational Arc offers a developmental sequence. The practices build on each other, and the thresholds move from foundational skills to more complex ones. If you're unsure where to start, the Arc is a good guide. But the practices are also modular. If your relationship is struggling with repair, you don't need to complete every earlier threshold before working on repair. Start where the need is. The Arc describes a developmental ideal; your actual path will be shaped by what your relationship is asking for right now.

One principle we've found useful: choose practices that are slightly ahead of where you are, not dramatically ahead. A practice that stretches you gently will integrate. A practice that requires capacities you haven't built yet will frustrate. Trust your felt sense of readiness.

## How to take on a practice

**Read the guide together.** Aloud or taking turns. Pause where something resonates. Discuss what comes up. The reading itself is relational; it's not homework to get through, it's a shared encounter with an idea.

**Set a shared intention.** Talk about why this practice matters. What would it look like in your life together — whether that's a shared home, a weekly meeting, or a friendship that gathers around these practices? What are you hoping will shift? The intention doesn't need to be precise. It needs to be honest and shared.

**Start in easy moments.** Don't introduce a new practice during a fight or a crisis. Begin when things are calm, when you have space, when the stakes are low. A practice first learned under pressure will be associated with pressure. A practice first learned in safety becomes a resource you can bring to difficulty later.

**Be specific about when you'll practice.** "We'll practice deep listening" is an aspiration. "We'll practice deep listening during our evening check-in" — or "during our Sunday calls" or "when our group meets on Wednesdays" — is a commitment. Tying the practice to a specific moment in your shared rhythm makes it more likely to happen. The practice becomes part of how you are together rather than an additional demand on it.

**Expect it to feel awkward.** New practices feel mechanical at first. You'll feel like you're performing rather than being genuine. This is normal and it passes. Every skill feels artificial before it becomes natural. The awkwardness isn't a sign that you're doing it wrong; it's a sign that you're doing something new.

**Support each other.** One of you will remember the practice more often than the other. One of you will find it easier. These differences aren't problems; they're opportunities to practice the very relational skills the system is building. Gentle reminders, not criticism. Encouragement, not scorekeeping.

## How to know when a practice is integrated

A practice is integrated when you do it without having to remember to do it.

It's not that you do it perfectly. You won't; no one does. It's that the practice has become part of how you are together. You pause before responding not because you're following a rule, but because pausing has become your natural rhythm. You check in with each other not because a guide told you to, but because checking in is simply what you do.

You'll notice it in retrospect more than in the moment. Someone will say something, and you'll realize: *a year ago, I would have reacted completely differently.* The practice won't feel like a practice anymore. It will feel like you.

Integration isn't binary. A practice can be mostly integrated and still require attention in difficult moments. That's fine. What you're looking for is a baseline shift: the practice has become your default rather than your effort.

## How to rotate

When a practice feels embodied, choose the next one.

There's no fixed timeline. Some practices integrate in weeks. Some take months. Some you'll return to at deeper levels after working with other practices that illuminated what you'd missed the first time. The pace is yours. No one is grading you.

When you're ready for a new practice, return to the guide you've been working with and acknowledge what you've built. Then choose the next one — again, by resonance, by need, or by following the Arc's developmental sequence. Take on one or two new practices while continuing to live the ones you've already integrated. The old practices don't stop; they just stop requiring conscious effort.

Over time, the rotation itself becomes natural. You develop a shared sense of when something has landed and when it's time to grow into the next thing. That shared sense is itself a relational skill — one you'll have built through the process of practicing together.

## How the tapestry builds

Here's what surprised us most: the whole became more than the sum of its parts.

Each individual practice was small. Manageable. Unimpressive on its own. But as they accumulated — presence woven with deep listening woven with emotional honesty woven with repair — something emerged that wasn't any single practice. It was a way of being together. A relational capacity that held us through difficulty, deepened our joy, and made possible things we couldn't have imagined when we started.

This is what the framework calls emergence: the relational field developing capacities that don't belong to either person alone. You can't plan for it. You can't force it. But if you keep weaving — one small practice at a time, consistently, honestly, together — it comes.

The tapestry is never finished. There's always another practice to take on, another layer to deepen, another threshold to cross. That's not a burden. It's the nature of a living relationship: it keeps growing as long as you keep tending it.

## Common questions

**What if we pick the wrong practice?** You won't. Any practice engaged honestly will develop your relational capacity. If you choose something that doesn't resonate after trying it, set it down and choose another. Nothing is wasted. Even the attempt teaches you something about what you need.

**What if one of us integrates faster than the other?** This will happen. It's not a competition. The person who integrates faster can support the other with patience rather than impatience. And the person who takes longer often brings a depth of integration that the faster learner missed. Different paces aren't a problem to solve; they're a relational dynamic to navigate together — which is itself a practice.

**What if we forget?** You will forget. Repeatedly. This isn't failure; it's the normal rhythm of building any new habit. When you notice you've forgotten, simply return. No guilt, no recrimination, no starting over. Just return. The practice of returning is as valuable as the practice itself.

**What if it feels mechanical?** It will, at first. A practice that feels mechanical is a practice that hasn't yet become embodied. Keep going. The felt sense of authenticity will come as the practice moves from something you do to something you are.

**What if life gets in the way?** It will. Illness, stress, crisis, exhaustion — all of these will interrupt your practice. When they do, let them. The practice will be there when you come back. A living relational system is resilient enough to survive interruption. What matters isn't unbroken consistency; it's the willingness to return.

---

This method isn't complicated. It's the opposite of complicated. One practice at a time. Together. Until it becomes part of you. Then the next one.

The transformation isn't in any single practice. It's in the accumulation — the slow, steady weaving of small acts of relational intention into a life that holds more than you thought possible.

May we trust the smallness of each practice. May we trust the patience of the process. May we trust that what we weave together, one thread at a time, will hold.
