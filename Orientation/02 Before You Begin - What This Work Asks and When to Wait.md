# Before You Begin

*What This Work Asks and When to Wait*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Before you enter the Covenant, before you take on your first practice, we want to have an honest conversation with you. Not to discourage you. Not to gatekeep. But because this work opens real territory, and real territory has real risks.

The Experience of We invites you into vulnerability, honesty, emotional depth, and sustained presence with another person. These are powerful forces. In the right conditions, they catalyze healing, connection, and genuine transformation. In the wrong conditions, they can cause real harm.

We believe you deserve to know what the right conditions are before you step through the door.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## What this work asks of each person

The practices in The Experience of We don't require perfection. They don't require expertise, emotional mastery, or a history of healthy relationships. They require something simpler and harder: willingness combined with a basic set of capacities.

**The capacity to pause.** When you're activated, flooded, angry, or afraid, can you stop before acting? Not perfectly. Not every time. But enough that your reactions don't become the only thing in the room. The practices will ask you to sit with discomfort rather than discharge it. If pausing feels impossible right now, that's worth paying attention to.

**The capacity to feel without being consumed.** This work asks you to contact your emotions directly. Grief, anger, tenderness, fear. If emotional experience currently overwhelms you to the point where you can't function, or if you're in a period where feelings are entirely inaccessible, the timing may not be right. Both flooding and numbness can shift with support. Neither one is permanent.

**The capacity to hear difficult things.** Your practice partner will eventually say things that are hard to hear. Observations about your patterns. Experiences of your impact. Honest reflections that don't match your self-image. Can you receive that without retaliating, shutting down completely, or making it dangerous for them to be honest? This capacity grows with practice, but some baseline has to be present at the start.

**The capacity to take responsibility for your impact.** Not for everything. Not as a performance. But genuinely. When your words or actions land in a way that hurts, can you hold that without collapsing into shame or deflecting into blame? Accountability is one of the foundational practices for a reason. It makes everything else possible.

**The willingness to be changed.** This is perhaps the most fundamental. The Experience of We will not leave you as it found you. If you enter this work determined to remain exactly who you are, the practices will feel like threats rather than invitations. Willingness to be changed doesn't mean abandoning yourself. It means holding yourself loosely enough that growth can happen.

None of these capacities need to be fully developed. They need to be present. A seed is enough. The practices will grow them. But if the seed isn't there yet, trying to force the work can do more harm than waiting.

---

## What this work asks of the relationship

The practices are designed for two people. The quality of the space between you matters as much as the capacities each person brings.

**Mutual willingness.** Both people need to choose this freely. If one person is dragging the other in, or if participation is driven by guilt, obligation, or fear of losing the relationship, the foundation isn't there. Coerced vulnerability is not vulnerability. It's exposure.

**Basic safety.** Not perfect safety. Not the absence of all conflict or discomfort. But enough safety that each person can be honest without reasonable fear of punishment, retaliation, or abandonment. If telling the truth in your relationship currently carries a cost that feels dangerous, that's critical information.

**Freedom from active abuse.** We need to say this directly. If your relationship includes patterns of physical violence, coercion, control, intimidation, or emotional abuse, these practices are not the right intervention. Not because the relationship is beyond hope. But because practices that ask for vulnerability, honesty, and emotional openness become weapons in an abusive dynamic. Accountability becomes a tool for control. Boundaries become things to punish. Co-regulation becomes enmeshment. The same practices that heal in safe conditions can deepen harm in unsafe ones. If this describes your situation, please seek professional support first. We say this with care, not judgment.

**Roughly comparable commitment.** The two of you don't need to be in the same place emotionally or developmentally. The practice of Navigating Different Capacities exists for exactly this reason. But both people need to be genuinely committed to the process. If one person is doing the work while the other is going through the motions, the asymmetry will eventually become its own source of harm.

## Locating yourself

You've just read what this work asks. Now, before you continue reading, we want to invite you to do something different. Stop analyzing. Start sensing.

The capacities we described above can be assessed with the mind. But the question of whether you're ready to enter this work lives deeper than the mind. It lives in your body, in your nervous system, in the felt quality of your response to what you've read so far.

So pause here. Feel your body. Notice your breath. And sit with these questions, not to answer them correctly, but to feel what arises:

**When I imagine doing this work with another person, what do I notice?**
- Openness. Curiosity. A quiet yes.
- Contraction. Tightness. A pulling back.
- Urgency. A need for this to work. A feeling that I can't wait.
- Numbness. Nothing registering at all.

**What am I hoping this will do?**
- Deepen something that already has a foundation.
- Fix something that feels broken.
- Help me feel less alone.
- Give me something I've been missing.

**What is present in my life right now?**
- At least one relationship where I feel basically safe.
- Enough stability that I could absorb some disruption.
- Access to my own emotions, at least some of the time.
- Space. Not unlimited, but enough.

There are no right answers. But there are honest ones. And the quality of your honesty here is the same quality the practices will ask of you later.

### What your responses might be telling you

**If you feel openness and curiosity without urgency,** if you have some stability and a willing partner, if the idea of being changed feels like an invitation rather than a threat: the conditions are likely present. Enter gently. Go slowly. Trust the process.

**If you feel a mix of desire and fear,** if some capacities feel strong and others feel fragile, if your life is stressful but not in crisis, if you want this but you're not sure your partner does: proceed with care. Consider starting with the foundational concepts or the quick starts before entering the full arc. Talk honestly with your partner about what you've read here. Let the conversation itself be a test of readiness.

**If you feel urgency, crisis, or the sense that you need this to survive,** if basic safety isn't present in the relationship, if you're seeking this as a primary source of stability you don't currently have: wait. Not because you don't deserve this work. Because you deserve conditions where it can actually help. Seek individual support first. Build the ground. The practices will be here when the ground is steady enough to hold them.

These aren't rigid categories. They're orientations. Most people will find themselves somewhere between them, and that's fine. The point isn't to sort yourself into a box. The point is to listen to what your body already knows about your readiness, and to trust what you hear.

One more thing. Before you decide, take one breath. A real one. Feel it enter. Feel it leave. And ask yourself, honestly:

*Do I have enough ground beneath me to hold a process that will move the ground?*

Your need for connection is not the problem. It never was. That need is biological, ancient, and legitimate. Our nervous systems were built for co-regulation. Our wellness depends on safe relational fields. The ache you feel for deeper connection isn't a deficit to fix before you're "ready." It's the very signal that brought you here, and it's telling the truth.

The question isn't whether you need this. Most people do. The question is whether you have enough stability right now to hold a practice that will temporarily open and reorganize before it settles. The practices surface what's hidden. They activate attachment wounds. They ask you to stay present with discomfort. That opening requires a minimum of ground, not self-sufficiency, not the absence of need, just enough floor to stand on while the room rearranges itself.

If that ground is present, even if it's imperfect, even if your need is acute, you can begin. If that ground is not present, if this would be your only source of safety, if there is no floor beneath you right now, then the most loving thing is to build some ground first, so that when you enter, the work can actually hold what it opens.

## The risks when conditions aren't met

We name these not to frighten you but because we've seen what happens when people enter deep relational work before the conditions are ready. The risks are specific, and they're worth understanding.

**Vulnerability becomes exposure.** The practices invite you to share what's tender, unfinished, and unprotected. In a safe relational field, this sharing is met and held. When safety isn't present, vulnerability doesn't land in a field. It lands on concrete. What was offered in trust gets used in arguments, brought up as evidence, or met with contempt. The person who opened learns that opening is dangerous. This is harder to recover from than never having opened at all.

**Accountability becomes a weapon.** The language of accountability, impact, and responsibility is precise and powerful. In a healthy dynamic, it helps both people grow. In an unhealthy one, it becomes a sophisticated tool for blame. "I'm just holding you accountable" can become a way to control, shame, or dominate while sounding like the practice itself. If you find that accountability only flows in one direction, that's a signal worth taking seriously.

**Co-regulation becomes enmeshment.** Co-regulation is the practice of settling together, of letting your nervous systems find shared coherence. It requires distinction. Two separate people, choosing to attune. Without healthy boundaries, co-regulation collapses into enmeshment: the inability to feel your own experience apart from the other person's. The practices distinguish carefully between these two states, but the distinction requires that both people maintain their own ground.

**Shadow work becomes retraumatization.** The practices invite you to explore what's been hidden, denied, or pushed away. Shadow material carries charge. In a well-held container, that charge can be metabolized. In a poorly held one, it detonates. Exploring your shadows with someone who can't hold the space, or who uses what emerges against you, doesn't heal. It retraumatizes.

**The spiral becomes a trap.** The developmental arc is a spiral. You return to earlier practices at deeper levels. In a healthy dynamic, this deepening is natural and welcome. In an unhealthy one, the language of "spiraling deeper" can become a justification for staying in a relationship that is actually causing harm. "We're just in a hard threshold" can become a way of normalizing what shouldn't be normalized.

## If you're not sure you're ready

If you've read this far and you're uncertain, that uncertainty is not a failure. It may be wisdom.

Here are some honest options:

**Start with individual work.** Therapy, somatic practice, trauma processing, or any form of supported inner work that helps you build the capacities we've described. Many people come to relational practice after a period of individual preparation. This isn't a lesser path. It's a responsible one.

**Read "Beginning on Your Own."** We wrote an orientation guide for people who want to engage with the practices before they have a practice partner or before conditions are right for full shared practice. The practices can be read, reflected on, and partially explored as an individual. The relational dimension comes later, when the conditions are ready.

**Start with the foundational concepts.** Understanding Separation, Understanding Reunion, Understanding Shared Reality, Understanding the Relational Field. These documents don't ask you to practice with anyone. They offer a framework for understanding what you're experiencing. Sometimes understanding is the first form of healing.

**Talk to a professional.** If what we've described about unsafe dynamics resonates with your situation, a therapist or counselor who specializes in relational dynamics can help you assess what's happening and what you need. This is not a detour from the work. It may be the most direct path into it.

**Wait.** Sometimes the honest answer is: not yet. Not never. Not yet. Conditions change. Capacities grow. Relationships shift. What isn't possible now may become possible later. Waiting is not giving up. It's trusting the timing of your own life.

## If unsafe dynamics emerge during practice

Sometimes the conditions seem right at the beginning and change as the work deepens. The practices surface hidden patterns. Dynamics that weren't visible at the start become visible under the pressure of genuine intimacy. This is not uncommon.

If you recognize, at any point during practice, that the conditions we've described are not being met, here is what we want you to know:

**You have permission to pause.** You can stop practicing at any time. You don't need to finish a threshold, complete a cycle, or reach a resolution before stepping back. Pausing is not quitting. It's the practice of recognizing what's true right now.

**You have permission to name what you see.** If something feels wrong, it's worth saying so. The practice of honesty includes the honesty of saying "this doesn't feel safe" or "something has shifted and I need to understand it." You don't need to have it figured out before you speak.

**You have permission to seek outside support.** A therapist, a trusted friend, a counselor, a support line. The relational field between two people is not always sufficient to hold what emerges within it. Seeking help is not a betrayal of the practice. It's an expression of the wisdom the practice is trying to cultivate.

**You have permission to leave.** The Covenant of Entry includes Conscious Uncoupling for a reason. Endings are relational acts. But an ending is sometimes the most honest, most caring, most courageous thing a person can do. If the relationship has become a place where you are consistently unsafe, leaving is not a failure of practice. It is practice. The deepest form of it.

## The relationship between this document and the Covenant

This document and the Covenant of Entry are companions, not competitors.

This document asks: *Are the conditions present for this work to be safe and generative?*

The Covenant asks: *Given that the conditions are present, what do we commit to as we enter?*

The sequence matters. Honest assessment first. Then commitment. Not the other way around.

If you've read this document and you recognize that the conditions are present, even imperfectly, even with uncertainty, then the Covenant is your next step. It formalizes the shared agreements that make practice possible. It becomes the point of return when things get hard.

If you've read this document and you recognize that the conditions are not yet present, honor that recognition. It's the same honesty the practices will ask of you later. You're already practicing.

## A final word

We wrote this document because we believe the most caring thing we can do is tell the truth about what this work asks. The Experience of We is powerful precisely because it touches real things: the nervous system, the emotional body, the deep patterns of attachment and defense that shape how we move through the world. Anything powerful enough to heal is powerful enough to harm. The difference is the conditions.

We trust you to assess your own conditions honestly. We trust you to know, or to learn, what you need. And we trust that if the time is right, you'll know that too.

Whenever you're ready, we'll be here.
