# Co-Regulating Together: *A Shared Practice for Settling Our Nervous Systems*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Watch what happens when a distressed child climbs into a parent's lap. The child's breathing is fast, their body tight, their face contorted. The parent holds them. Speaks softly. Breathes slowly. And gradually — not through explanation or instruction but through contact — the child's body settles. Their breathing slows. Their muscles release. They regulate, not alone, but *through* the other person.

This isn't something children outgrow. This is how human nervous systems work.

We were built to settle together. Our biology expects it. When we're stressed, overwhelmed, or activated, our nervous systems reach for other nervous systems — looking for the steady presence that signals safety, looking for the calm that helps us find our own.

Somewhere along the way, many of us learned that regulation is something we're supposed to do alone. Manage your emotions. Self-soothe. Keep it together. But the truth is, our nervous systems were never designed for solo operation. They are built to be in contact with each other, calming each other, sharing the load.

This guide is about reclaiming that capacity. Not as a technique we apply to each other, but as something that happens between us when we create the right conditions.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to settle their nervous systems together.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go**, especially the somatic practices — they work immediately
- **Returning regularly**, because co-regulation is a capacity that deepens the more we use it

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what co-regulation actually feels like in our bodies when our nervous systems settle together.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: the channels through which our nervous systems communicate and the conditions that allow settling to happen.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate co-regulation: the specific ways we learn to settle with each other.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Co-regulation is an experience

*Co-regulation is what it feels like when our nervous systems settle together.*

We experience co-regulation as a physical shift. Something in our body eases in the presence of another person. It's not a thought or a decision; it's something the body does.

When co-regulation is happening:

- Our breathing slows and deepens without us trying
- Our muscles release tension we didn't know we were holding
- Our heart rate settles
- A sense of ease or safety arrives in the body
- We feel held — not physically, necessarily, but in the sense that we're not carrying our state alone
- The other person's steadiness becomes available to us, and ours to them

When co-regulation is absent:

- We try to manage our states alone, and it's harder than it should be
- We feel isolated in our activation — stressed, anxious, or overwhelmed with no relief from connection
- Being with the other person doesn't help us settle, or actually makes us more activated
- We armor up rather than softening
- Regulation becomes effortful rather than something that flows between us

Most of us know both of these experiences. We know what it feels like to settle in someone's presence — the relief of a hug that actually lands, a voice that brings us down from activation, a hand on our back that says *I'm here*. And we know what it feels like when connection doesn't settle us — when we're together but still alone in our stress.

The difference is co-regulation. When it's present, settling is shared. When it's absent, we're each on our own.

**A reflection for us:** When have we felt our nervous systems settling together — one of us calming and the other following? What was that experience like?

## Co-regulation is a process

*Our nervous systems settle together through specific channels, and understanding these channels helps us use them consciously.*

### How our nervous systems communicate

Co-regulation isn't abstract. It happens through real, physical channels between our bodies:

- **Breath.** When we're near each other, our breathing patterns tend to synchronize. If one of us breathes slowly and deeply, the other's system picks up on it and begins to slow as well. This is one of the most direct pathways to shared settling.

- **Voice.** Our voices communicate directly to each other's nervous systems. A warm, slow, low-pitched voice signals safety. A tight, fast, high-pitched voice signals activation. The content of what we say matters less than how our voice sounds when we say it.

- **Face.** Our facial expressions are read instantly by each other's nervous systems. Soft eyes, a relaxed brow, a gentle expression — these signal safety. Tension in the jaw, hardness in the eyes, a flat expression — these signal caution or threat.

- **Touch.** Warm, steady physical contact directly calms the nervous system. Skin-to-skin contact releases oxytocin and reduces cortisol. This is one of the most powerful regulatory channels available to us.

- **Presence.** Even without words or touch, the quality of our presence communicates. A body that is settled and grounded sends different signals than a body that is tense and distracted. We sense this in each other, often without being able to name how.

### What makes co-regulation possible

Co-regulation doesn't happen automatically. It requires certain conditions:

- **Safety.** Our nervous systems won't open to each other if we feel threatened. Trust and safety are prerequisites; our bodies need to have learned that this person is safe before they'll accept regulation from them.

- **Willingness to be affected.** We have to let each other in. If we're defended or walled off, the other person's regulation can't reach us. Co-regulation requires a kind of permeability — letting the other person's state touch ours.

- **Mutuality.** Co-regulation isn't one person fixing the other. It's mutual influence. Both people participate — offering and receiving, giving and taking. When it becomes consistently one-directional, it stops being co-regulation and becomes emotional labor.

- **Some regulation to share.** If both of us are deeply activated, co-regulation is harder (though not impossible). It helps when at least one of us has some steadiness to offer. This is why individual regulation capacity matters — not as a replacement for co-regulation, but as something we bring to it.

### What co-regulation is not

It helps to be clear about what we're not talking about:

- **It's not one person calming the other down.** It's mutual. Both nervous systems are involved.
- **It's not emotional labor.** If one person is always regulating and the other is always receiving, that's an imbalance, not co-regulation.
- **It's not codependence.** We remain distinct. We can settle together while still being separate people with our own inner lives.
- **It's not a substitute for individual capacity.** We each need our own ability to regulate. Co-regulation supplements this; it doesn't replace it.

### The foundations of co-regulating together

Before we move into practices, here are the qualities that make co-regulation possible between us.

**Safety between us.** Our nervous systems won't open to someone they don't trust. Co-regulation requires that we've built enough safety that our bodies are willing to be vulnerable with each other — willing to be dysregulated in front of each other, willing to receive help settling. We practice building trust through consistent, reliable connection; not using each other's vulnerabilities against each other; creating conditions where it's safe to be stressed, upset, or activated together; and signaling safety through our tone, face, and presence.

**Willingness to receive.** Many of us are better at offering regulation than receiving it. We can be steady for the other person, but when it's our turn to be held, we stiffen. We deflect. We insist we're fine. We practice letting each other's calm actually reach us, not armoring up when the other person offers steadiness, being willing to be affected — to let someone else's presence change our state — and receiving without shame.

**Mutuality.** Co-regulation is a shared practice. Neither of us should always be the regulated one or always be the regulator. We practice taking turns — sometimes I offer, sometimes you offer; noticing if we've fallen into a fixed pattern where one of us always carries the load; both developing the capacity to give and to receive; and naming the pattern honestly if it becomes lopsided.

**A reflection for us:** How do we typically affect each other's emotional states? Do we tend toward mutual calming, mutual activation, or something else?

## Co-regulation is a practice

These are our best practices for developing co-regulatory capacity. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice settling into presence

The simplest co-regulation practice. No technique — just being together with awareness and letting our systems do what they naturally do.

- **We sit or stand comfortably together,** close enough to feel each other's presence.

- **We let ourselves arrive.** A few breaths. Letting go of what we were doing before. Landing here.

- **We become aware of each other.** Not just knowing the other is there, but feeling their presence — their body, their breath, their energy.

- **We let our systems settle.** We don't try to do anything. We just stay present together and notice what happens.

- **We give it time.** We stay for a few minutes. Settling isn't instant. The nervous system needs time to register safety and begin to ease.

Often, without doing anything at all, our breathing slows. Our shoulders drop. Something softens. This is our biology working as designed — settling in the presence of a safe other.

*When to use: As a daily practice. Before important conversations. When either of us is stressed. Anytime we want to settle together.*

---

### We practice breathing together

Breath is one of the most direct pathways to regulation. When we breathe together, our systems synchronize and settling deepens.

- **We get comfortable together.** Sitting facing each other, or side by side, or back to back — whatever feels natural.

- **We begin to breathe consciously.** We let our breath deepen and slow. We don't force it; we just invite the breath to become more deliberate.

- **We let our breaths synchronize.** Without forcing, we let our breathing begin to align. Inhaling together, exhaling together. This often happens naturally when we bring attention to it.

- **We extend the exhale.** Long exhales activate the calming branch of the nervous system. We try breathing in for a count of four, out for a count of six or eight.

- **We continue for several minutes.** We let the synchronized breathing work on our systems. There's no rush.

- **We notice the effect.** How do we feel now compared to when we started? What shifted?

If touch is welcome, we might place a hand on each other's chest or back to feel each other's breath. This adds a tactile channel to the respiratory one — two regulatory pathways at once.

*When to use: When either of us is activated. As a regular practice for building co-regulatory capacity. Before sleep. During moments of transition.*

---

### We practice voice as settling

Our voices speak directly to each other's nervous systems. A warm voice settles. A tense voice activates. This practice uses voice intentionally as a regulatory channel.

- **We notice our vocal tone.** What is our voice carrying right now? Speed, pitch, tension? Or warmth, slowness, ease?

- **We consciously warm our voices.** We let our voices become softer, slower, lower in pitch. We let warmth come through — not performed warmth, but the genuine warmth that arises from caring about the other person's state.

- **We speak simple things.** "I'm here with you." "We can settle together." "Take your time." "I'm not going anywhere." The content matters far less than the tone. The nervous system is listening to how we sound, not what we say.

- **We let the voice land.** The person receiving listens with their body, not just their ears. We let the sound enter and do its work.

This practice is especially powerful when one of us is activated and the other is relatively settled. The settled person's voice becomes a bridge — carrying regulation from one nervous system to the other.

*When to use: When one of us is stressed or activated. During co-regulation in difficult moments. As part of bedtime settling. Anytime voice can carry what words cannot.*

---

### We practice regulating touch

When touch is welcome, it's one of the most powerful co-regulatory channels available. Warm, steady physical contact directly calms the nervous system.

- **We check first.** Touch must be welcome. We ask or offer: "Would it help to hold hands?" "Can I put my arm around you?" "Would a hug help right now?"

- **We touch from a place of relative calm.** Anxious touch transmits anxiety. We try to let our touch carry steadiness — warm, firm, unhurried.

- **We use steady pressure.** Firm, steady contact tends to be more regulating than light or tentative touch. A solid hand on the back. A real hug, not a quick squeeze.

- **We let the contact do its work.** We don't rush. We stay in the contact and let both nervous systems respond.

- **We adjust based on what helps.** "Is this good?" "Do you want more or less?" We learn each other's preferences over time.

Forms of regulating touch we might explore: holding hands, sitting close with shoulders or legs touching, a hand resting on the back or chest, sitting back to back (feeling each other's breath and presence), a real sustained hug, gentle stroking of the arm or hair.

We each have different touch needs and preferences. Part of this practice is learning what actually settles each of us — and being willing to ask for it.

*When to use: When either of us needs settling and touch is welcome. As a regular part of daily contact. During and after difficult conversations.*

---

### We practice co-regulating through movement

Stillness isn't the only path to settling. Sometimes our bodies need to move — and moving together can be profoundly regulating.

- **We walk together.** Not fast, not with a destination in mind. A slow, shared walk where we fall into step with each other. The rhythm of walking together naturally synchronizes our systems.

- **We try gentle rocking or swaying.** Standing together, holding each other, letting our bodies sway gently. This is one of the oldest regulatory movements — it's why we rock babies, and why we sway when we're distressed. Doing it together adds the co-regulatory channel.

- **We try stretching or moving together.** Simple stretches, done side by side with awareness of each other. The combination of physical release and shared presence can be deeply settling.

- **We let the movement be slow and rhythmic.** Regulation lives in rhythm. Fast, erratic movement activates. Slow, rhythmic movement settles. We find a pace that feels calming to both of us.

- **We notice what happens afterward.** After moving together, we pause. We check in with our bodies. Often there's a noticeable drop in activation — a settling that arrived through the body rather than through stillness.

*When to use: When sitting still feels impossible. When one or both of us is restless or agitated. As a daily practice — a walk together is one of the simplest and most effective co-regulatory activities available.*

---

### We practice co-regulating through gaze

Eye contact is one of the most direct channels between nervous systems. When we look into each other's eyes with softness and warmth, something specific happens: our social engagement systems activate and our bodies begin to settle.

- **We face each other.** Sitting comfortably, close enough to see each other clearly.

- **We soften our gaze.** Not staring, not intense — soft. We let our eyes be warm and open.

- **We look at each other.** We hold gentle eye contact. Not forcing it — blinking naturally, looking away briefly if we need to — but returning to each other's eyes.

- **We notice what happens.** This can feel vulnerable, especially at first. We might notice our hearts racing, then settling. We might feel emotion arise. We might feel an unexpected intimacy. We stay with whatever comes.

- **We breathe while we look.** Combining breath with gaze deepens the effect. Slow breath, soft eyes — two regulatory channels working together.

This practice can feel intense or awkward at first. That's normal. The awkwardness usually fades within a minute or two, and what replaces it is often a surprising depth of connection and settling. Start with one or two minutes and build from there.

*When to use: As a regular deepening practice. When we want to strengthen our co-regulatory bond. When words feel insufficient and we want direct nervous system contact.*

---

### We practice co-regulating through difficulty

This practice is for the real moments — when one or both of us is activated, stressed, upset, or dysregulated and we need to settle together.

- **We recognize that regulation is needed.** One or both of us is activated. We name it: "I'm really stressed right now." "You seem wound up — want to settle together?"

- **We come together.** We move closer. We orient toward each other. This is the body's way of saying: I'm choosing connection over isolation.

- **Whoever is more settled offers their presence.** Through steady breath, warm voice, calm face, grounded body. Not by performing calm — by actually being as settled as we can, and letting that reach the other person.

- **The activated person lets the regulation land.** This is the harder part. Rather than staying caught in the activation spiral, we try to open. We let the other person's steadiness touch us. We receive.

- **We stay with it.** Settling takes time. We don't rush. We stay together until the activation subsides or eases to something manageable.

When both of us are activated: we acknowledge it honestly ("We're both activated right now"); we do what we can, because even slightly regulated presence helps; we use breath, touch, and movement together — the body-based channels work even when both minds are spinning; and we're patient, because it takes longer when both systems are struggling, and that's okay.

*When to use: When one or both of us is stressed, anxious, overwhelmed, or dysregulated. In the aftermath of conflict. During hard moments in life.*

---

### We practice building co-regulatory patterns

Individual moments of co-regulation are valuable. But the real power comes from building co-regulation into our daily rhythm — making it a regular part of how we are together, not something we only reach for in crisis.

- **We identify natural transition moments.** Waking up, coming home, before bed, after a stressful event — these are the moments when co-regulatory contact makes the biggest difference.

- **We build rituals.** A few minutes of breathing together each morning. A real hug when we reunite after time apart. Sitting together in the evening before bed, just being present. These become our co-regulatory touchpoints.

- **We make it normal.** Co-regulation isn't crisis intervention. It's daily maintenance. We normalize it the way we normalize eating together or saying goodnight.

- **We protect these moments.** We prioritize them. We don't let them get crowded out by tasks, screens, or busyness. They matter.

Over time, these patterns build a co-regulatory baseline in the relationship. Our nervous systems learn to expect settling together, and they begin to anticipate it — which means we start regulating faster and more easily, because the body already knows what's coming.

*When to use: As an ongoing orientation. Start by choosing one transition moment and building a co-regulatory ritual around it.*

---

### We practice repairing co-regulatory ruptures

Sometimes our attempts to co-regulate go wrong. One of us offers touch that doesn't land. A voice meant to soothe comes across as condescending. Our states clash instead of settling. This is normal. What matters is how we repair.

- **We recognize the rupture.** "That didn't help." "Something went wrong." We name it without blame.

- **We step back if needed.** If we're making things worse, a brief pause helps. Not withdrawal — just a moment to breathe.

- **We get curious.** What happened? Was touch unwelcome? Was voice tone off? Was one of us too activated to receive? We explore without accusing.

- **We try again with adjustments.** After understanding what went wrong, we make a different offer. "Let me try differently. Would it help if I just sat with you quietly?"

- **We stay connected through the learning.** Rupture isn't failure. It's information. We're learning each other's regulatory needs, and that learning sometimes involves getting it wrong first.

*When to use: Whenever a co-regulation attempt doesn't land. When we notice we're making each other more activated instead of less.*

## Working with challenges

**When one of us is significantly more activated.** Sometimes the gap between our states is large — one of us is highly activated while the other is relatively calm.

**What we've found helps:**

- **The more settled person offers what they can** without taking on the full activation. We can be steady without absorbing everything.
- **The activated person works to receive.** Even a small opening — a willingness to let the other's calm reach us — makes a difference.
- **We recognize limits.** The calm person can't infinitely absorb dysregulation. If it's too much, we name it honestly.
- **We use other resources too.** Individual regulation practices, movement, breath — these supplement what we offer each other.
- **We are patient.** High activation takes time to settle. We don't rush.

---

**When we dysregulate each other.** Sometimes we make each other worse. Our states clash, feed back on each other, and spiral upward rather than settling.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We recognize it early.** "We're making this worse right now." The sooner we catch the escalation, the easier it is to interrupt.
- **We take space if needed.** Brief separation isn't abandonment. Sometimes each of us needs to settle individually before we can settle together.
- **We come back.** After individual settling, we return to each other. The separation is temporary — a reset, not a retreat.
- **We get curious later.** What is it about this particular dynamic that escalates? Is there unresolved conflict? Old patterns? Understanding the pattern helps us interrupt it earlier next time.

---

**When one of us consistently gives more.** If one of us is always the regulator and the other is always the receiver, something is off. True co-regulation is mutual.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We name the pattern.** "I notice I'm usually the one offering regulation. I need to receive sometimes too."
- **We explore why.** Is one of us more comfortable giving than receiving? Is one of us not developing individual capacity? Is there a gender or role expectation at play?
- **We work to rebalance.** The over-giver practices receiving. The over-receiver practices offering. This may feel uncomfortable at first; that's the growth edge.
- **We seek support if needed.** If the pattern is deeply entrenched, professional help may be valuable.

---

**When co-regulation isn't enough.** Sometimes what we're facing is too big for co-regulation alone to handle. Severe trauma, acute mental health crises, overwhelming life circumstances.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We acknowledge the limits honestly.** "This is more than we can settle together." That's not failure; that's realism.
- **We seek additional support.** Therapy, community, crisis resources. Co-regulation is part of a larger system of care, not the whole system.
- **We stay connected.** Even when we need outside help, we continue co-regulating as part of a larger support network. The two are not mutually exclusive.

## The larger view

Co-regulation isn't just one good thing among many. It's the physiological foundation that makes deeper connection possible. Without regulation, we can't be truly present — dysregulated nervous systems narrow attention and pull us into survival mode. Without presence, we can't attune. Without attunement, we can't build trust. Without trust, we can't be vulnerable. Without vulnerability, we can't reach each other at depth.

When we co-regulate well, we create the biological conditions for everything else in this series to work. Settling together is the ground that all other relational capacities grow from.

Co-regulation isn't something we master and then have forever. It's a living capacity that responds to how we treat it. When we practice regularly, our capacity grows. Our systems learn each other. Settling becomes faster, easier, more natural. When we neglect it, the capacity dims. We become more isolated in our activation, more alone in our stress. This isn't discouraging; it means we're always choosing. Every day, we can choose to settle together. Every moment of activation is an opportunity to reach for each other rather than retreating into isolation.

When we co-regulate well, the effects extend beyond us. Settled people are more patient, more generous, more capable of care in every context. Our children, friends, and communities benefit from our regulated presence. We model what nervous system partnership looks like — and others recognize it, even if they can't name it. In a world of epidemic dysregulation, every pair of people who learn to settle together contributes to collective steadiness.

## Our commitment to co-regulation

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to learn to settle with each other. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We won't always get this right. We'll have days when we dysregulate each other. We'll sometimes forget to offer, or be unable to receive. We'll sometimes need more than we can give each other.

But we now have a shared practice. We know how co-regulation works — through breath, voice, face, touch, movement, and presence. We know what it needs — safety, willingness, mutuality. And we know that it's not optional; it's the biological foundation for the relationship we want to build.

May we settle together with growing ease. May we offer our steadiness generously. May we receive what the other offers without shame. May we become a place of calm for each other in a world that offers too little.

We were never meant to regulate alone. Now we don't have to. Let us settle together.
