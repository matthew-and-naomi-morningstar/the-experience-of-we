# Developing Awareness Together: *A Shared Practice for Sensing Our Relational Field*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Between us, there is always something happening. A quality. A temperature. A texture to how we are with each other right now — distinct from how we were with each other yesterday or how we'll be tomorrow.

Most of the time, we don't notice it. We notice the obvious things — a fight, a cold shoulder, a moment of deep warmth. But the subtler shifts pass by unregistered. The slight coolness that appeared when we changed topics. The openness that showed up when we sat down together after a long day. The way something tightened between us when we talked about money.

This guide is about learning to notice. Not to analyze, not to fix — just to sense what is actually happening in the space between us, so we can participate in it more consciously.

Awareness of the relational field is where the deeper work begins. Before we can work with anything between us, we have to be able to feel it.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or any people who want to develop shared sensitivity to the quality of their connection.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go** — many of these practices can begin the moment you read them
- **Returning regularly**, because awareness is a capacity that deepens with practice and can dull with neglect

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what relational awareness actually feels like when we are sensing the field between us.
- **Process** is how that awareness develops and unfolds: the stages of growing sensitivity that we move through over time.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate awareness: the specific exercises that sharpen our capacity to sense what is happening between us.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Awareness is an experience

*Awareness is what it feels like to actually sense what is happening between us.*

Relational awareness is a different kind of perception — not of the other person specifically, but of the quality of what exists in the space between us. It is the felt sense of our connection right now.

When awareness is present:

- We sense the temperature of our connection — warm, cool, neutral
- We notice shifts as they happen, not hours or days later
- We pick up on things that haven't been said but are present
- We feel the rhythm of our interaction — whether it's flowing or stuck
- We sense when the other person is really here and when they've drifted
- We notice our own state and how it's affecting the space between us

When awareness is absent:

- We move through interactions on autopilot
- We miss early signals — the coolness that precedes a fight, the openness that could have been met
- We misread each other — interpreting mood, tone, or behavior without actually sensing what's underneath
- We react to surface content while missing the relational undercurrent
- Problems accumulate because we didn't notice them when they were small

Awareness is not analysis. It's not trying to figure out what's going on. It's the simpler, more direct act of *sensing* what is present — the way you sense the temperature of a room when you walk in, before you think about it.

And awareness is receptive, not effortful. We're not scanning for problems or monitoring for threats. We're simply opening our perception to include what is happening between us, alongside everything else.

**A reflection for us:** When do we feel most aware of each other and the space between us? What does that shared awareness feel like in our bodies?

## Awareness is a process

*Developing relational awareness isn't a switch we flip. It unfolds through stages of growing sensitivity.*

### We learn what there is to sense

The first step is simply understanding that there is something to sense. That the space between us has qualities — and that these qualities carry real information about how our relationship is doing.

What we learn to sense:

- **Connection quality.** How close or distant do we feel? Is the connection strong, thin, warm, strained? This fluctuates constantly, and learning to track these fluctuations is foundational.

- **Energy.** What is the energy between us? Lively or flat? Tense or relaxed? Moving or stuck? Energy isn't metaphorical — it's the felt sense of vitality or depletion in our interaction.

- **Interaction quality.** Beyond what we're saying, how are we being together? Is our communication flowing or effortful? Is there ease or strain? Are we in rhythm or out of sync?

- **Individual states.** What is each of us bringing into the space? Stress, openness, fatigue, excitement, guardedness? Our individual states combine to create the shared quality.

- **History in the present.** Past interactions live in the current moment. Accumulated trust shows up as warmth. Unresolved hurt shows up as tension. Patterns repeat. Awareness includes sensing what from our history is active right now.

### We learn to sense without naming

Early on, we tend to rush from sensation to interpretation. We feel something in the field and immediately label it: "You're upset" or "Something's wrong." This collapses the sensing into a story.

The deeper practice is to stay with the raw sensation before we name it. To feel the quality of what's between us without immediately deciding what it means. The naming can come later. The sensing comes first.

### We develop continuous background awareness

At first, awareness is something we do intentionally — we pause, we check in, we sense the field. Over time, it becomes more like peripheral vision: an ongoing background sensitivity to relational quality that runs alongside everything else we're doing.

This background awareness is where the real value lives. It means we catch shifts early. We notice when something opens. We feel when something tightens. Not because we're monitoring, but because we've developed a sensitivity that registers this information naturally.

### We learn to share what we sense

Awareness that stays private is limited. When we learn to share our sensing with each other — gently, without judgment — we build a shared awareness that is richer and more accurate than either of us could develop alone.

### The foundations of awareness between us

Before we move into practices, here are the qualities that make awareness possible.

**Receptivity.** Awareness requires that we receive rather than reach. We don't grab at information about the field — we let it come to us. This means settling, softening, and opening our perception rather than sharpening it.

**Non-judgment.** What we sense is information, not verdict. If we sense distance between us, that's data — not proof that something is wrong with us or our relationship. Judgment shuts down sensing. Curiosity keeps it open.

**Honesty.** Awareness only works if we're willing to sense what's actually here — not what we wish were here or what we think should be here. Sometimes what's between us is uncomfortable. Honest awareness includes that.

**Patience.** Sensitivity develops over time. In the beginning, we may not sense much. That's normal. The capacity grows with practice, like any perceptual skill. We don't rush it.

**A reflection for us:** What tends to narrow our shared awareness? Are there patterns or situations where we consistently lose sight of each other?

## Awareness is a practice

These are our best practices for building shared sensitivity to the relational field. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice field sensing check-ins

A simple, structured practice for building the habit of sensing what's happening between us.

- **We settle together.** We take a moment to arrive — a few breaths, a shared pause, whatever helps us land in the present.

- **We each turn attention to the space between us.** Not to ourselves individually, not to the other person specifically, but to the quality of what exists between us right now. This is a subtle shift in attention — like looking at the air between two objects rather than at the objects themselves.

- **We sense without analyzing.** We just notice. Is there warmth? Coolness? Openness? Guardedness? Flow? Stuckness? Ease? Strain? We stay with the sensation rather than moving to interpretation.

- **We share what we notice.** Using simple language: "I notice some warmth between us." "There's a tightness I'm sensing." "The field feels open." We share as perception, not as fact.

- **We receive each other's sensing.** We listen without correcting or debating. We're not trying to reach consensus — we're building a shared picture from two perspectives.

- **We notice if anything shifted.** Sometimes the act of bringing awareness to the field changes the field. We notice that too.

*When to use: As a regular practice — daily or weekly. Also at the start of important conversations, or whenever we want to check the quality of our connection.*

---

### We practice reading the field through our bodies

The body registers relational information before the mind does. This practice uses physical sensation as the primary channel for sensing the field.

- **We bring attention to our bodies.** Not to thoughts about the relationship, but to actual physical sensation. What do we notice in our chest, our belly, our shoulders, our jaw, our hands?

- **We sense how our bodies respond to each other.** Do we relax in each other's presence right now, or tighten? Is there ease in our torso or bracing? Do our shoulders drop or rise?

- **We notice body responses during interaction.** As we talk, as topics change, as emotions move — what does the body do? A stomach clench when a certain subject comes up. A chest-opening when we feel received. A jaw tightening when we feel misunderstood.

- **We share what our bodies are telling us.** "My shoulders just dropped — something about what you said landed." "I notice my stomach tightening." We're not interpreting — we're reporting.

- **We trust the body's information.** The body often knows what's happening in the field before we have words for it. When the body says something is off, it's worth listening — even if we can't explain why.

This practice is especially valuable for people who find the concept of "sensing the field" too abstract. The body makes it concrete. The body always has an answer when the mind doesn't know what to say.

*When to use: During any interaction. Especially helpful when something feels off but we can't articulate why.*

---

### We practice continuous background awareness

This practice develops ongoing field sensitivity as a background capacity — not something we do in a check-in, but something that runs alongside our normal interaction.

- **We set an intention together.** We agree to maintain gentle background awareness of our relational quality throughout our time together.

- **We keep it light.** This isn't effortful monitoring. It's more like keeping the field in peripheral vision while we engage with whatever we're doing together.

- **We notice shifts.** When something changes — a subtle cooling, a sudden warmth, a tightening, an opening — we notice it. We don't have to act on it. We just register it.

- **We may or may not share.** Depending on context, we might mention what we notice ("something just shifted") or simply hold the awareness.

- **We check in periodically.** At natural pauses, we might briefly share: "How has the field been feeling to you this evening?"

Over time, this background awareness becomes more natural — less like a practice and more like a way of being. We simply become people who notice the quality of what's between us as we move through our day together.

*When to use: During extended time together. As an ongoing orientation in daily life.*

---

### We practice tracking transitions

The quality of connection between us changes most noticeably at transition points. This practice develops awareness of how the field shifts during transitions.

- **We identify transition moments.** Coming together after being apart. Shifting from one activity to another. Changing topics. One of us sharing something vulnerable. Moving from a screen to a conversation.

- **We pay special attention at transitions.** We notice: What happens in the field as we move through this transition? Does it open or contract? Warm or cool? Energize or deplete?

- **We sense before and after.** What was the quality of the field before the transition? What is it after? The difference tells us something.

- **We get curious about the shift.** Not to judge it, but to learn from it. "I noticed something warmed when we sat down together." "Something tightened when we switched from talking to checking our phones."

- **We share our observations.** We build a shared understanding of which transitions serve our connection and which tend to disrupt it.

This practice often reveals patterns we wouldn't have noticed otherwise. We discover that certain transitions predictably open the field — and others predictably close it. That knowledge becomes useful.

*When to use: Throughout daily life, especially during the transitions we tend to move through unconsciously — coming home, shifting between activities, beginning and ending conversations.*

---

### We practice noticing without naming

Sometimes we name what we sense too quickly, and the label replaces the experience. This practice develops the capacity to sense more than we can say.

- **We sit together in silence.** We let words drop away.

- **We sense the field without trying to name it.** We resist the urge to label what we're perceiving. We just sense.

- **We stay with direct experience.** What does the space between us actually feel like, before we categorize it? Can we sense qualities we don't have words for?

- **We hold the sensing for several minutes.** We stay present with the field, noticing but not naming. This is harder than it sounds — the mind wants to label.

- **Then we might share.** After the silent sensing, we can try to articulate what we noticed — knowing that words will only approximate the direct experience. We might find ourselves using unusual language: "It felt like something was settling." "There was a thickness that gradually thinned." This is good. It means we're reaching toward the actual experience rather than grabbing a convenient label.

*When to use: As a regular practice for deepening sensitivity. Especially helpful when our sensing has become routine or we want to access subtler layers.*

---

### We practice sharing what we're bringing

Our individual states contribute to the shared field. This practice develops awareness of what each of us adds to the space between us.

- **We check in with ourselves first.** Before we focus on the field, we notice: What state am I in? What am I bringing into our space right now?

- **We share honestly.** "I'm bringing some stress from work." "I'm feeling open and curious today." "I'm a bit depleted." We name what's actually true, not what we wish were true.

- **We receive without trying to fix.** We hear what the other is bringing without immediately trying to change it. We take in the information.

- **We sense how our states meet.** With awareness of what each of us is bringing, we notice how these combine in the field. Two depleted people create a different field than one depleted and one resourced. Two open people create a different field than one open and one guarded.

- **We adjust expectations if needed.** If one of us is exhausted, maybe this isn't the moment for a heavy conversation. If both of us are open, maybe it's a good time to explore something new. Our awareness informs our choices.

This practice is especially valuable because it distributes responsibility. Neither of us is solely responsible for the quality of the field. We both contribute. Knowing what we're each bringing helps us be realistic about what the field can hold right now.

*When to use: When coming together after time apart. At the start of intentional time together. Whenever the field feels off and we're not sure why.*

---

### We practice sensing the field in difficulty

It's easy to sense the field when things are going well. It's much harder — and much more valuable — to maintain awareness when we're activated, upset, or in conflict.

- **We name the commitment in advance.** In a calm moment, we agree: when things get hard between us, we will try to maintain some awareness of the field rather than losing ourselves entirely in our position.

- **When difficulty arises, we try to keep one thread of awareness on the field.** Even while we're feeling hurt, frustrated, or defensive, we try to notice: What is happening between us right now? What is the quality of this space?

- **We notice what happens to the field during conflict.** Does it contract? Harden? Heat up? Go cold? Does one of us withdraw while the other pursues? Can we feel the field distorting under the pressure of our activation?

- **We use field awareness to inform our choices.** If we sense the field contracting dangerously, we might pause. If we sense it hardening, we might soften our tone. The field gives us information we wouldn't have if we were only tracking our own position.

- **We don't expect perfection.** In the heat of conflict, awareness will narrow — this is what activation does. Even partial awareness is better than none. Even remembering to check the field once during a disagreement is practice.

This is an advanced practice. It asks us to hold awareness while we're emotionally activated, which goes against the nervous system's instinct to narrow focus during threat. Start with mild difficulty and build capacity gradually.

*When to use: During disagreements and conflicts. Start with lower-stakes tensions and build toward harder moments.*

---

### We practice tracking patterns over time

Single moments of awareness are valuable. But patterns are where the deeper intelligence lives. This practice develops our capacity to notice relational patterns across days, weeks, and months.

- **We pay attention to recurring qualities in the field.** Does tension tend to show up at the same times? Does openness appear in predictable contexts? Are there patterns to when we feel close and when we feel distant?

- **We notice seasonal and cyclical patterns.** Some relationships have weekly rhythms — easy on weekends, strained on weekday evenings. Some have longer cycles tied to work stress, family demands, or personal energy. We get curious about our rhythms.

- **We track what reliably warms and what reliably cools.** Over time, we build a map of what tends to bring us closer and what tends to create distance. This is different from guessing — it's built from repeated observation.

- **We share our pattern observations.** "I've noticed we tend to feel closest on Sunday mornings." "I think the field tightens when we haven't had time alone together in a while." We compare notes.

- **We use pattern awareness to make choices.** If we know that a certain dynamic predictably creates distance, we can address it. If we know that a certain activity predictably brings warmth, we can prioritize it. Pattern awareness turns observation into agency.

*When to use: As an ongoing practice over weeks and months. Periodic pattern-review conversations — perhaps monthly — can be especially revealing.*

## Working with challenges

**When we sense different things.** We won't always sense the same qualities in the field. This is normal and valuable — not a problem to solve.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We hold our sensing as perception, not fact.** "I'm sensing some distance" rather than "There's distance between us."
- **We get curious about the difference.** "Interesting — you're sensing warmth and I'm sensing something cooler. I wonder what each of us is picking up."
- **We look for what each perspective reveals.** Different vantage points capture different aspects of the same field.
- **We resist the urge to decide who's right.** Multiple perceptions can all be valid simultaneously.

---

**When awareness reveals something uncomfortable.** Sometimes what we sense isn't pleasant — distance, coldness, tension, disconnection.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We remember that awareness isn't the problem.** The difficulty was already there. Awareness just brought it into view.
- **We don't have to fix what we notice immediately.** Sensing clearly is the first step. Action can come later.
- **We hold what we sense with compassion.** For ourselves, for each other, for the relationship.
- **We trust that clear awareness serves the relationship** even when what we see is hard. Navigating blind is always worse.

---

**When we can't sense much.** Sometimes the field feels opaque. Our sensing feels dull or blank.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We don't force it.** Awareness can't be demanded.
- **We might need to settle more.** If we're too activated or too distracted, sensing is difficult. We slow down first.
- **We start with easier sensing.** Begin with our own state before trying to sense the field. Begin with the body before trying to sense something more subtle.
- **We accept that sensitivity develops with practice.** If we can't sense much today, that's fine. The capacity builds over time.

---

**When awareness becomes hypervigilance.** There's a difference between healthy awareness and anxious monitoring. Healthy awareness is relaxed and receptive — it trusts that we'll sense what needs sensing. Hypervigilance is tense and effortful — it scans for threat and creates anxiety about missing something.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We soften the effort.** If we notice ourselves tensing around awareness, we deliberately relax.
- **We remind ourselves that we don't have to catch everything.** The field will signal when something needs attention. We don't need to be on alert.
- **We let awareness be background, not foreground.** Like peripheral vision — present but not consuming.
- **We check our motivation.** Are we sensing out of curiosity and care? Or out of fear? The answer tells us whether we're in awareness or hypervigilance.

## The larger view

Awareness of the relational field is foundational to everything that follows in this work.

Before we can attune to each other, we have to sense what's happening between us. Before we can work with dissonance, we have to notice it. Before we can navigate catalyst moments, we have to recognize them. Before we can track our energy, we have to feel it.

This capacity builds on what comes before it: presence (being here together) and interoception (sensing our own bodies). We have to be here, and we have to have access to our own inner landscape, before we can sense the field between us. But once this awareness comes online, it becomes the perceptual foundation for all the relational work ahead.

Awareness isn't something we develop once and then possess. It's a living capacity that deepens with practice and can dull with neglect. Some days we'll sense clearly. Other days the field will feel opaque. Different seasons of life will bring different levels of sensitivity. The practice continues.

This isn't discouraging — it means we're always developing, always refining, always learning to sense more clearly. There is no ceiling. The field between us always has more to reveal.

When we develop the capacity to sense what's happening between us, we become more perceptive in all our relationships — with friends, family, colleagues, communities. We begin to sense the fields we walk through: the quality of a room, the temperature of a group, the unspoken dynamics in a conversation. We become people who notice — and that noticing makes us more capable of care in every context.

## Our commitment to awareness

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to develop shared sensitivity to the field between us — not as surveillance, but as care.

We won't always sense clearly. Our awareness will sometimes be sharp and sometimes clouded. We will sometimes sense different things. We will sometimes miss what seems obvious in hindsight.

But we now have practices for developing this capacity together. We know what there is to sense. We know how to sense it. And we know that this awareness is the foundation for everything else we want to build between us.

May we sense the field between us with receptivity and honesty. May we notice without judging. May we share what we sense with gentleness. May our awareness serve our connection.

The field between us is always communicating. Let us learn to listen together.
