# Tending Our Emotions Together: *A Shared Practice for the Feelings That Live Between Us*

---

Hello, dear companions.

This guide began with a movie night during the pandemic.

One of us was on a Zoom call with two lifelong friends, watching a movie together. The other was in the next room, watching a television series alone. The show she was watching included a scene with a graphic sexual assault — and it triggered her own trauma with sexual assault. She began showing clear signs of distress.

He noticed. She said she was fine. She wasn't.

But here's what changed everything: the trigger wasn't only affecting her. He could feel it — in his own body, in the quality of the air between them, in the sudden shift of something he couldn't name but couldn't ignore. Her acute distress was not contained inside her. It had entered the shared space. He was experiencing the trigger *with* her — not the same way she was, but as the felt impact of watching his companion and life partner in acute pain.

He told his friends that Naomi was having a powerful trigger and he needed to be with her in it. He sat with her. They co-regulated together — his settled presence offering her nervous system something to borrow from, her gradual settling offering his system the evidence that they were going to be okay. They returned, together, to their baseline sense of safety and connection.

Afterward, when they tracked what had happened, they realized something: the trigger was hers — rooted in her body, her history, her trauma. But the emotional event was *theirs*. It entered the field between them the moment it occurred. His body felt it. The atmosphere in the room changed. The quality of the evening shifted. The co-regulation that brought them back was a shared act — not one person fixing the other, but two nervous systems working as one system to metabolize what had happened.

This is the emotional field between us. And it is as real as the bodies that generate it.

We tend to think of emotions as private — mine to feel, yours to feel, each of us responsible for our own inner weather. And that's partly true. But it's radically incomplete. Because when two people share life closely, their emotional systems become coupled. Her trigger entered his body. His steadiness entered hers. The distress didn't stay inside the person who generated it. It entered the field — and the field shaped everyone in it.

Most relationships know this intuitively but have no language for it. "You're in a mood" is the closest most people get — and it places the emotion back inside the individual rather than naming what's actually happening: the field has a mood, and both people are swimming in it.

This guide is about developing that language. About recognizing that the We has emotional weather — patterns, cycles, climates — that can be tracked, understood, and tended. Not by managing each other's feelings. Not by demanding that someone change their emotional state. But by bringing the same quality of shared awareness to our emotional life that "Our Body" brought to our physical life: the recognition that what we feel, we feel together — and that tending the emotional field is one of the most important things two people can do.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, chosen family, or anyone who shares life closely enough that what one person feels, the others feel too.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing with honesty**, because naming shared emotional reality requires both people to be willing to see what's actually in the field
- **Returning regularly**, because the emotional field changes constantly, and what we learn about tending it deepens with every season of shared life

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what the shared emotional field feels like when it's nourished, what it feels like when it's troubled, and what changes when we start tending it consciously.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how individual emotions become a shared field, what shapes it, and how it operates as a system with its own patterns.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the concrete ways we track, tend, and nourish the emotional life we share.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Our emotions are an experience

*There is an emotional atmosphere that lives between us — a quality of feeling in the shared space that neither person fully creates and neither person fully controls. When we learn to sense it, we discover that we've been living inside it all along.*

### When the emotional field is nourished

There's a quality to a shared life where the emotional atmosphere is tended:

- **Warmth.** The space between us carries a baseline of warmth — not performance warmth, not effortful positivity, but a genuine quality of emotional generosity that both people can feel. Walking into the room feels like arriving somewhere good.
- **Settledness.** Both nervous systems are regulated — not because nothing difficult is happening, but because the field itself has a settling quality. The emotional atmosphere supports regulation rather than undermining it. Both people feel calmer together than they do apart.
- **Emotional room.** There is space in the field for whatever arises. Sadness can be present without overwhelming the system. Anger can surface without the field becoming dangerous. Joy can flow without triggering suspicion. The field is large enough to hold the full range of emotional experience without collapsing.
- **Responsiveness.** When the emotional weather shifts — when one person's mood changes, when something difficult enters the space — the field responds. Not reactively. Responsively. Both people sense the shift and orient toward it together, rather than pretending it isn't happening or leaving one person alone in it.
- **Shared emotional knowledge.** Both people understand the field's patterns — its rhythms, its sensitivities, its seasonal changes. "This is a tense time of year for us." "Sunday evenings tend to carry anxiety." "After we fight, the field needs a day to resettle." This knowledge is itself a form of tending.

### When the emotional field is troubled

When the emotional atmosphere is neglected, damaged, or chronically strained:

- **Ambient tension.** The field carries a low-grade stress that both people feel but neither addresses. It's like a hum in the room that everyone has stopped hearing — except the body hasn't stopped hearing it. Cortisol runs slightly higher. Patience runs slightly thinner. Everything takes slightly more effort than it should.
- **Emotional contagion without awareness.** One person's mood infiltrates the other without either person naming what's happening. She comes home anxious and within twenty minutes he's irritable — not because anything happened between them, but because anxiety is contagious and the field transmitted it. Without awareness, both people look for relational explanations for what is actually a field phenomenon.
- **Emotional scarcity.** There isn't enough emotional room for both people. One person's needs consume the available space, and the other's go unmet — not from cruelty, but from depletion. The field can only hold so much, and when one person's emotional demands are large, the other learns to compress.
- **Weather without pattern.** The emotional atmosphere seems to shift unpredictably — good days and bad days with no apparent logic. Without tracking, both people experience the field as chaotic rather than patterned. But there are almost always patterns. We just haven't learned to read them yet.
- **Both people feeling alone in the same room.** Perhaps the most painful state: two people physically together but emotionally isolated. Each carrying their own weather. No shared field to speak of — or a field so cold and thin that neither person feels held by it. The bodies are present. The emotional atmosphere is empty.

### The difference between individual emotions and the shared field

This distinction matters:

**Individual emotions** are what each person feels inside themselves — sadness, anger, joy, fear. These are real, they matter, and they're addressed in other guides (Processing Our Emotions Together, Tending Our Emotional Climate Together).

**The shared emotional field** is the atmosphere that exists *between* people — the quality of emotional life in the shared space. It's influenced by individual emotions but not reducible to them. Two happy people can create a tense field if the happiness is performative. Two people in grief can create a warm field if the grief is held together. The field has its own properties that emerge from the interaction of the people in it.

The practice of this guide is learning to sense, name, and tend the field itself — not just the individual emotions that feed it.

**A reflection for us:** What is the emotional atmosphere between us right now — not what each of us is feeling individually, but the quality of feeling in the shared space? If the field had a weather report, what would it say?

## Our emotions are a process

*The emotional field between us is not a metaphor. It is the felt, shared emotional reality that emerges when two or more nervous systems are in regular contact — a living system with its own patterns, its own health, and its own capacity to nourish or deplete the people inside it.*

### How individual emotions become a shared field

The emotional field forms through specific, observable mechanisms:

**Emotional contagion.** Emotions are literally contagious between people in close contact. This happens through mirror neuron activation, facial expression mimicry, vocal tone matching, and autonomic nervous system coupling. When one person is anxious, the other person's nervous system begins to register anxiety — not because they've decided to feel anxious, but because the body is wired to absorb the emotional states of nearby bodies. This is the most basic mechanism of the shared field: your feelings enter my body, and mine enter yours.

**Co-regulation and co-dysregulation.** When both nervous systems are settled, they settle each other further — a positive feedback loop that creates the quality of warmth and ease that characterizes a nourished field. When one or both systems are activated, the activation amplifies through the same loop — anxiety breeds anxiety, reactivity triggers reactivity. The field can spiral in either direction: toward greater settling or greater activation.

**Emotional accumulation.** The field has memory. The emotional residue of past experiences — fights that weren't fully resolved, joys that were shared, griefs that were held together, hurts that were repaired or left open — accumulates in the field as a kind of emotional sediment. Over time, this sediment creates the baseline character of the field: is it generally warm or generally cool? Generally safe or generally guarded? The field's character is the sum of everything that has been felt between these people.

**Relational feedback loops.** The field and the individuals shape each other continuously. A tense field makes both people more reactive, which makes the field more tense. A warm field makes both people more generous, which makes the field warmer. Understanding these loops is essential — because it means that tending the field is not just responding to what's present but shaping what emerges next.

### The field has its own patterns

Like the body's rhythms, the emotional field has patterns that can be tracked:

**Daily patterns.** The field often has a different quality in the morning than in the evening. After work is often the most volatile window — two nervous systems carrying the day's accumulation, colliding in a shared space. Mornings may be fresher. Weekends may carry a different emotional signature than weekdays.

**Seasonal patterns.** Holidays carry particular emotional weather — sometimes joy, sometimes dread, sometimes the complex mixture that family history creates. Winter may bring a particular quality. The anniversary of a loss may shade the field for days before anyone names why.

**Cyclical patterns tied to the body.** The hormonal cycles explored in earlier guides affect the emotional field directly. Her premenstrual phase may bring a specific quality to the field. His low-testosterone evenings may shift the atmosphere. These are not individual emotional events — they are body-level inputs that change the shared weather.

**Patterns tied to relationship history.** The field may carry particular charge around the anniversary of a betrayal, around topics that have been sites of repeated conflict, around transitions that echo past difficulties. These are the field's scars — places where the emotional tissue is thinner and more reactive.

### The field is bidirectional

This is essential to understand: the field shapes the individuals as much as the individuals shape the field.

A person who is generally calm can become anxious in a chronically anxious field. A person who is generally warm can become cold in a field that punishes warmth. A person who is generally available can become withdrawn in a field that is too demanding. The field has its own gravity — it pulls the people in it toward its prevailing state.

This means that tending the field is not just about managing individual emotions. It's about changing the conditions that produce those emotions in the first place. A person struggling with anxiety in a tense field doesn't just need individual emotional support. They need the field to change.

### Tending vs. controlling (the essential distinction)

The same line that runs through "Our Body" runs through here:

**Tending** sounds like: "The field feels heavy tonight. What's in it?" "I notice I'm carrying something that isn't just mine — I think it's between us." "What would help the atmosphere right now?" "I can feel that we're co-dysregulating. Can we pause and settle together?"

**Controlling** sounds like: "You need to calm down, your anxiety is affecting me." "Stop being negative, you're ruining the mood." "I need you to be in a better state so I can feel okay." "Your emotions are too much for this relationship."

Tending brings awareness to the shared field and invites both people to work with it. Controlling locates the problem in one person and demands they change for the other's comfort. The difference is the difference between tending a shared garden and blaming one plant for the weather.

This awareness is not about perfection or emotional optimization. It is about increasing clarity over time — developing a shared capacity to sense the field, name what's in it, and tend it together. Not getting every emotional moment right.

**A reflection for us:** What patterns does our emotional field carry? Are there times of day, times of year, or recurring situations where the field consistently shifts? And have we ever named these patterns together?

## Our emotions are a practice

These are our best practices for tending the emotional life we share. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We learn to sense the field

Developing the capacity to feel the emotional atmosphere as a shared reality, not just as individual feelings.

- **We check the field, not just ourselves.** Instead of only asking "How am I feeling?" we add: "What's the quality of the space between us right now?" The field has a felt quality — warm, tense, flat, alive, heavy, spacious — that can be sensed directly when we learn to attend to it.
- **We name the field out loud.** "The field feels heavy tonight." "There's a warmth between us right now." "Something is tense in our space and I don't think it belongs to either of us specifically." Naming the field makes the shared emotional reality speakable — and once it's speakable, it can be tended.
- **We distinguish field weather from individual weather.** "I'm feeling anxious — but I think the field is actually okay. This one is mine." Or: "I was fine until I walked in and now I feel tense — I think this is in the field, not in me." The distinction isn't always clean, but practicing it builds the capacity to sense what's shared and what's personal.
- **We develop a shared vocabulary.** Over time, we build language for the field's states: "Sunday evening tension." "Post-fight chill." "Morning generosity." "The heavy week before the anniversary." This vocabulary is itself a form of tending — it makes the field's patterns recognizable and nameable.

*When to use: As a foundational practice. When the emotional atmosphere feels off but neither person can name why. When we want to develop shared awareness of what lives between us emotionally.*

---

### We track the field's patterns

Building awareness of the emotional field's rhythms and recurring states.

- **We notice daily patterns.** When is the field warmest? When is it most vulnerable? What time of day does tension tend to arrive? What's the emotional quality of our mornings vs. our evenings? We map the daily emotional rhythm the way the cycle guides mapped the body's daily rhythm.
- **We notice seasonal and cyclical patterns.** How does the field shift across seasons, around holidays, during hormonal cycles, at particular anniversaries? We track these over months and years, building a picture of the field's longer rhythms.
- **We notice event-driven patterns.** What happens to the field after conflict? After sex? After a visit from family? After a stressful work period? We learn to anticipate the field's response to recurring events, which makes the shifts more navigable when they arrive.
- **We hold patterns as tendencies, not laws.** "The field is usually tense on Sunday evenings" is different from "Sunday evenings are always bad." We track patterns loosely, as information that helps us tend the field rather than predictions that constrain it.

*When to use: Over weeks and months. When emotional shifts seem random and we want to discover the pattern underneath. When we want to build a longer view of our emotional life together.*

---

### We tend the field's weather

Actively caring for the emotional atmosphere rather than passively enduring it.

- **We settle the field together.** When the field is activated — tense, anxious, reactive — we don't just try to settle ourselves individually. We tend the field: sitting together, breathing together, co-regulating deliberately. Not talking about the problem yet. First, changing the conditions in the field. Many conversations that go badly do so because the field was too activated to hold them.
- **We warm the field deliberately.** When the field is cold, flat, or distant, we bring warmth to it — through touch, through kind words, through shared laughter, through the deliberate act of orienting toward each other with generosity. Warming the field is not performing positivity. It's investing emotional energy into the shared space because the shared space is where we both live.
- **We clear what's accumulated.** When the field carries unprocessed residue — unspoken resentment, unaddressed hurt, accumulated stress — we name it and process it together rather than letting it build as sediment. "There's something between us that we haven't addressed. Can we clear it?" The field benefits from regular clearing the way the body benefits from regular rest.
- **We protect the field from external contamination.** Stress from work, family drama, world events — these enter the field through whoever carries them home. We develop awareness of what we're bringing in: "I'm carrying a lot of work stress tonight. I don't want it to infect our space. Can we take ten minutes for me to land before we engage?"

*When to use: When the emotional atmosphere needs active tending. When the field is activated and we need to settle it before addressing content. When the field has gone cold and needs deliberate warming.*

---

### We take responsibility for what we bring to the field

Developing honest awareness of how each person's emotional state shapes the shared atmosphere.

- **We track our own emotional input.** "I notice I've been bringing a lot of anxiety into the field this week." "I think my irritability is becoming the weather in our space." We develop the honesty to see how our individual emotional states are shaping what both people breathe.
- **We name what we're carrying before it enters the field unconsciously.** "I'm arriving with frustration from today. It's not about us, but it's in my body and it might affect the space." Naming what we carry at the door is one of the most powerful tending practices available. It gives the companion information and gives the field a buffer.
- **We own our contribution without owning the whole field.** "My anxiety is making the field more tense" is honest. "The field is tense because of me" is usually an overstatement — the field is co-created, and both people's states contribute. We take responsibility for our part without absorbing responsibility for the whole.
- **We ask for help when our state is overwhelming the field.** "I'm flooding and I can feel it affecting us. Can you help me settle?" "My grief is filling the room and I don't want it to become your grief — but I need you close." Asking for help with our emotional state is an act of field tending, not a burden on the companion.

*When to use: When one person's emotional state is clearly shaping the field. When we want to develop honest awareness of our emotional contribution. When we need to name what we're carrying before it contaminates the shared space.*

---

### We hold the field through difficulty

Developing the capacity to keep the emotional atmosphere safe enough to hold hard things.

- **We settle before we address.** When something difficult needs to be discussed — a conflict, a hurt, a fear — we tend the field first. Co-regulate. Touch. Breathe. Get the emotional atmosphere to a baseline of safety. Then bring the content. The same words, spoken in a settled field, land completely differently than in an activated one.
- **We monitor the field during hard conversations.** As the conversation unfolds, we track not just the content but the atmosphere. "I can feel us getting activated. Can we pause and resettle before we continue?" The field's state during a difficult conversation determines whether the conversation heals or harms.
- **We repair the field after rupture.** When the field has been damaged — by conflict, by harsh words, by withdrawal — we tend it deliberately afterward. Not just resolving the content of the disagreement, but attending to the quality of the atmosphere. "The content is resolved, but the field still feels fragile. Can we stay close tonight?"
- **We let the field grieve.** Sometimes the emotional weather between us is grief — for a loss, for a change, for what was and isn't anymore. We don't rush the field's grief any more than we'd rush an individual's. "Our field is grieving right now. Let's let it." The field processes at its own pace, and giving it room to do so is one of the deepest acts of emotional tending.

*When to use: Before, during, and after difficult conversations. When the field has been ruptured and needs repair. When the shared emotional weather is grief and needs time.*

---

### We nourish the field

Actively investing in the emotional atmosphere's health and vitality.

- **We create positive shared emotional experiences.** Not as distraction from difficulty, but as genuine nourishment. Shared laughter. Shared beauty. Shared meals eaten with attention. Shared silence that feels full rather than empty. These aren't luxuries. They are the emotional nutrition of the field — and without them, the field subsists on whatever enters it, which is often stress.
- **We celebrate the field's health when it's present.** "I love how our space feels right now." "The field between us has been so warm this week." "Something is alive between us tonight." Naming the good weather reinforces it — the field learns what's valued, the same way a body responds to what nourishes it.
- **We invest when things are good.** The best time to nourish the field is when it's already healthy. This is counterintuitive — when things are good, we tend to coast. But investing during good periods builds the reserves that carry the field through difficulty. The warm field can absorb a hard week. The depleted field can't absorb a hard hour.
- **We return to the field after absence.** After travel, after a busy period, after a time when the field wasn't tended — we consciously reconnect. Not just logistically. Emotionally. "We've been apart. Let me feel my way back into our space." The field needs re-entry, not just re-presence.

*When to use: When the field is healthy and we want to deepen that health. When we've been coasting and want to invest before the next hard season. When we're reuniting after absence.*

## Working with challenges

**When one person is emotionally louder than the other.** One person's emotional presence consistently dominates the field — their moods become the weather, and the other person's emotional life gets compressed.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Naming the pattern without blame.** "I notice your emotional states tend to fill the field, and mine tend to go underground. I don't think either of us is doing this on purpose." The pattern is usually a combination of temperament, conditioning, and habitual relational dynamics.
- **Creating space for the quieter person.** Actively asking: "What's in the field from your side?" "I've been feeling a lot — what have you been feeling that I might have missed?" The louder person making room is a field-tending act.
- **The louder person developing containment.** Not suppression — but the capacity to hold their emotional state without it automatically becoming the shared weather. "I'm feeling a lot right now. I'm going to hold it for a bit before I bring it into our space."

---

**When the field is chronically tense and neither person knows why.** The atmosphere is tight, reactive, or heavy — and both people attribute it to each other rather than to the field itself.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Naming the field as the subject.** "Something is tense between us and I don't think it's about either of us individually. The field is carrying something." This simple reframe — from "you're making me tense" to "the field is tense" — changes the entire dynamic.
- **Tracking back to the source.** When did the tension begin? What event, conversation, or unaddressed issue might have entered the field? Often the source is something specific that was never processed and settled into the emotional sediment.
- **Seeking support when the field won't shift.** Sometimes the field is carrying something that the two people inside it can't tend alone — deep relational patterns, trauma dynamics, accumulated damage. A therapist or facilitator who can work with the field as a system (not just the individuals) can sometimes shift what the couple cannot.

---

**When tending the field slides into emotional management of the companion.** "The field needs to settle" becomes "you need to calm down."

**What we've found helps:**

- **Catching the slide immediately.** "I think I just moved from tending the field to trying to manage your emotions. That's not the same thing." The awareness itself is the correction.
- **Returning to shared language.** "What does the field need right now?" is different from "What do you need to do differently?" The first invites shared tending. The second assigns individual responsibility.
- **Both people tending from their own position.** Each person can ask: "What am I contributing to the field right now, and what can I shift in myself?" Tending begins with self-awareness, not other-direction.

---

**When external stress chronically poisons the field.** Work stress, financial pressure, family conflict, world events — the field is receiving so much toxic input from outside that it can't maintain its own health.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Naming the source as external.** "Our field is struggling because of what's coming at us from outside, not because of anything between us." This distinction protects the relationship from absorbing blame for conditions it didn't create.
- **Creating boundaries around the field.** Deliberately protecting emotional space: screen-free time, evenings without discussing stressors, physical settling before engaging with each other. The field needs a membrane, and sometimes that membrane must be consciously maintained.
- **Lowering expectations for the field during hard seasons.** When external pressure is high, the field can't be at its best. Accepting a lower baseline during crisis — without abandoning tending entirely — prevents the depletion from becoming permanent.

---

**When the learning companion is at capacity.** Tending the emotional field requires awareness, and some days awareness isn't available.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Naming it.** "I don't have the capacity to tend the field today. I can be present, but I can't be attentive to the atmosphere." This is honest self-reporting, not abandonment.
- **Lowering the bar.** On low-capacity days, the tending is as simple as not making the field worse. Don't bring unnecessary stress. Don't start hard conversations. Rest near each other. Let the field be quiet.
- **Returning without guilt.** The field is resilient. A day or a week of minimal tending doesn't erase what's been built. We come back when we can.

## The larger view

We live inside emotional fields all the time — at work, in families, in communities, in friendships. The quality of the emotional atmosphere shapes everything that happens within it: how we think, how we communicate, how we make decisions, what we can tolerate, what we can create. And yet most of us have no practice for tending these fields. We treat emotions as individual weather — mine to manage, yours to manage — and wonder why the space between us feels the way it does.

The recognition that the We has emotions — that the relational field itself has an affective life that can be sensed, named, tracked, and tended — changes how we understand the work of relationship. It's not just about two people learning to communicate better. It's about two people learning to tend the emotional atmosphere they share, because that atmosphere is where everything else either thrives or struggles.

A relationship with a nourished emotional field can hold difficulty, navigate conflict, and recover from harm — because the baseline conditions support resilience. A relationship with a depleted emotional field struggles with everything — not because the people are failing, but because the atmosphere they're trying to operate in can't support what they're asking of it.

Tending the emotional field is not therapy. It's not emotional management. It's the recognition that the space between us has a life of its own — warm or cold, settled or activated, spacious or cramped — and that this life is our shared responsibility. Not because either of us caused it, but because both of us live in it.

## Our commitment to tending our emotions

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to recognize the emotional field between us and to tend it — not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will learn to sense the field. We will track its patterns. We will tend its weather — settling what's activated, warming what's cold, clearing what's accumulated. We will take responsibility for what we bring. We will hold the field through difficulty. We will nourish it when it's healthy.

We will not always do this well. We will sometimes flood the field with our own emotions without noticing. We will sometimes mistake field weather for individual weather. We will sometimes try to control each other's emotions in the name of tending the shared space. This is human.

But we now have a shared practice. We know that the We has emotions — that the emotional field between us is as real as the bodies that generate it. We know that this field can be sensed, named, and tended. We know that its health shapes everything else the relationship tries to do. And we know that two people who tend their shared emotional life with the same care they bring to their shared physical life are building something that can hold whatever comes.

May we sense what lives between us. May we name it with honesty. May we tend it with care. May we never forget that the atmosphere we share is the air we both breathe — and that what we nourish, nourishes us in return.

Let us tend what we feel — together.
