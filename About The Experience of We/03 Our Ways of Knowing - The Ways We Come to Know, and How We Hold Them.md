# Our Ways of Knowing

*The Ways We Come to Know, and How We Hold Them*

---

Hello, dear companions.

If a body of work is going to revise itself, to stay unfinished by design as The Experience of We does, then it owes you an honest answer to a simple question: *how does it come to know anything in the first place?* By what light does it decide what's true enough to build on, and what to let go?

There is a larger question underneath that one, and it may be the question this whole work turns on: how do living beings stay capable of discovering truth together, over time? Not "who is right," but "how do we become more right together?"

This is that answer. It's short on purpose. But it sits underneath everything else here, because it's what keeps "we might be wrong, and we'll keep revising" from quietly becoming "anything goes."

Our answer is not a single method. It is several, held together. We believe there are many real ways human beings come to know, each revealing something the others can't reach, each capable of distortion, none of them the whole truth alone. The work is not to crown one of them. It's to keep them in honest conversation.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## The ways we come to know

We name seven. The order is deliberate: it begins with the way of knowing most native to this work and moves outward. Think of them less as a list than as an ecology: each occupies a niche the others cannot fill, each contributes something the others cannot reach, and none can stand in for the rest.

### Relational Knowing

Some things can only be known *between* people. The truth of trust, of attunement, of rupture and repair, of resonance and dissonance, of a shared reality being built. These don't sit inside one person waiting to be reported; they show up in the space between us, and they can only be known from inside that space. This is the way of knowing most particular to The Experience of We, which is why it comes first. Much of what this work claims, it claims because it became visible within the relational field, not because any one of us reasoned it out alone.

### Somatic / Embodied Knowing

The body knows things before the mind has words for them. A tightening in the chest, a softening in the belly, a gut sense that arrives ahead of any thought. This is what the body registers *before, beneath, or beyond* what we can narrate, and it is not lesser for being wordless. We treat it as real information rather than noise to be overridden. Much of relational life is read here first, in the tissue, before it ever reaches language.

### Lived Experience

This is the whole of what we have actually encountered: felt, perceived, suffered, practiced, and integrated over time. Not a single sensation but the accumulated, digested experience of being alive, in a body, in relationships, across years. Lived experience is where abstraction meets the ground. A thing can be elegant in theory and false in the living; lived experience is how we find out.

### Science

The disciplined practice of observing carefully, testing what we believe against evidence, measuring, inviting challenge, and revising when the evidence demands it. Science is among the most powerful correctives human beings have ever built against our talent for fooling ourselves. We take it seriously, and this framework leans on it often, in neuroscience, physiology, and systems biology. We also hold it as one way of knowing among others, not the only door to the real.

### Systems Thinking

The study of relationships rather than things: patterns, feedback loops, emergence, constraints, and consequences as they move across scales. Systems thinking sees what gets missed when parts are examined in isolation, how a dynamic in one body echoes in a family, an economy, an ecology. Much of this framework is systems thinking applied to relationship. It has its own blind spot: not every aspect of reality becomes fully visible through this lens, and not every system-story is the whole story.

### Spiritual Perception

The human capacity to perceive meaning, sacredness, interconnection, moral salience, reverence, mystery, and participation in something larger than the isolated self. We do not treat spiritual perception as infallible revelation, doctrine, or exemption from inquiry. We treat it as one meaningful way human beings encounter reality: powerful, vulnerable to distortion, and therefore best held in relationship with the others. You do not have to accept any supernatural premise to recognize that humans genuinely experience awe, moral obligation, relational depth, and belonging. That experience is real data, even where its ultimate source remains open.

### Collective Wisdom

Knowledge no single lifetime could produce, carried instead by communities, traditions, elders, lineages, and cultures: the distilled experience of many people over long time. Collective wisdom holds tested answers to questions each of us would otherwise meet from scratch. It can also carry distortion forward unexamined, which is why it too is held under what comes next, rather than above it.

## The discipline that holds them: humility

These seven are instruments. Humility is not an eighth instrument; it is the posture with which we hold all of them.

Every way of knowing reveals something and obscures something. Each, left unchecked, tends toward the same failure: it begins to claim the whole truth for itself. Science can slide into *only the measurable is real.* Spiritual perception can slide into *my revelation cannot be questioned.* Lived experience can slide into *because I felt it, it must be universally true.* Collective wisdom can slide into *we have always done it this way.* Each of these is the same move, one good instrument mistaking itself for the whole.

Humility is the active practice of catching that move. It is the discipline of continually asking, of every way of knowing, including the ones we trust most: *what might this not be seeing?* In the language of this framework, humility is the epistemology's immune function, the thing that keeps any single way of knowing from becoming the one that rules. It works alongside the other immune function named in the [Living System charter](02%20The%20Experience%20of%20We%20Is%20a%20Living%20System%20-%20Why%20This%20Work%20Is%20Alive%2C%20and%20Must%20Stay%20That%20Way.md), the one that guards against the misuse of power. Both protect the same thing: a system honest enough to stay alive.

This is why we call humility a *practice* and not a mood. It isn't self-doubt, and it isn't false modesty. It is stewardship: the ongoing, active tending of our relationship to our own knowing.

And humility has a partner. If humility is the immune function that keeps any single way of knowing from claiming too much, curiosity is the drive that keeps us reaching for what we don't yet know. Humility says *I may be wrong*; curiosity says *let us find out.* One keeps the system from ossifying; the other keeps it growing. A living way of knowing needs both, because without curiosity, humility curdles into mere caution, and the whole thing goes still.

---

## In short

The Experience of We draws from relational knowing, somatic and embodied knowing, lived experience, science, systems thinking, spiritual perception, and collective wisdom. We treat each as meaningful. We treat none as final. Our commitment is not to certainty but to fidelity: the ongoing practice of refining understanding in service of life, relationship, and truth over time.

And fittingly, none of this was arrived at alone. This account was itself shaped by several ways of knowing brought into conversation, which is the only way we know to look honestly for what's true: together, and willing to be corrected.
