# Practicing Love Together: *A Shared Practice for Tending the Living Relational Field*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Love is not a feeling. It's not romance. It's not the intensity of early attraction or the comfort of familiar attachment.

Love is something larger — an emergent, self-reinforcing relational process in which two people commit to the ongoing co-regulation and co-metabolization of reality over time, prioritizing the continuity, integrity, and growth of the shared relational field itself.

In love, the relationship becomes a living thing — not just the two individuals connected, but the field between them: a third entity that breathes, grows, suffers, heals, and evolves. Love is the orientation that takes this living field as the primary unit of care. Decisions, actions, and expressions are aimed not at individual extraction but at the health and evolution of what we are together.

This understanding of love builds on everything that comes before. Love arises when authenticity has been practiced, vulnerability has been risked, trust has been built, safety has been established, co-regulation has become reliable, and co-metabolization has become possible. Love is the emergent property of these processes stabilizing into a self-reinforcing loop.

What many call "love" may actually be something else — attachment, longing, dependency, idealization — adaptive substitutes in systems that can't yet sustain true relational integration. This isn't a moral failing. These forms emerge when the conditions for real love aren't yet present. But they're not the same as love.

This practice is about cultivating the real thing: a love that functions as a living stabilizer, binding us together through demonstrated reliability, mutual responsiveness, and shared willingness to metabolize reality together — including pain, change, and uncertainty.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to cultivate love as a living relational process rather than just a feeling or an attachment.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go**, as ongoing orientation to the relationship
- **Returning regularly**, because love is not something we master once but something we keep tending

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what love feels like as a living process, the particular quality of being oriented toward the relational field, and what emerges when love is genuinely present.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how love arises from the other relational processes, how it sustains and deepens over time, and what distinguishes it from its substitutes.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate love: the concrete ways we tend the living relational field and orient ourselves toward its flourishing.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Love is an experience

*Love is what it feels like when the relational field comes alive — when something between us becomes a living thing we both orient toward and care for.*

### The felt sense of love

When love is present — not as feeling but as living process — there's a distinctive quality to experience:

- **We-ness.** A sense that there is a "we" that is real, not just a convenient word. The relationship has presence, substance, life.
- **Care for the field.** Our attention naturally includes the relationship itself — how it's doing, what it needs, where it's headed.
- **Willingness to be changed.** We're open to being transformed by the relationship, not just using it for our own purposes.
- **Long horizon.** Love includes the future. We think not just about today but about who we're becoming together over time.
- **Stability through flux.** Even when emotions fluctuate — joy, frustration, boredom, desire — there's something steady underneath: the commitment to the living field.
- **Coming home.** A sense of ground, of belonging, of place. Not the intensity of passion but the depth of genuine connection.

### What love is not

**Love is not constant positive feeling.** Love persists through difficult emotions — anger, hurt, disappointment — without these negating love itself.

**Love is not possession.** Love doesn't seek to own, control, or limit the other. It wants the other to flourish, even when that's inconvenient.

**Love is not dependency.** Dependency needs the other to be a certain way; love can hold the other's freedom.

**Love is not idealization.** Idealization loves an image; love loves the real person, including the parts that are disappointing or difficult.

**Love is not sacrifice.** Love doesn't require us to disappear ourselves. A healthy relational field includes the wellbeing of both individuals.

**Love is not stasis.** Love is a living process. It grows, changes, deepens, and sometimes struggles. It's never finished.

### Love's relationship to feeling

This understanding of love doesn't dismiss feeling — it contextualizes it. Feelings are information. They are part of what gets metabolized together. They matter.

But love isn't reducible to feeling. Love is the container within which feelings arise and are processed. It's the commitment that persists when feelings fluctuate. It's the field that holds everything else.

When we say "I love you," we're not just reporting a feeling. We're affirming a commitment to the living relational field — to its continuity, its health, its growth, its care through whatever comes.

**A reflection for us:** When does love feel most alive between us — not as sentiment but as a living force? What are we doing, or not doing, in those moments?

## Love is a process

*Love doesn't appear from nowhere. It emerges from the stabilization of other relational processes and sustains itself through positive feedback loops that deepen over time.*

### Love as emergent process

Love emerges from the stabilization of other relational processes:

**Authenticity** allows accurate information to flow, making real knowing possible.

**Vulnerability** enables trust to form through risked exposure met with care.

**Trust** creates the predictive model that allows the nervous system to expect safety.

**Safety** allows the nervous system to stop defending, freeing energy for connection.

**Co-regulation** establishes the capacity to settle together, to share regulatory resources.

**Co-metabolization** enables the joint processing of reality — meaning, emotion, complexity digested together.

When these processes stabilize into a self-reinforcing loop — each supporting the others, each made more possible by the others — love emerges as their integration. Love isn't separate from these processes; it's their culmination.

### The positive feedback loop of love

Once love establishes, it generates positive feedback loops that strengthen it:

- Love makes authenticity safer → more truth enters the field
- More truth deepens trust → vulnerability becomes easier
- Easier vulnerability builds safety → nervous systems relax further
- Relaxed systems co-regulate better → metabolic capacity increases
- Increased metabolic capacity → more of reality can be processed together
- Shared processing deepens the bond → love strengthens
- Strengthened love makes authenticity safer...

This is the virtuous spiral of living relational systems. Each turn of the cycle deepens what we are together.

### Love's relationship to time

Love is not a moment but a commitment across time. It includes:

**Continuity.** A commitment to the ongoing existence of the relationship — not that it will never end, but that we won't casually abandon it.

**Repair.** A commitment to return to each other after rupture, to restore what's been damaged, to rebuild what's been broken.

**Growth.** A commitment to the relationship's evolution — not just maintaining what we have but developing new capacities, new depths, new possibilities.

**Perseverance.** A commitment to face difficulty together rather than fleeing when things get hard. Love includes the hard parts.

### Distinguishing love from its substitutes

What often passes for love may actually be something else:

**Attachment** is the desire to maintain connection because we fear loss or need the other for our regulation. Attachment can exist without love; love can exist without anxious attachment.

**Longing** is the ache for connection, often intensified by distance or unavailability. Longing can be mistaken for love because it feels so strong. But longing is about what we don't have; love is about what we're building.

**Dependency** is the reliance on the other to meet needs we can't meet ourselves. Some dependency is natural; extreme dependency masquerades as love while actually consuming it.

**Idealization** is the projection of perfection onto the other, loving an image rather than a person. Idealization often collapses when reality intrudes.

**Passion** is intense desire, often accompanied by physiological arousal. Passion can be part of love, but it's not love itself — it fluctuates while love persists.

These aren't bad — they're human experiences, sometimes healthy, sometimes pointing toward love's possibility. But recognizing them for what they are helps us understand what love actually is.

**A reflection for us:** How do we each tend to express love? How do we each most need to receive it? Where are the gaps between giving and receiving?

## Love is a practice

These are our best practices for cultivating love together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We orient toward the relational field

Love means making the relationship itself an object of care.

- **We notice the relationship as an entity.** We recognize that there is a "we" that is more than the sum of two individuals.
- **We ask about the relationship's health.** "How are we doing?" becomes as natural as "How are you doing?"
- **We make decisions that consider the field.** When choices arise, we include their impact on the relationship, not just on ourselves.
- **We invest in the relationship directly.** Time, attention, and energy go not just to each person but to what's between us.
- **We speak of the relationship with care.** How we talk about "us" — to ourselves, to each other, to others — shapes the field.

*When to use: As ongoing orientation. When decisions arise that affect the relationship. When we want to strengthen our commitment to the living field between us.*

---

### We prioritize the relationship's life

The living relational field needs tending to thrive.

- **We protect relational time.** We ensure there's dedicated space for us — not just logistical coordination but genuine connecting.
- **We attend to the relationship's needs.** When the relationship is struggling, we address it rather than hoping it resolves on its own.
- **We don't sacrifice the relationship for other priorities indefinitely.** Work, children, external demands are real, but if the relationship consistently comes last, it withers.
- **We defend the relationship against threats.** Whether internal (patterns that damage us) or external (circumstances that strain us), we take protective action.
- **We invest in growth.** We read together, practice together, seek support together — actively developing what we're capable of.

*When to use: When the relationship is being consistently neglected. When we need to recommit to prioritizing us. When external demands are crowding out relational life.*

---

### We practice commitment as ongoing choice

Love is not a one-time decision but a continuous choosing.

- **We recommit regularly.** Not from doubt but from renewal — consciously choosing each other again and again.
- **We speak our commitment.** "I choose you." "I'm in this." "I want this relationship to continue and grow." Words matter.
- **We act our commitment.** Consistent behavior that demonstrates the choice we've made, not just in special moments but in ordinary ones.
- **We face commitment's challenges honestly.** When commitment is hard — when we're attracted elsewhere, when we're frustrated, when we wonder about other paths — we bring that to consciousness rather than acting it out or suppressing it.
- **We distinguish commitment from entrapment.** Commitment is chosen, renewed, alive. Entrapment is obligation without choice. We commit because we want to, not because we have to.

*When to use: When commitment feels like obligation rather than choice. When we want to renew our active choosing. When the relationship needs to hear that we're in it.*

---

### We tend the roots of love

Love depends on the other processes continuing to function.

- **We maintain authenticity.** We continue to align what's true with what's expressed, even when it's easier to hide.
- **We continue risking vulnerability.** We don't become so comfortable that we stop sharing what's real and risky.
- **We keep building trust.** Through consistent reliability, kept commitments, and patterns that prove trustworthiness.
- **We protect safety.** We remain attentive to what creates and threatens safety between us.
- **We practice co-regulation.** We continue settling together, sharing regulatory resources, calming each other's nervous systems.
- **We metabolize together.** We keep processing reality jointly — the hard stuff, the good stuff, the ongoing flow of experience.

*When to use: When love feels distant or thin. When we want to check on the health of our foundation. As regular maintenance of the relational system.*

---

### We repair when love is strained

Love is not destroyed by rupture — it's destroyed by unrepaired rupture.

- **We recognize when the relational field is damaged.** Not just when one of us is upset but when the relationship itself has been wounded.
- **We prioritize repair.** We don't let damage accumulate. We return to restore what's been broken.
- **We repair for the relationship, not just for ourselves.** "I want to repair this because the relationship matters" is different from "I want to repair this so I feel better."
- **We trust that repair is possible.** Love that has been broken and healed is often stronger than love that was never tested.
- **We learn from ruptures.** Each repair teaches us something about our vulnerabilities and strengths as a relational system.

*When to use: After any rupture or damage to the relationship. When distance has developed between us. When love feels uncertain or threatened.*

---

### We celebrate and nourish love

Love needs nourishment, not just maintenance.

- **We appreciate what we have.** We don't take the relationship for granted. We actively notice and express gratitude for what's good between us.
- **We create positive experiences together.** Joy, play, adventure, rest — experiences that feed the relationship and create shared memory.
- **We mark milestones.** Anniversaries, achievements, passages — we acknowledge the relationship's journey through time.
- **We ritualize connection.** Regular practices that affirm and nourish us: date nights, morning rituals, annual retreats, whatever forms work for us.
- **We speak love.** We tell each other what we value, appreciate, cherish. We let love be voiced, not just assumed.

*When to use: When love needs feeding. When we've focused too much on problems and too little on gifts. When we want to actively nourish what's good between us.*

---

### We grow love over time

Love is not static — it develops, deepens, transforms.

- **We expect love to change.** Early love differs from mature love. Both are real; they're not the same.
- **We welcome deepening.** As we know each other more fully, as we metabolize more of life together, as our roots intertwine — love deepens.
- **We work with plateaus.** Sometimes love feels stagnant. Rather than panicking, we explore what wants to happen next.
- **We don't chase intensity as a measure of love.** Deep love may feel quiet compared to passionate love. Quiet is not less.
- **We let love teach us.** The relationship itself has wisdom. We learn from what we're becoming together.

*When to use: When love feels like it should be different than it is. When we're comparing our love to other forms. When we want to understand love's natural evolution over time.*

---

### We face love's difficulties honestly

Love includes the hard parts — they can't be avoided, only faced.

- **We acknowledge when love is difficult.** Pretending it's always easy doesn't serve. We tell the truth about the challenges.
- **We stay through difficulty.** We don't leave at the first sign of trouble. We stay present to what's hard, working through rather than around.
- **We get support when needed.** Some difficulties are beyond what the relationship can resolve alone. Seeking help is wisdom, not failure.
- **We discern between workable difficulty and unsolvable problems.** Not every relationship can or should survive. We stay for good reasons, not just because leaving is hard.
- **We hold love and difficulty together.** The presence of difficulty doesn't mean the absence of love. They can coexist.

*When to use: When love is hard. When we're uncertain about whether to stay or go. When we need to face difficulties rather than avoiding them.*

## Working with challenges

**When love feels like it's gone.** Sometimes the feeling of love disappears, and we wonder if love is over.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Remember that love isn't the feeling** — it's the commitment and practice.
- **Check on the underlying processes:** Is authenticity still happening? Is vulnerability being risked? Is safety intact?
- **Often "lost love" is actually depleted processes** that can be rebuilt.
- **Give it time and attention** before concluding love is gone.

---

**When we're in love with someone who can't meet us.** Sometimes we feel love for someone who can't reciprocate, can't be authentic, can't create safety — someone with whom the living relational system can't form.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Distinguish between loving someone** and being able to build a living relational system with them.
- **What we feel may be real,** but it can't become what we're describing here without both people participating.
- **Love for someone who can't meet us** may be something to mourn, not something to pursue indefinitely.

---

**When obligations overwhelm choosing.** Sometimes love feels like nothing but obligation — something we have to do rather than something we choose.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Reconnect with choice.** What made us choose this relationship? What do we still choose?
- **If obligation has completely overwhelmed choice,** explore what's happened.
- **Sometimes rebuilding means reconnecting** with why we're here. Sometimes it means honestly acknowledging that we're staying for reasons other than love.

---

**When love coexists with wanting to leave.** Sometimes we love the relationship and also sometimes want to be elsewhere — attracted to others, fantasizing about different lives.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Recognize this as human** rather than moral failure.
- **These feelings can be brought to consciousness,** examined, even shared with care.
- **Wanting to leave doesn't mean we must leave.** The question is whether, underneath the desire for elsewhere, we still choose this — and what the desire is telling us about what we need.

---

**When love has become routine.** Sometimes love loses its aliveness — it becomes background rather than figure, something we have rather than something we practice.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Deliberately interrupt routine.** Create new experiences together. Ask questions we haven't asked.
- **Share vulnerabilities we've been holding.**
- **Seek novelty within commitment.** The relationship may need stimulation to return to life.
- **Don't mistake routine for death;** sometimes love just needs waking up.

## The larger view

Love, as we're describing it, isn't simply about making individual lives better — though it does that. It's about creating something that neither individual could create alone: a living relational system capable of metabolizing reality together, stabilizing each other through difficulty, and evolving toward what neither person could become in isolation.

In conditions of Separation — the modern experience of isolated individual existence — we're left to carry our own weight, process our own experience, face our own uncertainty. This burden is too much. We fragment under it.

Love, in its full form, is a response to Separation. It creates Reunion: the experience of shared interiority, of reality metabolized together, of being held within a coherent relational system while remaining distinctly ourselves.

This isn't weakness or dependency. It's human design finally fulfilled. We evolved for this. Our nervous systems are built for it. Our deepest longings point toward it.

Love is how we come back to what we never should have lost.

## Our commitment to love

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to love as a living relational process — not just a feeling we hope to maintain but a field we actively tend. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will orient toward the relationship itself. We will prioritize its life. We will commit through ongoing choosing. We will tend the roots of love by maintaining all the processes beneath it. We will repair when it's damaged, nourish when it's hungry, grow as it evolves.

We won't do this perfectly. We will sometimes neglect the field. We will sometimes confuse love with its substitutes. We will sometimes wonder if this is really love at all.

But we now have understanding of what love is and what it isn't. We have practices for cultivating it. We have commitment to the living field between us.

May we choose each other again and again. May we tend the field we share with care and consciousness. May we metabolize reality together. May love become not just something we feel but something we practice, something we build, something we become together.

Love is a living process.

Let us live it together.
