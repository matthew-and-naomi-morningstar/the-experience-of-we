# Resting Together: *A Shared Practice for Stillness and Ease*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Not all togetherness is active. Some of the deepest connection happens when we're doing nothing at all.

We live in a world that celebrates doing. We bond over activities. We connect through conversation. We fill our time together with plans and projects and processing. All of this is valuable. But there's another way of being together that we often overlook: resting.

Resting together is presence without performance. It's being in the same space, not talking, not doing — just existing alongside each other. It's the companionable silence that needs no filling. It's stillness shared.

There's a particular intimacy in being able to rest with someone. It means we don't have to entertain them. We don't have to be interesting or productive or engaged. We can simply be — and they can simply be — and that's enough. This acceptance of each other's bare existence is a form of love.

When we rest together, we replenish. We down-regulate our systems. We step out of the stream of doing and remember that we aren't only what we produce or perform. We are, and we are together, and that is sufficient.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity for stillness, silence, and ease.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or taking turns
- **Actually resting** — not just reading about it
- **Returning to this guide** when busyness has taken over
- **Building shared vocabulary** for inviting rest

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what resting together feels like, the quality of companionable stillness, and what it creates between us.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: what shared rest is, why it matters, and what blocks it.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the concrete ways we create and protect space for resting together.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Resting together is an experience

*Resting together is what it feels like to be in shared stillness — the ease, companionship, and quiet contentment that arise when we stop doing and simply exist alongside each other.*

### When we rest together

There's a distinctive quality to shared stillness:

- **Ease.** Nothing is required. We can let go.
- **Companionship.** We're not alone, but we're also not performing or engaging.
- **Quiet.** Silence is comfortable, not awkward.
- **Softness.** The body relaxes. The mind settles.
- **Presence.** We're here with each other, simply being.
- **Contentment.** This is enough. We don't need anything to happen.

In resting together, the usual pressures lift. We're not trying to connect — we're already connected. We're not doing relationship — we're just in it.

### The quality of companionable silence

Silence between people can feel many ways. In resting together:

- It's not awkward — it's peaceful.
- It's not empty — it's full of presence.
- It's not lonely — we're together in it.
- It's not waiting — nothing needs to come next.

This is different from tense silence, or distracted silence, or avoiding silence. This is chosen stillness, mutual ease.

### When rest is absent

Without shared rest:

- **Exhaustion.** We're always doing, never replenishing.
- **Performance.** We feel we must be interesting, engaging, on.
- **Depletion.** The relationship takes energy but doesn't restore it.
- **Disconnection.** We're active together but not still together.
- **No settling.** Our nervous systems don't fully down-regulate in each other's presence.

The absence of rest can make even a good relationship feel tiring.

### The depth of stillness

There are different depths of rest:

- **Surface rest:** We're sitting together, not doing anything particular, but somewhat distracted.
- **Quiet together:** We've settled. Silence is intentional and comfortable.
- **Deep stillness:** We're profoundly at ease. Time slows. Presence deepens.

All levels are valuable. The deepest levels become possible when we practice and trust the practice.

**A reflection for us:** When have we experienced genuine shared rest — not parallel distraction, but real stillness together? What was the quality of that experience?

## Resting together is a process

*Shared rest works through stillness, silence, and presence — creating the conditions for replenishment, intimacy, and regulation that activity alone can't provide.*

### What resting together is

Resting together is being in shared presence without activity, conversation, or demands. It includes:

**Stillness.** Not moving (much). Letting the body settle.

**Silence.** Not talking (much). Letting words fall away.

**Ease.** Not efforting. Not trying to produce anything.

**Togetherness.** Not alone. Sharing the space and silence.

**Presence.** Here, now, aware — just not doing.

### Why shared rest matters

Resting together serves essential functions:

**Replenishment.** Activity depletes. Rest restores. Shared rest restores the relationship.

**Intimacy.** Being able to be still with someone indicates deep trust. We don't have to fill the space.

**Regulation.** Resting together can down-regulate our nervous systems, especially if we're co-regulating.

**Balance.** Relationships need activity and rest, like breathing needs inhalation and exhalation.

**Sustainability.** A relationship that never rests burns out. Rest makes the active times possible.

**Appreciation of being.** Rest reminds us that we value each other's existence, not just what we do together.

### What blocks shared rest

Many forces work against stillness:

**Busyness culture.** We're taught that doing is valuable and not-doing is lazy.

**Discomfort with silence.** Silence can feel awkward until we learn to be in it.

**Performance pressure.** We feel we should be entertaining or engaging.

**Guilt.** Resting can feel indulgent when there's so much to do.

**Different rest needs.** One person may want more rest than the other.

**Screens.** Devices fill the space that could be empty.

### Rest versus parallel distraction

Resting together isn't the same as:

- Both being on our phones
- Both watching TV without connection
- Being in the same room but entirely separate

These can be fine, but they're not shared rest. Shared rest involves presence — we're aware of each other even in stillness. We're not just parallel; we're together.

**A reflection for us:** What makes it hardest for us to rest together? Is it guilt, restlessness, the pull of devices, or something else?

## Resting together is a practice

*These are our best practices for cultivating shared rest. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.*

### We create space for stillness

Making room for shared rest.

- **We build rest time into our time together** — not as afterthought, but as intention.
- **We clear distractions:** phones away, screens off, environment settled.
- **We choose comfortable positions:** couch, bed, floor, chairs facing each other or side by side.
- **We signal the shift:** "Let's just be together for a while."
- **We protect the time from interruption.**
- **We treat rest as valuable,** not as the absence of value.

*When to use: When we want to rest together. When busyness has dominated. When we need to replenish.*

---

### We practice comfortable silence

Learning to be quiet together without awkwardness.

- **We intentionally stop talking** and settle into silence.
- **We let the silence be** — not rushing to fill it.
- **We notice if discomfort arises,** and we stay anyway.
- **We discover that silence can be full,** not empty.
- **We make small sounds when they're natural** — sighs, shifts — without needing to narrate.
- **We emerge from silence gently,** when it's time.

*When to use: When we want to deepen our capacity for stillness together. When silence has been uncomfortable. When we want to build the muscle of quiet.*

---

### We rest without guilt

Letting go of the pressure to be productive.

- **We give ourselves permission:** rest isn't laziness, it's necessity.
- **We don't apologize for resting** or justify it.
- **We remind each other:** "We're allowed to just be."
- **We notice the productivity guilt** when it arises, and we choose rest anyway.
- **We trust that resting now** enables doing later.
- **We value our being** as much as our doing.

*When to use: When guilt threatens to interrupt rest. When we need to reclaim rest as legitimate. When productivity pressure is high.*

---

### We rest our bodies together

Bringing physical relaxation into shared stillness.

- **We choose positions that allow our bodies to relax:** lying down, leaning on each other, supported by cushions.
- **We let go of tension** — consciously releasing holding patterns.
- **We might touch lightly** — holding hands, feet touching, simple physical contact.
- **We breathe together,** letting breath slow and deepen naturally.
- **We let our bodies rest,** not just our minds.
- **We notice how the body softens** when we truly rest.

*When to use: When we want embodied rest. When we're physically tired. When the body needs to let go.*

---

### We rest after intensity

Using stillness to recover together.

- **After difficult conversations,** we rest together — letting the intensity settle.
- **After busy periods,** we rest together — replenishing what was spent.
- **After conflict,** we might rest side by side — rebuilding safety in stillness.
- **After excitement,** we rest — letting the system come back to baseline.
- **We recognize rest as part of the rhythm** — not separate from life but integrated into it.
- **We use rest deliberately,** not just when we collapse.

*When to use: After processing. After busyness. After conflict. After excitement. As part of the natural rhythm.*

---

### We appreciate bare presence

Valuing existence beyond doing.

- **We take moments to simply appreciate** that the other person is here.
- **We let go of any agenda** — nothing to accomplish, nothing to discuss.
- **We value them for being,** not for what they provide.
- **We receive the same appreciation** — being valued for existence itself.
- **We let rest be an expression of this valuing:** we don't need you to perform, just to be.
- **We rest in the simple fact** of being together.

*When to use: When we want to deepen appreciation. When the relationship has become too transactional. When we want to honor bare existence.*

---

### We protect rest as sacred

Treating stillness as essential, not optional.

- **We prioritize rest** as we prioritize other important things.
- **We guard against busyness** encroaching on rest time.
- **We say no to other things** to preserve space for stillness.
- **We recognize that rest isn't the absence of relationship** but a form of it.
- **We build rest into our shared life** as a non-negotiable.
- **We return to rest regularly,** not just occasionally.

*When to use: When rest keeps getting pushed aside. When we want to establish rest as priority. When stillness has become too rare.*

## Working with challenges

**When we have different rest needs.** One of us wants more stillness than the other.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Negotiate.** Find a level that works for both.
- **The one who needs less can give the gift of stillness;** the one who needs more can appreciate active time.
- **We don't have to be identical.** Taking turns works: sometimes we rest together, sometimes one rests alone.
- **The key is that shared rest happens enough** to serve the relationship.

---

**When silence feels awkward.** We can't quite settle into quiet together.

**What we've found helps:**

- **This is common at first.** Practice in small doses — start with a few minutes.
- **Let the awkwardness be there;** it usually passes.
- **Don't try to force comfort;** just stay with it.
- **Over time, silence becomes natural.** If it stays uncomfortable, explore what's underneath: is there tension we're avoiding?

---

**When we feel guilty about resting.** We can't shake the feeling we should be doing something.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Name the guilt.** Recognize where it comes from (culture, upbringing, belief systems).
- **Consciously choose rest anyway.**
- **Remind each other:** "We're allowed to rest."
- **Practice until the guilt softens.** Consider that rest is productive — it enables future productivity.
- **Reframe:** this is care for ourselves and our relationship.

---

**When rest becomes avoidance.** We're using stillness to avoid things we should address.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Be honest about what's happening.** Rest should feel like rest, not escape.
- **If there's something we're avoiding,** we should address it — then rest.
- **True rest comes after taking care of** what needs care.
- **Check in:** "Are we resting, or are we avoiding?"

---

**When rest is interrupted.** Life keeps breaking into our stillness.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Protect the space more deliberately.** Phones off. Door closed. Time bounded.
- **Communicate to others** that we're unavailable.
- **If interruptions are truly unavoidable,** work with what's possible — even five minutes of genuine stillness is valuable.
- **Build rest into times** when interruption is less likely.

## The larger view

In a world of constant stimulation and endless doing, the ability to rest together is countercultural — and essential.

Rest isn't the absence of living. It's part of living fully. We can't be always on, always engaged, always productive. We need the rhythm of activity and stillness, doing and being. When we share rest with someone, we include them in the whole of our life — not just the active parts.

There's something profound about being able to be still with another person. It says: we don't need to fill the space. We don't need to perform. We don't need to prove our value. We can simply exist together, and that's enough. This mutual acceptance of bare presence is a deep form of love.

Rest is also where we integrate. In stillness, what we've experienced can settle. What we've learned can consolidate. What we've been through can be absorbed. Shared rest is shared integration — we metabolize our life together.

May we learn to be still with each other. May we rest in the simple fact of shared presence. May we find in stillness the peace that activity can't provide.

Sometimes the best thing to do is nothing. Let us do nothing — together.
