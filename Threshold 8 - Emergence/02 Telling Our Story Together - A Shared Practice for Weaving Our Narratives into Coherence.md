# Telling Our Story Together: *A Shared Practice for Weaving Our Narratives into Coherence*

---

Hello, dear companions.

We are always telling stories.

Not just around campfires or dinner tables — constantly, silently, inside our own minds. We narrate our days. We construct explanations for why things happen. We build narratives about who we are, what we're like, what we value. We tell ourselves stories about the people in our lives — what they're really like, what they mean when they say things. And we tell ourselves stories about our relationships — how they began, what defines them, where they're heading.

These stories aren't decorations laid over a more fundamental reality. They are the lens through which we experience it. The story we tell about an event shapes how we feel about it. The story we tell about a person shapes how we treat them. The story we tell about our relationship shapes what we notice, what we expect, and how we respond.

When our stories are in sync — when we're telling compatible, mutually informed narratives about ourselves, each other, and our connection — something clicks into alignment. Communication flows more easily. We feel understood. We have common ground to stand on.

When our stories fall out of sync — when we're carrying significantly different narratives about the same experiences — the disconnection may be invisible at first, but it's real. We begin to talk past each other. We feel unseen. We're baffled by reactions that don't make sense from within our own narrative.

This is the practice: telling our stories to each other, examining them together, co-creating a shared narrative of our relationship, and keeping all of these stories flexible and current as we grow and change. When we align our stories, we align our worlds.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to tell, examine, and align the stories they carry about themselves, each other, and their relationship.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go**, trying the exercises as they arise naturally
- **Returning regularly**, because storytelling is not something we master once but something we keep deepening

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it feels like when our stories are in sync versus out of sync, and the particular quality of being understood through shared narrative.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how stories form, how they shape perception, how they diverge, and how they can be woven back together.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate shared storytelling: the concrete ways we share, examine, update, and co-create our narratives.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Telling our story is an experience

*Telling our story together is what it feels like when our narratives align — when we're carrying compatible, mutually informed stories about ourselves, each other, and what we share.*

### When our stories are in sync

There's a particular quality to being in narrative alignment:

- **Being understood.** We say something and it lands. The other person gets it because they're working from the same basic narrative framework.
- **Common ground.** We have a shared version of reality to stand on. Disagreements happen within a shared frame, not across completely different frames.
- **Coherent memory.** Our shared past makes sense to both of us. We remember things compatibly, even if not identically.
- **Predictability.** We can anticipate how the other will interpret things because we know their story.
- **Ease.** Less needs to be explained. The relationship can move through the world without constant narrative negotiation.

### When our stories are out of sync

Narrative misalignment has its own felt quality:

- **Talking past each other.** Words leave our mouths but don't arrive. The other person hears something different from what we meant.
- **Being unseen.** The other person relates to their version of us rather than to who we actually are.
- **Confusion.** Their reactions don't make sense. Their interpretations seem to come from nowhere.
- **Parallel realities.** We're in the same room but living in different versions of the relationship.
- **Exhaustion.** Every conversation requires extra work to bridge the narrative gap.

### The three stories in every relationship

At any given time, our relationship contains at least three active narratives:

**My story.** How I narrate my own experience — my history, my feelings, my needs, my perspective on what's happening between us.

**Your story.** How you narrate your own experience — your history, your feelings, your needs, your perspective.

**Our story.** The shared narrative of the relationship — how we met, what we've been through, what defines us, where we're heading.

All three need attention. If we only work on "our story" without sharing "my story" and "your story," the shared narrative may paper over real differences. If we only attend to individual stories without co-creating a shared one, we have two detailed personal narratives that never meet.

**A reflection for us:** How do we each tell the story of our relationship? Where do our versions align, and where do they diverge?

## Telling our story is a process

*Stories don't just exist — they form, shape perception, diverge, and can be woven back together through recognizable dynamics.*

### We are always storying

Narrative isn't something humans choose to do. It's how we organize experience. Our brains automatically construct sequences, causality, meaning. *This happened, then that happened, because of this, which means that.*

We can't not story. At any given moment, each of us is carrying an active narrative about our lives, ourselves, and our relationship. These narratives aren't static records. They're living constructions — shaped by memory, emotion, current state, and the stories we've told before. They are selective. They emphasize some things and omit others.

### Stories shape perception

Our narratives don't just describe what we see. They determine what we see.

If our story about the relationship is "things are getting better," we'll notice signs of improvement. If our story is "things are falling apart," we'll notice evidence of decline.

This creates a self-reinforcing loop: our story shapes what we notice, what we notice confirms our story, the confirmed story shapes what we notice next. If we're both carrying similar stories, this loop reinforces a shared reality. If we're carrying different stories, we're literally living in different versions of the same relationship.

### How stories diverge

Stories diverge in every relationship. Two different people, with different histories and vantage points, will naturally construct different narratives about the same experiences.

The question isn't whether our stories diverge, but whether we know they have.

**Silent divergence** is dangerous. We assume our stories match. We don't check. Over time, we accumulate increasingly different narratives — and neither of us knows the other is carrying a different version.

**Known divergence** is workable. When we know our stories differ, we have something to work with. We can examine the divergence, understand what produced it, decide together how to hold it.

### What makes stories go out of sync

**We stop telling each other.** Without regular exchange, our stories drift in isolation.

**We tell stories about each other rather than to each other.** We develop narratives about the other person's motivations and share these with everyone except them. The stories calcify because they're never tested.

**One story dominates.** Power dynamics influence whose narrative becomes the "official" version. The other person's story goes underground.

**We resist updates.** One of us grows or reinterprets something, but the other clings to the original version.

**We confuse our story with the person.** We forget that our narrative about the other is a narrative. We stop checking it against their actual experience.

### Narrative flexibility

The most important quality our stories can have is flexibility — the capacity to be updated, revised, and expanded as we learn and grow.

Flexible stories can accommodate new information. When we discover something that changes our understanding, a flexible story can incorporate it.

Rigid stories can't. They resist revision. They hold their shape even when reality has changed. A rigid story filters out contradicting evidence and accumulates only confirmation.

Rigidity often comes from fear — that updating will be destabilizing, that acknowledging we were wrong will undermine our sense of reality. But the cost of rigid stories is higher: they lock us into outdated versions of each other, making growth impossible.

**A reflection for us:** Are there chapters of our story that we tell differently — and what would it mean to weave those different versions together?

## Telling our story is a practice

These are our best practices for cultivating shared storytelling. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We share our stories with each other

The foundation of all synchronized storytelling.

- **We set aside time for storytelling** — not conversation squeezed into gaps but dedicated space where our stories can unfold.
- **One person tells while the other receives.** When it's our turn to tell, we share something significant — a formative experience, a turning point, something that shaped who we are.
- **We tell it as it lives in us now** — with feelings and significance, not just facts.
- **The listener receives without correcting or comparing.** Not the time to offer alternative interpretations or relate it to our own experience. Time to receive.
- **The listener reflects what they heard.** "I heard how much that meant to you." "I can feel how that shaped your sense of trust."
- **We switch roles.**

This practice can be revisited endlessly. We have lifetimes of stories. The more we share, the richer our understanding becomes.

*When to use: Regularly, as ongoing practice. When we want to know each other more deeply. When our individual narratives have been developing in isolation.*

---

### We examine each other's stories together

Looking more deeply with curiosity and care.

- **We choose a story** that was recently shared.
- **The listener asks questions from genuine curiosity:** "What was it like to be in that moment?" "How do you understand that experience now?" "What did that teach you about yourself?" "How does that story connect to who you are now?"
- **We look for themes and patterns together.** Does this story connect to others? Does it reveal a recurring theme?
- **We offer reflections gently** — as possibilities, not pronouncements. The teller is the authority on their own story.
- **We let new understanding emerge.** Sometimes examining a story together reveals something the teller hadn't seen.

*When to use: After stories have been shared. When we want to deepen understanding beyond the surface. When we want to help each other understand our own lives.*

---

### We tell the story of us

Every relationship has a story — often never explicitly articulated. This practice makes it visible.

- **We begin at the beginning.** Together, tell the story of how the relationship started. Each person contributes their version.
- **We move through the chapters.** What have been the significant periods? The turning points?
- **We name the defining experiences** — joyful and difficult. Our story isn't just the good parts.
- **We articulate what defines us.** If someone asked, "What kind of relationship is this?" — what would we say together?
- **We notice where the story diverges.** These moments aren't problems — they're the most interesting parts, showing where our experiences differ.
- **We hold the story lightly.** This is a draft, not a final version. Our story is alive.

*When to use: When we've never explicitly co-created our shared narrative. When we want to strengthen our sense of "us." When the relationship is at a transition point and needs re-storying.*

---

### We update our narratives together

Stories need updating. We grow. We see old events in new light.

- **We name the story that needs updating** — about ourselves, about the other, or about the relationship.
- **We tell the old version first.** Make it explicit. Sometimes just saying it reveals how outdated it has become.
- **We share what prompted the update.** What experience, realization, or new information is asking the story to change?
- **We offer the new version** — tentatively, as proposal rather than decree.
- **The other person responds.** Does it make sense? Does it align with their experience? Do they have adjustments?
- **We grieve the old story if needed.** Letting go of a familiar narrative deserves acknowledgment.
- **We practice holding the new version.** Updated stories are fragile. The old one will reassert, especially under stress.

*When to use: When we've outgrown a story we've been carrying. When reality has changed and our narrative hasn't. When we sense a story is no longer serving us.*

---

### We work with narrative conflict

Sometimes we discover we're carrying fundamentally different stories about the same experience.

- **We name the divergence without assigning blame.** "We have different stories about this." Not: "Your version is wrong."
- **Each person tells their version fully.** Without interruption, without correction.
- **We look for what's true in both.** Most conflicts aren't cases where one person is right. They're cases where two people experienced different dimensions of the same event.
- **We acknowledge the difference without forcing resolution.** Some conflicts can be resolved; some can't. Sometimes the most honest thing is: "We experienced this differently, and we can hold both versions."
- **We notice what the conflict reveals.** Narrative divergences show us different sensitivities, priorities, wounds. That information is valuable.

*When to use: When we discover significant narrative divergence. When we're confused about how we could remember things so differently. When we need to honor both experiences without requiring one to be wrong.*

---

### We listen for whose story dominates

In every relationship, there are patterns around whose narrative gets more weight.

- **We reflect individually first.** Whose story tends to become the "official" version? Whose interpretation usually wins?
- **We share what we noticed** — vulnerably, without attack.
- **We look at the mechanisms.** Volume? Confidence? Emotional intensity? One person habitually deferring?
- **We ask the marginalized story to speak.** Deliberately reverse the pattern. The quieter person shares their version; the louder person practices receiving with equal weight.
- **We make a shared commitment.** One thing we'll try differently.

*When to use: When one person feels consistently unheard. When we want to examine power dynamics in our storytelling. Periodically, to keep both voices present.*

---

### We tell our story through creative expression

Not all stories are best told in words. Some live in image, sound, movement.

- **We choose a medium** — music, visual art, writing, collage, movement. Unfamiliarity can be an advantage; it bypasses rehearsed narratives.
- **We choose a theme or prompt.** "Express something about our relationship you've never put into words." "Create something that represents what this year has felt like."
- **We create separately, then share.**
- **We receive before interpreting.** Let it affect us before analyzing it.
- **We explore together.** The creator explains; the receiver shares what they noticed.
- **We notice what emerged that words couldn't carry.**

*When to use: When verbal storytelling feels stuck. When we want to access layers that conversation doesn't reach. When we want to widen the channels our stories travel through.*

---

### We keep our stories current

Synchronized storytelling as ongoing orientation rather than occasional practice.

- **We share regularly** — not just dramatic stories but small, daily narratives.
- **We notice when we're constructing a narrative about the other** and check it against their experience rather than treating it as fact.
- **We hold all our stories loosely enough** to update when reality asks us to.
- **We pay attention to whose story is getting more airtime** and correct the balance.
- **We ask:** "Is the story I'm carrying still accurate? Does your version match mine?"

*When to use: Continuously, as relational hygiene. As ongoing orientation to narrative awareness. When we want to prevent silent divergence.*

## Working with challenges

**When sharing feels too vulnerable.** Some stories carry shame or pain that makes them difficult to tell.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Start small.** Share the edges of a story — "There's something I want to tell you but I'm not sure I'm ready."
- **Let the other person's response tell us** whether the space is safe enough.
- **The listener can help:** "I'm here. Share as much or as little as feels right."

---

**When examining feels like criticism.** The line between curious exploration and critique can be thin.

**What we've found helps:**

- **The teller names their experience:** "That question felt like criticism."
- **The examiner adjusts.** The key is intention — are we exploring to understand, or probing to correct?
- **If the teller consistently feels critiqued,** the examiner needs to slow down and check in more.

---

**When our stories about each other feel fixed.** When narrative has calcified — "You always..." "That's just how you are."

**What we've found helps:**

- **Acknowledge the fixity directly:** "I'm carrying a rigid story about you. I want to loosen it."
- **Look for evidence that contradicts** the fixed narrative.
- **The other person can name the story they feel trapped in:** "It feels like you see me as someone who can't handle emotions. I'd like to be seen differently."

---

**When one person's story consistently dominates.** When one person's experience has become invisible, their story marginalized.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Deliberate, sustained rebalancing** — not one conversation but ongoing commitment.
- **The dominant voice practices restraint.**
- **The marginalized voice practices assertion,** trusting that this time the story will be received.
- **This is slow work.**

---

**When we discover a major narrative divergence.** When we've been carrying profoundly different stories about something central.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Let the disorientation be present.**
- **Work through it carefully** using the narrative conflict practice.
- **Remember that major divergences usually didn't form through malice** — they formed through honestly experiencing the same thing differently.
- **The goal is to understand** how two people could have been living in such different stories — and to build a more complete shared narrative.

## The larger view

Our story is never finished. It's being written right now — in this conversation, in this silence, in this moment of reading together.

The story of us includes everything that came before, but it's not determined by it. Old chapters can be reread with new understanding. Recurring themes can be redirected. The story we've been telling doesn't have to be the story we continue to tell.

This is the deepest power of telling our story together: it reminds us that we are co-authors, not characters. We're not passive participants in a narrative beyond our control. We are actively constructing our stories — and we can construct them together, with increasing intentionality, flexibility, and care.

---

## Our commitment to telling our story

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to keep our stories alive, in dialogue, and in relationship with each other. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will share our stories with each other rather than letting them develop in isolation. We will examine each other's narratives with curiosity rather than judgment. We will co-create and regularly update the story of us. We will hold all our stories loosely enough to revise and firmly enough to stand on.

We won't always do this well. We will sometimes carry stories about each other that we forget to check. We will sometimes let one voice dominate. We will sometimes resist updates that truth requires.

But we now have practices that can bring us back into alignment. We know how to share, examine, co-create, update, and work with conflict. We know that stories shape perception and that perception shapes reality.

May we tell our stories to each other with honesty and vulnerability. May we listen to each other's stories as though hearing them for the first time. May we hold the story of us with enough flexibility to grow and enough coherence to orient by.

We are always storying.

Let us story together.
