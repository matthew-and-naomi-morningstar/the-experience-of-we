# Developing Shared Values Together: *A Shared Practice for Building a Compass Between Us*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Every relationship faces moments where two legitimate perspectives point in different directions.

We've both listened well. We've both shared honestly. We understand each other's positions. And yet — the path forward isn't clear. Not because one of us is wrong, but because we're seeing from different places, prioritizing different things, drawn by different currents.

In these moments, understanding alone can't resolve the tension. What we need is something we've agreed to orient toward — a shared sense of what matters most, deeper than either individual perspective. Not a rule book. Not a compromise. A compass.

That compass is our shared values: the things we've deliberately chosen to hold as most important in our life together. Not the values we inherited, not the values we assume the other shares, but the values we've named together, negotiated together, and committed to honoring — especially when honoring them is hard.

This guide is about building that compass. Not finding it — building it. Because shared values aren't discovered ready-made. They're co-created through honest conversation about what each of us holds dear, where those commitments align, where they diverge, and what we choose — together — to place at the center.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to name, align, and live by a set of values that belong to the relationship itself.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go**, especially the values conversations — they work immediately and may surprise us
- **Returning regularly**, because shared values aren't static; they evolve as we do

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it feels like to be aligned in values, and what it feels like when our values clash.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how values form, how shared values are co-created, and what happens when values conflict.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to develop shared values: the specific ways we discover, negotiate, and live by a shared compass.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Shared values are an experience

*Shared values are what it feels like when we know — in our bodies, not just our minds — that we're oriented toward the same things.*

### When we're aligned in values

Value alignment isn't abstract. It has a felt quality:

- **Decisions become easier.** When we're oriented toward the same things, we spend less energy arguing about direction and more energy moving together. There's a natural convergence in how we weigh options.
- **Conflict has a floor.** We may disagree about how to get somewhere, but we agree about where we're going. That shared orientation gives even heated disagreements a container — we know we're on the same team.
- **We feel understood at a level deeper than opinions.** Knowing that someone shares our values — not just our preferences or our views, but the things we hold as most important — creates a particular kind of intimacy. We're known in our foundations.
- **There's a sense of coherence.** The relationship has a direction that both people can feel. We're not just two people who happen to be together; we're two people building something together, oriented by what we both care about.
- **Trust deepens.** When we know what each other holds as sacred, we can predict — not perfectly, but roughly — how the other person will navigate difficult moments. That predictability is a form of safety.

### When values are misaligned

Value misalignment has its own signature:

- **Decisions become battles.** Without a shared compass, every choice becomes a negotiation from scratch. Neither of us has a reference point the other will accept.
- **Conflict has no resolution.** We argue, compromise, revisit, and argue again — because the disagreement isn't about this specific issue but about fundamentally different orientations. The same fight keeps returning in different clothing.
- **We feel lonely in our convictions.** Holding a value that our partner doesn't share — or doesn't understand — creates a particular kind of isolation. We're alone in what matters most to us.
- **Resentment builds quietly.** When we repeatedly act against our values to accommodate our partner — or when they do the same — resentment accumulates. Neither person is living according to their compass, and both feel the cost.
- **The relationship feels directionless.** Without shared values, we can be deeply connected emotionally but still feel like we're drifting. Connection without direction can feel hollow over time.

### The difference between assumed and developed values

Many relationships operate on assumed shared values — values we've never discussed but believe we agree on. This assumption is one of the most common sources of relational shock:

- **We assumed honesty meant the same thing.** For one of us, honesty means full disclosure. For the other, it means not lying — but some things are private. We discover the gap only in crisis.
- **We assumed we agreed about loyalty.** For one, loyalty means prioritizing the relationship above all else. For the other, loyalty means being honest even when the truth is uncomfortable. These definitions can collide.
- **We assumed our priorities were the same.** Family, career, freedom, security, growth, stability — we may have assumed alignment without ever checking. The differences surface when a real choice has to be made.

Developed values are different. They've been spoken aloud, examined, negotiated, and agreed upon. They may still need updating — they will — but they've been made explicit. The relationship knows what it's oriented toward because both people have said so.

**A reflection for us:** Have we ever been surprised to discover we held different values about something important? What happened?

## Shared values are a process

*Shared values don't arrive fully formed. They emerge through a specific process of discovery, negotiation, and ongoing refinement.*

### Where values come from

Before we can develop shared values, it helps to understand where our individual values originate:

**Family of origin.** Many of our deepest values were absorbed from our families — not taught explicitly, but demonstrated through what was rewarded, punished, modeled, and ignored. Some of these values serve us well. Others may be operating without our conscious endorsement.

**Culture and community.** The wider culture we grew up in shapes what we hold as important: individualism or collectivism, achievement or contentment, self-expression or conformity. These values often feel like "just how things are" rather than choices — which makes them harder to examine.

**Formative experiences.** Specific events shape values powerfully. A betrayal makes loyalty central. A period of scarcity makes security paramount. A transformative relationship makes intimacy non-negotiable. Our histories write our priorities.

**Deliberate choice.** Some values are chosen consciously — adopted because we've thought carefully about what matters and decided to orient toward it. These tend to be the most robust because they've been examined rather than inherited.

Most of us carry a mixture: inherited values we've never questioned, experiential values we've never articulated, and chosen values we hold deliberately. Developing shared values begins with making all of these visible — first to ourselves, then to each other.

### How shared values are co-created

Shared values emerge through a specific relational process:

**Discovery.** We learn what each of us actually values — not what we think we should value, but what we genuinely orient toward. This requires honesty and sometimes courage, because our real values may not match our stated ones.

**Examination.** We look at our values together. Where do they align naturally? Where do they diverge? Are there values one of us holds that the other doesn't share? Are there values we assumed we shared but don't?

**Negotiation.** Where our values diverge, we negotiate — not to force agreement, but to find a shared orientation that honors both people. This might mean one of us adopts a value that wasn't previously central. It might mean we find a higher-order value that encompasses both of our individual ones. It might mean we agree to hold certain values individually while sharing others.

**Articulation.** We name our shared values explicitly. Not as a formal contract — as a living declaration. "We value honesty, even when it's uncomfortable." "We value growth over comfort." "We value kindness before being right." These become our compass.

**Testing.** Shared values become real only when they're tested — when following them costs something. A value we hold when it's easy but abandon when it's hard isn't a value. It's a preference. The real values reveal themselves under pressure.

**Revision.** As we grow and change, our values evolve. What was paramount five years ago may have shifted. New experiences reveal new commitments. Developing shared values isn't a one-time exercise — it's an ongoing conversation that the relationship keeps having.

### Why values conflict happens — and what it means

Value conflicts are among the most painful and most important moments in a relationship:

**Not all value conflicts are resolvable.** Some differences in what we hold as most important are genuine and deep. One person values adventure; the other values stability. One person values independence; the other values togetherness. These aren't misunderstandings to be resolved — they're real tensions to be navigated.

**Value conflicts reveal what's at stake.** When values clash, we discover what we're actually willing to fight for. This information is precious — even when the fight is painful. A relationship that never encounters value conflict probably hasn't gone deep enough to find it.

**The goal isn't perfect alignment.** No two people will share every value. The goal is enough shared orientation to navigate together, combined with enough respect for each other's individual values that difference doesn't become a threat.

**How we handle value conflict matters more than whether it occurs.** Do we listen to each other's values with genuine respect? Do we try to understand why the other holds what they hold? Do we negotiate in good faith? Do we find creative ways to honor both? The process of working with value conflict is itself a test of shared values.

**A reflection for us:** If we had to name the three values most central to our relationship, would we name the same three? Have we ever discussed this explicitly?

## Shared values are a practice

These are our best practices for developing and living by shared values. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice discovering our individual values

Before we can build shared values, we need to know what each of us actually holds as most important.

- **We each reflect independently first.** What do I value most? Not what I think I should value — what do I actually organize my life around? We spend time with this question honestly before bringing answers to each other.
- **We look at behavior, not just beliefs.** Our real values are revealed by what we do, not what we say. Where do we spend our time? What do we fight for? What do we sacrifice for? What can't we tolerate? These patterns reveal our operative values.
- **We name inherited vs. chosen values.** "I value achievement — but I think I inherited that from my family. I'm not sure it's really mine." Distinguishing what we've chosen from what we've absorbed is an essential act of self-knowledge.
- **We share what we find.** "Here's what I've discovered about what I actually value." We bring our findings to each other with honesty, even if some of what we find is surprising or uncomfortable.
- **We listen without evaluating.** When our partner shares their values, we receive them as information — not as something to agree or disagree with yet. We want to understand before we negotiate.

*When to use: As a foundational exercise when beginning values work. When we suspect our stated values don't match our operative ones. When life changes prompt a reassessment of what matters.*

---

### We practice mapping where our values meet

Identifying the natural overlaps and the genuine divergences between us.

- **We lay our values side by side.** Literally or figuratively, we compare what each of us has identified. Where do we see the same things? Where do we see different things?
- **We celebrate the overlaps.** Where our values naturally align, we name it: "We both value honesty." "We both value family." "We both hold growth as essential." These natural alignments are the foundation of our shared compass.
- **We get curious about the divergences.** Where our values differ, we don't panic or rush to resolve. We explore: "Tell me why this is so important to you." "What would it cost you to let this go?" Understanding the weight behind the value matters more than the label.
- **We look for hidden alignments.** Sometimes values that seem different are actually pointing toward the same deeper thing. "I value freedom" and "I value security" might both be expressions of wanting to feel safe enough to be ourselves. We look beneath the surface.
- **We name the genuine tensions.** Some divergences are real and irreducible. We name them honestly rather than papering over them: "You value spontaneity and I value planning. That's a genuine difference. Let's figure out how to hold both."

*When to use: After individual values discovery. When we want to understand the values landscape between us. When a conflict reveals a divergence we hadn't mapped.*

---

### We practice negotiating shared values

The active co-creation of a value system that belongs to the relationship — not imposed by either person, but built together.

- **We start with the easy ones.** Where our values naturally align, we claim them explicitly: "This is a shared value. We both hold this." Claiming shared values strengthens the foundation before we address the harder territory.
- **We negotiate the tensions honestly.** Where our values diverge, we enter real negotiation: what can we build that honors both? This might mean finding a higher-order value that encompasses both of our individual ones. "You value freedom; I value security. Our shared value: we create a life that's stable enough to be safe and flexible enough to be alive."
- **We distinguish between shared values and respected individual values.** Not every value needs to be shared. Some values belong to one person, and the other person respects them without adopting them. "Adventure is your value, not mine — but I respect it and I'll make space for it."
- **We check for genuine agreement.** "Are you truly agreeing with this, or are you accommodating?" Shared values must be real. A value one person holds under pressure from the other isn't shared — it's imposed.
- **We articulate our shared values simply.** Once negotiated, we name them in plain language: "We value honesty over comfort." "We value each other's growth, even when it changes things." "We value repair over being right." The simpler the language, the more useful the compass.

*When to use: After mapping our values landscape. When we want to explicitly build a shared compass. When a conflict reveals the need for a shared reference point.*

---

### We practice using values as a compass

Putting shared values to work — using them to navigate decisions, conflicts, and crossroads.

- **Before a decision, we consult the compass.** "What do our shared values say about this?" We explicitly reference our values rather than defaulting to habit, power dynamics, or whoever argues more persuasively.
- **In conflict, we return to shared ground.** "We're disagreeing about how to handle this. What value are we both trying to honor?" Often, naming the shared value beneath the disagreement reveals a path that both of us can accept.
- **We let values break ties.** When two perspectives are both legitimate, we ask: which direction is more aligned with what we've said matters most? The compass doesn't make the decision effortless, but it provides a reference point that both of us have agreed to trust.
- **We notice when we're acting against our values.** "We said we value honesty, but I think we're both avoiding this conversation." We use our shared values as a mirror — not to shame each other, but to notice when we've drifted from our own commitments.
- **We credit the compass when it works.** When shared values guide us through a hard moment, we name it: "We made that decision because we value X. That worked." Positive reinforcement strengthens the values as living guides rather than abstract ideals.

*When to use: During decisions large and small. In conflict, as a shared reference point. When we notice ourselves drifting from what we've said matters most.*

---

### We practice having values conversations

Regular, dedicated conversations about what we value — not waiting for crises to reveal our commitments.

- **We schedule values conversations.** Not every week — but periodically, perhaps quarterly or at natural transition points. "Let's check in on our values."
- **We ask each other open questions.** "What matters most to you right now?" "Has anything shifted in what you hold as important?" "Is there a value we haven't named that's been operating between us?"
- **We discuss values in context.** When something happens — a decision, a conflict, a life change — we ask: "What did this reveal about what we value?" Concrete situations make values conversations grounded rather than abstract.
- **We make space for change.** "I think my values are shifting." This is normal and important. We receive it as information rather than threat.
- **We practice naming values we admire in each other.** "I admire that you value directness." "I love that you hold kindness so central." Naming what we see builds mutual understanding and appreciation.

*When to use: Periodically, as relational maintenance. At life transitions. When we sense that our compass might need recalibration.*

---

### We practice navigating value conflicts

When our values genuinely clash — when there's no easy synthesis — we need specific skills for navigating the tension without destroying the relationship.

- **We acknowledge the conflict as real.** "We hold different values here, and that's creating tension." We don't pretend the tension away or dismiss the other person's value as wrong.
- **We seek to understand before we seek to resolve.** "Help me understand why this matters so deeply to you." We enter the other person's value world and try to see from inside it.
- **We look for creative solutions.** Can we find a way to honor both values, even partially? Can we take turns? Can we find a third option that neither of us has considered?
- **We identify which values are non-negotiable.** Some values can be compromised; others can't — and we need to know which are which. "I can flex on this. But on this other thing, I can't."
- **We stay connected through the disagreement.** Value conflicts can feel existential — like they threaten the foundation. We stay physically and emotionally connected even as we disagree about what matters. The relationship is larger than any single value conflict.
- **We accept imperfect resolution.** Some value conflicts don't resolve cleanly. We learn to live with tension — two people who love each other and hold different things as sacred. This is not failure. This is the complexity of genuine togetherness.

*When to use: When a genuine value conflict surfaces. When the same argument keeps returning (often a sign of an unnamed value conflict). When we need to navigate a difference that understanding alone can't bridge.*

---

### We practice living our values under pressure

Values are tested in the difficult moments, not the easy ones. This practice develops the capacity to hold our shared compass when holding it costs something.

- **We name the cost.** "Following our value of honesty means having this conversation we've been avoiding." "Following our value of growth means supporting this change even though it scares me." We name what the value is asking of us.
- **We support each other in the hard follow-through.** When one of us is struggling to act according to our shared values, the other offers support — not pressure, but companionship. "I know this is hard. I'm here."
- **We notice when pressure makes us abandon our values.** Stress, fear, conflict, and fatigue all push us toward expedient action rather than values-aligned action. We develop awareness of this pattern: "I think I'm about to act against what we've said matters."
- **We repair when we fail.** We will act against our values. Both of us. When it happens, we acknowledge it, understand what pulled us off course, and recommit. The failure isn't the betrayal — the refusal to acknowledge and repair would be.
- **We let hard moments strengthen the compass.** Every time we hold our values under pressure, the compass becomes more trustworthy. We note these moments: "That was hard, and we held to what we said mattered. That means something."

*When to use: When following our values requires sacrifice or discomfort. When external pressure pushes us toward acting against what we've committed to. When we want to build the real-world credibility of our shared compass.*

---

### We practice updating our values

A living relationship needs a living compass. This practice ensures our shared values evolve as we do.

- **We revisit periodically.** "Do these values still represent what matters most to us?" What was central five years ago may have shifted. We check rather than assume continuity.
- **We honor what's changed.** "I used to value achievement above almost everything. Now I value presence more." We receive each other's value evolution with interest rather than alarm.
- **We renegotiate when needed.** If one person's values have shifted significantly, the shared compass may need recalibration. We enter this renegotiation with the same care we brought to the original conversation.
- **We add values that experience has revealed.** "After what we went through, I realize we value resilience — and we never named it before." Life teaches us what we hold dear. We update the compass to reflect what we've learned.
- **We release values that no longer serve.** Some values were appropriate for an earlier phase of the relationship but no longer fit. We let them go with gratitude for what they gave us, rather than clinging out of habit.

*When to use: At major life transitions. When our shared values feel outdated. When one or both of us has changed significantly. Annually, as a minimum rhythm.*

---

### We practice teaching our values to the relational field

Shared values become most powerful when they're woven into the fabric of daily life — not just consulted in crises but lived as an ongoing orientation.

- **We reference our values in small moments.** "I'm telling you this because we value honesty." "I'm supporting this because we value your growth." Naming the value in everyday decisions builds it into the relational culture.
- **We make decisions through the lens of values.** Before we act, we filter: is this aligned with what we've said matters? Over time, this becomes second nature rather than a deliberate step.
- **We tell the story of our values.** "We became people who value repair after that time we almost lost each other." Values have histories. Knowing the story deepens the commitment.
- **We model our values for others.** Our children, friends, and communities see how we orient. The values we live become legible to the people around us — often more powerful as demonstration than as instruction.
- **We let our values shape the culture of the relationship.** Over time, shared values stop being something we consult and become something we inhabit. The relationship itself becomes organized around what we've said matters. This is values work at its most mature — when the compass isn't a tool we pick up but a direction we're already moving.

*When to use: Continuously, as an orientation. When we want to move from consulting our values to embodying them. When we want our shared compass to become part of who we are together.*

## Working with challenges

**When we discover a fundamental value difference.** Not a surface disagreement — a genuine divergence in what we hold as most important. One of us values freedom above security. One of us values family above career. One of us holds a spiritual commitment the other doesn't share.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We don't catastrophize.** Value difference doesn't mean relational death. Many strong relationships hold genuine value tensions.
- **We look for the layer beneath.** Different values sometimes express the same underlying need. We explore what's underneath before concluding the difference is irreconcilable.
- **We find the workable space.** Can we honor both values through creative arrangement? Can we give each other room to live their individual values while maintaining shared ones?
- **We're honest about deal-breakers.** Some value differences are genuinely incompatible. If so, honesty serves both people better than pretending.

---

**When stated values and lived values don't match.** We say we value honesty, but we keep avoiding hard conversations. We say we value equality, but one person's needs consistently take priority.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We look at behavior, not declarations.** Our lived values — what we actually do — are more truthful than our stated values.
- **We name the gap without shaming.** "We say we value this, but I notice we're not living it. Can we look at that together?"
- **We investigate the gap.** Is the stated value aspirational rather than actual? Is something blocking us from living it? Is the lived value actually a better reflection of what we hold?
- **We choose honestly.** We either recommit to the stated value and change our behavior, or we update the stated value to match reality. Both are honest moves. The dishonest move is maintaining the gap.

---

**When one person's values dominate.** In many relationships, one person's values quietly become the shared values — through force of personality, habit, power dynamics, or the other person's tendency to accommodate.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We check for genuine mutuality.** "Are these really our shared values, or are these your values that I've adopted?" This question takes courage from both people.
- **The dominant person practices making space.** "I want to hear what you actually value, not what you think I want to hear."
- **The accommodating person practices speaking.** "Here's what I actually hold as important — and it's different from what we've been operating on."
- **We rebuild from honest ground.** If the shared values have been lopsided, we renegotiate — starting with genuine individual discovery from both people.

---

**When life changes make old values obsolete.** A value that was central when we were young and building may not fit when we're older and settling. A value forged in crisis may not serve in stability. Life changes can render a previously functional compass unreliable.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We notice when the compass feels off.** "Our values used to guide us clearly, but lately they don't seem to fit."
- **We honor what the old values gave us.** They served a purpose. We can release them with gratitude rather than resentment.
- **We enter fresh negotiation.** Who are we now? What matters to us now? We revisit the discovery process with the same openness we brought the first time.
- **We trust the process.** We've built a shared compass before. We can build a new one. The skill transfers even when the content changes.

## The larger view

Shared values are the relational field's immune system and its navigation system in one.

As immune system, they protect the relationship from drift, from the slow erosion of what matters, from the accumulation of decisions that serve convenience rather than conviction. When we act against our values, something in the field registers it — a dissonance that calls us back. Without shared values, this signal doesn't exist. Anything goes, and gradually everything does.

As navigation system, shared values give the relationship direction that both people have chosen. This is profoundly different from direction imposed by one person, by circumstance, or by default. A relationship guided by shared values is a relationship with agency — two people choosing together how they want to move through the world.

The development of shared values is also one of the most intimate acts a relationship can undertake. When we say "this is what matters most to us," we're not just agreeing on priorities. We're co-creating a moral center — a shared sense of what's worth sacrificing for, what's worth fighting for, what's worth being uncomfortable for. This shared center becomes one of the deepest bonds between us, more durable than passion, more resilient than habit.

And like all living things, shared values must be tended. They must be spoken, tested, revised, and recommitted to. A compass that sits in a drawer is not a compass — it's a relic. A compass that's held, consulted, recalibrated, and trusted becomes an extension of the hands that hold it.

---

## Our commitment to shared values

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice building and living by a shared compass. Not perfectly, but deliberately.

We won't always get this right. We'll sometimes act against our stated values under pressure. We'll sometimes discover that our real values differ from what we'd assumed. We'll sometimes struggle to negotiate tensions that feel irresolvable. We'll sometimes let one person's values dominate without realizing it. We are human, and the work of aligning two inner worlds is never finished.

But we now have a shared practice. We know how to discover what we each hold as most important. We know how to map where our values meet and where they diverge. We know how to negotiate a shared compass that both of us can trust. And we know that the compass is a living thing — something we tend, test, and update as we grow.

May we know what we value and speak it clearly. May we listen to what each other values with genuine respect. May we build a compass that guides us through what we can't yet see. May we hold to what matters most, especially when it's hardest.

A relationship that knows what it stands for can stand through anything. Let us build that compass together.
