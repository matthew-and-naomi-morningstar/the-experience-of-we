# Sense-Making Together: *A Shared Practice for Understanding What Is Happening*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Something is happening between us, and we're not sure what it is.

This is one of the most common experiences in relationship. We can feel that something has shifted, that something is off, that something is brewing — but we can't quite name it. Or we have different ideas about what's going on. Or we're each reacting to a situation we haven't actually understood together.

Sense-making is the practice of figuring out *what is happening*. Not what it means, not what we should do about it, not whose fault it is — but simply: what is actually going on here?

This sounds simpler than it is. At any moment, countless things are happening. We each notice different things. We each bring different frames. We can live through the same event and have genuinely different perceptions of what occurred.

Alone, we are limited to what we can see from where we stand. Together, we have access to more — more perspectives, more observations, more ways of noticing. Sense-making together is how we combine what we each perceive into an understanding that neither of us could reach alone.

This guide is about developing that capacity: the practice of pooling our observations, finding patterns together, and arriving at a shared understanding of what is actually happening — in our relationship, in our situation, in our lives.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to understand what is happening between them and around them.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go** — try the sense-making exercises with real situations between you
- **Returning regularly**, because every new situation requires new sense-making

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it actually feels like when we are making sense of something together, and what it feels like when we can't.
- **Process** is how sense-making works: the phases it moves through and the conditions it requires.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to develop this capacity: the specific exercises that help us understand what is happening, together.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Sense-making is an experience

*Sense-making is what it feels like when confusion gives way to clarity — when we move from "what is going on?" to "oh, this is what's happening."*

Successful sense-making feels like orientation. We were lost, and now we have a map. We were confused, and now we understand — not everything, but enough to know where we are.

When sense-making is working between us:

- We feel like we're looking at the same situation, even if we see it differently
- Confusion gives way to clarity — not complete certainty, but enough understanding to orient
- We build on each other's observations rather than talking past each other
- Patterns emerge that neither of us saw alone
- We arrive at a shared understanding of what is happening, which makes everything else easier — deciding what to do, figuring out what it means, knowing how to respond

When sense-making breaks down:

- We feel like we're in different realities, describing different situations
- Confusion persists — we can't get oriented, can't figure out what's going on
- Conversations go in circles without building toward understanding
- We each have a piece but can't put them together
- We react to situations we haven't actually understood, which usually makes things worse

The difference often comes down to whether we're genuinely trying to understand together, or whether we've collapsed sense-making into something else — defending our interpretation, pushing for a particular conclusion, or assuming we already know what's happening.

**A reflection for us:** When have we successfully made sense of something confusing together — arriving at shared understanding from shared bewilderment? What was that like?

## Sense-making is a process

*Sense-making moves through distinct phases — from gathering observations to finding patterns to articulating and testing our understanding.*

### What sense-making is (and isn't)

Sense-making is how we answer the question: *What is happening here?*

This is different from meaning-making, which asks: *What does it mean? Why does it matter?*

And it's different from decision-making, which asks: *What should we do about it?*

**Sense-making** is the prior step — understanding the situation before we interpret or respond to it.

- Sense-making: "We've been more distant with each other for the past few weeks. There's tension when we talk about schedules. You've been working late, and I've been more withdrawn."
- Meaning-making: "This distance might be about the unresolved conversation we never finished."
- Decision-making: "Let's set aside time this weekend to really talk."

When we try to skip sense-making and jump straight to meaning or decision, we often get it wrong — because we're responding to a situation we haven't actually understood.

### Why we need each other for this

Individual sense-making has real limits. We can only notice what we notice. We can only see from where we stand. Our history shapes what patterns we recognize. Our needs orient our attention toward certain signals and away from others.

Together, we have:

- **More perspectives** — you notice things I miss, and I notice things you miss
- **Error correction** — you can catch where my perception is skewed, and I can catch yours
- **Pattern recognition** — patterns visible across our combined observations may be invisible from either view alone
- **Humility** — engaging each other reminds us that our individual understanding is partial

Sense-making together is not just individual sense-making done in proximity. It is a different process — one that integrates multiple perceptions into understanding that no individual could reach.

### The phases of sense-making

Sense-making moves through phases, though not always linearly:

- **Gathering signals** — What do we each notice? What are we observing? What stands out?
- **Processing together** — What patterns emerge across our observations? What connects?
- **Contextualizing** — What larger picture does this fit into? What history or systems are relevant?
- **Articulating** — How would we describe what's happening? Can we put our understanding into words?
- **Testing** — Does this understanding hold up? What doesn't fit? What might we be missing?

These phases often cycle — articulating may reveal gaps that send us back to gathering, testing may require more contextualizing.

### The conditions sense-making needs

Sense-making requires certain conditions to work:

- **Enough safety** — If we're too activated, sense-making collapses into defense or debate
- **Shared intention** — We both need to genuinely want to understand, not to win or be right
- **Perspectives actually shared** — Before we can integrate observations, we need to hear each other's
- **Epistemic humility** — We each need to hold our current understanding lightly enough to revise it
- **Adequate time** — Rushing produces pseudo-understanding; real sense-making has its own pace

### The foundations of sense-making between us

Before we move into practices, here are the qualities that make shared sense-making possible.

**Genuine curiosity about what's happening.** Sense-making requires that we actually want to understand — not confirm what we already think, not prove a point, not get the other person to see it our way. The question "what is happening?" must be genuine.

**Willingness to be surprised.** If we already know what we'll find, we're not sense-making — we're confirming. Real sense-making is open to discovering something unexpected, something that doesn't fit our assumptions.

**Tolerance for uncertainty.** Sense-making often involves a period of not-knowing — gathering observations without yet seeing the pattern, holding pieces that don't yet fit together. We need to tolerate this uncertainty rather than rushing to premature clarity.

**Treating all observations as data.** When we're making sense of something together, both of our observations matter — even when they seem to conflict. "You notice X and I notice Y" is data, not a problem. Both observations are telling us something.

**A reflection for us:** How do we each tend to approach making sense of things? Do our styles complement or compete?

## Sense-making is a practice

These are our best practices for developing shared sense-making capacity. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice gathering signals together

The first phase of sense-making is collecting what we each observe — the raw material that we'll process into understanding.

- **We cast attention broadly.** Before narrowing, we notice widely. What's happening? What's present? What do we each see, feel, sense?

- **We share observations, not conclusions.** "I notice that we've been quieter at dinner" rather than "You've been distant." We describe what we observe, leaving interpretation for later.

- **We include anomalies.** We pay special attention to what surprises us, what doesn't fit, what seems odd. Anomalies often carry important information.

- **We pool our signals.** We make our individual observations available to each other. "Here's what I'm noticing — what are you noticing?"

- **We don't filter for agreement.** If you notice something I don't, that's useful — even if I'm skeptical. At this phase, we're gathering data, not evaluating it.

*When to use: At the beginning of any sense-making conversation. When we're trying to understand a situation together. When something has happened and we want to know what's going on.*

---

### We practice finding patterns together

Once we've gathered observations, we process them together — looking for patterns, connections, and structure.

- **We look for what recurs.** What shows up in multiple observations? What appears across both of our perceptions?

- **We name emerging shapes.** As patterns emerge, we name them provisionally. "It seems like there's a dynamic where..." "I'm noticing a pattern of..." Naming makes patterns available for examination.

- **We build on each other.** "Building on what you said, I also notice..." "That connects to something I've been seeing..." We let insights compound.

- **We hold multiple threads.** Complex situations have multiple patterns. We resist collapsing too quickly into a single story.

- **We test emerging patterns.** As patterns appear, we check them against our signals. Does this pattern account for what we're seeing? What doesn't fit?

*When to use: After gathering signals. When we have observations that haven't yet resolved into understanding.*

---

### We practice contextualizing together

Sense-making requires placing what we observe within larger frames — history, systems, circumstances.

- **We zoom out.** What larger dynamics is this part of? What's been happening over time? How does this fit into the bigger picture?

- **We trace the arc.** How did we get here? What led to what we're observing? Where does this seem to be going?

- **We consider multiple frames.** What does this look like through different lenses? What's the emotional dimension? The practical dimension? The relational history?

- **We locate in time.** Is this new or recurring? Beginning, middle, or end? Building or resolving?

*When to use: When we have observations and emerging patterns but need to understand how they fit into the larger picture.*

---

### We practice articulating understanding

As patterns and context come together, understanding begins to form. This understanding needs to be put into words — so we can examine it, refine it, and share it.

- **We propose formulations.** "Here's how I'd describe what's happening..." "My current sense is..." We offer our emerging understanding explicitly.

- **We use tentative language.** "It seems like..." "What I'm seeing is..." This signals that understanding is still forming — not a verdict.

- **We check for resonance.** "Does this capture what you're seeing?" "Does this feel right to you?" Understanding should resonate across both perspectives.

- **We refine together.** First articulations are rarely adequate. We build toward increasingly accurate formulations — adjusting language, adding nuance, correcting distortions.

- **We hold understanding lightly.** Even well-articulated understanding is provisional. Situations continue to unfold. We stay open to revision.

*When to use: When patterns and context have come together into emerging understanding. When we need to name what we're seeing.*

---

### We practice testing our understanding

Understanding needs to be checked — examined for gaps, tested against reality, validated or revised.

- **We check against our signals.** Does our understanding account for what we observed? What doesn't fit?

- **We seek disconfirmation.** We actively look for evidence against our understanding. What would prove us wrong? What have we possibly missed?

- **We predict and observe.** If our understanding is accurate, what should we see? We watch for confirmation or disconfirmation.

- **We stay provisional.** Even validated understanding is provisional. As the situation evolves, we update our sense of what's happening.

*When to use: After articulating understanding. Periodically, to check if our sense of what's happening is still accurate.*

---

### We practice sense-making about us

Some of the most important sense-making happens when the subject is our relationship itself — understanding what's happening between us.

- **We treat our relationship as something we can observe.** Just as we might observe an external situation, we observe our relational dynamics. "What's happening in our relationship right now?"

- **We share what we each see.** My perception of what's happening between us may differ from yours. Both perceptions are data.

- **We look for patterns in our relating.** What dynamics keep recurring? What shapes do our interactions take? What triggers what?

- **We stay curious rather than defensive.** Sense-making about us can feel personal. We try to approach it with the same curiosity we'd bring to any other domain — genuine interest in understanding, not defending.

- **We distinguish sense-making from blame.** Understanding what's happening is not the same as assigning fault. We're trying to see clearly, not prosecute.

*When to use: When something feels off between us. When patterns are emerging that we want to understand. When we want to step back and see our relationship clearly.*

---

### We practice embodied sense-making

Sometimes cognitive processing reaches its limit and we need to include what our bodies know — the felt sense that informs understanding.

- **We check in with our bodies.** As we gather signals and look for patterns, we notice what's happening somatically. What do our bodies sense about this situation?

- **We include felt sense as data.** "Something about this feels unfinished" or "My gut says there's more here" are legitimate observations. The body often perceives before the mind can articulate.

- **We name the felt quality.** Even when we can't explain it cognitively, we can describe what the situation feels like. Heavy? Stuck? Contracted? Unresolved? These felt qualities are information.

- **We integrate head and body knowing.** We don't privilege felt sense over evidence, or cognition over embodiment. Both contribute to understanding.

*When to use: When cognitive processing has hit a wall. When something doesn't make logical sense but still feels important. When we want to include the full range of knowing.*

---

### We practice updating our sense

Understanding is not achieved once and then held forever. Situations evolve. New information emerges. Sense-making is ongoing.

- **We notice when our understanding stops fitting.** The sense we made last week may not apply to what's happening now. We stay alert to signals that our understanding needs revision.

- **We share new observations.** When we notice something that doesn't fit our current understanding, we share it rather than dismissing it.

- **We revisit explicitly.** Periodically, we check: "Does our understanding of what's happening still seem accurate? Has anything changed?"

- **We update without drama.** Revising our understanding is not a failure of the original sense-making. It's the natural evolution of understanding in response to unfolding reality.

*When to use: Regularly, as ongoing practice. When something no longer fits our understanding. When time has passed since we last made sense of something together.*

## Working with challenges

**When we can't agree on what's happening.** Sometimes our observations differ so significantly that we can't arrive at a shared understanding. We genuinely see different situations.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We resist the urge to resolve too quickly.** Sometimes holding divergent understandings is more honest than forcing false agreement.
- **We map the divergence precisely.** "You see X, I see Y. Let's understand exactly where our perceptions differ."
- **We look for what might explain both.** Is there something happening that would look like X from your position and like Y from mine?
- **We accept that some divergence is irreducible.** Sometimes we see differently, and that's the truth of our situation.

---

**When one of us is attached to a particular understanding.** Sometimes one or both of us has a stake in a particular version of events — because it protects us, or validates us, or supports a conclusion we've already reached.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We notice the attachment.** "I notice I'm attached to seeing it this way. Let me try to hold it more lightly."
- **We get curious about the stake.** What would it mean if our understanding were wrong? What are we protecting?
- **We return to observations.** We ground back in what we each actually noticed, before interpretation.
- **We prioritize understanding over being right.** We remind ourselves that accurate understanding serves us better than comforting understanding.

---

**When we keep going in circles.** Sometimes sense-making conversations loop — we cover the same ground repeatedly without building toward understanding.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We name the loop.** "I notice we keep coming back to the same place. What's keeping us stuck?"
- **We try a different mode.** If talking isn't working, we try writing observations first. Or we include embodied sensing. Or we take a break.
- **We check for hidden disagreement.** Loops often indicate unacknowledged divergence. We surface what might be unspoken.
- **We accept partial understanding.** Sometimes we can only get so far right now. Partial clarity is better than endless circling.

---

**When sense-making keeps getting derailed.** Sometimes we try to understand what's happening and end up somewhere else entirely — arguing, defending, explaining, or going off on tangents.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We notice the derailment.** "I notice we stopped sense-making and started [arguing/defending/etc.]."
- **We return to the question.** "What are we trying to understand? Let's come back to that."
- **We check our regulation.** Derailment often indicates activation. We may need to settle before continuing.
- **We separate sense-making from other processes.** Understanding what's happening is a distinct step. We can do meaning-making, decision-making, or repair after we understand — not instead of.

## The larger view

Sense-making is the foundation that other relational capacities depend on.

Before we can respond to a situation, we need to understand it. Before we can repair a rupture, we need to understand what happened. Before we can make a decision together, we need to understand the landscape we're deciding in.

When sense-making works between us, everything else becomes easier. When it fails, we're responding to situations we haven't actually understood — which usually makes things worse.

Sense-making is not something we complete. It is an ongoing process of orienting to reality as it unfolds. The situation we understood last month may have changed. The patterns we recognized last week may have shifted. The understanding we arrived at yesterday may need updating today. This is not exhausting; it is how we stay connected to what is actually happening rather than navigating by outdated maps. Continuous sense-making keeps us current with our lives.

The capacity to make sense of things together extends far beyond our relationship. Teams that can make sense of their challenges together work more effectively. Communities that can understand their situations together respond more wisely. Families that can see what's happening together navigate life with more coherence. In a world of increasing complexity — where information overwhelms and signals contradict — the capacity to make sense together is increasingly precious.

## Our commitment to sense-making

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice understanding what is happening together. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We won't always do this well. We will jump to conclusions. We will defend our version of events. We will confuse sense-making with arguing. We will sometimes see so differently that shared understanding eludes us.

But we now have a shared practice. We know what sense-making is and what it requires. We know the phases it moves through. We know how to recover when it breaks down. And we know how much easier everything else becomes when we actually understand what we're dealing with.

May we observe before interpreting. May we gather before concluding. May we stay curious about what is actually happening. May we build understanding together that neither of us could reach alone.

Let us understand together.
