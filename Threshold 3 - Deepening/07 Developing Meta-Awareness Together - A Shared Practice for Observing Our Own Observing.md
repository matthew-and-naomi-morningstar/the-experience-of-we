# Developing Meta-Awareness Together: *A Shared Practice for Observing Our Own Observing*

---

Hello, dear companions.

There is awareness. And then there is awareness of awareness.

With mindfulness, we learn to observe our experiences — to notice thoughts, feelings, and sensations without being completely absorbed in them. But we can still be lost in the process of observing itself. We're "doing" awareness without being aware that we're doing it. We're looking, but not seeing that we're looking.

Meta-awareness is the capacity to observe our own observing. Where is my attention right now? What is the quality of my awareness — is it clear or foggy, narrow or wide, tense or relaxed? Have I been present, or did I drift away without noticing? How am I relating to what I'm experiencing?

This is awareness turning back on itself — the mind becoming aware of its own functioning. It's the difference between looking through a window and noticing that you're looking through a window. The view doesn't change, but something fundamental shifts.

In relationship, meta-awareness is what lets us notice our patterns of attention, catch ourselves when we've disconnected, and understand how we're showing up in each moment. It's the capacity that lets us ask: How am I being aware right now? And how is that affecting us?

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to observe their own processes of observation and attention.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or taking turns
- **Practicing the exercises** with patience — this is subtle territory
- **Returning to this guide** as meta-awareness deepens over time
- **Building shared vocabulary** for talking about awareness itself

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what meta-awareness feels like, what its absence feels like, and the quality of awareness becoming aware of itself.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: what meta-awareness is, what it enables, and how it relates to earlier capacities.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the concrete ways we develop the capacity to observe our own observing.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Meta-awareness is an experience

*Meta-awareness is what it feels like when awareness becomes aware of itself — the distinctive quality of observing not just what we experience, but how we experience it.*

### When meta-awareness is present

There's a distinctive quality when awareness becomes aware of itself:

- **Knowing that we know.** We're not just aware — we're aware that we're aware.
- **Seeing the process.** We notice not just what we're experiencing, but how we're experiencing it.
- **Wider perspective.** We're not just in the experience — we can see the whole field, including our own position in it.
- **Self-correcting.** We notice when attention has wandered and can redirect it.
- **Transparency.** The process of awareness itself becomes visible, not just its objects.

With meta-awareness, we might notice: *I'm paying attention to what they're saying, but my attention is tight and narrow — I'm listening for threat. Let me soften and widen.* We're aware of our content (what they're saying), our process (tight, narrow listening), and we can adjust.

### When we're fused with process

Without meta-awareness, we're lost in the process of experiencing without seeing that we're doing it:

- **Unaware of awareness.** We're conscious, but not conscious that we're conscious.
- **Process invisible.** We notice objects of experience but not how we're relating to them.
- **Lost without knowing.** Attention wanders and we don't notice it's wandered until later.
- **Pattern blind.** We have habitual ways of attending but can't see them as patterns.
- **No self-correction.** We can't adjust what we can't see.

This isn't unconsciousness — we're awake and experiencing. But we're fused with the process. We're looking through awareness like looking through a window, never noticing the window itself.

### The quality of awareness

Meta-awareness perceives not just content but the quality of awareness itself:

**Clarity or fog.** Is awareness clear and sharp, or hazy and unclear?

**Width.** Is attention narrow and focused, or wide and panoramic?

**Depth.** Is awareness surface-level or penetrating deeply?

**Stability.** Is attention steady or jumping around?

**Tone.** Is awareness relaxed or tense? Open or contracted? Curious or defensive?

**Presence.** Is awareness fully here, or partially absent, checked out, dissociated?

These qualities shape everything we experience, but we usually don't notice them. Meta-awareness makes them visible.

### The recursive nature

There's something strange about meta-awareness: it's awareness observing awareness. This can feel like:

- A kind of doubling — being both the observer and observing the observer
- Awareness folding back on itself
- A sense of "stepping back" to a more fundamental position
- The discovery that there's always another level of "watching"

This isn't a trick or an infinite regress. It's our mind developing awareness of its own process — which includes the capacity to recognize itself.

**A reflection for us:** When have we noticed ourselves observing our own observing — becoming aware of how we're paying attention, not just what we're paying attention to?

## Meta-awareness is a process

*Meta-awareness develops through building on earlier capacities — awareness, interoception, mindfulness — and turning the observing capacity back on itself, making the process of awareness visible and adjustable.*

### What meta-awareness is

Meta-awareness is awareness of awareness — the capacity to observe our own observing, to notice how we're noticing, to be conscious of consciousness itself.

It includes:

**Awareness of attention.** Where is my attention right now? Where has it been? Where is it drawn?

**Awareness of awareness quality.** How am I aware? Clear, foggy, narrow, wide, tense, relaxed?

**Awareness of process.** How am I processing experience? What am I doing with what I'm noticing?

**Awareness of absence.** Noticing when awareness has lapsed — catching that we weren't present.

**Awareness of patterns.** Recognizing our habitual ways of attending over time.

### The constraint meta-awareness removes

In the awareness development sequence, meta-awareness removes the constraint of **fusion with process**.

- **Presence** removes absence — now we're here, available, in the same moment together.
- **Interoception** removes somatic disconnection — now we sense what's happening inside our bodies.
- **Awareness** removes field unconsciousness — now we notice what's happening between us.
- **Mindfulness** removes automatic identification — now we observe without becoming.
- **Meta-awareness** removes fusion with process — now we observe our own observing.

With mindfulness, we can observe thoughts, feelings, and sensations. But we might be so absorbed in the process of observing that we don't notice the process itself. Meta-awareness brings that process into view.

### How meta-awareness develops

Meta-awareness builds on the earlier capacities:

**First, presence.** We're here — available, landed, in the same moment together.

**Then, interoception.** We can sense what's happening inside our own bodies.

**Then, awareness.** We can sense what's happening between us — the quality of the relational field.

**Then, mindfulness.** We can observe experience without being identified with it.

**Now, meta-awareness.** We turn that observing capacity back on itself — observing the observer.

This isn't necessarily linear — these capacities develop together and reinforce each other. But meta-awareness requires some foundation in the earlier capacities. We need to be able to observe before we can observe our observing.

### Meta-awareness and self-knowledge

Meta-awareness gives us access to a deeper level of self-knowledge. We learn:

- **Our patterns of attention.** What do we habitually notice? What do we habitually miss?
- **Our awareness defaults.** Do we tend toward narrow focus or diffuse attention? Tense or relaxed awareness?
- **Our vulnerability points.** When does awareness collapse? What triggers fusion or dissociation?
- **Our best functioning.** What quality of awareness serves us? When are we clearest?

This knowledge isn't available without meta-awareness. We can't see patterns in attention if we can't observe attention itself.

### Meta-awareness in relationship

In relationship, meta-awareness enables:

**Catching disconnection.** We notice when we've checked out, drifted away, stopped being present — and can return.

**Seeing how we're attending.** Are we listening with open curiosity or defensive scanning? Meta-awareness reveals this.

**Noticing what we're missing.** By seeing our patterns of attention, we can recognize what we habitually overlook.

**Adjusting in real-time.** We can shift how we're attending based on what we notice is needed.

**Sharing the process.** We can tell our companion not just what we're experiencing but how we're experiencing it.

**A reflection for us:** What patterns in our awareness do we tend to miss? What would change if we could see not just what we experience but how we experience?

## Meta-awareness is a practice

*These are our best practices for developing shared meta-awareness. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.*

### We notice where attention is

Developing awareness of attention's location and movement.

- **We pause periodically and ask:** Where is my attention right now?
- **We notice:** Is it on my companion? On my own thoughts? On something else entirely? Nowhere in particular?
- **We track attention's movement:** Where was it a moment ago? Where is it drawn?
- **We share with each other:** "I notice my attention was on my own defensiveness. I'm bringing it back to you."
- **We develop the habit of checking:** *Where am I attending?*
- **We recognize that knowing where attention is** gives us the ability to redirect it.

*When to use: As ongoing practice. During conversations. When we suspect we've disconnected. When we want to be more present.*

---

### We observe the quality of awareness

Noticing not just what we're aware of, but how we're aware.

- **We check in with ourselves:** What is the quality of my awareness right now?
- **We notice:** Is it clear or foggy? Narrow or wide? Tense or relaxed? Open or contracted?
- **We develop vocabulary** for describing awareness quality and share it with each other.
- **We notice how awareness quality affects what we perceive:** When awareness is narrow, what do we miss? When it's tense, how does that distort?
- **We practice adjusting:** If awareness is too narrow, we consciously widen. If too tense, we soften.
- **We share with each other:** "I notice my awareness is really contracted right now — I'm probably missing things."

*When to use: As reflective practice. When something feels off in perception. When we want to optimize how we're showing up.*

---

### We catch ourselves when awareness lapses

Noticing when we've gone unconscious.

- **We practice catching the moment** when we realize attention wandered: *Ah, I was gone.*
- **We don't judge the lapse** — we appreciate the catching. The meta-awareness that notices is the capacity we're developing.
- **We track:** Where did I go? What happened to awareness?
- **We share when we catch ourselves:** "I just realized I wasn't present for the last minute. What did I miss?"
- **We help each other:** if one of us seems absent, we gently inquire: "Are you with me?"
- **We recognize that catching absence is meta-awareness at work** — the fact that we notice we were gone is the skill.

*When to use: Continuously — this is the practice of waking up repeatedly. When we realize we've been absent. When we want to build the catching muscle.*

---

### We observe our patterns of attention

Recognizing habitual ways of attending over time.

- **We reflect together:** What do I habitually pay attention to? What do I tend to miss?
- **We notice our defaults:** Do I scan for threat? Do I focus on tasks over feelings? Do I attend to my own experience more than yours?
- **We explore the patterns:** When did these form? What purpose do they serve?
- **We share what we observe in ourselves:** "I notice I have a pattern of attending to what's wrong rather than what's working."
- **We share what we observe in each other** — gently, with curiosity: "I notice you seem to attend more to my words than to my tone. Does that match your sense?"
- **We consider:** Are these patterns serving us? What might we want to shift?

*When to use: As reflective practice. When we want to understand our attentional habits. When patterns are affecting the relationship.*

---

### We observe how we're processing

Noticing what we do with what we notice.

- **We ask:** What is my mind doing with this experience?
- **We notice:** Am I judging? Analyzing? Comparing? Planning? Defending? Simply receiving?
- **We recognize that processing happens** whether we notice it or not — meta-awareness makes it visible.
- **We share the process with each other:** "I notice I'm already forming a response instead of just listening."
- **We catch ourselves in unhelpful processing** and can choose to shift: "I notice I'm judging this. Let me just observe."
- **We become curious about our processing patterns:** What's my default way of handling experience?

*When to use: During conversations. When our responses seem automatic. When we want to catch ourselves in reactive processing.*

---

### We develop real-time meta-awareness

Maintaining awareness of awareness during interaction.

- **We practice holding dual attention:** attending to our companion AND attending to our own attending.
- **We notice in real-time:** Am I present? Is my attention where I want it? What's the quality of my awareness?
- **We use brief internal check-ins** during conversation: a moment's pause to sense how we're doing.
- **We share when relevant:** "Hold on — I just checked in with myself and I'm noticing I've gotten really narrow. Let me take a breath."
- **We build the capacity for simultaneous awareness:** experiencing AND observing the experience AND observing the observing.
- **We recognize this is subtle** and develops gradually — we're building a new capacity.

*When to use: During important conversations. When we want to stay conscious during interaction. As ongoing practice in relational presence.*

---

### We support each other's meta-awareness

Making awareness of awareness a shared practice.

- **We establish meta-awareness as a shared value:** "We want to be aware of how we're being aware."
- **We check in with each other about awareness:** "Where's your attention right now?" "How's your awareness quality?"
- **We share observations without judgment:** "I notice you seem really focused — almost tunneled. What's happening?"
- **We help each other catch lapses:** "It seems like you left for a moment. Where did you go?"
- **We appreciate each other's meta-awareness:** "I noticed you caught yourself and came back. That was skillful."
- **We recognize that building this capacity together** accelerates what either could do alone.

*When to use: As ongoing relational practice. When we want to support each other's development. When meta-awareness is needed in the moment.*

## Working with challenges

**When meta-awareness feels too abstract.** We understand the concept but can't find it in experience.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Start concrete.** "Where is my attention right now?" is a meta-awareness question with a specific answer.
- **"Did I just notice that my mind wandered?"** is meta-awareness happening.
- **We don't need to grasp the philosophy** — we just need to catch the simple moments of awareness noticing itself.
- **The abstraction becomes real through practice.**

---

**When we get lost in meta-levels.** We try to observe our observing and get confused about what we're doing.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Don't make it complicated.** Meta-awareness is simply noticing what awareness is doing.
- **It's not an infinite regress** — it's a simple check-in: Where's attention? How's awareness? That's enough.
- **If we're getting tangled,** we've probably made it too complex.
- **Return to the simple question** and the immediate noticing.

---

**When meta-awareness disrupts flow.** Observing our observing takes us out of natural engagement.

**What we've found helps:**

- **This is common at first** — like any new skill, it takes foreground attention before becoming background.
- **With practice, meta-awareness becomes light and non-disruptive.** We can be fully engaged AND aware of our engagement.
- **If it's pulling us out,** we can practice in lower-stakes moments until it becomes more fluid.

---

**When we use meta-awareness to avoid engagement.** We observe ourselves rather than being present with our companion.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Meta-awareness should enhance presence, not replace it.** If we're using it to withdraw, we've moved from observation to avoidance.
- **Check:** Am I observing in service of connection, or to escape it?
- **The goal is to be more present,** not to have a better view from a distance.
- **Return to the relationship itself.**

---

**When our partner's awareness seems different from what they report.** What they say about their awareness doesn't match what we perceive.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Both are data.** Their report is about their inner experience. Our observation is about what we perceive from outside. These can differ.
- **Share observations as observations:** "My sense was that you were checked out — but you're saying you were present. Can we be curious about that gap?"
- **Stay humble** — meta-awareness of someone else's awareness is limited.

## The larger view

Meta-awareness is the capacity to notice how our awareness is functioning. It's the capacity that makes all other development possible, because it lets us see what we're doing — not just experience our experiences, but understand how we experience.

Without meta-awareness, we're subject to our patterns. With it, we can see them. What we can see, we can work with. What we can't see runs us.

In relationship, meta-awareness creates the possibility of true meeting. We're not just having experiences in each other's presence — we're aware of how we're showing up, what we're attending to, and how our awareness is functioning. This lets us take responsibility for our consciousness itself, not just our actions.

Meta-awareness is also the doorway to fluid awareness — the capacity to consciously guide where awareness goes. Before we can steer, we need to see the vehicle. Meta-awareness shows us the vehicle: our own consciousness in operation.
