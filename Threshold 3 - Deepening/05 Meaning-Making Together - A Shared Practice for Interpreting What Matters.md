# Meaning-Making Together: *A Shared Practice for Interpreting What Matters*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Understanding what is happening is only the beginning.

We can see a situation clearly — know the facts, recognize the pattern, orient to what's occurring — and still feel unsettled. Because the question that haunts us is not just *what is happening* but *what does it mean*? Why does it matter? What are we supposed to make of it? What does it say about us, about our future, about what we value?

This is the territory of meaning-making: interpreting significance.

And increasingly, we do this alone. Each of us constructs our own interpretation. Each of us draws our own conclusions about what things mean. We can share an experience and walk away with entirely different understandings of its significance — feeling isolated in our interpretation, unsure whether our sense of what matters is real.

This guide is about reclaiming meaning-making as a shared practice. Not so that we arrive at a single interpretation, but so that we don't have to make meaning in isolation. So that we can bring our interpretations into the space between us, see what resonates and what diverges, and sometimes — when it's genuine — build meaning together that neither of us could have reached alone.

Meaning made together is more robust than meaning made alone. It has relational ground. It can be held when individual holding fails.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to interpret what matters.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing as we go** — try the meaning-making exercises with real situations between you
- **Returning regularly**, because meaning evolves as we evolve

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it actually feels like when we are making meaning together, and what it feels like when we can't.
- **Process** is how meaning-making works: the phases it moves through and the conditions it requires.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to develop this capacity: the specific exercises that help us interpret significance together.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Meaning-making is an experience

*Meaning-making is what it feels like when significance emerges — when we move from "this happened" to "this is what it means."*

Shared meaning-making feels like deepening. We were at the surface, and now we're connected to something that matters. We were alone in our interpretation, and now we're holding meaning together.

When meaning-making is working between us:

- We feel less alone in our interpretations — our sense of what matters is witnessed, tested, and sometimes shared
- Significance emerges that neither of us saw alone — something becomes visible between us
- We understand not just what happened but why it matters, what it says about us, where it fits in the larger story
- Our interpretations are enriched by each other's — even when we see things differently, the exchange is generative
- Meaning gains relational ground — it's not just "what I think" but something we've built or explored together

When meaning-making breaks down:

- We feel isolated in our interpretations — alone with our sense of what things mean
- We can't understand why the other person sees it differently, or we dismiss their interpretation
- Surface-level responses substitute for genuine exploration of significance
- We impose our meaning on each other, or we abandon our own to accommodate
- Significance feels thin, uncertain, or contested rather than rich and shared

The difference often comes down to whether we're willing to genuinely explore what things mean together — or whether we're defending an interpretation, avoiding the vulnerability of deep meaning, or rushing past significance to action.

**A reflection for us:** What experiences have we been through together that we've made meaning from — that we understand differently because we went through them together?

## Meaning-making is a process

*Meaning-making builds on sense-making — taking what we understand is happening and exploring what it signifies.*

### The distinction between sense-making and meaning-making

Sense-making asks: *What is happening?*
Meaning-making asks: *What does it mean that this is happening?*

These are related but different questions:

- **Sense-making** produces understanding of the situation
- **Meaning-making** produces interpretation of significance

For example:

- Sense-making: "We've been spending less time together lately. Our evenings have become parallel — each of us doing our own thing in the same room."
- Meaning-making: "This might mean we're drifting apart." Or: "This might mean we're comfortable enough to be alone together." Or: "This might mean we're both exhausted and need rest more than we need connection right now."

Notice: the sense-making is the same; the meaning-making can go multiple directions. This is why the distinction matters. Different meanings can be made from the same understanding — and exploring those different meanings together is exactly what this practice is about.

### What meaning-making involves

Meaning-making goes beyond observing what's happening. It involves:

- **Interpretation** — assigning significance. Not just "this happened" but "this is what it means that this happened."
- **Valuation** — determining what matters. What's important here? What's at stake? What do we care about?
- **Implication** — drawing out consequences. What follows from this? What does it suggest about the future?
- **Narration** — placing events within story. What chapter is this? How does this fit the arc of our relationship, our lives?
- **Purpose** — connecting to larger meaning. How does this relate to what we live for?

Meaning is not something we find waiting to be discovered. It is something we *make* — actively constructing interpretation through the lens of our values, history, and relationship.

### The layers of meaning

Meaning operates at different layers:

- **Personal meaning** — What does this mean to me, given my history and values?
- **Relational meaning** — What does this mean for us, for our relationship?
- **Contextual meaning** — What does this mean in this particular situation, this season of life?
- **Existential meaning** — What does this mean in the largest frame?

These layers can align or conflict. Something can have positive personal meaning but concerning relational meaning. Something can be trivial contextually but profound existentially. Shared meaning-making often involves navigating across these layers — discovering where our meanings align and where they live at different levels.

### The foundations of meaning-making between us

Before we move into practices, here are the qualities that make shared meaning-making possible.

**Grounding in shared understanding first.** Meaning-making builds on sense-making. Before we can interpret what something means, we need shared understanding of what is happening. If we're interpreting different situations, our meanings will inevitably diverge without us understanding why.

**Willingness to be vulnerable.** Meaning-making exposes what we care about — our values, our hopes, our fears. Sharing our interpretation of significance is inherently vulnerable. If it doesn't feel safe to share what things really mean to us, we'll stay at the surface.

**Tolerance for multiplicity.** Shared meaning-making does not always produce a single interpretation. It may produce multiple meanings held together. This is not failure — it is often richness. We need to tolerate interpretive diversity without rushing to collapse it.

**Connection to what actually matters.** Deep meaning-making touches purpose — what we live for, what ultimately matters to us. When meaning-making stays only at the surface level of interpretation, it misses the depth where significance really lives.

**A reflection for us:** Are there experiences between us that are still waiting for meaning — things we've been through but haven't yet made sense of together?

## Meaning-making is a practice

These are our best practices for developing shared meaning-making capacity. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice grounding in shared understanding

Meaning-making begins by grounding in the sense-making that precedes it — making sure we have a shared understanding of what we're interpreting.

- **We review what we understand together.** "Here's what we understand about what's happening..." We articulate the sense-making that will be the foundation for meaning-making.

- **We check if it's sufficient.** "Is our understanding of the situation solid enough to interpret what it means?" Sometimes more sense-making is needed before meaning-making can proceed.

- **We name the transition.** "Now that we understand what's happening, let's explore what it means." Explicitly shifting from sense-making to meaning-making helps us move together.

- **We resist jumping ahead.** If we start interpreting significance before we've understood the situation, we often interpret something that isn't there — or miss something that is.

*When to use: At the beginning of any meaning-making conversation. When we notice we're interpreting before we've really understood what happened.*

---

### We practice surfacing individual meanings

Before we can build meaning together, we need to know what this means to each of us individually.

- **We invite interpretation openly.** "What does this mean to you?" We give each person genuine space to share their sense of significance.

- **We go deeper than opinions.** Not just "what do you think?" but "what does this mean to you? What does it touch? What significance does it have from where you sit?"

- **We listen for values.** As we each share our meaning, we notice what values are implicit. Meaning reveals what we care about.

- **We witness without debating.** At this stage, we receive meanings rather than challenging them. The goal is to surface, not to evaluate.

*When to use: After grounding in shared understanding. Before trying to build shared meaning.*

---

### We practice exploring divergence and convergence

With individual meanings surfaced, we explore the landscape — where do our interpretations converge? Where do they diverge?

- **We map the meaning-landscape.** Where are our interpretations similar? Where do they differ? We create a picture of how we're each making meaning of this.

- **We get curious about difference.** Where meanings diverge, we explore why. What values, histories, or positions produce different interpretations? Difference is data, not a problem to solve.

- **We don't rush to resolve.** We stay curious about divergence before trying to close it. Often there's something important to learn in the space where our meanings differ.

- **We look for unexpected convergence.** Sometimes interpretations that seem different on the surface share deeper commonality. We look for that.

*When to use: After surfacing individual meanings. When we want to understand the full landscape before moving to integration.*

---

### We practice building meaning together

Now we move from exploring individual meanings to creating interpretation that emerges from the collective — meaning that is genuinely shared, not just aggregated.

- **We build on each other.** "Building on what you said, I'm also seeing..." We let meanings combine and extend.

- **We look for emergence.** What interpretation becomes possible when we hold our meanings together? What can we see collectively that neither of us saw individually?

- **We name what's taking shape.** As shared meaning begins to emerge, we articulate it. "It seems like together we're arriving at..."

- **We test for resonance.** Proposed shared meaning needs to be checked. "Does this capture something real?" "Does this resonate for you?"

- **We allow multiplicity.** Shared meaning-making may produce multiple interpretations, not just one. We can hold several meanings together rather than forcing a single conclusion.

*When to use: After exploring divergence and convergence. When we want to create meaning together, not just understand our separate meanings.*

---

### We practice connecting meaning to implication

Meaning gains reality when it shapes how we live. This practice connects interpretation to what follows from it.

- **We draw out implications.** "If this is what it means, what follows?" We let meaning lead to consequence.

- **We connect to action.** "What does this meaning suggest we should do?" Meaning becomes real when it informs how we act.

- **We test meaning by its implications.** If the implications of an interpretation seem wrong, the interpretation may need revision. What follows from meaning is a check on the meaning itself.

- **We hold implications lightly.** Implications are provisional. As meaning evolves, so do its consequences.

*When to use: After arriving at shared meaning. When we want meaning to translate into how we live.*

---

### We practice holding and evolving meaning

Shared meaning needs to be held over time — maintained, remembered, and allowed to evolve as we evolve.

- **We articulate clearly.** We put shared meaning into words. Clarity supports holding. We might even write it down for significant interpretations.

- **We return to it.** Shared meaning needs to be revisited, not just created once. "Does this meaning still hold? Does it need updating?"

- **We let it evolve.** Meaning is living, not fixed. As circumstances change, as we learn more, meaning may need to shift.

- **We hold with care.** Shared meaning took work to create. We treat it with respect — even as we remain open to its evolution.

*When to use: After creating shared meaning. As ongoing practice — periodically checking whether our meanings still fit.*

---

### We practice meaning-making about our relationship

Some of the most important meaning-making happens when we interpret the significance of our relationship itself — what it means, where it is in its story, what matters about how we are together.

- **We treat our relationship as something with meaning.** Just as events have significance, so does our relationship. "What does our relationship mean? What is it for?"

- **We explore what our patterns signify.** "What does it mean that we relate this way? What does our dynamic say about us?"

- **We place ourselves in story.** "Where are we in the story of our relationship? What chapter is this? What arc are we in?"

- **We connect to what we live for.** "How does our relationship connect to what ultimately matters to us?"

- **We stay curious rather than fixed.** The meaning of our relationship is not static. It evolves as we evolve. We stay open to new interpretations.

*When to use: Periodically, as a way of staying connected to the significance of what we share. During transitions. When we want to deepen our understanding of why this relationship matters.*

---

### We practice working with meaning that diverges

Sometimes our meanings don't converge. We interpret significance differently, and no amount of exploration produces shared meaning. This practice helps us hold that honestly.

- **We normalize divergence.** "It may be that this genuinely means different things to us." Divergent meanings are not necessarily a failure of the practice.

- **We map the divergence clearly.** We understand exactly where and why our interpretations differ. Even if we can't share meaning, we can share understanding of our difference.

- **We look for partial sharing.** Perhaps some aspects of meaning can be shared even if others can't. We find what common ground exists.

- **We honor multiplicity.** "We can hold multiple meanings without needing to force unity." This is sometimes the honest outcome.

- **We explore meta-meaning.** "What does it mean that we interpret this differently?" Sometimes the divergence itself is meaningful.

*When to use: When meaning-making has not produced convergence. When we've genuinely tried and our interpretations remain different.*

## Working with challenges

**When one person's meaning dominates.** Sometimes shared meaning-making becomes one person's meaning imposed on the other. The "shared" interpretation is actually one person's interpretation adopted — or submitted to — by the group.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We explicitly invite all interpretations.** Before any building, we make sure each person's meaning is on the table.
- **We check directly.** "Is this genuinely shared, or are some views not included?"
- **We slow down.** Give tentative meanings time to develop before confident interpretations take over.
- **We name the pattern.** "I want to make sure we're building this together, not adopting one view."

---

**When we avoid depth.** Sometimes we stay at the surface — deflecting with humor, staying abstract, or rushing past meaning to action. We sense there's something significant here, but we don't touch it.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We name it.** "I notice we're staying at the surface. What would it take to go deeper?"
- **We model vulnerability.** Share our own meaning first — what this really touches in us.
- **We start with lower stakes.** Build the meaning-making muscle before applying it to the hardest topics.
- **We create permission.** "Can we talk about what this really means to us?"

---

**When we settle too quickly.** Sometimes we arrive at a shared meaning before we've really explored — before individual meanings are surfaced, before divergence is examined, before building has genuinely happened.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We slow down.** "Let's make sure we've really explored before we settle."
- **We invite divergence.** "Does anyone see it differently? What are we missing?"
- **We test the meaning.** "Does this really capture what's significant here — for both of us?"
- **We unsettle if needed.** "I'm not sure we've gone deep enough. Can we open this back up?"

---

**When meaning feels disconnected from reality.** Sometimes we create shared meaning that's internally coherent but doesn't fit the situation — interpretation that's wishful, defensive, or disconnected from what's actually happening.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We return to sense-making.** "Does this interpretation fit what we actually understand is happening?"
- **We test against evidence.** "What would we expect to see if this meaning is accurate?"
- **We invite challenge.** "What would suggest this interpretation is wrong?"
- **We stay humble.** Even meaning we love may need revision if reality doesn't support it.

## The larger view

Meaning-making is what gives our lives significance. Without it, events happen but don't matter. With it, even difficult experiences become part of a story that makes sense.

When we can make meaning together — interpreting significance in the space between us rather than alone in our separate minds — several things become possible. We can act together because we share interpretation, not just information. Meaning held together can weather what meaning held alone cannot. We're not just sharing space — we're sharing significance, inhabiting a common meaningful world. And together, we can touch questions of ultimate meaning that feel too large to approach alone.

The meanings we make are not fixed. They evolve as we evolve, as circumstances change, as we learn more about ourselves and each other. An interpretation that served us last year may need updating. A meaning we couldn't access before may suddenly become available. The story we've been telling about our relationship may need a new chapter. This is not instability; it is the natural life cycle of meaning. We hold our interpretations lightly enough that they can grow with us.

When we develop the capacity to make meaning together, we become part of healing something larger. In a fragmented world where people increasingly make meaning in isolation — each in their own interpretation-bubble — the practice of genuinely interpreting together is countercultural. It rebuilds the shared meaning-making capacity that communities, families, and societies need.

## Our commitment to meaning-making

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice interpreting what matters together. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We won't always do this well. We will sometimes impose our meaning. We will sometimes avoid depth. We will sometimes settle too quickly on interpretations that need more exploration. We will sometimes hold meanings that don't fit reality.

But we now have a shared practice. We know what meaning-making involves. We know the phases it moves through. We know how to work with divergence and convergence, how to build together, how to hold what we create.

May we bring our interpretations to each other with vulnerability. May we receive each other's meanings with curiosity. May we build significance together that neither of us could reach alone. May we hold our meanings with both commitment and humility.

Let us make meaning together.
