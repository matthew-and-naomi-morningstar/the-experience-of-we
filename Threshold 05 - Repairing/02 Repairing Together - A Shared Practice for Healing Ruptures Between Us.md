# Repairing Together: *A Shared Practice for Healing Ruptures Between Us*

---

Hello, dear companions.

We will hurt each other.

Not intentionally, not maliciously — but hurt each other nonetheless. There will be moments when our connection breaks, when trust is damaged, when one of us wounds the other. Misunderstandings will occur. Words will land wrong. Needs will go unmet. Boundaries will be crossed. Promises will slip.

This is not a sign that our relationship is failing. This is a sign that our relationship is real.

The question is not whether rupture will happen between us but what happens after. Some relationships rupture and never heal; the wound festers, distance grows, connection slowly dies. Other relationships rupture and repair; the wound is tended together, connection is restored, and something stronger emerges from the healing.

The difference is not luck. It is practice. Repair is something we learn to do together: recognizing rupture, being with the pain, taking responsibility, acknowledging impact, making amends, and rebuilding trust. Like any shared practice, it improves the more we do it.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to repair the inevitable ruptures in their relationship.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or taking turns
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing the exercises together**, including when actual repair is needed
- **Returning regularly**, because repair is not something we master once but something we keep choosing

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what rupture feels like, what being in the broken space feels like, and what repair and restoration feel like when they happen.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how rupture occurs, how we move through it, and how repair restores connection.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate repair: recognizing rupture, being with it, taking responsibility, acknowledging impact, making amends, and receiving repair.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Repair is an experience

*Repair is what it feels like when something broken between us is tended and restored.*

### What rupture is

Rupture is a break in our relational connection — a moment when the felt sense of being together is interrupted, damaged, or lost.

**Minor ruptures:** A moment of disconnection, a misattunement, a small misunderstanding. These happen constantly and often repair naturally with a glance, a touch, a quick acknowledgment.

**Moderate ruptures:** A more significant breach — a hurtful comment, a broken commitment, a failure to show up when needed. These require more intentional repair.

**Major ruptures:** Serious violations of trust — betrayal, sustained harm, deep boundary violations. These require extensive repair work, and some may challenge what our relationship can hold.

**What rupture feels like between us:**
- Loss of safety or trust
- Disconnection or distance
- Hurt, anger, or resentment
- Withdrawal or defensive activation
- The sense that something is "off" between us

### Why rupture is inevitable

Rupture is not a sign that we have failed. It is inevitable because we are different, we are imperfect, we are complex, life is difficult, and intimacy creates vulnerability. The closer we are, the more ways there are to hurt each other.

Expecting a rupture-free relationship is expecting something impossible. What we can develop is our shared capacity for repair.

### What repair offers

Repair is the restoration of our relational connection after rupture — healing the break together, rebuilding trust, returning to secure attachment.

**Repair builds trust.** Paradoxically, successfully repaired ruptures often create more trust than never having ruptured. We learn that rupture is survivable, that we will take responsibility, that our connection can be restored.

**Repair prevents accumulation.** Unrepaired ruptures don't disappear. They accumulate as resentment, distance, and unspoken grievance. Regular repair prevents this toxic buildup.

**Repair enables risk.** Knowing repair is possible allows us to be vulnerable. We can try things, be imperfect, take risks, because we know mistakes can be mended.

**A reflection for us:** When has a repair between us actually worked — when has a rupture been healed rather than just papered over? What made the difference?

## Repair is a process

*Repair isn't a single act. It moves through phases — from recognition through responsibility to rebuilding — and each phase matters.*

Repair moves through phases, though not always linearly:

**Recognition.** We see together that rupture has occurred. We acknowledge that something is broken between us.

**Being with the rupture.** Before rushing to fix, we allow the pain of rupture to be present. We don't skip over the hurt to get to resolution.

**Responsibility.** The one who caused harm owns their part. They take accountability for what they contributed.

**Acknowledgment.** The impact is recognized and validated. The hurt person's experience is received and honored.

**Making amends.** What can be done to address the harm is done. Things are made right where possible.

**Rebuilding.** Trust and connection are restored over time. Changed behavior demonstrates that repair is real.

### What repair is not

- Pretending rupture didn't happen
- Minimizing the harm
- Demanding quick forgiveness
- Making excuses
- Keeping score

### Our roles in rupture and repair

**When one of us caused harm,** repair asks us to: recognize that our action created the rupture, take responsibility without excuse, listen to and acknowledge the impact, make amends where possible, and demonstrate change over time.

**When one of us was harmed,** repair asks us to: allow ourselves to feel and express our hurt, communicate the impact so the other can understand, be open to receiving genuine repair, and work with forgiveness as it naturally emerges.

**When both of us contributed,** we each take responsibility for our part without using the other's contribution to minimize our own. We take turns — one person's repair doesn't require the other to simultaneously repair. We avoid "what about" responses that deflect accountability.

**A reflection for us:** What's our typical repair process? Who tends to initiate? How long does it usually take? What would we like to change?

## Repair is a practice

These are our best practices for repairing ruptures between us. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We recognize rupture together

Repair begins when we recognize together that rupture has occurred.

- **We notice the signs.** Is there distance between us? Is one of us withdrawn, hurt, or activated? Has something shifted?
- **Either of us can name it.** "Something feels off between us." "I think we need to repair something." "Did something happen that hurt you?"
- **We agree that rupture occurred.** We don't have to agree about fault or details yet. We just acknowledge that something needs attention.
- **We commit to repair.** "Let's work on this together."

What we might say: "I sense something's wrong between us. Can we talk about it?" "I think I may have hurt you. I want to understand what happened." "Are we okay? It doesn't feel like we're okay."

*When to use: When we sense disconnection. When something feels off. When one of us seems hurt or withdrawn. As the first move toward repair.*

---

### We allow space to be with rupture before rushing to repair

Not all ruptures can or should be repaired immediately. Sometimes we need to be with the pain before we can move toward healing.

- **We acknowledge that repair may not be possible yet.** One of us might be too hurt, too activated, or too overwhelmed to engage in repair.
- **We don't rush.** The urge to "fix it" quickly is often about our own discomfort, not about what the wounded person needs.
- **We stay present without solving.** We can be with each other in the rupture without immediately trying to resolve it.
- **We communicate what we need.** "I need some time before I can talk about this." "I'm not ready to repair yet, but I'm not leaving."
- **We hold the intention to repair.** Even when repair isn't possible now, we maintain commitment to it: "I want to repair this when we're both ready."
- **We take care of ourselves and each other.** Being in rupture is painful. We tend to our own regulation while staying connected enough that the other doesn't feel abandoned.

Rushing to repair before the hurt person is ready can feel dismissive. Sometimes the pain needs to be witnessed and held before it can be healed.

*When to use: When rupture is significant. When one person isn't ready to repair. When the urge to fix quickly would skip over necessary grief or processing. When we need to be in the pain together before we can move through it.*

---

### We take responsibility when we caused harm

When we are the one who caused harm, we take responsibility fully and without excuse.

- **We name what we did specifically.** "I said X." "I didn't do Y." We name the behavior clearly.
- **We own it fully.** We don't qualify or justify. Not "I did X but only because..." Just "I did X."
- **We separate intention from impact.** Even if we didn't intend harm, harm occurred. We own the impact regardless of intention.
- **We avoid "but."** "I'm sorry but..." negates the apology.
- **We stay present.** We resist the urge to flee, defend, or rush past this moment.

What taking responsibility sounds like: "I was wrong to do that." "I made a mistake." "That was my fault, and I'm sorry."

What it doesn't sound like: "I'm sorry you feel that way." "I was wrong, but you also..." "If I hurt you, I'm sorry."

*When to use: When we caused the rupture. When our action or inaction hurt the other person. As the essential move that makes repair possible.*

---

### We acknowledge the impact of our actions

The person who caused harm demonstrates understanding of how their behavior affected the other.

- **We ask about their experience.** "How did that affect you?" "What was that like for you?"
- **We listen without defending.** When they tell us the impact, we listen. We don't explain, justify, or correct.
- **We reflect what we hear.** "What I'm hearing is that when I did X, you felt Y."
- **We validate the impact.** "It makes sense that you would feel that way." We don't argue with their experience.
- **We express genuine regret.** We let ourselves feel the weight of having hurt them. "I'm truly sorry I caused you that pain."

Acknowledgment lets the hurt person know they are seen, their experience is real, and they are not alone with it. This is often more healing than the apology itself.

*When to use: After taking responsibility. When the hurt person needs to feel understood. When we want our repair to actually land.*

---

### We express our hurt when we were harmed

For repair to work, the hurt must be expressed — not as attack, but as information about impact.

- **We know what we feel.** Before expressing, we clarify internally. Hurt? Anger? Fear? Betrayal?
- **We choose to express.** We decide to share our experience rather than hide it, act it out, or let it fester.
- **We use "I" statements.** "I felt hurt when..." "I experienced..." We focus on our experience, not their character.
- **We are specific about impact.** "When you did X, I felt Y." We help them understand what their behavior caused.
- **We express without attacking.** The goal is to communicate impact, not to wound in return.

What expressing hurt sounds like: "When you said that, I felt dismissed and small." "I felt abandoned when you didn't show up." "That comment really hurt me."

*When to use: When we need the other person to understand how we were affected. When our pain needs to be witnessed. When we want repair to address what actually happened.*

---

### We make amends where possible

Making amends means doing what can be done to address the harm — not just saying sorry but taking action.

- **The person who caused harm asks what would help.** "What would help make this right?" "What do you need from me?"
- **The person who was harmed expresses what they need,** if they know.
- **Amends are offered and received.** Based on what is needed and what can genuinely be provided.
- **Promises are kept.** If we commit to something, we do it. Broken promises in repair cause additional rupture.
- **We accept that some things cannot be undone.** We acknowledge this honestly while still doing what can be done.

Forms amends might take: Changed behavior going forward. Concrete actions to address specific harm. Commitment to work on underlying patterns. Simply being present with the pain we caused.

*When to use: After responsibility is taken and impact is acknowledged. When something can be done to make things right. When words alone aren't enough.*

---

### We receive repair when it's offered

When our partner offers repair, receiving it is its own practice.

- **We notice if we block.** Do we deflect apologies? ("It's fine.") Dismiss attempts to reconnect? ("Whatever.")
- **We let repair land.** When genuine repair is offered, we let it in. We don't brush it off.
- **We receive what is offered.** Our partner is trying to make things right. We let their effort matter.
- **We don't require perfection.** Repair attempts may be imperfect. If the core is genuine, we work with what's offered.
- **We acknowledge their effort.** "Thank you for taking responsibility." "I appreciate you hearing me."

Repair is bidirectional. If we can't receive repair, rupture can't heal, regardless of what our partner offers.

*When to use: When repair is being offered. When we notice ourselves deflecting or dismissing. When we want to let healing happen.*

---

### We have repair conversations for significant ruptures

When we need to repair something significant, a structured conversation helps.

- **We find the right time.** Not in the middle of another conflict, not when highly dysregulated, not in public.
- **We open with intention.** "I want to repair what happened between us."
- **The person who caused harm takes responsibility** and names what they did.
- **The person who was harmed shares the impact.** The other listens fully.
- **Impact is acknowledged with genuine regret.**
- **Amends are discussed.** "What would help make this right?"
- **Commitment to change is made.** What will be different going forward?
- **We close together.** "Thank you for working through this with me."

*When to use: For moderate to major ruptures. When quick repair isn't sufficient. When we need dedicated time and space to heal something significant.*

---

### We practice micro-repairs in daily life

Many ruptures can be repaired immediately with small acknowledgments.

- "Sorry, I interrupted you."
- "That came out wrong. Let me try again."
- "I was dismissive just now. That wasn't fair."
- "I see I hurt you. I'm sorry."

These small repairs, done consistently, prevent accumulation and keep our connection healthy.

*When to use: Constantly. For minor ruptures. As ongoing maintenance of our connection. To build a culture where repair is normal.*

## Working with challenges

**When one of us is still very angry.** Sometimes the hurt person is still very activated when repair is attempted.

**What we've found helps:**

- **The angry person is allowed to be angry.** We don't require calm as a prerequisite for repair.
- **The person who caused harm receives the anger without counter-attacking.** This is part of accountability.
- **If anger becomes harmful, we can pause** with commitment to return.

---

**When defensiveness arises.** The person who caused harm may become defensive — explaining, justifying, minimizing.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Notice the defensiveness and name it.** "I notice I'm getting defensive."
- **Breathe and ground.** Return to taking responsibility.
- **The hurt person can gently name it.** "That feels like defending. Can you just hear me?"

---

**When repair isn't accepted.** Sometimes one of us offers repair and the other isn't ready to receive it.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Remember that repair can't be forced.** Ask if there's something more needed.
- **Give time.** Some repair takes longer.
- **Don't withdraw the repair offer** because it wasn't immediately accepted.
- **Stay available.**

---

**When we both caused harm.** When we both contributed to the rupture, repair becomes more complex.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Take turns.** Don't try to repair simultaneously.
- **Don't use their harm to minimize ours.** Each of us takes full responsibility for our part.
- **We may need to repair in stages.**

---

**When the same rupture keeps happening.** Some ruptures are patterns — they recur because underlying issues haven't been addressed.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Name the pattern.** Get curious about what's underneath.
- **Look for systemic solutions,** not just incident-by-incident repair.
- **We may need outside support.** Stay committed; patterns take time to change.

---

**When repair feels impossible.** Sometimes rupture is so significant that repair feels impossible.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Acknowledge the severity.** Ask honestly: "Is this repairable for us?"
- **Seek outside support if needed.** Give extensive time; major repair is slow.
- **Accept that some relationships may not survive some ruptures.**

## The larger view

Repair isn't one relational skill among many. It's the practice that makes all other relational growth possible — because without the ability to heal what breaks, we can't risk the vulnerability that growth requires.

There is something remarkable about relationships that have been broken and healed. They are often stronger than relationships that never broke — stronger because both people now know that rupture is survivable, that responsibility will be taken, that connection can be restored.

We can build a relationship where repair is ordinary — not a crisis response but a regular practice. We expect rupture; we don't treat it as catastrophe. We repair quickly; we don't let wounds fester. We repair small things; we don't wait for major ruptures. We normalize taking responsibility — it becomes ordinary to say "I was wrong." We make repair safe: admitting fault doesn't lead to attack. We appreciate when repair happens: "Thank you for working through that with me."

Discerning genuine repair matters too. Signs of genuine repair include full responsibility without excuse, clear understanding of impact, genuine remorse that is felt rather than performed, concrete amends offered, changed behavior over time, and patience rather than demanding quick forgiveness. Signs of pseudo-repair include apologizing but making excuses, saying "I'm sorry" without changed behavior, rushing to "move on" without addressing impact, becoming defensive when impact is expressed, and expecting immediate forgiveness. When we notice pseudo-repair, we name what's missing: "I hear your apology, but I need to feel that you understand how this affected me."

When we model that harm can be acknowledged, pain can be held, and trust can be rebuilt, we offer that possibility to everyone around us. In a world where rupture is often met with avoidance or escalation, every moment of genuine repair is a quiet act of restoration — a choice to mend rather than abandon.

## Our commitment to repair

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice repairing together. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will recognize when something has broken. We will allow space to be with the pain when needed. We will take responsibility for our part. We will listen to each other's hurt. We will make amends where we can. We will receive each other's repair attempts with openness.

We will not do this perfectly. We will sometimes fail to recognize rupture. We will sometimes get defensive. We will sometimes struggle to forgive. We will sometimes cause the same harm again.

But we now have a shared practice. We have a common understanding of what repair looks like between us. We have words and structures we can use when we need them.

May we have the courage to cause less harm — and the humility to repair when we do. May we take responsibility fully. May we receive each other's pain with tenderness. May our relationship become stronger through every breaking and mending.

We will hurt each other. This is inevitable. But we will also heal each other. This is our practice.

Let us repair together.
