# Assuming and Embodying Positive Regard Together: *A Shared Practice for Holding Each Other as Fundamentally Worthy*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Before anything else can happen between us — before vulnerability, before trust, before growth — there's a more fundamental question: How do we hold each other?

Do we hold each other as fundamentally worthy? As deserving of care, respect, and acceptance simply because we exist? Or does our regard depend on performance — on being good enough, pleasing enough, right enough?

The quality of our regard for each other shapes everything. It determines whether vulnerability feels safe or dangerous. It determines whether feedback feels like care or attack. It determines whether we can grow together or must defend ourselves against each other.

Positive regard isn't a feeling we wait to have. It's an orientation we choose — a commitment to holding each other's fundamental worth as unquestionable, regardless of what happens between us. This doesn't mean approving of everything. It means separating who someone is from what they do, and never putting their essential worthiness on trial.

When positive regard is present, everything else becomes possible. When it's absent, nothing else can truly land.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to hold each other as fundamentally worthy — with genuine acceptance and clear boundaries.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or taking turns
- **Practicing the exercises** when we're regulated and open
- **Returning to this guide** when positive regard has been strained
- **Building shared vocabulary** for talking about how we hold each other

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it feels like to be held in positive regard, to hold another with positive regard, and what changes when regard becomes conditional.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how positive regard forms, what undermines it, and how it connects to safety, trust, and growth.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the concrete ways we cultivate, maintain, and repair positive regard between us.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Positive regard is an experience

*Positive regard is what it feels like to be held as fundamentally worthy — and to hold another that way — the quality of acceptance that doesn't depend on performance or approval.*

### Being held in positive regard

There's a distinctive quality to being held by someone who regards us as fundamentally worthy:

- **Safety.** We can be ourselves without performing. We don't need to earn our place.
- **Spaciousness.** There's room for us to be imperfect, to struggle, to not know.
- **Being seen.** We're perceived as we actually are, not as a collection of flaws to be fixed.
- **Permission.** We can grow and change without it meaning we were wrong before.
- **Rest.** We can stop defending our worth. It isn't in question.

This is different from being liked or approved of. Someone can hold us in positive regard while disagreeing with us, while being frustrated with us, while asking us to change a behavior. The regard remains — it's the behavior that's in question, not our fundamental worth.

### Holding another in positive regard

When we hold someone in positive regard, we experience:

- **Warmth.** An underlying care that persists through difficulty.
- **Openness.** Willingness to see them freshly, beyond our stories and judgments.
- **Separating person from behavior.** When they do something hurtful, we can address the action without attacking who they are.
- **Assuming good intent.** Interpreting their actions through the lens of "they're doing their best" rather than "they're trying to harm me."
- **Steadiness.** Our regard doesn't fluctuate wildly based on their performance.

### When regard becomes conditional

We can feel when positive regard has been withdrawn or made conditional:

- **Walking on eggshells.** We're monitoring ourselves, trying not to trigger disapproval.
- **Performance anxiety.** We're trying to earn regard rather than resting in it.
- **Defensiveness.** We're protecting our worth because it feels under attack.
- **Shame.** Not "I did something bad" but "I am bad."
- **Withdrawal.** It doesn't feel safe to be seen, so we hide.

Conditional regard says: "I will hold you as worthy if..." Positive regard says: "I hold you as worthy. Period."

### The mutual field

When both people hold each other in positive regard, something emerges between us:

- **A safe field.** The space itself feels trustworthy.
- **Permission to be human.** Both of us can make mistakes without catastrophe.
- **Honest communication.** We can say hard things because the relationship can hold them.
- **Growth orientation.** We're oriented toward becoming, not defending.
- **Resilience.** Ruptures can be repaired because fundamental worth isn't in question.

**A reflection for us:** When do we feel most deeply held in the other's positive regard — most trusted as fundamentally good and worthy?

## Positive regard is a process

*Positive regard develops through understanding what it is, what erodes it, and how the crucial distinction between behavior and being makes it possible to address anything while keeping regard intact.*

### What positive regard is

Positive regard is the orientation of holding someone as fundamentally worthy — deserving of respect, care, and acceptance simply because they exist.

It isn't:
- **Approval of everything they do.** We can hold someone in positive regard while setting boundaries, giving feedback, or saying no.
- **Absence of conflict.** Positive regard doesn't mean never disagreeing. It means disagreeing without attacking worth.
- **Ignoring harm.** We can name when behavior causes harm while still holding the person as worthy.
- **A feeling we wait to have.** Positive regard is a commitment, a practice, an orientation we choose — not an emotion that either comes or doesn't.

It is:
- **Separating being from doing.** The person's essential worth is distinct from their actions.
- **Assuming good intent.** Starting from the assumption that they're doing their best given their current capacities and circumstances.
- **Unconditional acceptance.** Acceptance that doesn't depend on performance.
- **A foundation, not a reward.** Regard isn't something they earn — it's something they receive because they're human and they're ours.

### Why positive regard matters

Positive regard creates the conditions for everything else:

**It enables vulnerability.** We can only risk being truly seen if we trust that what's seen will be held with care. When regard is conditional, vulnerability is too dangerous — we might reveal something that costs us our place.

**It enables trust.** Trust includes trusting that our fundamental worth will be honored. If regard fluctuates, trust can't stabilize.

**It enables growth.** People change when they feel safe enough to examine themselves honestly. Conditional regard creates defensiveness; positive regard creates openness to seeing what needs to change.

**It enables repair.** When ruptures happen, repair requires both people to believe the other is worth repairing with. Positive regard maintains that belief through the rupture.

**It enables honest feedback.** We can hear hard things when we trust that the speaker still holds us as worthy. Without that trust, feedback feels like attack.

### What erodes positive regard

Positive regard can be worn down by:

**Accumulated resentment.** Unaddressed hurts pile up until we can no longer see the person clearly — only the injuries.

**Contempt.** When we begin to see the other as fundamentally lesser — stupid, worthless, beneath us — positive regard has collapsed.

**Chronic disappointment.** When expectations are repeatedly unmet, we may start to withdraw regard as protection.

**Dehumanization through conflict.** In heated moments, we may forget the other's humanity and treat them as enemy rather than partner.

**Our own shame.** When we don't hold ourselves in positive regard, it's harder to extend it to others.

### The relationship between behavior and regard

This is the crucial distinction: we can address behavior without withdrawing regard.

"What you did hurt me" is different from "You are a hurtful person."
"That behavior isn't acceptable" is different from "You aren't acceptable."
"I need you to change this" is different from "You're not good enough."

When we conflate behavior and being, every piece of feedback becomes an attack on worth. When we separate them, we can address anything while keeping regard intact.

This doesn't mean tolerating harmful behavior. It means responding to harmful behavior in a way that doesn't also attack the person's fundamental humanity.

**A reflection for us:** When is positive regard hardest to maintain between us? What conditions or behaviors make it difficult to see each other as fundamentally worthy?

## Positive regard is a practice

*These are our best practices for cultivating positive regard together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.*

### We assume good intent

Choosing to interpret each other's actions through a generous lens.

- **When our companion does something that bothers us,** we pause before assigning motive.
- **We ask ourselves:** "What's the most generous interpretation of this?"
- **We remember that they're doing their best** given their current state, capacity, and understanding.
- **We voice the generous interpretation:** "I'm assuming you didn't mean to hurt me with that."
- **If we genuinely can't find a generous interpretation,** we ask rather than assume: "Help me understand what was happening for you."
- **We practice this especially when it's hard** — when we're hurt or triggered is exactly when generous interpretation matters most.

*When to use: Continuously, as an ongoing orientation. When something bothers us. When we notice ourselves assigning negative intent. When conflict is beginning.*

---

### We separate being from doing

Learning to address behavior without attacking worth.

- **When we need to give feedback or express hurt,** we focus on the specific behavior, not the person's character.
- **We use language that maintains the distinction:** "That action hurt" instead of "You're hurtful." "I didn't like that behavior" instead of "I don't like you." "That wasn't okay" instead of "You're not okay."
- **We explicitly affirm regard while addressing behavior:** "I love you AND that behavior isn't working for me."
- **We notice when we're slipping into character attack** and correct course.
- **We receive feedback in the same spirit** — hearing the behavior being addressed, not taking it as an attack on our worth.

*When to use: When giving feedback. When receiving feedback. When addressing conflict. When hurt has occurred.*

---

### We speak our regard

Making positive regard explicit rather than assuming it's known.

- **We tell each other directly** that we hold them as worthy: "I believe in you." "I respect you." "Your worth isn't in question for me."
- **We express regard especially when it might be doubted** — after conflict, after failure, after shame.
- **We name what we value about who they are,** not just what they do.
- **We remind each other that our regard isn't conditional** on performance: "You don't have to earn your place with me."
- **We receive these expressions** — letting them land rather than deflecting or dismissing.

*When to use: Regularly, not just in crisis. After ruptures or conflicts. When we sense the other doubting their worth. When regard might feel uncertain.*

---

### We examine our regard honestly

Checking whether our regard has become conditional.

- **We ask ourselves:** "Am I holding them as fundamentally worthy right now, or am I making them prove themselves?"
- **We notice signs that regard has slipped:** contempt (eye-rolling, dismissiveness, seeing them as lesser), score-keeping (tallying their failures), global judgments ("They always..." "They never..." "They're just...")
- **We're honest about what's happened:** "I notice my regard for you has slipped. I want to work on that."
- **We explore what eroded it:** resentment, disappointment, hurt, fear?
- **We recommit:** "I want to hold you as worthy even when I'm struggling to feel it."

*When to use: As regular reflective practice. When we notice contempt or dismissiveness. When the relationship feels strained. When we sense we're making regard conditional.*

---

### We repair regard when it's been damaged

Working to restore positive regard after it's been withdrawn.

- **We acknowledge when regard has slipped:** "I haven't been holding you well. I'm sorry."
- **We name what happened without making excuses:** "I was hurt, and I let that turn into contempt."
- **We recommit explicitly:** "I want to return to holding you as fundamentally worthy."
- **We ask what the other needs** to feel held again: "What would help you trust my regard?"
- **We give repair time** — trust in regard rebuilds through consistent demonstration, not just words.
- **We accept repair when offered** — letting the other rebuild their regard for us even after they've withdrawn it.

*When to use: After contempt or character attack. When trust in regard has been damaged. When we've made regard conditional and want to restore it.*

---

### We hold ourselves in positive regard

Extending to ourselves what we offer each other.

- **We practice the same separation of being from doing** with ourselves: "I did something harmful" is different from "I am a harmful person."
- **We assume good intent toward ourselves:** we were doing our best given our state and capacity.
- **We speak regard to ourselves:** "I am worthy even when I fail."
- **We notice when self-contempt is affecting** our capacity to extend regard to others.
- **We support each other in self-regard:** "I see you being hard on yourself. I want you to know I hold you as worthy."

*When to use: When we're struggling with shame. When self-criticism is harsh. When we notice self-contempt affecting the relationship.*

---

### We create a positive regard field

Making positive regard the baseline environment of our relationship.

- **We establish positive regard as an explicit commitment:** "In this relationship, we hold each other as fundamentally worthy."
- **We name it as a value we're practicing,** not just a feeling we hope to have.
- **We remind each other of this commitment** when it's being tested.
- **We create rituals that reinforce regard** — regular expressions of what we value about each other.
- **We return to this baseline after conflicts:** "No matter what just happened, I still hold you as worthy."

*When to use: As ongoing orientation. When establishing or renewing relationship commitments. After difficult periods.*

## Working with challenges

**When positive regard is genuinely difficult to access.** Sometimes the hurt is so deep, the behavior so harmful, that positive regard feels impossible.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Acknowledge the difficulty honestly:** "I'm struggling to hold you as worthy right now." This is more respectful than pretending a regard we don't feel.
- **Explore what's blocking it** — usually accumulated hurt that needs to be addressed.
- **Positive regard doesn't mean tolerating the intolerable.** Sometimes we need to address the harm before we can restore the regard.
- **Seek support if needed** — a therapist or trusted third party can help when regard has collapsed.

---

**When positive regard feels performative.** We're saying the words but not feeling the reality.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Performance is a start** — sometimes the feeling follows the action.
- **Investigate the gap:** What's preventing genuine regard? Often there's unspoken resentment, unaddressed hurt, or unexamined assumptions.
- **Name the gap:** "I want to hold you with positive regard, but I'm struggling. Can we explore what's in the way?"

---

**When one person extends regard but doesn't receive it.** Imbalance in the positive regard field.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Name the imbalance:** "I offer regard more easily than I receive it."
- **Explore what makes receiving difficult** — often it's old shame, distrust of care, or belief that we don't deserve positive regard.
- **Practice receiving deliberately:** let the regard land, say "thank you," don't deflect.
- **The giver can ask:** "Did that reach you?" and the receiver can be honest about what blocks reception.

---

**When we confuse positive regard with never having negative feelings.** Believing that positive regard means we shouldn't feel hurt, angry, or frustrated.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Positive regard coexists with all other feelings.** We can hold someone as worthy AND be furious with them.
- **The feelings are information; regard is an orientation.** Suppressing feelings doesn't serve positive regard — it builds resentment that eventually corrodes regard.
- **Express the feelings while maintaining the regard:** "I'm very angry right now, and I still hold you as worthy."

---

**When past history makes positive regard hard to trust.** We've been burned before — regard was withdrawn, used as a weapon, or never genuine.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Trust in regard builds slowly** through consistent experience.
- **The person extending regard can be patient with distrust:** "I understand you're not sure you can trust my regard yet. I'll keep offering it."
- **Small, consistent demonstrations matter more** than grand gestures.
- **Name the history:** "I'm afraid to trust positive regard because of what's happened before."
- **Work together on building the track record** that allows trust to grow.

## The larger view

Positive regard isn't just a relationship technique. It's a stance toward human beings — a recognition that every person carries inherent worth that isn't earned, can't be lost, and doesn't depend on performance.

When we hold each other in positive regard, we're practicing something larger: the orientation that makes human flourishing possible. People grow in the presence of those who believe in their worth. They shrink in the presence of those who make worth conditional on performance.

This extends beyond our relationship. How we hold each other shapes how we hold ourselves and how we hold others. A relationship grounded in positive regard becomes a training ground for regarding all beings with the dignity they inherently deserve.

## Our commitment to positive regard

By practicing with this guide, we commit to holding each other as fundamentally worthy — not because we've earned it, but because we are.

We will practice assuming good intent. We will separate being from doing, addressing behavior without attacking worth. We will speak our regard aloud. We will examine honestly when our regard has slipped. We will repair when regard has been damaged. We will extend to ourselves the positive regard we offer each other.

We won't always do this well. We'll sometimes slip into contempt, character attack, conditional regard. We'll sometimes withdraw our regard when we're hurt.

But now we have a practice for returning. We know that positive regard is a choice, not just a feeling. We know that it can be restored even after it's been damaged. We know that it's the foundation on which everything else we want to build together depends.

May we hold each other as fundamentally worthy. May our regard remain steady through the inevitable storms. May we create a field where both of us can rest, grow, fail, repair, and become.

We are worthy of positive regard. Let us offer it — and receive it — together.
