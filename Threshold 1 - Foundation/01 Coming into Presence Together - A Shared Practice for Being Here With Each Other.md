# Coming into Presence Together: *A Shared Practice for Being Here With Each Other*

---

Hello, dear companions.

There’s something that happens when two people are actually here with each other.

Not just in the same room. Not just talking. But *here*. Landed, available, in the same moment at the same time.

Most of us know the difference. We know what it feels like when someone is truly with us: their attention soft and steady, their body settled, their whole self available.

And we know what it feels like when someone is physically present but actually somewhere else: distracted, planning, scrolling through their mind while we speak.

The first feels like being met. The second feels like being alone in company.

This guide is about the first. About how we practice landing here together, staying here together, and finding our way back when we drift.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

---

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to be present with each other.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn  
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions  
- **Practicing as we go**, especially the arriving practices — they work immediately  
- **Returning regularly**, because presence is not something we master once but something we keep choosing

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what presence actually feels like when we are truly here with each other.  
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: the movements of arriving, staying, and returning that presence involves.  
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate presence: the specific habits and exercises that help us be here together.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

## Presence is an experience

*Presence is what it feels like when someone is actually here.*

We experience presence between us as a quality of contact. It’s the felt difference between someone who is with us and someone who is near us. These are not the same thing.

When we’re present together:

- There’s a quality of stillness, even in conversation  
- Our bodies relax — something settles when we know the other person is here  
- We feel received, not just heard  
- Silence becomes comfortable rather than something to fill  
- We sense each other more clearly — tone, expression, energy  
- Being together feels nourishing rather than effortful

When presence between us is missing:

- We feel lonely even in the same room  
- Conversation stays on the surface, skimming  
- We miss each other while sitting side by side  
- We come away from time together feeling vaguely unsatisfied  
- Connection feels like something we're performing rather than something we're in

Presence isn’t a particular emotion. We can be present with each other in joy, in grief, in boredom, and in tension. Presence is not about what we feel but about whether we’re actually here to feel it together.

And presence isn’t intensity. We can be quietly present washing dishes together. We can be deeply present during a walk where very little is said. Presence is not about the volume of the experience. It’s about whether we’re in it together.

**A reflection for us:** When was the last time we felt truly present with each other — not performing, not multitasking, but actually here? What did it feel like?

## Presence is a process

*Being here together isn’t a single act. It involves three ongoing movements: arriving, staying, and returning.*

### Arriving together

Arriving is how we transition from wherever our attention was to being here with each other.

We arrive from different places. One of us might be coming from a stressful workday. The other might be caught in worry about tomorrow. Both of us might be half-looking at something on a screen. Arriving means noticing where our attention actually is and choosing to bring it here: to this room, this moment, this person.

Arriving is needed:

- When we first come together after being apart  
- Before important conversations  
- When we notice we've been in the same space but not actually with each other  
- Any time we want to shift from proximity to presence

### Staying together

Staying is the discipline of remaining here over time. Arriving is a moment. Staying is sustained.

Our attention naturally wanders. This isn’t a flaw; it’s how our minds work. Staying present doesn't mean preventing the wander. It means continuing to choose each other over the pull of our distractions.

Staying is challenged by:

- Devices and notifications  
- Our individual habits of mental escape  
- Discomfort or difficulty between us  
- The sheer momentum of a mind that’s used to being somewhere else

### Returning to each other

Returning is what we do when we notice that one or both of us has drifted. It’s the gentle act of coming back.

This is the most important of the three movements. Because we *will* drift. Both of us. Repeatedly. The practice is not to prevent wandering but to notice it and return. Each return is the practice — not a deviation from it.

Over time, our capacity for noticing absence and returning to each other builds. The gap between drifting and returning gets shorter. The return itself becomes more natural.

### The foundations of presence between us

Before we move into practices, here are the qualities that make shared presence possible.

**Mutual location.** Both of us are here. Not in the past, not in the future, not on our phones or in our worries. Our attention and our bodies are in the same place at the same time.

**Mutual availability.** We are both open to being affected by each other. Not defensive, not performing, not going through the motions. Available to perceive, respond to, and be changed by what’s happening between us.

**Mutual contact.** We are in actual contact — sensing each other, feeling what’s present, meeting what’s here. Not separated by layers of thought or distraction. There’s a live connection between us.

**Gentleness.** Presence practice works only in a climate of gentleness. When we notice absence — in ourselves or each other — we respond with kindness, not criticism. Harsh correction creates fear of noticing, which makes it harder to return.

**A reflection for us:** Which of the three movements — arriving, staying, or returning — do we find most natural? Which feels most difficult for us?

## Presence is a practice

These are our best practices for cultivating shared presence. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice the shared pause

The simplest presence practice. A momentary stop before engaging, allowing us to land with each other.

- **Before any significant moment together, we pause.** Before an important conversation, before a meal, before coming together after time apart — we stop at the threshold.  
    
- **We take a breath together.** One conscious breath signals transition. We might say: "Let's take a breath together."  
    
- **We arrive in our bodies.** We each feel our feet, our hands, our weight. We land in our physical selves.  
    
- **We turn toward each other.** We make eye contact or simply feel each other's presence. A simple acknowledgment: "I'm here."  
    
- **Then we proceed.** Only after arriving together do we move into what's next.

The shared pause is small. It takes ten seconds. But those ten seconds change everything about what follows, because we are actually here for it.

*When to use: Before important conversations, when reuniting after time apart, before meals, before any moment we want to share with real presence.*

---

### We practice grounding together

Grounding uses physical sensation to anchor both of us in the present moment. When our minds are busy, the body is always here.

- **We feel our feet on the ground.** We notice the contact, the weight, the floor holding us. We might share: "I can feel my feet."  
    
- **We feel our seat or our weight.** If sitting, we feel the chair supporting us. We let our weight settle downward.  
    
- **We feel our hands.** We notice them. We might hold each other's hands, or simply become aware of our own.  
    
- **We feel gravity together.** We let ourselves feel the pull of the earth, our bodies being held. This sounds abstract, but it's immediately physical: a letting-go of the muscles that keep us braced.  
    
- **From this ground, we turn toward each other.** Physically settled, we become available to each other. "I'm here with you now."

*When to use: When one or both of us feels scattered, anxious, or caught in thought. When we need stable ground before engaging.*

---

### We practice sensory arrival

Sensory arrival uses what we can see, hear, and feel to land us both in the same present moment.

- **We name what we see.** Taking turns, we each name something we notice. "I see the light on the wall." "I see your face."  
    
- **We name what we hear.** We each name a sound. "I hear the rain." "I hear the clock."  
    
- **We name what we feel physically.** We each name a body sensation. "I feel warmth on my arms." "I feel the chair against my back."  
    
- **We arrive through shared sensing.** By attending to immediate sensory experience together, we land in the same moment.  
    
- **We acknowledge arrival.** "I'm here now." "Me too."

This practice is especially helpful when one or both of us is stuck in our heads — worried, planning, ruminating. The senses are always in the present tense. They pull us out of time-travel and into the room.

*When to use: When caught in thought, when entering shared time, when wanting to land in the same moment.*

---

### We practice the arrival check-in

This practice makes the transition from absence to presence explicit between us.

- **One of us initiates.** "Can we arrive together?" Or simply: "Hey, I want to be present with you."  
    
- **We both pause.** Whatever we were doing, we stop.  
    
- **We make contact.** Eye contact, a touch, or simply orienting toward each other.  
    
- **We each share one thing.** Something about how we're arriving. "I'm coming from a stressful call." "I'm feeling scattered." "I'm happy to see you."  
    
- **We acknowledge each other.** We let the other know we received what they shared. A nod, a word, a squeeze of the hand.  
    
- **We acknowledge shared presence.** "Okay — I'm here now." "Me too. Let's be here together."

The arrival check-in is particularly useful because it honors reality. We don't pretend we were already present. We name where we're coming from, and we consciously choose to arrive.

*When to use: Before important conversations, when reuniting, when noticing we've been together but not present.*

---

### We practice mutual anchoring

Anchoring gives us a shared reference point that helps both of us stay present over longer periods.

- **We choose a shared anchor.** Something we can both attend to: physical contact like holding hands, awareness of each other's breathing, a shared focal point in the room.  
    
- **We keep part of our attention on the anchor.** While engaging in conversation or activity, we both maintain background contact with our shared anchor.  
    
- **When either of us notices wandering, we return to the anchor.** The anchor is home base. When we drift, we come back to it.  
    
- **We let the anchor stabilize our shared presence.** Our attention can move and flow naturally — it doesn't need to be rigid. But the anchor gives us something to return to, a thread that keeps us connected even as our minds wander.

Over time, we find that the anchor becomes less necessary; the habit of shared presence builds its own momentum. But in the beginning, having something tangible to return to is genuinely helpful.

*When to use: During longer conversations, during time together where attention is easily pulled, when we want sustained presence.*

---

### We practice presence check-ins

Regular check-ins help us stay present together over longer stretches of time rather than drifting without noticing.

- **We agree to check in.** At the beginning of our time together, we decide to do periodic presence check-ins.  
    
- **Either of us can call a check-in.** At any point: "Presence check?" or "Are we still here?"  
    
- **We both pause and assess.** Are we here? Have we drifted? We share honestly: "I'm here." Or: "I drifted — let me come back."  
    
- **We re-arrive if needed.** If either of us has wandered, we use one of our arriving practices to land together again.  
    
- **We continue with renewed presence.**

The check-in takes a few seconds. It interrupts nothing important, and it often reveals that we drifted without realizing it. The noticing itself is the practice.

*When to use: During extended time together, during important conversations, anytime we want to actively maintain shared presence.*

---

### We practice staying through difficulty

One of the most common reasons we leave presence is discomfort. Something hard arises — tension, disagreement, an awkward silence — and we check out. This practice builds our capacity to stay.

- **We notice when difficulty arises.** Something uncomfortable shows up between us.  
    
- **We notice the impulse to leave.** Each of us might feel the pull to check out, change the subject, reach for a phone, or withdraw into ourselves.  
    
- **We name it if that helps.** "This is hard. I notice my mind wants to wander." "Me too. Can we stay present together?"  
    
- **We stay anyway.** Together, we choose to stay present rather than flee. Not forcing resolution, not performing ease — just staying here with each other and with what's hard.  
    
- **We support each other in staying.** If one of us starts to drift, the other can gently invite return: "I'm still here."

This is where presence practice becomes more than a nice-to-have. The ability to stay present with each other through difficulty is one of the most important capacities a relationship can develop. It changes everything, because what we can stay present with, we can actually work through.

*When to use: When discomfort arises between us, when we're tempted to avoid something, when staying matters more than comfort.*

---

### We learn to notice absence

We cannot return from absence without first noticing it. This practice develops our ability to recognize when presence between us has faded.

- **We learn each other's absence signatures.** What does it look like when the other person is not present? Distracted eyes? Flat responses? Checking the phone? A kind of mechanical quality in conversation? We share what we notice in ourselves and learn to recognize it in each other.  
    
- **We give permission to name it.** We agree that either of us can gently say: "You seem far away. Are you here?"  
    
- **We celebrate the noticing.** When either of us catches absence — in ourselves or each other — that’s success, not failure. The noticing *is* the return beginning.  
    
- **We stay kind.** We name absence with warmth, not accusation. "I notice you drifted. Would you come back?" Not "You're not listening again."

The difference between naming absence with gentleness and naming it with frustration is enormous. One invites return. The other creates shame, which drives people further away from presence.

*When to use: As an ongoing awareness. Also as a specific practice when we want to develop our ability to catch absence earlier.*

---

### We practice gentle return

When one or both of us notices absence, the return should be an invitation, not a correction.

- **We notice the absence.** One or both of us recognizes that presence has faded.  
    
- **We name it without judgment.** "I was gone for a minute." "We seem to have drifted."  
    
- **We invite return.** "Come back to me?" "Let's land here again." Like inviting someone home, not dragging them back.  
    
- **We arrive together again.** Using any of our arriving practices, we land in the present moment together.  
    
- **We continue without dwelling.** Without analyzing the absence or making it a problem, we move forward with renewed presence.

The key word is *gentle*. Harsh correction teaches us to hide our absence rather than acknowledge it. Gentleness teaches us that returning is safe, which means we return sooner.

*When to use: Anytime presence has faded and we want to come back to each other.*

---

### We practice calling each other back

This is the active form of invitation: using our voice, our presence, or our touch to call the other person home when they've drifted.

- **We notice the other has drifted.** Their eyes are glazed, they're elsewhere, their responses have gone flat.  
    
- **We choose to invite return.** Not to criticize. To offer connection.  
    
- **We use gentle language.** "Hey, come back?" "Where did you go?" "I miss you, are you here?"  
    
- **We wait.** We give them a moment to land.  
    
- **We welcome them back.** "There you are." No judgment. Just warmth.

This is an act of care. We are not policing each other's attention. We are offering the gift of being wanted — of someone noticing our absence and asking us to return. Most of us have deep hunger for this. To be missed, even across the distance of a few minutes of distraction.

*When to use: When we notice the other person has drifted and we want to invite them back.*

---

### We create presence in ordinary moments

Formal practices matter, but presence really develops in the everyday moments that seem too small to count.

- **When we greet each other,** we try to actually arrive. A hug with attention. Eye contact. A moment of settling before we launch into logistics.  
    
- **When we eat together,** we put phones away. We arrive before eating. We are here for the meal and for each other.  
    
- **When we talk,** we turn toward each other. We put down what we're doing. We don't listen while doing something else.  
    
- **When we're doing mundane things together** — cooking, cleaning, walking — we let these be times of shared presence rather than parallel distraction.  
    
- **When we notice absence,** we name it and re-arrive rather than accepting that we're together but not actually with each other.

None of these require special time. They require only the choice to be here rather than elsewhere. Over time, these small choices build a culture of presence in the relationship: a shared expectation that when we are together, we are *together*.

*When to use: Continuously, as an orientation in daily life.*

---

### We practice being together in silence

Silence is where presence becomes most clear. When there is nothing to say and nothing to do — just two people sharing the same moment — we discover how much of our togetherness depends on activity and how much can simply *be*.

- **We sit together with no agenda.** No conversation required. No activity. Just being in each other's company.  
    
- **We notice what arises.** Does the silence feel comfortable? Awkward? Restful? Tense? We don't need to fix whatever we find. We simply notice.  
    
- **We let the silence be enough.** We resist the urge to fill it with talk or activity. We let the silence hold us.  
    
- **We notice what happens in our bodies.** Do we settle? Does anxiety rise? Can we relax into the quiet?  
    
- **We acknowledge what we experienced.** Afterward, we might share: "That was peaceful." "I noticed I wanted to fill the silence." "I felt really close to you."

Many of us have never practiced being with another person in deliberate, shared silence. It can feel vulnerable; there’s nothing to hide behind. But this vulnerability is also what makes it powerful. In silence, we practice being enough for each other without performance.

*When to use: Regularly, as a way of deepening the quality of presence between us. Especially helpful when our togetherness has become overly busy or task-oriented.*

## Working with challenges

**When we have very different attention styles.** Some of us are naturally more present. Others have busier minds, more restless attention, or conditions like ADHD that make sustained presence genuinely harder.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We don't make one style the standard.** The more present person doesn't get to be the "better" one. We're working with different nervous systems.  
- **We meet in the middle.** The more present person practices patience. The busier-minded person practices intention.  
- **We find what helps each of us.** Some people stay more present with movement, touch, or something to hold — like a fidget toy\! We learn each other's conditions for presence.  
- **We value effort, not perfection.** The practice is about showing up, not about being flawless at it.

---

**When devices keep pulling us away.** Screens are specifically designed to capture and hold attention. This is not a personal failing; it’s an engineering problem.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We create device-free zones.** Certain spaces — the dinner table, the bedroom — are screen-free.  
- **We create device-free times.** The first fifteen minutes after coming together. Meals. Before bed.  
- **We put phones out of sight.** Not just face-down; physically away from us. Out of sight reduces the pull. And we turn off our notification sounds.  
- **We treat this as something we protect together** rather than something one person enforces on the other.

---

**When absence has become our normal.** Some relationships have spent so long in parallel distraction that real presence feels unfamiliar or even uncomfortable.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We start small.** Five minutes of deliberate presence is better than an hour of trying and failing.  
- **We expect awkwardness.** Being truly present after a long absence can feel strange. This is normal.  
- **We build gradually.** We don't try to overhaul our patterns overnight. We add presence to one moment at a time.  
- **We keep going.** The unfamiliarity fades. What felt awkward begins to feel like coming home.

---

**When presence brings up difficult feelings.** Sometimes when we actually slow down and are here with each other, feelings surface that we've been avoiding: grief, loneliness, resentment, longing.

**What we've found helps:**

- **We understand this is a feature, not a bug.** Presence reveals what's actually here. That includes what we've been not-feeling.  
- **We don't blame presence for what it reveals.** The difficult feelings were already there. Presence just gave them room to be noticed.  
- **We stay if we can.** This is a version of staying through difficulty. The feelings that surface in presence are often the ones most needing our attention.  
- **We seek support if needed.** If what surfaces is overwhelming, we may need the support of a loved one or a professional. That's not failure; that's wisdom.

## The larger view

Presence isn’t one relational skill among many. It’s the ground on which all other connection operates.

Without presence, we can’t attune to each other; attunement requires actually being here to sense what's happening. Without presence, we can’t co-regulate; our nervous systems settle together through contact, not proximity. Without presence, we can’t build trust; trust forms through thousands of small responsive moments, and responsiveness requires being here for them.

Every other practice in this series depends on presence. It’s the foundation beneath the foundation.

Presence isn’t something we achieve and then have. It’s an ongoing act of choosing — choosing to be here, choosing each other, choosing this moment. Our attention will always wander. We will always need to return. Different seasons of life will bring different challenges to presence. The practice continues as long as we’re in relationship.

This isn’t discouraging. It means that every moment is a fresh opportunity to arrive. No matter how long we've been absent, the present moment is always available, and we can always meet each other in it.

When we're present with each other, we become more capable of presence with everyone. Our children, friends, and communities feel the difference between people who are actually here and people who are going through the motions. In a world of epidemic distraction, every moment of genuine mutual presence is a quiet act of resistance — a choice to actually meet rather than merely coexist.

## Our commitment to presence

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice being present with each other. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will not always be here. Our minds will wander. We will get lost in phones, in worries, in plans. We will sit side by side in separate worlds more often than we want.

But we now have a shared practice. We know what presence feels like and why it matters. We know how to arrive, how to stay, and how to return. And we know that the returning is the practice; that coming back, again and again, is not failure but faithfulness.

May we arrive here, again and again. May we stay with each other through whatever comes. May we return to each other with gentleness when we drift. May we give each other the gift of actually being here.

We were not meant to be alone in the present moment. We have each other.

Let us be here together.
