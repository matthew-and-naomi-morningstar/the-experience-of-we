# Learning to Recognize Non-Verbal Cues Together: *A Shared Practice for Reading the Unspoken*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Most of what we communicate, we never say.

Our words carry only a fraction of the message. The rest travels through other channels: the face, the body, the voice, the eyes, the energy of presence. A sigh says what sentences cannot. A tightening of the jaw speaks volumes. The way someone's body turns toward us or away, the speed of their breath, the light in their eyes — all of this is communication, constant and eloquent.

When we learn to read these unspoken channels, we gain access to a richer, more honest stream of information. Bodies don't lie the way words can. Someone may say "I'm fine" while their shoulders carry the weight of the world. Someone may say "I'm not upset" while their jaw clenches and their eyes harden. When we can read these signals, we can respond to what's actually happening, not just what's being said.

This is also the foundation of attunement — the felt sense of being in sync with another person. Attunement operates largely through non-verbal perception: sensing shifts in energy, reading micro-expressions, feeling the quality of presence. When we develop this capacity together, we become able to sense each other more accurately, to catch distress early, and to know when something unspoken needs attention.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to read and respond to non-verbal communication.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing the exercises** with curiosity and gentleness
- **Returning regularly**, because non-verbal literacy is something we keep developing, not something we master once

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it feels like to be read accurately, to read another, and what shifts when we attend to the unspoken.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how non-verbal communication works, what it reveals, and how we can develop skill in perceiving it.
- **Practice** is what we actively do to cultivate non-verbal awareness: the concrete ways we learn to recognize and work with non-verbal cues.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Non-verbal awareness is an experience

*There's a distinctive quality to perceiving and being perceived beyond words.*

### When we're read accurately

When we're read accurately, several things register at once:

- **Being truly seen.** Someone is picking up what we're not saying — the fuller truth.
- **Relief.** We don't have to find words for everything. Our state is being received.
- **Intimacy.** Someone knows us well enough to read our signals.
- **Vulnerability.** We can't hide as easily. What we're feeling shows.
- **Connection.** We're being met on a deeper channel than words alone.

When someone reads our non-verbal cues accurately and responds to them, we feel known in a way that word-only communication can't provide.

### When we read another accurately

Reading non-verbal cues has its own quality:

- **Perception.** We're taking in information beyond words — seeing the whole person.
- **Attunement.** We're sensing into their state, feeling our way toward understanding.
- **Clarity.** We see what's really happening, not just what's being said.
- **Compassion.** Often what we see evokes care — we perceive the struggle beneath the surface.
- **Responsibility.** With perception comes the question of how to respond to what we see.

Reading the unspoken is an act of attention — a willingness to perceive more than is offered explicitly.

### When non-verbal cues are missed

When the unspoken goes unread:

- **Disconnection.** We're only receiving partial information.
- **Misunderstanding.** We respond to words while missing the emotional truth.
- **Loneliness.** We're not being fully seen, even when we're talking.
- **Buildup.** What isn't perceived can't be addressed. The unspoken accumulates.
- **Confusion.** Something feels off, but we can't identify what.

Missing non-verbal cues means missing the richest part of communication.

### The unspoken channel

Non-verbal communication flows constantly:

- Even in silence, bodies are speaking
- Even when words say one thing, the body may say another
- The speed, texture, and quality of communication all carry meaning
- What's avoided or left unsaid is itself a signal
- Energy, presence, and aliveness communicate beyond any specific cue

This channel is always on. The question is whether we're tuned in.

**A reflection for us:** What non-verbal signals does our companion send that we've learned to read? What signals do we send that we wish they understood better?

## Non-verbal awareness is a process

*Understanding how non-verbal communication works helps us develop skill in perceiving it.*

### What non-verbal cues include

The unspoken channel carries many kinds of information:

**Facial expression.** The face is enormously expressive — emotions flash across it, often before we're aware of them ourselves. Micro-expressions can reveal feelings we're trying to hide.

**Body posture.** How we hold ourselves speaks: open or closed, toward or away, tense or relaxed, collapsed or expanded.

**Gesture.** Hand movements, shifts in position, self-touching, reaching toward or pulling away.

**Eyes.** Where we look, how long we hold gaze, whether eyes are soft or hard, bright or dull.

**Voice.** Beyond words: tone, pace, pitch, volume, rhythm, tremor, breath in the voice.

**Breathing.** Fast or slow, shallow or deep, held or flowing.

**Tension patterns.** Where the body holds: jaw, shoulders, hands, belly.

**Proximity and orientation.** How close we are, whether we're facing or turned away.

**Energy and presence.** The harder-to-name quality of aliveness, openness, or withdrawal.

**What's not said.** Silences, topic avoidances, things conspicuously left unmentioned.

### Why non-verbal matters

Non-verbal communication is significant because:

**Volume.** Research suggests that the majority of emotional communication is non-verbal. Words are the smaller channel.

**Honesty.** Bodies don't lie as easily as words. Non-verbal cues often reveal what's being hidden or not yet conscious.

**Speed.** Non-verbal signals often appear before words, expressing reactions in real-time.

**Consistency checking.** When words and body match, we trust the message. When they conflict, non-verbal is usually more reliable.

**Depth.** Emotional states that are hard to articulate often express clearly through body, voice, and face.

### Everyone has a baseline

Each person has their own non-verbal patterns:

- Resting facial expressions that don't mean what they might in someone else
- Habitual postures and gestures
- Typical pace and tone of voice
- Personal baseline of energy and presence

Reading someone accurately requires knowing their baseline — what's normal for them — so we can perceive departures from it.

This is why partners can read each other better than strangers: we've learned each other's baseline. We know what "normal" looks like, so we can see when something's different.

### Non-verbal matching and mismatching

Sometimes words and body match: "I'm happy" with a relaxed face and bright eyes. This is congruent communication.

Sometimes they don't match: "I'm fine" with tight jaw, crossed arms, and clipped tone. This is incongruent communication.

When we notice incongruence, something important is signaled: the words aren't the whole story. This isn't necessarily deception — often the person isn't fully aware of what their body is expressing. But it's an invitation to attend more carefully.

## Non-verbal awareness is a practice

These are our best practices for developing shared non-verbal awareness. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We learn each other's baseline

Studying what's normal to perceive what's different.

- We observe each other over time, noting patterns: resting face, typical posture, usual energy level, normal voice quality.
- We share what's normal for us: "When I'm thinking hard, I get quiet — it doesn't mean I'm upset."
- We learn to distinguish baseline patterns from meaningful signals: "Your jaw is tighter than usual — what's happening?"
- We update our understanding as each other changes over time.
- We appreciate that knowing each other's baseline is an ongoing learning, not a fixed achievement.
- We recognize that accurate reading depends on knowing what we're comparing to.

*When to use: Ongoing, as foundation for non-verbal reading. When we're not sure if something is a signal or just their normal. When we want to improve accuracy.*

---

### We attend to the body, not just words

Widening our attention to include the unspoken.

- When our companion is speaking, we look at their body as well as listening to their words.
- We notice: face, posture, hands, breathing, tension, energy.
- We let the body inform our understanding of what they're communicating.
- We hold words and non-verbal together: What is the whole message, spoken and unspoken?
- We practice shifting attention between content (what they're saying) and form (how they're saying it).
- We develop the habit of asking ourselves: What is their body telling me?

*When to use: During any conversation. When we want fuller understanding. When words alone don't seem like the whole picture.*

---

### We notice when words and body don't match

Using incongruence as a signal.

- We watch for moments when what's said and what's shown don't align.
- We note it internally without immediately challenging: *Their words say fine, but their body says stressed.*
- We stay curious rather than accusatory; incongruence isn't lying — it's often unconscious.
- We might gently inquire: "You're saying you're okay, but I'm noticing some tension. What's happening?"
- We trust what we see, even when words say otherwise — not to prove them wrong, but to open a fuller conversation.
- We hold both: "I hear that you're fine, and I notice your body seems to be holding something. Both can be true."

*When to use: When we perceive mismatch. When something seems off despite reassuring words. When we want to address what's under the surface.*

---

### We check our readings

Treating our perceptions as hypotheses, not facts.

- We recognize that we can misread — our perception is influenced by our own state, history, and projections.
- We offer our readings tentatively: "I notice your shoulders are up — I'm wondering if you're stressed. Am I reading that right?"
- We give them the chance to confirm, correct, or explore.
- We don't insist we're right when they say we're not — we hold our reading lightly.
- We learn from corrections: if we keep misreading certain signals, that's useful information.
- We remember that we're learning to read them — accuracy develops over time.

*When to use: Always. Any time we're acting on non-verbal perception. When we want to avoid projection or assumption.*

---

### We name what we see

Making the unspoken speakable.

- We develop shared vocabulary for non-verbal observations: "You're doing that thing with your jaw." "I can see you're activated."
- We name our own non-verbal states: "My shoulders are up — I'm feeling tense about this."
- We invite each other to name what they see in us: "What do you notice in my body right now?"
- We make the unspoken channel explicit, which develops skill and builds intimacy.
- We use names that are descriptive, not interpretive: "Your eyes look sad" rather than "You're depressed."
- We appreciate that naming brings the unspoken into shared awareness where it can be worked with.

*When to use: When we want to develop shared vocabulary. When bringing something unspoken into words would help. When we want to train each other in reading us.*

---

### We track changes in real-time

Noticing shifts as they happen.

- During conversations, we notice when something shifts: face changes, body tightens or relaxes, energy moves.
- We treat shifts as significant: something just happened.
- We might comment: "Something just shifted — I saw your face change. What happened?"
- We pay attention to what triggered the shift: what was said, what topic was approached.
- We use shifts as doorways; they often point to something important.
- We stay present to the unfolding rather than locking into a static read.

*When to use: During important conversations. When we want to catch what's happening in real-time. When we notice something shifted and want to explore it.*

---

### We attend to our own non-verbal communication

Noticing what we're broadcasting.

- We develop awareness of our own non-verbal signals: What is my face doing? How am I holding my body? What is my tone?
- We notice when our body might be communicating something we haven't consciously acknowledged.
- We share what we notice: "I'm aware my arms are crossed — I don't mean to be closed off. I'm just cold."
- We take responsibility for our non-verbal messages: what we express non-verbally affects our companion.
- We work toward congruence: aligning what we feel, what we say, and what our body expresses.
- We invite feedback: "What are you reading from me right now?"

*When to use: When we want to be more aware of what we're communicating. When we suspect our body is saying something we haven't addressed. When we want to invite feedback.*

## Working with challenges

**When we misread cues.** We perceive something that isn't there, or misinterpret what we see.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Accepting that misreading is inevitable.** We're making inferences, not reading minds.
- **Checking our readings.** "I'm sensing you're upset — am I getting that right?"
- **Updating without defensiveness.** When corrected, we adjust rather than insist.
- **Noticing patterns in our misreading.** Are we prone to certain projections? Accuracy improves with practice and feedback.

---

**When we see something they're denying.** Their body says one thing, their words another, and they insist the words are true.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Holding both possibilities.** They may be unaware of what their body is showing. They may not be ready to acknowledge it.
- **Sharing our observation without insisting.** "I hear that you're fine. I'm also noticing some things that make me wonder. I'll trust what you say, and I'm here if there's more."
- **Leaving the door open.** Sometimes the most we can do is name what we see and let them come to it in their own time.

---

**When we project our own state onto them.** We see in them what we're feeling ourselves.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Checking our own state first.** Before interpreting their non-verbal cues, we ask: What am I feeling right now? Might I be projecting?
- **Using tentativeness.** "I'm not sure if this is me or you, but I'm sensing tension."
- **Asking them to help clarify.** Their perspective helps us distinguish perception from projection.
- **Recognizing that self-awareness is the antidote to projection.**

---

**When non-verbal reading feels invasive.** They feel exposed by our perception, like they can't have privacy.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Remembering that reading cues doesn't require acting on them.** We can perceive without demanding. We can notice without naming every observation.
- **Asking permission.** "I'm noticing something — would you like me to share what I see?"
- **Respecting their right to privacy.** Just because we can read something doesn't mean we should call it out.

---

**When we have very different non-verbal styles.** One of us is expressive; the other is more contained. Reading becomes difficult.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Learning their particular language.** Contained expression doesn't mean less feeling — it means subtler signals.
- **Studying the smaller cues.** We pay closer attention to what might be easy to miss.
- **Asking for guidance.** "Your face doesn't move much — help me learn what to look for when you're upset."
- **Appreciating the difference** rather than judging one style as better.

## The larger view

Non-verbal communication is the older language. Before words, there were bodies — faces, gestures, sounds, presence. This channel still runs constantly beneath our verbal exchanges, carrying the emotional truth of what's happening.

When we develop skill in reading this channel, we become able to perceive more of reality. We see beneath surfaces. We catch what's being avoided. We feel shifts in energy before they become conflicts. We attune to each other at a level that words alone cannot reach.

This capacity is also the foundation of empathy. To feel with another person, we must first perceive them accurately. Non-verbal reading gives us access to their actual state — not just what they're telling us, but what they're showing us. From accurate perception, compassion naturally arises.

In a world that often privileges words, attending to the unspoken is countercultural. But it's also a return to something fundamental: the recognition that we are bodies communicating with bodies, presences affecting presences, and that most of what matters passes without words.
