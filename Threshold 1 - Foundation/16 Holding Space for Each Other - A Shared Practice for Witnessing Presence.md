# Holding Space for Each Other: *A Shared Practice for Witnessing Presence*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Sometimes what we need isn't to be fixed, not to be advised, not to be made to feel better. Sometimes what we need is simply to be witnessed — to have someone present with us while we experience what we're experiencing, without interference.

This is holding space: pure presence without agenda. We're not trying to change anything, solve anything, or make anything better. We're simply being with. We offer our presence as a container — a safe, steady space within which the other can experience whatever needs to move through them.

This is harder than it sounds. We're wired to fix. When someone we love is in pain, we want to make it stop. We offer advice, solutions, reassurance. We try to talk them out of their feelings or rush them through to the other side. All of this comes from care — but it can also communicate that their experience is too much, that it needs to change, that we can't simply be with them in it.

Holding space says something different: *I am here. You can experience whatever you're experiencing. I will stay present. I won't try to change you or fix you. I trust your process. I will witness.*

This is one of the most profound gifts we can offer another person.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to hold space for each other's experiences without fixing or interfering.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or taking turns
- **Practicing the exercises** when neither person is in crisis
- **Returning to this guide** when the urge to fix has taken over
- **Building shared vocabulary** for asking for and offering held space

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what it feels like to have space held for us, to hold space for another, and the quality of witnessing presence.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: what holding space is, what it requires, and how it differs from other forms of support.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the concrete ways we develop the capacity to witness without interfering.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Holding space is an experience

*Holding space is what it feels like to be witnessed without interference — the permission, safety, and trust that arise when someone offers their presence without trying to change what we're going through.*

### When space is held for us

There's a distinctive quality to being witnessed without interference:

- **Permission.** We can feel what we feel without pressure to change it.
- **Safety.** Someone is here — we're not alone — but they're not trying to control our experience.
- **Trust.** Their presence communicates confidence in our capacity to move through this.
- **Spaciousness.** There's room for whatever needs to happen. No rushing.
- **Being accompanied.** We're witnessed, not abandoned; supported, not managed.

When someone truly holds space, we often find that our experience moves more freely. Without the pressure to change, change becomes possible.

### When we hold space for another

Holding space has its own internal quality:

- **Stillness.** We're not doing; we're being. Our inner activity quiets.
- **Groundedness.** We offer stability — a steady presence amidst their storm.
- **Restraint.** The impulse to fix arises, and we don't follow it.
- **Openness.** We're receptive to whatever they're experiencing without needing it to be different.
- **Trust.** We believe in their capacity to move through this at their own pace.

Holding space isn't passive — it takes real presence and attention. But the activity is internal, not external. We're doing something by not doing.

### When fixing replaces witnessing

In contrast, when we try to fix instead of witness:

- **Pressure.** The person feels pushed to change, resolve, or feel better.
- **Invalidation.** The message received is: your experience isn't okay as it is.
- **Loneliness.** Even though someone is trying to help, we feel unseen.
- **Resistance.** Our experience digs in, defended against the attempt to change it.
- **Disconnection.** The helper is meeting their own discomfort, not our need.

The urge to fix often comes from love — but it can land as abandonment of our actual experience.

### The quality of witnessed experience

Experience moves differently when it's witnessed:

- Emotions that are allowed can complete themselves. Grief that is held can grieve itself out.
- Thoughts that are witnessed can clarify. Confusion held without impatience can find its way.
- States that aren't pushed against can transform. What we resist persists; what we witness can shift.

This is the paradox: by not trying to change the experience, we create conditions where change becomes possible.

**A reflection for us:** When has someone's pure witnessing presence — without fixing or advising — been exactly what we needed? What did it feel like to be held that way?

## Holding space is a process

*Holding space works through offering pure witnessing presence — being with rather than doing to — and understanding what distinguishes it from fixing, rescuing, or abandoning.*

### What holding space is

Holding space is offering pure witnessing presence — being with another person's experience without trying to change, fix, solve, or improve it.

It includes:

**Presence.** We're fully here — available, attentive, grounded.

**Non-interference.** We're not trying to alter their experience in any way.

**Containment.** We offer ourselves as a steady container within which their experience can unfold.

**Trust.** We have confidence in their capacity to move through this.

**Witness.** We see them, acknowledge their experience, and stay present to it.

### What holding space is not

Holding space is often confused with other things:

**Not fixing.** We're not offering solutions, advice, or strategies.

**Not reassuring.** We're not trying to make them feel better with "It'll be okay" or "You're fine."

**Not distracting.** We're not changing the subject or lightening the mood.

**Not interpreting.** We're not explaining their experience to them or analyzing it.

**Not relating.** We're not sharing our own similar experiences (unless asked).

**Not rescuing.** We're not trying to save them from their experience.

All of these can be appropriate in other contexts. But when someone needs space held, they need presence without agenda.

### Why we want to fix

The impulse to fix is natural. It comes from:

**Love.** We don't want them to suffer.

**Discomfort.** Their pain is hard for us to witness.

**Helplessness.** We feel we should do something — just being feels insufficient.

**Habit.** We're trained to solve problems, not sit with them.

**Our own anxiety.** Their unsettled state unsettles us, and fixing would calm us too.

Understanding these impulses helps us recognize them without acting on them.

### When holding space is needed

Space-holding is particularly valuable:

**When emotions need to move.** Grief, fear, anger — these need expression, not management.

**When someone is processing.** They're working something through and need a witness, not a guide.

**When the situation can't be fixed.** Loss, illness, circumstances beyond control.

**When they've explicitly asked.** "I don't need advice, I just need you to listen."

**When fixing has failed.** When repeated attempts to help have pushed them further away.

**When presence is the medicine.** Sometimes the simple fact of not being alone is what heals.

### The difference between holding space and abandoning

Holding space is active, not absent:

**Abandonment:** "I can't help you with this. Good luck."
**Holding space:** "I'm here with you while you go through this."

**Abandonment:** Leaving them alone because we can't fix it.
**Holding space:** Staying present precisely because we can't — and don't need to — fix it.

Holding space is engaged non-doing. We're fully present; we're just not interfering.

**A reflection for us:** Which is harder for us: holding space without fixing, or receiving held space without performing? What makes each difficult?

## Holding space is a practice

*These are our best practices for developing the capacity to hold space together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.*

### We offer presence without agenda

The core practice of holding space.

- **When our companion is going through something difficult,** we offer to simply be present.
- **We let go of any goal for the interaction** — we're not trying to produce any particular outcome.
- **We bring our full attention to them,** grounded in our own body.
- **We stay with whatever arises,** without pushing toward resolution.
- **We communicate through our presence:** *I'm here. You're not alone. Take the time you need.*
- **We let silence be okay.** We let tears be okay. We let not-knowing be okay.

*When to use: When someone is processing emotion. When situations can't be fixed. When our companion asks for presence rather than advice. When fixing has failed.*

---

### We notice and restrain the urge to fix

Working with our own impulse to interfere.

- **We notice when the urge arises:** the impulse to offer advice, reassurance, perspective, or solutions.
- **We recognize it as coming from care** — and from our own discomfort.
- **We don't act on it.** We let the urge be there without following it.
- **We might internally note:** *There's the fixing impulse. I'm going to stay with witnessing.*
- **We return our attention** to simply being present with them.
- **We trust that not-fixing** is itself a profound form of help.

*When to use: Continuously while holding space. When we feel the pull to do something. When our own discomfort with their pain wants to act.*

---

### We stay grounded in our own body

Offering stable presence through our own regulation.

- **While holding space,** we maintain connection with our own body — our feet, our breath, our center.
- **We don't lose ourselves** in their experience. We stay present as ourselves.
- **We regulate our own nervous system** so we can offer steady presence.
- **If we become too activated,** we can take a breath, feel our feet, find our ground.
- **Our groundedness is part of what we offer** — a stable shore beside their moving waters.
- **We can be moved by their experience** without being swept away by it.

*When to use: Throughout holding space. When we feel ourselves becoming ungrounded. When their experience is intense.*

---

### We let their experience complete itself

Trusting the natural movement of emotion and process.

- **We recognize that experiences want to complete themselves** when allowed.
- **We don't rush them toward resolution.** We trust the timing of their process.
- **We stay present through waves** — emotion often moves in cycles, intensifying before releasing.
- **We don't interpret pauses** as invitations to fix. We let silence be part of the process.
- **We communicate trust:** "Take all the time you need."
- **We witness the natural movement** that happens when experience isn't interfered with.

*When to use: When we're tempted to hurry them along. When the process seems slow. When we want to trust their natural healing capacity.*

---

### We use minimal, non-directive responses

Staying present verbally without taking over.

- **If we speak, we keep it simple and non-directive:** "I'm here." "I'm with you." "Take your time." "That sounds really hard."
- **We avoid anything that directs their process:** advice, interpretation, bright-siding, relating.
- **We match their pace** — if they're quiet, we can be quiet.
- **We let our presence speak louder** than our words.
- **We make space for them** to go wherever they need to go.

*When to use: When some verbal acknowledgment feels appropriate. When silence feels too distant but we still want to hold space.*

---

### We ask what kind of support they need

Clarifying whether they want space held or something else.

- **We can ask directly:** "Do you want me to just be with you, or would advice be helpful?"
- **We offer options:** "I can listen, or I can problem-solve, or I can just sit with you. What would help?"
- **We follow their lead.** If they want advice, we can shift. If they want presence, we hold space.
- **We check in as things unfold:** "Is this what you need? Would something else help?"
- **We don't assume we know what they need** — we ask.
- **We respect their answer,** even if it's different from what we'd want to give.

*When to use: At the beginning of a difficult conversation. When we're unsure what kind of support is wanted. When we want to make sure we're offering what they need.*

---

### We practice receiving held space

Learning to let ourselves be witnessed.

- **When we're struggling,** we practice asking for held space: "I don't need solutions. I just need you to be with me."
- **We let ourselves be witnessed** without performing or managing our experience.
- **We trust their presence.** We don't take care of them while they're trying to hold space for us.
- **We let the experience move** however it moves, trusting we're held.
- **We receive the gift of witnessing** — letting it land.
- **We appreciate them** for simply being present.

*When to use: When we need space held. When we're learning to receive this kind of support. When we want to build the practice from the receiving side.*

## Working with challenges

**When we can't stop ourselves from fixing.** The urge is too strong — we keep offering advice or solutions.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Notice without judgment.** Each time we catch ourselves, we're building awareness.
- **Try sitting on our hands** (literally or metaphorically).
- **Remind ourselves:** my job right now is to be, not do.
- **If we slip, we can acknowledge it:** "I'm sorry — I went into fixing mode. Let me just be with you."
- **Practice in lower-stakes moments first.**

---

**When their pain is too much for us to witness.** We become too activated to hold steady presence.

**What we've found helps:**

- **This is real and important to honor.** We can only hold space from relative regulation — if we're drowning, we can't be a stable shore.
- **Take care of our own nervous system:** breathe, ground, feel our feet.
- **If it's too much, it's honest to say:** "I'm struggling to stay grounded. Let me take a moment."
- **Sometimes we need to step away briefly** to return with steadier presence.
- **Know our limits.**

---

**When they want fixing but ask for space.** They say they want presence, but keep looking to us for solutions.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Hold the space anyway.** Sometimes people don't know what they need until they experience it.
- **We can gently reflect:** "It sounds like part of you wants me to fix this. I'm going to keep just being here — is that okay?"
- **They might need to settle into being witnessed** before they can receive it.
- **Trust the process.**

---

**When holding space feels like doing nothing.** We feel useless, like we should be helping.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Reframe what "help" means.** Being present IS doing something — something profound.
- **Remember times we've been truly witnessed:** was that nothing?
- **We're not doing nothing;** we're offering our regulated presence, our attention, our faith in their process.
- **This is one of the most valuable things we can give.**

---

**When they stay stuck despite our presence.** They don't seem to be moving through — they're just stuck.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Trust the process even when it's slow.** Not everything resolves in one sitting.
- **We're offering what we can offer;** we're not responsible for their timeline.
- **Check in:** "Is there something else that would help?"
- **Sometimes stuck is part of the process.** Sometimes they need something other than held space.
- **Stay curious, stay humble, stay present.**

## The larger view

Holding space is one of the most countercultural things we can do. We live in a fix-it culture — a culture that believes every problem should be solved, every pain should be resolved, every difficulty should be overcome. We're uncomfortable with sitting in the fire.

But some things can't be fixed. Loss can't be fixed. Mortality can't be fixed. The fundamental uncertainties of human existence can't be fixed. And even things that can eventually be resolved — grief, confusion, difficult emotions — often need first to be witnessed, not managed.

Holding space teaches us something about the nature of healing. It heals not through doing but through being. It heals through presence, through companionship, through the simple fact of not being alone. It heals by creating conditions where natural processes can complete themselves.

When we hold space for another, we also practice holding space for ourselves — for the parts of us that don't need fixing, that need only to be witnessed. This capacity extends outward: to our families, our communities, to the world. In a fix-it culture, the radical act of simply being present is itself a kind of medicine.

## Our commitment to holding space

By practicing with this guide, we commit to developing our capacity to witness each other without interference — to offer presence without agenda, containment without control.

We will offer presence without needing to fix. We will notice and restrain our urge to make things better. We will stay grounded in our own bodies while we witness. We will trust each other's process and timing. We will use minimal responses that hold rather than direct. We will ask what kind of support is needed. We will practice receiving held space as well as offering it.

We won't always do this well. We'll sometimes fix when presence was wanted. We'll sometimes be too activated to offer steady ground. We'll sometimes rush toward resolution.

But now we have a practice for pure presence. We know that witnessing is itself a form of help. We know that not-doing can be the most powerful thing we offer. We know that being accompanied through difficulty is one of the deepest needs we have.

May we learn to hold space without agenda. May we trust each other's capacity to move through difficulty. May we offer the profound gift of witnessing presence.

Sometimes the greatest love is simply to stay. Let us stay — together.
