# Practicing Deep Listening Together: *A Shared Practice for Full Receptive Presence*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Most of the time, we don't really listen.

We hear words while planning our response. We filter what's said through our own experience, our own agenda, our own interpretations. We wait for pauses so we can speak. We interrupt; if not out loud, then internally, finishing sentences in our minds, jumping ahead to conclusions.

This is not listening. It is a kind of simultaneous translation, where we convert what someone says into our own terms before we've truly received it.

Deep listening is something else entirely. It is full receptive presence — attending to another person with our whole being, not just our ears. It is creating space for them to unfold, without rushing, without fixing, without making it about us. It is receiving before responding, understanding before reacting, witnessing before interpreting.

When we are deeply listened to, something profound happens. We feel met. We feel valued. We often discover what we think and feel in the very act of being heard. The listener's presence draws us out, gives us permission, creates safety for expression.

To offer this gift — and to receive it — is one of the deepest practices of relationship.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity for deep listening and full receptive presence.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or in turn
- **Pausing often** to discuss what resonates or raises questions
- **Practicing the exercises** in dedicated time, then extending into daily life
- **Returning regularly**, because listening can become shallow without attention, and this guide can help us find our way back

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what deep listening feels like, both as listener and as the one being heard, and what its absence feels like.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: what deep listening requires, what blocks it, and how it connects to understanding and connection.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the concrete ways we develop the capacity to listen with our whole being.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Deep listening is an experience

*Deep listening is what it feels like when someone receives us with their whole being — and what it feels like to offer that reception.*

### When we listen deeply

There's a distinctive quality to genuine receptive presence:

- **Absorption.** Our attention is fully on the speaker. Nothing else is pulling at us.
- **Spaciousness.** We're not in a hurry. There's room for whatever needs to emerge.
- **Emptiness.** We've set aside our own thoughts, agenda, and response. We're open, receiving.
- **Curiosity.** We want to understand — not to confirm what we think, but to truly receive what they're offering.
- **Stillness.** Our inner chatter quiets. We're simply present.

In deep listening, we're not doing much — we're being. We're not preparing, analyzing, or waiting. We're simply here, fully available to receive.

### When we are deeply heard

Being on the receiving end of deep listening feels like:

- **Being seen.** Someone is actually taking us in, not just hearing sounds.
- **Safety.** We can say what's true without fear of immediate judgment or response.
- **Spaciousness.** We have room to unfold, to find our words, to discover what we think.
- **Importance.** What we're saying matters enough for someone to truly attend.
- **Discovery.** We often find that being heard helps us understand ourselves better.

When someone listens deeply, we exist more fully. We feel met in a way that casual hearing never provides.

### When listening is shallow

In contrast, shallow listening feels like:

- **Divided attention.** They're here, but not fully. Something else is pulling at them.
- **Waiting.** They're waiting for us to finish so they can speak.
- **Translation.** They're converting what we say into their own terms before we've finished.
- **Agenda.** They're listening for something specific — to confirm, to argue, to fix.
- **Rushing.** There's pressure to wrap up, to get to the point.

We can tell when we're not being truly heard. Something essential is missing. We don't feel received.

### The listener's experience

Deep listening isn't passive. It has its own quality:

- **Effort.** It takes real attention to stay fully present without drifting.
- **Restraint.** We hold back our impulses to respond, fix, or relate.
- **Reception.** We're taking something in — not just words, but meaning, feeling, presence.
- **Intimacy.** Deep listening brings us close. We're entering the other's world.
- **Fullness.** Paradoxically, receiving deeply can feel full and nourishing, not empty.

**A reflection for us:** When have we felt truly, deeply listened to by each other — not just heard, but received? What made the difference?

## Deep listening is a process

*Deep listening isn't a single act; it's an unfolding of attention, reception, restraint, and presence that creates the conditions for genuine understanding.*

### What deep listening is

Deep listening is full receptive presence — the act of attending to another person with our whole being, creating space for them to be heard completely before we respond.

It includes:

**Attention.** All of it, not divided. We're not planning, not distracted, not elsewhere.

**Reception.** We're taking in what's offered — words, yes, but also tone, feeling, body, meaning.

**Restraint.** We hold back our impulse to respond, interpret, fix, or relate to our own experience.

**Patience.** We're not rushing toward conclusion. We let things unfold at their own pace.

**Openness.** We receive without filtering through our agenda or assumptions.

**Presence.** We're fully here — available, grounded, with the speaker.

### What deep listening is not

Deep listening is often confused with other things:

**Not waiting to speak.** If we're planning our response, we're not listening. Deep listening receives first, responds later.

**Not agreeing.** Listening deeply doesn't mean we agree with what's said. It means we receive it fully before forming our response.

**Not fixing.** The impulse to solve problems can block reception. Deep listening receives the problem before moving to solutions.

**Not relating.** "That reminds me of when I..." can derail the speaker. Deep listening stays with their experience, not ours.

**Not just silence.** Being quiet while internally distracted is not deep listening. Presence matters more than silence.

### What blocks deep listening

Many forces work against receptive presence:

**Internal noise.** Our own thoughts, worries, and reactions can drown out the speaker.

**Preparation.** Formulating our response while they're still speaking.

**Filtering.** Hearing only what fits our expectations or agenda.

**Judgment.** Evaluating what's said rather than receiving it.

**Impatience.** Wanting them to get to the point, finish faster.

**Defensiveness.** When what's said feels threatening, we stop receiving and start protecting.

**Distraction.** Phones, environment, fatigue — anything that pulls attention away.

**Assumption.** Thinking we already know what they'll say, so we don't really listen.

### Why deep listening matters

Deep listening is not just polite; it's foundational to relationship:

**It creates safety.** Being fully received makes it safe to express what's true.

**It builds trust.** Consistent deep listening demonstrates that the other matters.

**It enables understanding.** We can't understand what we haven't truly received.

**It supports attunement.** Deep listening is how we sense another's inner world.

**It helps the speaker.** Being heard often helps people clarify their own thoughts and feelings.

**It prevents misunderstanding.** Most miscommunication comes from incomplete listening.

**A reflection for us:** What are our most common listening blocks? What tends to take us out of full receptive presence when the other is speaking?

## Deep listening is a practice

These are our best practices for cultivating deep listening together. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice dedicated listening

Creating time for listening without other demands.

- **We set aside time** specifically for one person to speak and the other to listen.
- **The listener's only job is to receive** — no responding, no questions, no reactions (unless invited).
- **The speaker speaks freely,** knowing they won't be interrupted or answered.
- **The listener attends fully:** eyes on the speaker, body oriented toward them, attention unwavering.
- **After a set time, we switch roles.**
- **At the end, we share** what the experience was like — both speaking into deep listening and offering it.

*When to use: As regular practice to build listening capacity. When one person needs to be heard without response. When we want to train ourselves in pure reception.*

---

### We empty before we listen

Preparing ourselves to receive.

- **Before our companion speaks, we take a moment** to set aside our own agenda.
- **We consciously release:** What do I want from this conversation? What am I hoping to hear? What am I afraid they'll say? We put these down.
- **We empty our hands, metaphorically** — we can't receive if we're already holding something.
- **We take a breath.** We arrive in our body. We orient our attention to the person in front of us.
- **We remind ourselves:** My job right now is to receive, not to prepare a response.
- **We begin listening** from this emptied, available place.

*When to use: Before important conversations. When we notice we're bringing an agenda to listening. When we want to be fully receptive.*

---

### We listen beneath words

Receiving what's communicated beyond and beneath language.

- **We listen not just to words** but to: tone, pace, rhythm, volume, emotion, energy.
- **We notice the body:** posture, gesture, face, eyes, movement.
- **We sense into what's not being said:** hesitations, gaps, what's being avoided.
- **We ask ourselves:** What is this person actually communicating? What do they want me to understand?
- **We attend to the whole person,** not just the content of their speech.
- **We recognize** that sometimes what's most important isn't in the words at all.

*When to use: Always — this is full listening. When words and tone don't match. When we sense something beneath the surface.*

---

### We resist the urge to respond

Staying in reception mode rather than switching to response mode.

- **We notice when the impulse to respond arises** — the urge to comment, correct, relate, fix, or answer.
- **We don't follow the impulse.** We stay in listening mode.
- **We let them continue.** We keep receiving.
- **We trust** that we'll have a chance to respond later — right now, our job is to receive.
- **We might internally note:** *I want to respond to that. I'll hold it for now.*
- **We stay present** to what they're saying now, not to our internal reaction to what they said before.

*When to use: Throughout listening. When we notice ourselves preparing to speak. When the impulse to respond threatens to interrupt reception.*

---

### We give presence, not just silence

Being actively present rather than passively quiet.

- **Deep listening is not just being quiet** — it's being fully here.
- **We offer our presence:** our attention, our care, our availability.
- **We use minimal encouragers sparingly** if helpful: "Mm-hmm," "Yes," "I'm with you" — not to interrupt but to signal presence.
- **We stay with them** in our attention, even in their pauses.
- **We let our face, body, and eyes** communicate that we're here and receiving.
- **We recognize** that presence is active — it takes energy and attention to be fully available.

*When to use: Always. When silence might feel like absence. When we want to communicate "I'm here with you."*

---

### We hold what we receive

Containing what's been shared rather than immediately responding.

- **When they finish speaking, we don't rush** to respond.
- **We let what they've shared settle.** We hold it.
- **We might take a breath,** letting the words land fully.
- **We ask ourselves:** Do I really understand what they've shared? Is there more they want to say?
- **We might say:** "Thank you. Let me take that in for a moment."
- **Only when we've truly received** do we move toward responding.

*When to use: After they finish speaking. Before responding. When what's been shared is important or complex.*

---

### We practice listening in conflict

Bringing deep listening into the hardest moments.

- **In conflict, listening is most difficult** and most needed.
- **We recognize** that our defensiveness blocks reception — and we try to soften it.
- **We commit to understanding** their position before defending our own.
- **We ask ourselves:** What are they trying to communicate? What do they need me to understand?
- **We resist the urge** to counter each point — we receive the whole first.
- **We remind ourselves** that listening doesn't mean agreeing — we can hear fully and still have a different perspective.

*When to use: During disagreements. When we feel defensive. When understanding has broken down.*

---

### We support each other's speaking with listening

Recognizing that deep listening creates the conditions for authentic expression.

- **We understand** that how we listen shapes what the other can say.
- **When we listen deeply,** we give permission for more to emerge.
- **We create safety through our reception** — our non-judgment, our patience, our presence.
- **We notice** when our listening helps them find words, go deeper, or discover something.
- **We appreciate this as a gift we give:** the space for them to unfold.
- **We recognize** that we're not just receiving information — we're participating in their expression.

*When to use: As ongoing orientation. When we want to help someone access what they need to say. When expression seems blocked.*

## Working with challenges

**When we can't stop preparing our response.** Our mind keeps formulating answers instead of receiving.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Notice it happening** — that's the first step. Each time we catch ourselves preparing, we gently return to listening.
- **Remind ourselves:** there will be time to respond later.
- **Focus on their voice, their face, their body** — sensory attention can quiet the planning mind.
- **Practice in lower-stakes conversations first** to build the habit before bringing it into harder moments.

---

**When what they're saying is hard to hear.** The content triggers defensiveness that blocks reception.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Acknowledge internally that this is hard:** *I'm having a reaction to this. It's making it hard to listen.*
- **Try to receive the difficulty as information** rather than threat.
- **Breathe. Stay with the body.** Remember that listening isn't agreeing — we can hear them fully and still have our own perspective.
- **If it's too much, it's okay to say:** "I'm having a hard time listening right now. Can we take a break?"

---

**When they go on too long.** Our attention fatigues and we start to drift.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Listening has limits** — this is real. Try re-engaging: focus on their face, their voice, their emotion.
- **Ground in the body.** Physical sensation can re-anchor wandering attention.
- **If we're too fatigued to listen well, it's honest to say:** "I want to hear this fully, and I'm noticing my attention is fading. Can we take a break or continue later?"

---

**When they want us to respond and we're trying to listen.** They keep asking for our reaction, but we haven't finished receiving.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Sometimes people need response, not just reception.** Deep listening is a practice, not a rule.
- **We can ask:** "Do you want me to just listen, or do you want me to respond as you go?"
- **We can offer:** "I want to make sure I understand first — then I'll share my thoughts."
- **We adapt to what they need** rather than rigidly holding to our own practice.

---

**When we disagree with what we're hearing.** Disagreement makes it hard to receive openly.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Disagreement and listening are not opposites.** We can receive something fully and still disagree.
- **The goal is to understand their position completely** — which actually makes disagreement more productive.
- **We listen not to agree but to understand.** We can hold our disagreement lightly while we receive: *I see this differently, and I want to understand how they see it.*

## The larger view

Deep listening is not just a communication technique. It is an act of love.

When we listen deeply, we say without words: *You matter. What you experience matters. I want to understand your world.* This is one of the most profound gifts we can offer another person.

In a world of distraction and partial attention, deep listening has become rare. We are surrounded by noise and information but starved for true reception. When someone really listens — when they set aside their own agenda and simply receive — it can feel like being seen for the first time.

This capacity extends beyond relationship. Deep listening to another person trains us in receptivity itself — the willingness to receive reality as it is before we filter, interpret, or respond. This receptivity is the ground of wisdom.

May we learn to listen with our whole being. May we receive each other fully before we respond. May our presence create space for truth to emerge.

To be deeply heard is to be loved. Let us listen together.
