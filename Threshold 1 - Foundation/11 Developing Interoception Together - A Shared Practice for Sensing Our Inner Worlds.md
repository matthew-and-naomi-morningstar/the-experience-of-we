# Developing Interoception Together: *A Shared Practice for Sensing Our Inner Worlds*

---

Hello, dear companions.

Before we can name what we feel, we must sense it. Before we can communicate our inner state, we must perceive it. Before we can regulate ourselves — or co-regulate with each other — we must have access to the body's information.

This is interoception: the sense of what's happening inside.

Most of us live somewhat disconnected from this inner sensing. We're trained to attend outward — to tasks, to others, to thoughts. The body becomes background noise, noticed only when it screams loud enough to interrupt. We lose touch with the subtle signals: the tightness in the chest that signals anxiety before we've named it, the settling in the belly that signals safety, the constriction in the throat that signals words unspoken.

Without interoception, we fly blind. We don't know what we're feeling until it overwhelms us. We can't communicate inner states we haven't perceived. We miss the body's wisdom — its constant stream of information about what's happening, what's needed, what's true.

In relationship, interoception is foundational. We can't attune to each other if we can't first sense ourselves. We can't co-regulate if we can't perceive our own nervous system states. We can't share what we're experiencing if we don't know what we're experiencing.

This practice develops the capacity to sense — to turn attention inward and perceive the body's inner landscape with increasing clarity and nuance.

~Matthew and Naomi Danielle Morningstar

## How we use this guide

We created this guide to be read and practiced together — by partners, friends, family members, or anyone who wants to develop their shared capacity to sense their inner worlds and share that sensing with each other.

We recommend:

- **Reading together**, aloud or taking turns
- **Practicing the exercises** in a quiet, settled space
- **Returning regularly** to this guide to deepen interoceptive capacity
- **Building shared vocabulary** for inner sensations

We've structured this guide based on the **Golden Triangle**, a framework that helps us understand and consciously shape our experiences. By focusing on three key aspects — **Experience**, **Process**, and **Practice** — we can gain a deeper understanding of our relational world and learn to cultivate the states we want to live in together.

- **Experience** is the felt sense itself: what interoceptive awareness feels like, what its absence feels like, and what becomes possible when we can sense ourselves clearly.
- **Process** is how that experience develops and unfolds: how interoception works, what blocks it, and how it connects to emotional awareness and relational capacity.
- **Practice** is what we actively do: the concrete ways we develop and share our capacity for inner sensing.

The Golden Triangle recognizes the dynamic, cyclical nature of these elements, with each one supporting and enhancing the others in an ongoing process of growth and transformation.

The "we" throughout this guide refers to all of us — whoever is reading and practicing together right now.

We suggest limiting yourself to 1-2 practices at a time, giving each one space to become a natural part of how we are with each other before moving on to the next.

## Interoception is an experience

*Interoception is the felt sense of what's happening inside our bodies — the direct perception of our inner landscape.*

### When we can sense inside

There's a distinctive quality to having interoceptive access:

- **Groundedness.** We're in our bodies, not floating above them.
- **Information.** A constant stream of data about our inner state is available.
- **Early signals.** We catch emotions, needs, and reactions when they're still subtle — before they become overwhelming.
- **Nuance.** We perceive differences between states that might otherwise blur together.
- **Self-knowledge.** We know what we're feeling, not just what we're thinking.

With interoception, the body becomes an ally — a source of information rather than just a vehicle we move through the world in.

### When we're somatically disconnected

Without interoceptive access:

- **Numbness.** We can't sense what's happening inside. It's blank, or vague, or inaccessible.
- **Surprise.** Emotions seem to come from nowhere because we missed the early signals.
- **Confusion.** "How do you feel?" is genuinely hard to answer. We don't know.
- **Living from the neck up.** We're identified with thoughts but disconnected from the body that holds them.
- **Missing information.** The body knows things the mind doesn't, but we can't access that knowing.

Many of us have normalized this disconnection. We don't know what we're missing because we've never had consistent access.

### The vocabulary of sensation

Interoception perceives sensations — not emotions directly, but the physical experiences that accompany and often constitute emotions.

**Location:** Where in the body is the sensation? Chest, throat, belly, shoulders, jaw, hands, face...

**Quality:** What is the sensation like?
- Temperature: warm, cool, hot, cold
- Movement: pulsing, vibrating, still, flowing, churning
- Density: tight, loose, heavy, light, thick, thin
- Texture: smooth, rough, sharp, dull
- Pressure: expanding, contracting, pressing, releasing

**Intensity:** How strong is the sensation? Subtle, moderate, intense, overwhelming...

Learning this vocabulary helps us perceive and communicate what we're sensing. "There's a tightness in my chest" is interoceptive. "I'm anxious" is an interpretation — useful, but one step removed from the direct sensing.

### What becomes possible

When interoceptive capacity develops:

- **Emotional clarity.** We know what we're feeling because we can sense the body's signature of each emotion.
- **Earlier intervention.** We catch activation early, when it's easier to work with.
- **Better communication.** We can share our inner experience with precision.
- **Self-regulation.** We can work with what we can sense.
- **Attunement to others.** Sensing ourselves is the foundation for sensing others.
- **Embodied wisdom.** The body's intelligence becomes accessible — gut feelings, intuitions, somatic knowing.

**A reflection for us:** How well do we each sense what's happening inside our own bodies? When has sharing that inner sensing deepened our connection?

## Interoception is a process

*Interoception isn't a single moment of noticing; it's an ongoing process of turning attention inward and learning to perceive the body's signals with increasing clarity.*

### What interoception is

Interoception is the perception of internal bodily states — the sense that tells us what's happening inside.

It includes sensing:
- **Visceral states:** Heartbeat, breathing, digestion, hunger, fullness, nausea
- **Muscular states:** Tension, relaxation, holding, releasing
- **Temperature:** Internal warmth or coolness
- **Pain and pleasure:** The body's signals of damage or well-being
- **Emotional signatures:** The physical sensations that accompany feelings
- **Energy states:** Fatigue, alertness, activation, calm
- **Spatial sensations:** Expansion, contraction, openness, closure

Unlike our external senses, interoception points inward. It tells us about the body's interior rather than the world outside.

### Interoception and emotion

Emotions are not just mental experiences. They are bodily events — patterns of physiological activation that we then interpret.

Fear involves a racing heart, shallow breathing, tension, mobilization for action.
Sadness involves heaviness, slowing, sometimes pressure behind the eyes, sometimes hollowness in the chest.
Joy involves lightness, expansion, warmth, energy.

Interoception gives us access to these bodily signatures before they're labeled as emotions. This is valuable because:

- The body knows before the mind. Sensing the chest tightening tells us something is happening before we've figured out we're anxious.
- Labels can be wrong. We might think we're angry when the body is actually signaling fear. Interoception gives us the raw data.
- Sensation is workable. We can breathe into a tight chest in a way we can't directly "fix" anxiety.

### What blocks interoception

Many forces work against interoceptive awareness:

**Cultural training.** Many cultures prioritize thinking over sensing. We're taught to analyze, not to feel.

**Busy lives.** When we're constantly doing, there's no space to sense. Interoception requires a certain slowing down.

**Trauma.** Dissociation from the body can be protective when the body holds overwhelming experience. But it comes at the cost of interoceptive access.

**Overstimulation.** Modern life bombards us with external input, drowning out the body's subtler signals.

**Numbing.** Substances, distraction, and busyness can all serve to keep us from feeling what's inside.

**Practice with disconnection.** If we've spent years not sensing, the capacity atrophies. The neural pathways aren't exercised.

### Interoception in relationship

In relationship, interoception serves several crucial functions:

**Self-knowledge for communication.** We can only share what we're experiencing if we can perceive it. "I notice my chest is tight and my breathing is shallow" gives our companion real information.

**Foundation for attunement.** Sensing another person's inner state begins with sensing our own. The same capacity that perceives our heartbeat can learn to perceive shifts in another's energy.

**Co-regulation.** When two nervous systems work together to settle, interoception lets us track the process. Are we settling? Are we still activated?

**Tracking the relationship.** The body responds to relational dynamics. Interoception lets us notice when something feels off — even before we can name what it is.

**A reflection for us:** What internal signals do we tend to ignore — in ourselves and in each other? What would change if we paid closer attention?

## Interoception is a practice

These are our best practices for developing shared interoceptive capacity. This is not an exhaustive list; we encourage you to adjust these and add your own.

### We practice sensing together

Building basic interoceptive capacity in shared practice.

- We find a quiet space and settle together — sitting or lying down, comfortable, unhurried.
- We close our eyes or soften our gaze.
- We take a few breaths to arrive.
- One person guides (or we take turns, or we move through together in silence):
  - "Bring your attention to your body as a whole. What's the general sense of it right now?"
  - "Notice your breathing. Where do you feel it most? What's its quality?"
  - "Scan from head to feet, or feet to head. What sensations do you notice along the way?"
  - "Is there a place that calls for attention? Rest your awareness there. What do you perceive?"
- After several minutes, we share what we noticed — using sensation words, not just emotion labels.
- We receive each other's sharing with curiosity, not interpretation.

*When to use: As regular practice to build interoceptive capacity. When we want to come into our bodies together. As a settling practice before difficult conversations.*

---

### We name sensations, not just emotions

Developing precision in our inner sensing vocabulary.

- When we share how we're feeling, we include the bodily sensation, not just the emotional label.
- Instead of only "I'm anxious," we add: "There's a tightness in my chest and my shoulders are up near my ears."
- Instead of only "I'm sad," we add: "There's a heaviness in my whole body, and something pressing behind my eyes."
- We practice describing sensation with specificity: location, quality, intensity, movement.
- When our companion shares a sensation, we might ask: "Where do you feel that in your body? What's its quality?"
- We build a shared vocabulary over time — learning each other's somatic signatures.

*When to use: Whenever sharing emotional states. When we want to communicate with precision. When we're not sure what we're feeling and want to start with what we can sense.*

---

### We track sensation during conversation

Bringing interoceptive awareness into real-time interaction.

- During important conversations, we periodically check in with our body: What's happening inside right now?
- We notice changes: Did something just shift? Did I tighten? Did something settle?
- We share what we notice when it's relevant: "I notice my chest just tightened when you said that."
- We get curious about each other's somatic experience: "What's happening in your body right now?"
- We use body information as data: if the body is signaling something, it's worth attending to.
- We learn each other's patterns: "When your jaw tightens like that, I've noticed it often means something's bothering you."

*When to use: During meaningful conversations. During conflict. When something important is being discussed. When we sense something is happening but aren't sure what.*

---

### We sense into our relational space

Using interoception to perceive the quality of connection.

- We sit facing each other, close enough to sense each other's presence.
- We take a few breaths and bring attention to our own body first.
- Then we expand awareness to include our companion — not just visually, but sensing their presence.
- We notice: What happens in my body when I'm aware of you? What shifts?
- We notice the space between us: Does it feel open? Closed? Warm? Tense?
- We share what we sensed, without needing to explain or interpret.
- We use this practice to attune — sensing the relational field through the body's response to it.

*When to use: When we want to sense the quality of our connection. Before important conversations. When something feels off and we want to perceive it more clearly.*

---

### We track activation and settling

Developing awareness of nervous system states.

- We learn to recognize our body's signatures of activation:
  - What does mobilization (fight/flight) feel like in my body? (Heart racing, shallow breathing, tension, heat...)
  - What does immobilization (freeze/collapse) feel like? (Heaviness, numbness, slowing, cold...)
  - What does regulated calm feel like? (Steady breathing, open chest, relaxed muscles, warmth...)
- We practice noticing where we are on this spectrum at any given moment.
- We share with each other: "I notice I'm activated right now — my heart is beating fast and my muscles are tense."
- We track settling: "I can feel myself starting to calm — my breathing is slowing, my shoulders are dropping."
- We support each other's regulation by sharing our states and being present with them.

*When to use: During stress or conflict. When co-regulating. When we want to know our own nervous system state. When we want to support each other's settling.*

---

### We listen to the body's knowing

Accessing somatic wisdom.

- We bring a question or situation to the body: "What does my body know about this?"
- We sense into the body's response — not analyzing, but perceiving.
- We notice: Is there a "yes" or "no" quality? An opening or closing? A drawing toward or pulling away?
- We stay with the sensing long enough for it to clarify — the body's knowing sometimes emerges slowly.
- We share with each other: "When I sense into this decision, my body feels [sensation]. I think it's telling me..."
- We don't override the body's knowing with the mind's analysis — we hold them together.

*When to use: When making decisions. When something feels off but we can't name why. When we want to access intuition. When the mind is confused and we want the body's input.*

---

### We restore connection after disconnection

Returning to interoceptive awareness when we've lost it.

- We notice when we've gone numb, spaced out, or disconnected from the body.
- We name it to each other: "I notice I've disconnected — I can't really sense what I'm feeling right now."
- We use gentle anchors to return:
  - Feel your feet on the floor
  - Notice the weight of your body against the chair
  - Place a hand on your chest or belly and feel it rise and fall
  - Notice five things you can sense right now
- We give ourselves time — reconnection doesn't always happen instantly.
- We approach disconnection with compassion: it served a purpose, even if it's not serving us now.

*When to use: When we've gone numb. When we're dissociated. When we can't answer "What are you feeling?" because we genuinely don't know.*

## Working with challenges

**When we can't sense much.** We try to turn attention inward and find mostly blankness.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Start with what's most obvious.** This is common, especially when starting out. Interoceptive capacity develops with practice; the breath is usually accessible.
- **Use touch.** A hand on the chest or belly gives the brain something to notice.
- **Start with external sensations.** The feeling of sitting in the chair, then gradually move inward.
- **Be patient.** The pathways are being built. What seems like blankness is actually the beginning of sensing.

---

**When sensing feels overwhelming.** We turn inward and get flooded.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Titrate.** Small doses rather than full immersion. Touch the sensation briefly, then return to neutral.
- **Have an anchor.** Feet on floor, breath, the sense of the companion's presence.
- **Develop pendulation.** Move between sensing the difficult thing and sensing something neutral or positive.
- **Consider professional support.** If overwhelm is consistent, a somatic therapist can help build capacity safely.

---

**When our sensations don't match our partner's expectations.** We report what we sense and they want us to feel something different.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Remember that interoception is self-reported and personal.** No one else can tell us what we're feeling inside.
- **Hold curiosity rather than expectation.** Our companion might say: "I notice you're saying you feel calm, but I'm sensing tension from you — can we explore that together?"
- **Honor the person sensing as the authority on their own inner experience.** Differences in perception can be explored, not corrected.

---

**When disconnection has been protective.** Numbing kept us safe from overwhelming experience.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Honor the protection before trying to dissolve it.** The body disconnected for good reasons.
- **Approach with gentleness.** "We're going to slowly rebuild connection with a body that learned to protect you by going quiet."
- **Go slowly.** Build resources first — the capacity to settle, the sense of safety with our companion. Reconnection is an invitation, not a demand.
- **Seek professional support when trauma is involved.** This isn't failure; it's wisdom.

---

**When we sense different things.** Partners have different interoceptive experiences of the same interaction.

**What we've found helps:**

- **Both are real.** We're sensing different bodies with different histories.
- **Share differences as valuable information.** "Interesting — when that happened, my chest opened, and yours tightened. What do you make of that?"
- **Explore with curiosity.** Different sensations are information about our different relationships to the same experience, not a question of whose sensing is "right."

## The larger view

Interoception is not just a personal capacity. It is the foundation of embodied relationship — the capacity that lets us be present as bodied beings rather than disembodied minds.

When we develop interoceptive awareness, we're not just learning to feel our own bodies. We're learning to be present in a way that makes deeper connection possible. We're recovering access to the body's intelligence — millions of years of evolved wisdom that operates beneath and beyond thought.

In relationship, interoception creates the possibility for attunement that is body-to-body, not just mind-to-mind. It allows for co-regulation that actually works with the nervous system. It gives us the information we need to communicate what we're experiencing and to perceive what our companion is experiencing.

The body is not separate from relationship. It is the ground on which relationship happens. Developing interoception is developing our capacity to show up fully, as embodied beings, in connection with each other.

## Our commitment to interoception

By reading this guide together, we've made a commitment: to practice developing our capacity to sense our inner worlds and to share that sensing with each other. Not perfectly, but intentionally.

We will practice tuning in to our bodies. We will learn to name sensations with precision. We will track our inner states during conversation. We will sense into our relational space. We will listen to the body's wisdom. We will restore connection when we've lost it.

We will not always do this well. We will sometimes space out, go numb, disconnect. We will sometimes miss the body's signals or struggle to find words for what we sense.

But we now have a shared practice. We know that interoception is a capacity that develops over time. We know that the body holds information we need. We know that sensing ourselves is the foundation for sensing each other.

May we learn to sense our inner worlds with clarity and nuance. May we share what we perceive with honesty and trust. May our bodies become sources of wisdom for our relationship.

We are not just minds. We are embodied beings. Let us sense that embodiment together.
